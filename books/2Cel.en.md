# THE SECOND LIFE OF SAINT FRANCIS

## PROLOGUE <a id="prolog"></a>

<aside>
In the name of our Lord Jesus Christ. Amen. To the Minister General of the Order of Friars Minor. Here begins the Prologue.
</aside>

<p id="1">
[1] It pleased the holy assembly of the general chapter long ago, and you, most reverend father — not without the dispensation of divine counsel — to lay upon our littleness the charge that we, to whom from constant companionship with him and mutual intimacy more than to others he became known through long-continued experience, should set down the deeds and even the sayings of our glorious father Francis, for the consolation of those now living and the remembrance of those to come.
[2] Therefore with humble devotion we hasten to obey these holy commands, which it is in no way permitted to set aside;
[3] yet, weighing more deeply the weakness of our powers, we are struck with a just fear, lest a matter so worthy, not handled as it demands, should draw upon us from others nothing but displeasure.
[4] For these things, <ref to="Wis 16:20">which deserve the savor of all sweetness</ref>, we dread may be rendered insipid by the unworthiness of those who serve them, and so what we have attempted be charged rather to presumption than to obedience.
[5] For if the outcome of so great a labor were to await only the scrutiny of your good will, blessed father, and there were no need for it to come forth to the ears of the public, we would most gladly receive either instruction from your correction or gladness from your approval.
[6] For who, amid so great a variety of words and deeds, could weigh all things upon the scale of fine examination, <ref to="2 Macc 14:20">so that of all who hear there be one judgment concerning each thing</ref>?
[7] But since with simple heart we seek the profit of all and of each, we urge that those who read may interpret kindly, and so bear with or guide the simplicity of those who report, that the reverence due to him of whom we speak may be preserved unharmed.
[8] Our memory, like that of unlearned men, dulled by the long passage of time, cannot reach the swift flights of his subtle words and the astonishing renown of his deeds, which even the quickness of a practiced mind, though set before it, could scarcely grasp.
[9] Let, then, the authority of him who has so often repeated his command excuse before all the faults of our want of skill.
</p>

<p id="2">
[1] In the first place this little work sets forth certain wondrous matters concerning the conversion of Saint Francis, which for this reason were not included in the Legends formerly composed about him: that they never came to the knowledge of their author. [2] Thereafter we intend to express, and with watchful diligence to make plain, what was the good will of our most holy father, both in himself and in his followers — that will <ref to="Rom 12:1">well-pleasing and perfect</ref> — in every exercise of heavenly discipline and in the pursuit of the highest perfection, which he ever maintained before God in holy affections and before men in his examples. [3] Certain miracles are interwoven, as the fitting occasion for placing them presents itself. [4] With slender and modest effort, accordingly, we describe whatever comes to hand, desiring to oblige the slower of mind and likewise, if we can, to please the studious. [5] We pray therefore, most kindly father, that you would be willing to consecrate with your blessing the not-to-be-despised little gifts of this labor, which we have sought out with no little toil, correcting what is amiss and pruning away what is superfluous, so that those things which by your learned judgment are approved as well said may, <ref to="Gen 1:28; Eph 2:20-21">together with your name, truly Crescentius, everywhere increase and be multiplied in Christ</ref>. Amen.
</p>

THE FIRST WORK.
Here begins the <ref to="Isa 26:8">“memorial in the longing of the soul”</ref> concerning the deeds and words of our most holy father Francis.
On his conversion.
</aside>

## Chapter I — How he was first called John, and afterward Francis; how his mother prophesied concerning him, how he too foretold things to come concerning himself, and of his patience while in chains <a id="op1-1"></a>

<p id="3">
[1] Francis, the servant and friend of the Most High, upon whom divine providence bestowed this name so that, through a singular and unwonted appellation, the report of his ministry might the more swiftly become known to the whole world, was called John by his own mother, when <ref to="Eph 2:3">from a son of wrath</ref>, <ref to="John 3:5">born again of water and the Holy Spirit</ref>, he was made a son of grace. [2] This woman, a friend of all honesty, displayed in her character a certain mark of virtue, rejoicing in some privilege of likeness to that holy Elizabeth, both <ref to="Luke 1:57-63">in the bestowing of a name upon her son</ref> and likewise in the spirit of prophecy. [3] For when the neighbors marveled at Francis's greatness of soul and the honesty of his ways, she, as if instructed by a divine oracle, would say: <ref to="Luke 1:66">"What think you this my son shall be</ref>? By the grace of his merits, know that he shall come to be a <ref to="Matt 5:9">son of God</ref>." [4] This was indeed the opinion of not a few, to whom Francis, once grown somewhat older, gave great pleasure by his very good pursuits. [5] He always banished from himself everything that might sound injurious to anyone, and, a youth of refined manners, he seemed to all to have been born not of the stock of those parents who were called his. [6] The name John, therefore, pertains to the work of the ministry that he undertook, while the name Francis pertains to the spreading of his fame, which, once he was fully converted to God, soon reached everywhere concerning him. [7] He therefore held the feast of John the Baptist above the feasts of all the saints as the most celebrated, the dignity of whose name impressed upon him the trace of a mystical power. [8] <ref to="Matt 11:11">Among those born of women there arose none greater</ref> than the one; among the founders of religious orders there arose none more perfect than the other. [9] An observation surely worthy of proclamation.
</p>

<p id="4">
[1] John prophesied while still enclosed within the secret recesses of his mother's womb; Francis, set within the prison of the world and as yet unaware of the divine plan, foretold things to come. [2] Indeed, when at a certain time no small slaughter arose from the clash of war between the citizens of Perugia and those of Assisi, Francis was taken captive along with many others, and, bound together with the rest, he endured the squalor of prison. [3] His fellow captives were swallowed up in sorrow, wretchedly lamenting the fortune of their captivity; <ref to="Ps 34:9">Francis rejoiced in the Lord</ref>, laughing at his chains and scorning them. [4] In their grief they rebuked him for his merriment amid the fetters, reckoning him mad and out of his wits. [5] Francis answered as a prophet: "For what do you suppose I rejoice? Another meditation lies beneath: one day I shall be venerated as a saint throughout the whole world." [6] And truly so it is: everything he said was fulfilled. [7] Now there was at that time among the other fellow captives a certain knight, proud and altogether insufferable; and when all the rest resolved to cast him out, the patience of Francis was not broken. [8] He bore with the unbearable man and recalled them all to peace with him. [9] Capable of every grace, <ref to="Sir 24:25; Acts 9:15">a chosen vessel of the virtues</ref>, he already poured forth charisms on every side.
</p>

## Chapter II — Concerning the poor knight whom he clothed, and the vision of his own calling that he beheld while still in the world <a id="op1-2"></a>

<p id="5">
[1] Set free from his chains, after a brief interval of time had passed, he grew gentler in his compassion toward the needy. [2] Henceforth he resolved, <ref to="Tob 4:7">that he would not turn his face away from any poor man,</ref> whosoever should plead in the name of the love of God when they begged. [3] One day he chanced to meet a poor and nearly naked knight, to whom, prompted by compassion, he generously gave for the love of Christ the very garments he himself was wearing, finely fashioned as they were. [4] In what did he here fall short of that most holy Martin, save that, though they were of one purpose and one deed, they were nonetheless unlike in the manner of it? [5] The one gave his garments before all the rest; the other, having first given away all that he had, gave his clothing last of all: <ref to="Isa 16:14">each a poor man and one of little means,</ref> they lived in the world, and each entered heaven rich. [6] The former, a knight but poor, covered the poor man with a garment cut in two; the latter, no knight but a rich man, clothed the poor knight in a whole garment. [7] Each, having fulfilled the command of Christ, was found worthy to be visited by Christ in a vision—the one praised for his perfection, the other most graciously invited to that which he yet lacked.
</p>

<p id="6">
[1] For straightway there was shown to him in a vision a splendid palace, in which he beheld a varied array of arms and a most beautiful bride. [2] <ref to="Gen 6:14">In his dreams Francis is called by name</ref>, and by the promise of all these things he is enticed. [3] And so he plans to go into Apulia to take up arms, and, having lavishly furnished himself with all that was needful, he hastens to attain the rank of knightly honor. [4] The spirit of the flesh was suggesting to him a carnal interpretation of the vision he had seen, <ref to="Col 2:2-3">whereas in the treasures of God's wisdom</ref> there lay hidden a far more glorious meaning. [5] For one night, as he slept, a certain one addressed him a second time in a vision, and earnestly inquired whither he wished to go. [6] When Francis had unfolded to him his purpose, and said that he was setting out for Apulia to serve as a soldier, <ref to="Luke 7:4">he was earnestly asked by him</ref>: who could do better for him, the servant or the lord? "The lord," said Francis. [7] And the other: "Why then do you seek the servant in place of the lord?" And Francis: "What do you want me to do, <ref to="Acts 9:6">Lord</ref>?" [8] And the Lord said to him: "Return," he said, <ref to="Gen 32:9">"to the land of your birth</ref>, for the fulfillment of your vision shall come about through me, and it shall be spiritual." [9] Without delay he returns, <ref to="1 Pet 5:3">now made a pattern of obedience</ref>, and, renouncing his own will, from a Saul he is made a Paul. [10] He is cast down, and hard blows beget sweet words; Francis indeed turns his carnal arms into spiritual ones, and in place of military glory he takes up a divine command. [11] And so, while many marveled at his unwonted gladness, he kept saying that he would become a great prince.
</p>

## Chapter III - How a throng of young men set him up as their lord, that he might host them at a feast, and concerning his transformation <a id="op1-3"></a>

<p id="7">
[1] <ref to="Eph 4:13">He begins to be transformed into the perfect man</ref>, and to become another from what he was. [2] And so, having returned home, <ref to="Ezek 23:27">the sons of Babylon pursue him</ref>, and as he strains toward one thing they drag him, unwilling, toward another. [3] For the throng of young men of the city of Assisi, who had once held him as the leader of their frivolity, still go on inviting him to their festive banquets, where wantonness and ribaldry are forever served. [4] They choose him as their captain, having often tasted his liberality, by which they knew without doubt that he would foot the bill for them all. [5] They make themselves obedient <ref to="Luke 15:16">that they might fill their bellies</ref>, and they suffer themselves to be subjected that they might be sated. [6] He did not spurn the honor offered him, lest he be marked as a miser, and amid his sacred meditations he is mindful of courtesy. [7] He prepares a sumptuous banquet, he doubles the savory dishes, <ref to="Isa 28:8; Prov 26:11">glutted with which, even to vomiting</ref>, they defile <ref to="Gen 10:11">the city squares</ref> with their drunken ditties. [8] Francis follows after, <ref to="Exod 12:11">bearing a staff in his hands like a master</ref>; but little by little he withdraws himself from them in body, <ref to="Matt 22:37">he who already with his whole mind</ref> had grown wholly deaf to those revels, <ref to="1 Chr 23:30">singing in his heart to the Lord</ref>. [9] So great, as he himself related, was the divine sweetness with which he was then suffused that, unable to speak, he could not be moved from the spot at all. [10] A certain spiritual affection then swept over him, snatching him up toward things invisible, and by its power he judged all earthly things to be of no moment but utterly worthless. [11] Truly astonishing is the condescension of Christ, <ref to="2 Pet 1:4">which to those engaged in the lowest things grants the greatest</ref>, <ref to="Ps 31:6">and amid the flood of many waters</ref> <ref to="John 17:10">preserves what is His own</ref> and advances it. [12] <ref to="Matt 14:15-21">For Christ fed the multitudes with loaves and fishes</ref>, nor did He drive sinners away from His banquet. [13] When they sought to make Him king, <ref to="Matt 14:23; John 6:15">He took flight and went up the mountain to pray</ref>. [14] <ref to="Col 2:2">They are the mysteries of God</ref> that Francis attains, <ref to="Job 22:2">and to perfect knowledge</ref> he is led even while unknowing.
</p>

## Chapter IV — How, clad in a poor man's garments, he ate with the beggars before the Church of Saint Peter, and concerning the offering he made there <a id="op1-4"></a>

<p id="8">
[1] But already he stood forth as the foremost lover of the poor; already those sacred beginnings breathed out what he was destined perfectly to become. [2] Time and again, therefore, stripping off his own garments, he clothed the poor, <ref to="Matt 22:37">striving to make himself like them not yet by the performance of the deed, but already with his whole heart</ref>. [3] When on a certain occasion he was setting out on pilgrimage to Rome, for love of poverty he laid aside his fine clothing and, covered with the garments of a certain poor man, he sat down joyfully among the poor in the square before the Church of Saint Peter—a place teeming with the needy—and, accounting himself one of them, ate eagerly in their company. [4] He would have done the like many times over, had he not been hindered by shame in the presence of those who knew him. [5] Astonished, when he drew near to the altar of the prince of the Apostles, to see how meager were the offerings made there by those who came, he flung money with a full hand upon the spot, declaring that he ought especially to be honored by all whom God had honored above the rest. [6] To poor priests as well he repeatedly furnished ornaments for the church, <ref to="Rom 13:7">rendering to all the honor due to them, down to the lowest rank</ref>. [7] For, being about to take up an apostolic mission, and being wholly sound in the catholic faith, he was from the very beginning full of reverence toward the ministers and the ministries of God.
</p>

## Chapter V — How, as he was at prayer, the devil set a woman before him; and concerning the answer that God gave him; and what he did with the lepers <a id="op1-5"></a>

<p id="9">
[1] Behold, thus beneath a worldly habit he bears a soul devoted to religion, and seeking out solitary places apart from the throng, he is again and again admonished by the visitation of the Holy Spirit. [2] For he is drawn away and lured by that sovereign sweetness which from the very beginning had so fully poured itself over him that it never departed from him so long as he lived. [3] But while he frequented hidden places as suited to prayer, the devil strove by a malicious contrivance to drive him from such pursuits. [4] He cast into his heart the image of a certain woman, monstrously hunchbacked, a dweller in his own city, who presented to all a dreadful sight. [5] He threatened to make him like her, should he not turn back from what he had undertaken. [6] But <ref to="Eph 6:10">strengthened by the Lord</ref>, he rejoiced that an answer of salvation and grace was granted him; [7] "Francis," God said to him in the spirit, "in exchange for things loved carnally and vainly, take now spiritual things, and accepting bitter things in place of sweet, despise your very self, if you would know me; for, the order being reversed, the things I tell you shall grow sweet to your taste." [8] At once he is compelled to obey the divine commands, and he is led to the proof of the matter. [9] For Francis, who by nature abhorred lepers above all the wretched monstrosities of the world, encountered one day a leper face to face, as he was riding near Assisi. [10] Though this stirred in him no small loathing and horror, yet, lest as a transgressor of the command he should break the bond of the faith he had given, he leapt down from his horse and ran to kiss the man. [11] <ref to="Esth 8:4; Acts 3:5">When the leper stretched out his hand as though to receive something</ref>, <ref to="Gen 43:21">he carried back money together with a kiss</ref>. [12] <ref to="Ps 75:7">And mounting his horse at once</ref> and turning this way and that, though the field lay open and free on every side, with no obstacles set in the way, he saw that leper no more. [13] <ref to="2 Cor 7:4">Filled thereupon with wonder and joy</ref>, after a few days he took care to perform a like deed. [14] He went to the dwellings of the lepers, and giving money to each leper, <ref to="Sir 29:5">he kissed their hands</ref> and their mouths. [15] <ref to="Prov 27:77">Thus he takes bitter things in place of sweet</ref>, and manfully prepares himself to keep the rest.
</p>

## Chapter VI — Concerning the image of the Crucified that spoke to him, and the honor that he showed to it <a id="op1-6"></a>

<p id="10">
[1] Already wholly changed in heart, and soon to be changed in body as well, he was walking one day beside the church of Saint Damian, which was all but ruined and abandoned by everyone. [2] When, <ref to="Matt 4:1">led by the Spirit</ref>, he entered it to pray, he prostrated himself before the crucifix in supplication and devotion, and, stirred by unwonted visitations, he found himself other than he had been when he came in. [3] To him, while he was thus moved, there happened a thing <ref to="John 9:32">unheard of through all the ages</ref>: the image of Christ crucified, parting the lips of its painted face, spoke to him. [4] <ref to="Isa 40:26">Calling him by his name</ref>, it said: "Francis, go, repair my house, which, as you see, is wholly falling into ruin." [5] Trembling, Francis was struck with no small amazement, and was rendered almost beside himself in his speech. [6] He made himself ready to obey; he gathered his whole self to the command. [7] But since he felt within himself an unspeakable transformation, which he himself could not express, it is fitting that we keep silence. [8] From that time the compassion of the Crucified was fixed in his holy soul, and, as may piously be believed, the marks of the venerable passion were impressed more deeply upon his heart—though not yet upon his flesh.
</p>

<p id="11">
[1] <ref to="John 9:32"> A wondrous thing, and unheard of in our age </ref>! [2] Who is not astonished at these things? Who has ever known the like? [3] Who can doubt that the Crucified appeared to Francis as he was now returning to his true homeland—Francis, to whom, while he had not yet fully renounced the world outwardly, Christ spoke from the wood of the cross by a new and unheard-of miracle? [4] From that hour, therefore, his soul melted away, <ref to="Acts 20:28">as the Beloved spoke to him </ref>. [5] A little while after, the love of his heart laid itself bare through the wounds of his body. [6] From that time on he could not, for this reason, hold back from weeping, and even with a loud voice he lamented the Passion of Christ as though it were forever set before his eyes. [7] He filled the roadways with his groans, and would admit no consolation as he recalled the wounds of Christ. [8] He chanced to meet a certain close friend, and when he had disclosed to him the cause of his grief, the friend was at once moved to bitter tears. [9] Yet he did not forget to attend to the care of that holy image, nor did he let its command slip by through any negligence. [10] <ref to="Luke 19:15"> Without delay he gave money to a certain priest </ref>, that he might buy a lamp and oil, so that the sacred image should not be cheated, even for a moment, of the honor of its due light. [11] Then, tireless, he hastened to accomplish what remained, lending unfailing labor to the repairing of that church. [12] <ref to="Acts 20:28; Gen 15:1"> For although the divine word had been spoken to him concerning that Church which Christ purchased with His own blood </ref>, he was unwilling to become supreme all at once, <ref to="Rom 8:9">but would pass over little by little from the flesh to the spirit </ref>.
</p>

## Chapter VII — On the Persecution by His Father and His Brother in the Flesh <a id="op1-7"></a>

<p id="12">
[1] But as he devoted himself <ref to="1 Tim 2:10">to works of piety</ref>, his earthly father pursued him, and judging the service of Christ to be madness, he tore at him everywhere with curses. [2] So the servant of God summoned a certain man of the common people, simple enough, and taking him in the place of a father, asked that whenever his father heaped up curses, this man in turn should bless him. [3] Truly he turned the prophet's word into deed, and showed by his actions what the other expresses in speech: <ref to="Ps 108:28">"They will curse, and you will bless."</ref> [4] He gave back to his father the money <ref to="3 Kgs 13:1">which the man of God</ref> had wished to spend on the work of the aforesaid church, the bishop of the city having so counseled him—a man indeed of great piety—because it was not lawful to spend anything from ill-gotten gains upon sacred uses. [5] And as <ref to="Acts 10:27">many</ref> who had gathered listened, <ref to="John 13:19">he said: "From now on I shall say</ref> freely: Our Father, <ref to="Matt 6:9">who art in heaven</ref>, and not father Pietro di Bernardone, to whom, behold, I not only return the money, but also surrender my very clothing. Naked, therefore, shall I go to the Lord." [6] O the generous spirit of the man, for whom now Christ alone suffices! [7] <ref to="1 Kgs 2:27">The man of God was found</ref> to be wearing a hairshirt then beneath his garments, rejoicing more in the substance of virtues than in their appearance. [8] His brother by blood, after the manner of their father, pursued him with venomous words. [9] One morning, in the season of winter, while he beheld Francis—covered in worthless rags and trembling with cold—giving himself to prayer, that wicked man said to a fellow citizen of his: "Tell Francis to sell you now a penny's worth of his sweat." [10] <ref to="1 Kgs 2:27">When the man of God</ref> heard this, exceedingly cheered, he answered with a smile: "Truly I shall sell this most dearly to my Lord." [11] Nothing was more true, for not only a hundredfold but even a thousand times more did he receive in this life, <ref to="Matt 19:29">and in the life to come he gained eternal life, not for himself alone, but for many besides</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter VIII — Of the bashfulness which she overcame, and the prophecy concerning the poor virgins <a id="op1-8"></a>

<p id="13">
[1] He therefore set himself to reverse the order of his former pampered habits, and to bring his body, now grown wanton, back to the goodness of nature. [2] One day the man of God was walking through Assisi, begging oil to keep the lamps burning in the church of San Damiano, which at that time he was repairing. [3] But seeing a crowd of men standing at play before the house he wished to enter, he was overcome with shame and drew back his foot. [4] Yet, lifting up that noble spirit of his toward heaven, <ref to="Jas 3:1">he reproached his own slackness and passed judgment upon himself</ref>. [5] At once he turned back to the house and, with unconstrained voice before them all, laid open the cause of his embarrassment, and <ref to="Jer 23:9">like one drunk in spirit</ref> begged for oil in the French tongue, and obtained it. [6] With burning fervor he stirred them all to the work of that church, and, <ref to="Gen 23:10">in the hearing of all</ref>, speaking in French with a clear voice, he prophesied that there would one day be a monastery of holy virgins of Christ in that very place. [7] <ref to="Isa 4:4">For whenever he was filled with the ardor of the Holy Spirit</ref>, <ref to="Ps 44:1">pouring forth burning words from within</ref>, he would speak in French, for he foreknew that among that people in particular he was to be honored, and venerated with a special reverence.
</p>

## Chapter IX - On Food Sought from Door to Door <a id="op1-9"></a>

<p id="14">
[1] From the moment he began to serve the Lord who is common to all, he ever loved to do what was common, shunning in all things that singularity which is befouled with the taint of every vice. [2] For when he was toiling at the work of that church which he had received as a charge from Christ—having from a man of great delicacy become a rustic laborer and a patient bearer of toil—the priest to whom the church belonged, seeing him worn down by unceasing exertion, was moved by compassion and began to provide him daily with some special fare, though it was not savory, for the man was poor. [3] Yet commending the priest's discretion and embracing his kindness, he said to himself: "You will not find this priest everywhere, who will always supply you such things. [4] This is no life for one who professes poverty; it does not become you to grow accustomed to such fare; little by little you will slide back to the things you scorned, and once again drain yourself away upon delicacies. [5] Rise up now, sluggard, and beg from door to door for scraps all mingled together!" [6] Therefore he went begging from door to door through Assisi for cooked food, and when he beheld a bowl filled with diverse morsels, <ref to="Job 7:14">he was at first shaken with horror, </ref><ref to="Ps 76:4">but, mindful of God </ref>and victorious over himself, he ate it with delight of spirit. [7] Love softens all things and makes every bitter thing sweet.
</p>

## Chapter X - On the Renunciation of Possessions by Brother Bernard <a id="op1-10"></a>

<p id="15">
[1] A certain Bernard of the city of Assisi, who afterward became a son of perfection, when by the example <ref to="1 Kgs 2:27">of the man of God </ref> he resolved to despise the world utterly, humbly sought his counsel. [2] Consulting him, therefore, he spoke thus: "If someone, O father, had long possessed the goods of some lord and wished no longer to retain them, what would be the most perfect course to take concerning them?" [3] All should be surrendered to that lord from whom he had received them, <ref to="1 Kgs 2:27">the man of God answered.</ref> [4] And Bernard said to him: "All that I have I recognize as <ref to="Rom 15:15">given to me by God,</ref> and at your counsel I now stand ready to surrender it back to him." [5] "If you wish," said the saint, "to prove your words by deeds, let us enter the church at first light, and taking up the book of the Gospel, let us seek counsel from Christ." [6] <ref to="Matt 27:1">So when morning had come they entered the church,</ref> and having first devoutly offered prayer, they opened the book of the Gospel, resolving to do whatever counsel first should present itself. [7] They opened the book, and Christ disclosed to them his counsel: If you would be perfect, go and sell all that you have, <ref to="Matt 19:21">and give to the poor.</ref> [8] A second time they repeated it, and there came forth: <ref to="Luke 9:3">Take nothing for your journey.</ref> [9] They added this a third time, and they found: Whoever wishes to come after me, <ref to="Matt 16:24; Luke 9:23">let him deny himself.</ref> [10] Without hesitation Bernard fulfilled all these things, nor did he transgress even one jot of this counsel. [11] In a short time very many were turned away from the most gnawing cares of the world, and, with Francis as their guide, <ref to="Gen 30:25">they returned to their homeland,</ref> to the infinite Good. [12] <ref to="Phil 3:14">It would be a long matter to recount of each one how they attained the prize of the heavenly calling.</ref>
</p>

## Chapter XI - Concerning the parable which he set forth before the lord Pope <a id="op1-11"></a>

<p id="16">
[1] At the time when he presented himself with his companions before Pope Innocent to seek a rule for his way of life, when the Pope perceived that his proposal of conduct surpassed human strength, like a man endowed with the utmost discernment, he said to him: "Pray, my son, to Christ, that he may show us through you his will, so that, once it is known, we may more securely grant your pious desires."
[2] The saint assented to the command of the supreme shepherd, and with full confidence ran to Christ; he prayed earnestly, and exhorted his companions to make devout supplication to God.
[3] Why say more? By praying he carried back an answer, and brought to his sons the tidings of salvation.
[4] <ref to="Matt 13:3"> Christ's intimate address in parables </ref> is made known: "Francis," he said, "thus you shall speak to the Pope: A certain woman, poor but beautiful, dwelt in a certain wilderness.
[5] A certain king fell deeply in love with her on account of her great loveliness; he gladly entered into union with her and begot of her most comely sons.
[6] When they were now grown and nobly reared, their mother addressed them: "Do not," she said, "be ashamed, my beloved, that you are poor, for you are all the sons of that great king.
[7] Go joyfully to his court, and ask of him yourselves whatever you need."

## Chapter XVI - On his foreknowledge of things to come, and how he entrusted his Order to the Roman Church, together with a certain vision <a id="op1-16"></a>

<p id="23">
[1] Continually advancing in the merits and virtue of his life, the holy father, now that the offspring of his sons was spreading everywhere in manifold number and grace, and was <ref to="Ezek 17:6; Ps 18:5">stretching out branches to the very ends of the earth</ref>, branches marvelous in the abundance of their fruit, began ever more often to ponder anxiously <ref to="Ps 143:12">how this fresh planting of theirs</ref> <ref to="Eph 4:3">might be preserved and grow, bound together by the bond of unity</ref>. [2] He saw then that many were raging like wolves <ref to="Luke 12:32">against the little flock</ref>, and that some, <ref to="Dan 13:52">grown old in the very newness of these evil days</ref>, were seizing an occasion to do harm. [3] He foresaw that certain things contrary to holy peace and unity might arise among his very sons, and—as often happens even among the elect—he feared that some would prove rebellious, <ref to="Col 2:18">puffed up by the perception of their own flesh</ref>, disposed in spirit to quarrels and prone to scandals.

<p id="24">
[1] <ref to="3 Kgs 13:1"> When the man of God </ref> had often turned these and similar thoughts over in his mind, on a certain night, surrendered to sleep, <ref to="Dan 10:7">he beheld this vision </ref>. ## Chapter II — How He Recognized That a Certain Man, Who Was Reputed to Be a Saint, Was in Fact a Fraud <a id="op2-2"></a>

<p id="28">
[1] There was a certain brother who, to outward appearance, was conspicuous for his manner of life of surpassing holiness, yet exceedingly solitary in his ways. [2] <ref to="1 Cor 7:5">Devoting himself at all times to prayer</ref>, he kept silence with such severe rigor that he had grown accustomed to make his confession not in words but by gestures. [3] In the words of Scripture he conceived a great ardor, and on hearing them he would thrill with wondrous sweetness. [4] In short, he was held by all to be thrice holy. [5] It happened that the blessed father came to that place, saw the brother, and heard him called a saint. [6] But when <ref to="Luke 4:15">all were commending and magnifying</ref> him, the father replied: "Cease, brothers, and do not praise to me in this man the contrivances of the devil. [7] <ref to="Matt 22:16">Know in truth</ref> that this is a temptation of the devil and a fraudulent deception. [8] This is certain to me, and proven above all by this, that he is unwilling to make his confession." [9] <ref to="Gen 21:11">The brothers took this hard</ref>, and especially the saint's vicar. [10] "And how," they said, "could it be true that beneath so many signs of perfection the inventions of deceit lay hidden in play?" [11] To them the father said: "Let him be admonished to confess once or twice a week; and if he does not do so, <ref to="John 5:32">you shall know that what I have said is true</ref>." [12] The vicar <ref to="Luke 9:10">takes him aside</ref>, and at first indeed converses familiarly and pleasantly with him, then at last enjoins him to confess. [13] The man refused, and <ref to="Job 21:5; Job 29:9">laying his finger to his mouth</ref> and shaking his head, he signaled that he would by no means confess. [14] The brothers fell silent, fearing the scandal of a false saint. [15] <ref to="Luke 15:3">Not many days after</ref> he willingly departed from religion, turned back to the world, and <ref to="Prov 26:11">returned to his vomit</ref>. [16] At last, doubling his crimes, he was deprived of penitence and of life alike. [17] Singularity is ever to be guarded against, for it is nothing other than a beautiful precipice. [18] Concerning it, it has become known by the experience of many singular men that <ref to="Ps 106:26">they mount up to the heavens and go down to the depths</ref>. [19] Mark nonetheless the power of devout confession, which not only makes a man holy but reveals him to be so.

## Chapter III - A Similar Example concerning Another. Against Singularity <a id="op2-3"></a>

<p id="29">

<p id="34">
[1] A little later something not unlike this happened in another case. [2] One of the brothers refused to submit himself to the saint's vicar, but followed a certain other brother as his own master. [3] Warned, however, by a messenger from the saint, who was present, <ref to="Matt 15:30">he at once threw himself at the feet of the vicar</ref>, and, scorning his first master, he obeyed the one whom the saint had appointed over him as superior. [4] But the saint drew a sigh from deep within and said to his companion, whom he had sent as the messenger: "I saw, brother, the devil upon the back of the disobedient brother, holding his neck fast bound. [5] Subdued by such a rider, and spurning the bridle of obedience, the brother followed the reins at his prompting. [6] And when I prayed," he said, <ref to="2 Cor 12:8">"to the Lord</ref> on the brother's behalf, suddenly the confounded demon departed." [7] With such powers of sight this man flourished, having eyes feeble toward bodily things but keen toward spiritual ones. [8] And what wonder is it if he is weighed down by a vile burden, <ref to="Isa 3:8">who refuses to bear the Lord of majesty</ref>? [9] There is no middle ground, I say: <ref to="Matt 11:30">either you will carry a light burden</ref>, by which you are the more carried yourself, <ref to="Matt 18:6">or, with a millstone hung about your neck</ref>, <ref to="Zech 5:7">iniquity will sit upon a weight of lead</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter VII — How he delivered the people of Greccio from the bites of wolves and from hail <a id="op2-7"></a>

<p id="35">
[1] In the friars' dwelling at Greccio the saint took up his abode with delight, both because he beheld it rich in poverty, and because, in a more secluded little cell built upon a jutting rock, he was free to devote himself the more unhindered to heavenly contemplation. [2] This is the very place where, in an earlier time, he commemorated the birth of the Child of Bethlehem <cfr. 1Re 16,18)>, becoming himself a child with the Child. [3] Now it happened that the inhabitants of the region were beset by manifold afflictions; for a multitude of ravening wolves devoured not only beasts but men as well, while hail, with each yearly storm, laid waste their grain and their vineyards. [4] One day, as blessed Francis was preaching to them, he said: <ref to="Wis 7:25">"To the honor and praise of almighty God</ref>, hear <ref to="Ps 29:10">the truth that I proclaim to you</ref>. [5] <ref to="1 John 1:9">If each of you confesses his sins</ref>, <ref to="Luke 3:8">and brings forth worthy fruits of penance</ref>, I pledge to you that this whole pestilence will depart, and that the Lord, looking upon you, will multiply you in temporal goods. [6] Yet," he said, <ref to="Jer 28:7">"hear this also</ref>: <ref to="Acts 17:3">again I announce to you</ref>, <ref to="Prov 26:11">that if, ungrateful for these benefits, you return to your vomit</ref>, the plague will be renewed, the punishment doubled, <ref to="Josh 22:18">and a greater wrath will rage against you</ref>."
</p>

<p id="36">
[1] And so it came to pass, by the merits and prayers of the holy father, <ref to="John 4:53">that from that hour </ref> the disasters ceased, the perils vanished, <ref to="Dan 3:50">and neither wolves nor hailstorms did them any further harm </ref>. [2] Indeed, what is more remarkable still, whenever hail swept across the fields of their neighbors, on drawing near to the bounds of these people it either stopped there or turned aside to some other quarter. [3] <ref to="Wis 11:4">Once rest had been granted </ref>, <ref to="Ps 106:38">they multiplied exceedingly </ref>, <ref to="Tob 12:3">and were filled to overflowing with temporal goods </ref>. [4] But prosperity wrought its accustomed effect: <ref to="Job 57:27">for their faces were covered with fatness </ref><ref to="Job 15:27"> and they were blinded by the grease of temporal things, or rather by their filth </ref>. [5] At last, sliding back into worse ways, they forgot the God <ref to="Ps 105:21">who had saved them </ref>. [6] Yet not with impunity, for the judgment of divine justice avenges a first fall less severely than a relapse. [7] <ref to="Sir 36:8">The wrath of God was stirred up </ref> against them, and with the evils that had departed now returned, the sword of men was added besides, and a pestilence commanded from heaven carried off a great many; [8] <ref to="Rev 8:7">in the end the whole stronghold was consumed by avenging flames </ref>. [9] Surely it is just that those who turn their backs on benefits should come at last to ruin.
</p>

## Chapter VIII — How, while preaching to the people of Perugia, he foretold the discord that was to come among them, and concerning his commendation of unity <a id="op2-8"></a>

<p id="37">
[1] After some days, when once the blessed father had come down from the aforesaid cell, he said to the brothers who were present with a complaining voice: "Many evils have the men of Perugia done to their neighbors, <ref to="Ezek 28:2; Jer 8:11">and their heart is lifted up to their own disgrace</ref>. [2] Nevertheless, the vengeance of God is near, <ref to="Jer 46:10; Jer 15:2">and his hand is upon the sword</ref>." [3] And so, when a few days had slipped by, he rose up in the fervor of the spirit and directed his course toward the city of Perugia. [4] Clearly the brothers were able to judge that he had seen some vision in his cell. [5] <ref to="Deut 31:12">Coming therefore to Perugia, when the people had gathered together,</ref> he began to preach; [6] and when the knights on horseback, as is their custom, <ref to="Luke 11:28">were racing about and, bearing arms in their martial games, hindered the word of God,</ref> the saint, turning to them, <ref to="Luke 9:55; Mark 7:34">groaned</ref> and said: [7] "O the pitiable madness of wretched men, who do not consider, <ref to="Rom 2:3">nor fear the judgment of God</ref>! [8] <ref to="Isa 66:2; John 16:13">But hear what the Lord announces to you through me, his poor little one</ref>: [9] The Lord," he said, <ref to="Ps 36:34">"has exalted you</ref> <ref to="Ezek 5:7; Ezek 11:12">above all who are round about you</ref>; for which reason you ought to show yourselves kinder to your neighbors and more pleasing to God. [10] But, ungrateful for grace, <ref to="4 Kgs 11:11">with armed hand</ref> you assail your neighbors, you slay and lay them waste. [11] I tell you, <ref to="Matt 23:38; Job 24:12">it shall not be left unavenged</ref>, but for a graver punishment God will make you fall by civil war, so that one shall rise up against another in mutual sedition. [12] Indignation shall teach those whom kindness has not instructed." [13] <ref to="Luke 15:13">Not many days after,</ref> strife arose among them, arms were seized against their neighbors, the common soldiers raged against the knights, and, the sword turned about, the commoners against the nobles; [14] at last the contest was waged with such savagery and slaughter that even the neighbors, whom they had offended, grieved over them. [15] A judgment worthy of praise! For indeed, since they had departed from the One and the Most High, it was necessary that no unity should remain even among themselves. [16] There can be no stronger bond in the commonwealth than a devout love toward God, a sincere and unfeigned faith.
</p>

## Chapter IX - Concerning the woman to whom he foretold that her husband, now wicked, would become good <a id="op2-9"></a>

<p id="38">
[1] <ref to="1 Kgs 2:27">In those days, when the man of God was passing through</ref> Cella di Cortona, a certain noblewoman from the town called Volusiano, hearing of this, hastened to him, and wearied by the long journey, <ref to="Deut 28:56">for she was exceedingly delicate and tender</ref>, at last reached the saint. [2] Seeing her exhaustion and her gasping, broken breath, the most holy father, moved to pity, said to her: "What is it you wish, my lady?" And she replied: "Father, <ref to="Gen 27:38">that you may bless me</ref>." [3] And the saint asked: "Are you married, or unmarried?" She answered, saying: "Father, <ref to="John 4:17">I have a husband</ref> who is exceedingly cruel, and whom I endure as an adversary in the service of Jesus Christ; and this is my chief sorrow, <ref to="Luke 2:14; 2 Cor 5:8">that the good will</ref> which the Lord has breathed into me I cannot carry out, my husband hindering me. Therefore I beg you, holy one, pray for him, <ref to="Dan 5:22">that the divine mercy may humble his heart</ref>." [4] The father marvels at a manly courage in a woman, an aged wisdom in a young girl, and, deeply moved by compassion, he says: "Go, <ref to="Jdt 12:23">blessed daughter</ref>, and know that consolation concerning your husband shall soon be granted to you." [5] And he added: "You shall tell him, on God's behalf and mine, <ref to="Sir 4:28; Sir 40:7">that now is the time of salvation</ref>, and hereafter of justice." [6] <ref to="Ps 23:5">Having received the blessing</ref>, the woman returns, finds her husband, and delivers the word. [7] <ref to="Acts 10:44">Suddenly the Holy Spirit fell upon him</ref>, and, made new out of the old, he was brought to answer thus, <ref to="Eph 4:2">with all gentleness</ref>: "My lady, <ref to="Josh 22:27">let us serve the Lord</ref>, <ref to="Gen 19:19">and save our souls</ref> within our own house." [8] The wife replied: "It seems to me that continence is, as it were, a kind of foundation to be laid in the soul, <ref to="Eph 2:20">and that the remaining virtues are at length to be built upon it</ref>." [9] "And this," said he, "pleases me as much as it pleases you." [10] Thereafter, living a celibate life for many years, on the same day—the one a morning burnt offering, <ref to="4 Kgs 16:15">the other as an evening sacrifice</ref>—they happily passed away. [11] Happy woman, who thus softened her lord unto life! In her is fulfilled that saying of the Apostle: <ref to="1 Cor 7:14">The unbelieving husband was saved through the believing wife</ref>. [12] But such women, to use the common proverb, can today be counted on the fingers.
</p>

## Chapter X — How, by the spirit, he recognized the brother who had given offense to another brother, whom he foretold would leave the religious life <a id="op2-10"></a>

<p id="39">
[1] Two brothers came on a certain occasion from the Terra di Lavoro, of whom the elder heaped many offenses upon the younger. He was, I say, no companion but a tyrant.
[2] The younger, however, bore all things for God's sake with wondrous silence.
[3] When they had come to Assisi and that younger brother had entered into the presence of Saint Francis (for indeed he was a familiar of his), the saint said among other things: "How has your companion conducted himself toward you on this journey?"
[4] He answered: "Quite well enough, dearest father."
[5] The saint said to him: "Beware, brother, lest under the appearance of humility you tell a lie. For I know how he has behaved toward you; but wait a little while and you shall see."
[6] The brother marveled exceedingly, <ref to="Acts 21:4">how he had come to know through the spirit things so far removed</ref>.
[7] <ref to="Luke 15:13">And so, not many days after</ref>, having scorned the religious life, <ref to="Rom 14:13; Matt 5:22">he who had given offense to his brother was snatched away to the world outside</ref>.
[8] <ref to="Sir 19:21">It is without doubt a sign of perversity and a plain proof of a failing mind</ref>, to refuse, upon the same road, to share the same purpose with a good fellow traveler.
</p>

## Chapter XI — Concerning the young man who came to the Order, whom he discerned was not led by the Spirit of God <a id="op2-11"></a>

<p id="40">
[1] In those same days a certain noble youth from Lucca came to Assisi, desiring to enter the order. [2] When he was presented to Saint Francis, <ref to="2 Chr 6:13">with bended knees</ref> he <ref to="Heb 5:7">offered up his prayers with tears</ref>, that Francis might receive him. [3] But the <ref to="1 Kgs 2:27">man of God</ref>, <ref to="Acts 3:4">gazing intently upon him</ref>, <ref to="Acts 21:4; Rom 8:14">at once perceived through the Spirit that this youth was not led by the Spirit</ref>. [4] And he said to him: "Wretched and carnal man, <ref to="Acts 5:3">why do you trust that you can lie to the Holy Spirit and to me</ref>? Your weeping is carnal, <ref to="Acts 8:21">and your heart is not with God</ref>. [5] Go," he said, <ref to="Mark 8:33; Gal 5:10">"for you relish nothing of the things of the spirit"</ref>. [6] When he had spoken these words, word came that the youth's parents were standing <ref to="John 18:16">at the door</ref>, <ref to="Luke 11:54">seeking to seize their son</ref> and to lead him back once captured; <ref to="John 18:29">and going out to them</ref>, he returned at last of his own free will. [7] At this the brothers marveled, <ref to="Ps 150:1">praising the Lord in his holy one</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter XII — Concerning a certain cleric whom he healed, and whom he foretold would suffer worse things on account of his sin <a id="op2-12"></a>

<p id="41">
[1] At the time when the holy father lay sick in the palace of the bishop of Rieti, a certain canon named Gideon, a slippery and worldly man, seized by illness and beset on every side by pains, was lying upon his little bed. [2] Having himself carried before Saint Francis, he begged him with tears to be sealed by him with the sign of the cross. [3] To him the saint replied: <ref to="Gal 5:16">"When once you lived according to the desires of the flesh</ref>, <ref to="Sir 17:24">not fearing the judgments of God</ref>, how shall I sign you with the cross?" [4] And he added: "I sign you," he said, <ref to="Acts 4:10">"in the name of Christ</ref>; yet know that you shall suffer worse things <ref to="Prov 26:11">if, once delivered, you return to your vomit</ref>." [5] And he went on: <ref to="Matt 12:45">"On account of the sin of ingratitude, things ever worse than the former</ref> are visited upon one." [6] And so, when the sign of the cross had been made over him, at once the man who had lain crippled rose up sound, and bursting into praise he cried: "I am delivered!" [7] But the bones of his loins resounded, in the hearing of many, as when dry sticks are snapped by the hand. [8] Yet after a short while had passed, <ref to="Judg 3:7">forgetful of God</ref>, he gave his body back to unchastity. [9] When one evening he was dining in the house of another of his fellow canons, and was sleeping there that night, suddenly the roof of the house collapsed upon them all. [10] But while the others escaped death, the wretched man alone was caught and slain. [11] Nor is it any wonder if, as the saint had said, <ref to="Matt 12:45">worse things followed than the former</ref>, since for a pardon received one ought to be grateful, and a crime repeated displeases doubly.
</p>

## Chapter XIII - Concerning a Certain Brother Who Was Tempted <a id="op2-13"></a>

<p id="42">
[1] <ref to="John 11:6">While the saint was abiding in that same place</ref>, a certain spiritual brother of the Marsican custody, who was tormented by grievous temptations, <ref to="Ps 13:1">said in his heart</ref>:
[2] "Oh, if only I might ever have with me something—even but a little—of the nail-clippings of Saint Francis, I truly believe that this whole tempest of temptations would scatter away, and, by the Lord's favor, calm would return."
[3] Having therefore obtained leave, he betook himself to the place and laid his trouble before one of the holy father's companions.
[4] To him the brother replied: "I do not think it possible for me to give you any of his nails, for although we sometimes pare them, he orders them to be thrown away and forbids us to keep them."
[5] At once the brother is summoned, and he is bidden to go to the saint, who was asking for him: "Seek me out a pair of shears, my son," he said, <ref to="Deut 21:12">"with which you may straightway trim my nails."</ref>
[6] The other brought out the iron tool, which he had already taken into his hands for this very purpose, and gathering up the clipped parings, he handed them over to the brother who had asked for them; and he, receiving them devoutly and preserving them still more devoutly, was at once set free from every assault.
</p>

## Chapter XIV - Concerning the man who offered cloth, in accordance with what the saint had first requested <a id="op2-14"></a>

<p id="43">
[1] In that same place, clothed in a worn-out tunic, the <ref to="Job 29:16">father of the poor </ref> said once to one of his companions, whom he had appointed as his guardian: "I should wish, brother, if you could manage it, that you might find me cloth for a single tunic." [2] Hearing this, the brother turned over in his mind how he might procure cloth at once so necessary and so humbly requested. [3] <ref to="Jas 4:13"> On the morrow </ref>, however, at the very break of dawn, he set out for the gate, meaning to go to the town for the cloth: <ref to="Luke 22:10">and behold, a certain man </ref> sitting beside the doorway, wishing to speak with him; [4] who said to the brother: "For the love of God, take from me cloth enough for six tunics, and keeping one indeed for yourself, distribute the rest for my soul, as it pleases you." [5] Filled with joy, the brother returned to brother Francis and announced the offering that had come from heaven. [6] To him the father said: "Take the tunics, for this man was sent so that he might relieve my need in this way. [7] Thanks be," he said, <ref to="1 Kgs 9:5; Ps 39:18">"to him who alone seems to be mindful of us </ref>."
</p>

## Chapter XV - How he invited his physician to a meal when the brothers had nothing, and how great a bounty the Lord suddenly provided; and concerning God's providence toward his own. <a id="op2-15"></a>

<p id="44">
[1] While the blessed man was staying in a certain hermitage near Rieti, a physician visited him daily to treat his eyes. [2] One day the saint said to his companions: "Invite the doctor and give him an excellent meal." [3] The guardian answered him, saying: "Father, <ref to="Luke 14:9">we say it with a blush</ref>, we are ashamed to invite him, so poor are we at present." <ref to="John 1:26">The saint answered, saying</ref>: <ref to="Matt 20:32; John 9:27">"What do you wish that I should say it again</ref>?" [4] The physician, <ref to="Mark 14:70">who was standing by</ref>, said: "And I, my dearest brothers, will count your scarcity as a delicacy." [5] The brothers hurried, and set upon the table all the abundance of their larder—namely a little bread, not much wine—and, that they might dine more sumptuously, the kitchen sent along a few vegetables. [6] Meanwhile the table of the Lord (cf. Mal 1:7) had compassion on the table of his servants; <ref to="Luke 13:25">there was a knocking at the door</ref>, and they ran to it at once. [7] And behold, a certain woman offered a basket full of fine bread, stuffed with fish and crayfish pastries, and heaped on top with honey and grapes. [8] The table of the poor exulted at this sight, and, the cheap fare being set aside for the morrow, the precious food was taken today. [9] <ref to="Matt 14:27">But the physician, sighing, spoke and said</ref>: "Neither you, brothers, as you ought, nor we of the world rightly know the holiness of this man." [10] They would at last have been sated, had not the miracle satisfied them more than the dish. [11] <ref to="Prov 30:17">Thus that fatherly eye in no way despises</ref> his own; rather, the greater their want, the greater the providence with which he feeds his beggars. [12] At a more bountiful table the poor man is fed than the tyrant, by as much as God is more lavish in generosity than man. How he freed Brother Ricerius from temptation. 44a [1] A certain brother named Ricerius, noble both in character and in birth, so presumed upon the merits of blessed Francis that he firmly believed a man would truly merit divine grace if he obtained the gift of that saint's goodwill, or, if he lacked it, would merit the wrath of God. [2] And since he longed all the more ardently to obtain the benefit of intimacy with him, <ref to="Gen 32:7; Jdt 8:8">he greatly feared</ref> lest the saint should detect in him some hidden fault, by occasion of which it might happen that he be removed further from his favor. [3] Therefore, when a fear of this kind was daily and grievously afflicting the aforesaid brother, and he was revealing his thought to no one, it happened that one day, troubled in his usual way, he approached the cell in which blessed Francis was praying. [4] <ref to="1 Kgs 2:27">The man of God, knowing at once his coming and his mind</ref>, kindly called him to himself and spoke thus: "Let no fear henceforth, my son, no temptation disturb you, for you are most dear to me, and among those especially dear I love you with a singular affection. [5] Come to me securely whenever it pleases you, and depart from me freely according to your wish." [6] That brother was greatly astonished and rejoiced at the words of the holy father, and thereafter, secure in his love, he grew also, just as he had believed, <ref to="Titus 2:11">in the grace of the Savior</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter XVI - Concerning two brothers whom he blessed as he came forth from his cell, whose longing he had perceived through the Spirit <a id="op2-16"></a>

<p id="45">
[1] It was the custom of Saint Francis to pass an entire day alone in his cell, never returning to the brothers unless the necessity of taking food pressed upon him. [2] Yet he did not come out to dine at fixed hours, for the more ravenous hunger of contemplation more often claimed him wholly for itself. [3] Now it happened on one occasion that two brothers, <ref to="1 Pet 2:12">men whose manner of life was worthy of God</ref>, came from afar to the place of Greccio. [4] The whole purpose of their coming was to see the holy man and to receive his long-desired blessing. [5] <ref to="Luke 2:45">Coming, therefore, and not finding him</ref>, since he had already withdrawn from the company to his cell, they were grieved beyond measure; [6] and since his uncertain coming forth foretold a long delay, ascribing this to their own deserts, they departed disconsolate. [7] But while the companions of blessed Francis were accompanying them and consoling them in their desolation, when they had now withdrawn <ref to="Luke 22:41">about a stone's throw from the place</ref>, suddenly the holy man called out after them and said to one of his companions: [8] "Tell my brothers who have come here that they should look back toward me." [9] And when the said brothers turned their faces toward him, he marked them with the sign of the cross and blessed them most affectionately. [10] But they, made the more joyful as they had now more profitably obtained both their purpose and a miracle, <ref to="Luke 24:35">returned praising and blessing the Lord</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter XVII — How, through prayer, he drew water from a rock and gave it to a thirsting peasant <a id="op2-17"></a>

<p id="46">
[1] On one occasion the blessed Francis, wishing to go off to a certain hermitage so that there he might more freely give himself to contemplation, since he was no little weakened, obtained the loan of an ass for riding from a certain poor man. [2] And when, in the summer days, following <ref to="3 Kgs 13:14">the man of God</ref> as he <ref to="Josh 2:16">climbed the mountains</ref>, <ref to="John 4:6">the peasant, wearied by the journey along a rougher and longer road</ref>, before he reached the place fainted and grew faint from the burning of thirst. [3] He cried out urgently after the saint, <ref to="Deut 13:17">and begged him to have mercy on him</ref>; he declared that he would die unless he were revived by the kindness of some draught. [4] <ref to="Luke 4:34">The holy one of God</ref>, who was ever compassionate toward the afflicted, leapt down from the ass without delay, and with his knees fixed upon the ground stretched out his palms to heaven, <ref to="Col 1:9">not ceasing to pray</ref> until he perceived that he had been heard. [5] "Hasten," he said to the peasant, "and there you will find living water, which at this very hour Christ has mercifully brought forth from the rock for you to drink." [6] Astonishing the condescension of God, which stoops so readily to his servants! [7] The peasant drank water from the rock (cfr. 48,21) by the power of the one who prayed, <ref to="Deut 32:13">and drew a draught from the hardest stone</ref>. [8] <ref to="Ps 1:3">No running water</ref> had been there before, nor, as was carefully sought, could any thereafter be found. [9] What wonder, <ref to="Luke 4:1">if a man full of the Holy Spirit</ref> reflects in himself the marvelous deeds of all the just? [10] For he who by the gift of a special grace is joined to Christ does nothing great if he works things like those of the rest of the saints.
</p>

## Chapter XVIII — Concerning the little birds nourished by him, one of which met its death on account of greed <a id="op2-18"></a>

<p id="47">
[1] One day blessed Francis was seated at table with the brothers when certain little birds, a male and a female, came—anxious for the rearing of their newly hatched young—and, just as they wished, daily received <ref to="Matt 15:27">crumbs from the saint's table</ref>.
[2] The saint rejoiced in such creatures, fondled them as was his custom, and deliberately offered them their portion of grain.
[3] One day the father and mother presented their offspring to the brothers, as though reared at their expense, and having committed the nestlings to the brothers, they appeared in that place no more.
[4] The young grew tame among the brothers, and perching upon their hands, dwelt in the house not as guests but as residents.
[5] They shunned the gaze of worldly men, and professed themselves to be the wards of the brothers alone.
[6] The saint observed this and marveled, and he invited the brothers to share his joy: "See," he said, "what our brothers the redbreasts have done, as though they were endowed with reason!
[7] For they have said: 'Behold, brothers, we present to you our little ones, who have been nourished by your crumbs.
[8] Dispose of them as you please; we are passing on to other dwellings.'"
[9] And so they grew altogether tame among the brothers and took their food together in harmony.
[10] But avarice shattered the concord, when the arrogance of the greater bird began to persecute the lesser ones.
[11] For the larger bird, once filled to its liking, drove the rest away from the food.
[12] "See," said the father, "what this greedy one does; full and sated itself, it grudges its hungry brothers.
[13] For this it shall yet perish by an evil death."
[14] Vengeance at once followed the saint's word.
[15] The troubler of the brothers climbed upon a little vessel of water to drink, and suddenly, drowned in the water, it perished; nor was any cat found, nor any beast that would have dared to lay hands upon what the saint had cursed.
[16] A dreadful evil is the avarice of men, when it is thus punished in birds. 17 To be feared too is the sentence of the saints, which vengeance follows with such readiness.
</p>

## Chapter XIX - Concerning the little birds nourished by him, one of which met its death on account of greed <a id="op2-19"></a>

<p id="48">
[1] On another occasion he spoke prophetically of Brother Bernard, who had been the second brother in the order, in this manner: "I tell you <ref to="Matt 12:45">that to Brother Bernard there have been assigned, for his testing, the subtlest of demons and, among others, the more wicked of spirits</ref>, whose zeal, though it ever keeps watch <ref to="Rev 6:13; Rev 9:1">to make a star fall from heaven</ref>, <ref to="Ruth 3:18">yet the outcome of the matter shall be otherwise</ref>. [2] He will indeed be troubled, goaded, and afflicted, but in the end he will triumph over all." </p>

## Chapter XX — Concerning a brother under temptation, who wished to obtain some writing from the saint's own hand <a id="op2-20"></a>

<p id="49">
[1] While the saint remained shut away in his cell on Mount Alverna, one of his companions yearned with great longing to possess some refreshing writing about the words of the Lord, briefly noted down by the hand of Saint Francis. [2] For he believed that by this means he might escape, or at least bear more lightly, the grievous temptation that tormented him—a temptation not of the flesh but of the spirit. [3] Pining with such longing, he was afraid to disclose the matter to the most holy father; but what man did not tell him, the <ref to="1 Cor 2:10">Spirit revealed</ref>. [4] For one day blessed Francis called him, saying: "Bring me parchment and ink (cf. 2 John 12), for I wish to write down the words of the Lord and his Praises, <ref to="Ps 76:7">which I have pondered in my heart</ref>. [5] Once the things he had asked for were brought without delay, he wrote with his own hand the Praises of God and the words he wished, and at the last a blessing for the brother, saying: <ref to="Gen 28:2">"Take for yourself</ref> this little parchment, and keep it carefully until the day of your death." [6] At once that whole temptation was put to flight; the writing was preserved, and afterward it worked wonders.
</p>

## Chapter XXI - Concerning the same brother, to whom he gave a tunic in accordance with his desire <a id="op2-21"></a>

<p id="50">
[1] In this same brother another marvel of the holy father shone forth. [2] For at the time when he lay sick in the palace at Assisi, <ref to="Wis 2:1; Matt 16:7">the said brother pondered within himself, saying</ref>: [3] "Behold, the father draws near to death, <ref to="Ps 76:3">and my soul would be greatly consoled</ref>, if after his death I might have my father's tunic." [4] As though <ref to="Ps 20:3">the desire of his heart</ref> had become the petition of his lips, after a little while blessed Francis called him and said: "To you I hand over this tunic; [5] take it, that henceforth it may be yours; for though I bear it while I live, yet at my death let it pass to you." [6] Amazed at so deep an insight of the father, the brother at last took the tunic with consolation, and afterward holy devotion carried it across into France.
</p>

## Chapter XXII - Concerning the parsley found at his command among the wild herbs by night <a id="op2-22"></a>

<p id="51">
[1] In the final hours of his illness, longing one night amid the darkness to eat parsley, he humbly asked for it. [2] When the cook was summoned to fetch it, he replied that he could gather none in the garden at that hour, <ref to="John 8:14">saying </ref>: "I have plucked parsley daily, and I have cut it back so much that even in broad daylight I can scarcely find any there. How much less, now that the darkness has fallen, shall I be able to make it out among the other herbs." [3] To him the saint replied: "Go, brother, and do not let it trouble you; bring back the first herbs your hand happens to touch." [4] The brother went into the garden, and pulling up the wild herbs that first met his hand as he saw nothing, he carried them into the house. [5] The brothers looked at the wild herbs, and turning them over with greater care, they found among them parsley, leafy and tender. [6] Eating a little of it, the saint was much strengthened. [7] Then the father said to the brothers: <ref to="Phil 4:1">"Dearest brothers </ref>, fulfill a command at the first word, and do not wait for what is said to be repeated. [8] Plead no impossibility as your excuse, for even if I myself were to order something beyond your strength, obedience would not lack the strength to do it." [9] <ref to="Rev 19:10">Thus far the spirit of prophecy </ref> has commended the privilege of the spirit.
</p>

## Chapter XXIII - On the famine which he foretold would come after his death <a id="op2-23"></a>

<p id="52">
[1] Holy men are sometimes compelled, by the prompting of the Holy Spirit, to speak certain wondrous things about themselves, <ref to="1 Kgs 3:7">namely when either the glory of God demands that the word be revealed</ref>, or when the order of charity requires it for the edification of one's neighbor. [2] Hence it was that the blessed father one day related to a certain brother <ref to="John 19:20">whom he greatly loved</ref> <ref to="1 Macc 15:32">these words</ref> which he had then brought back from the secretary of the Majesty, who was familiar to him. [3] "Today," he said, "there is a servant of God upon the earth on whose account, so long as he himself lives, <ref to="Gen 26:1">the Lord does not permit famine to rage among men</ref>." [4] There was nothing of vanity in this, but a holy report which—for our edification, in holy and modest words—that holy charity which seeks not its own things (cf. 1 Cor 13:5) poured forth; nor was the prerogative of Christ's so wondrous love toward his servant to be passed over in useless silence. [5] <ref to="John 3:11">For we all who have seen know</ref> how quiet and peaceful, as long as Christ's servant lived, the times ran their course, with how great an abundance of all good things they overflowed. [6] For there was no famine of the word of God (cf. Am 8:11), since the words of the preachers were then most full of power, <ref to="2 Tim 2:15">when the hearts of all the hearers were approved by God</ref>. [7] Examples of holiness shone forth in religious image, nor had the hypocrisy of whitewashed men yet infected such great saints, <ref to="2 Cor 11:13">nor had the teaching of those who transfigure themselves</ref> introduced such great curiosity. [8] Deservedly, therefore, did temporal goods abound, since thus the eternal goods were truly held in love by all.
</p>

<p id="53">
[1] Once he had been taken away, the whole order of things was utterly overturned, and <ref to="Wis 2:15">all was changed</ref>; <ref to="Luke 21:9">for wars and rebellions</ref> grew strong on every side, and the slaughter of manifold deaths suddenly overran many kingdoms. [2] A monstrous famine too spread itself far and wide, and its cruelty, which surpasses the bitterness of all other things, devoured great multitudes. [3] For necessity then turned everything into food, and forced into human jaws what custom had not even allowed the beasts to eat. [4] Indeed bread was made from the shells of nuts and the bark of trees, and—to speak with some restraint—as became plain from the confession of a certain man, paternal devotion, driven by hunger, did not grieve at the death of its own children. [5] <ref to="Matt 24:45">But that it might shine forth clearly who was that faithful servant</ref>, for love of whom the divine reckoning held back its hand from vengeance, the blessed father Francis, a few days after his death, revealed plainly to a brother—to whom while living he had foretold the coming disaster—that he himself was that servant of the Lord. [6] For on a certain night, while that brother was sleeping, <ref to="John 11:28">he called him with a clear voice, saying</ref>: "Brother, now the famine has come which, so long as I lived, the Lord did not permit <ref to="Ps 104:16">to come upon the earth</ref>." [7] Roused by the voice, the brother afterward <ref to="Esth 15:9">related everything in order</ref>. [8] But on the third night after this the saint appeared to him again and repeated similar words.
</p>

## Chapter XXIV - On the Brightness of the Saint and Our Own Ignorance <a id="op2-24"></a>

<p id="54">
[1] It ought to seem strange to no one that the prophet of our age shone forth with such privileges; for, released from the murky cloud of earthly things and not subject to the pleasures of the flesh, his intellect flew free to the heights and entered pure into the light. [2] <ref to="Wis 7:26"> Thus, irradiated by the splendors of the eternal light </ref>, he drew from the Word what resounded in his words. [3] Alas! how unlike him are we today, who, <ref to="Job 37:19">wrapped in darkness </ref>, are ignorant even of what is necessary! [4] From what cause do you suppose this comes, save that, being friends of the flesh, we too are mired in the dust of worldly affairs? [5] <ref to="Lam 3:41"> Surely, if we were to lift up our hearts together with our hands to heaven </ref>, if we were to choose to hang fixed upon things eternal, we might perhaps know what we do not know: God and ourselves. [6] He who dwells in the mire must of necessity see only mire; for one whose eye clings to heaven it is impossible not to behold heavenly things.
</p>

## Chapter XXV - In Praise of Poverty <a id="op2-25"></a>

<p id="55">
[1] <ref to="Ps 83:7">Set in this vale of tears,</ref> this blessed father <ref to="Ps 30:2">scorns the riches that leave the sons of men</ref> all the poorer; for, ambitious of a loftier summit, <ref to="Sir 47:10">with his whole heart</ref> he yearns after poverty. [2] Observing how dear she had been <ref to="Heb 7:3">to the Son of God,</ref> though now <ref to="Esth 9:28">spurned by the whole world,</ref> he hastens to wed her <ref to="Jer 31:3">with everlasting love.</ref> [3] Become, therefore, <ref to="Wis 8:2">a lover of her beauty,</ref> that he might cleave the more firmly to his bride <ref to="Gen 2:24; Matt 19:5">and that the two might be one in spirit,</ref> he not only <ref to="Gen 2:24; Mark 10:7">forsook father and mother</ref> but set aside all things whatsoever. [4] Henceforth he binds her to himself in chaste embraces, <ref to="Gal 2:5">nor for an hour</ref> does he suffer to be other than her husband. [5] To his sons he would call her the way of perfection, the pledge and earnest of eternal riches. [6] No man was ever so greedy for gold as he for poverty, <ref to="Matt 13:45-46">nor anyone more anxious to guard a treasure than he was to keep this pearl of the Gospel.</ref> [7] In this above all was his eye offended, if he should see anything among the brothers, at home or abroad, contrary to poverty. [8] In truth, from the beginning of his religious life until his death, he possessed nothing else, rich only in a single tunic, a little cord, and his breeches. [9] His poor habit declared where he was laying up his riches. [10] Hence joyful, hence secure, hence unencumbered for the race, he rejoiced to have exchanged perishable treasures a hundredfold.
</p>

## Chapter XXVI — On the Poverty of Dwellings <a id="op2-26"></a>

<p id="56">
[1] He taught his brothers to build their dwellings poor and humble, fashioning their little cottages of wood rather than stone, and in the plainest of designs. [2] Often, when he preached on poverty, he would press upon the brothers that saying of the Gospel: "The foxes have holes, and the birds of the air have nests, <ref to="Matt 8:20; Luke 9:58">but the Son of God had nowhere to lay his head"</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter XXVII - Concerning the house which he began to tear down at the Portiuncula <a id="op2-27"></a>

<p id="57">
[1] <ref to="2 Tim 4:6"> On a certain occasion, when the chapter was to be held at Saint Mary of the Portiuncula and the time was already at hand </ref>, the people of Assisi, observing that there was no house there, and without the knowledge and in the absence of the man of God (cf. 2 Kgs 4:42), with the greatest haste built a house for the chapter. [2] When the father at length returned, he looked upon the house, and bearing it ill, grieved with no gentle sorrow. [3] At once he rose up first to demolish the building, climbed onto the roof, and <ref to="Luke 5:19">tore away the slates and tiles </ref><ref to="Ezek 20:34"> with a mighty hand </ref>. [4] He commanded the brothers as well to climb up and to cast down from on high this monstrous thing set against poverty. 5 For he used to say that whatever in that place might seem to smack of presumption would quickly spread throughout the order and be taken by all as a precedent. [6] He would therefore have leveled this house to its very foundations, had not the knights who stood by withstood the fervor of his spirit, declaring that it belonged to the commune and not to the brothers.
</p>

## Chapter XXVIII - Concerning the house at Bologna, from which he cast out the sick <a id="op2-28"></a>

<p id="58">
[1] Returning at one time from Verona and wishing to pass through Bologna, he heard that a house of the brothers had recently been built there. [2] At the mere sound of the words "a house of the brothers," he turned his step aside, and not entering Bologna, he passed by another way. [3] He then commanded the brothers to leave the house in haste. [4] On account of this the house was abandoned, and even the sick did not remain behind but were cast out along with the rest. [5] Nor was leave to return granted until lord Hugh, then bishop of Ostia and legate in Lombardy, proclaimed publicly in his preaching that the aforesaid house was his own. [6] <ref to="John 21:24"> He who bears witness and writes these things </ref> is the very one who at that time was cast out of the house while sick.
</p>

## Chapter XXIX - Concerning the cell made in his name, which he refused to enter <a id="op2-29"></a>

<p id="59">
[1] He was unwilling that the brothers should dwell in any little lodging unless there were a clear patron to whom the ownership properly belonged. [2] For <ref to="Exod 12:49">the laws of pilgrims</ref> he ever sought in his sons—namely, to be gathered beneath another's roof, to pass through in peace, to thirst after their homeland. [3] Indeed, even in the hermitage of Sarteano, when a brother who was questioned by another as to whence he came replied, "From the cell of Brother Francis," the saint, hearing this, answered: [4] "Since you have laid Francis's name upon the cell, making it my property, seek another dweller for it; for I myself shall lodge in it no longer henceforth. [5] The Lord," he said, "when he stood in prison, <ref to="Matt 4:2">where he prayed and fasted forty days</ref>, did not have any cell made there, nor any house, but remained beneath the rock of the mountain. [6] We can follow him in the manner here laid down, holding nothing as property, although we cannot live without the use of dwellings."
</p>

## Chapter XXX - On Poverty in the Use of Everyday Things <a id="op2-30"></a>

<p id="60">
[1] Not only did this man hate the arrogance of houses, but he also greatly shrank from possessing many or precious household furnishings. [2] He loved nothing on the tables, nothing among the vessels, that might recall the world to mind, so that everything might sing of pilgrimage, everything of exile.
</p>

## Chapter XXXI - An example concerning the table prepared on the day of Easter at Greccio, and how, after the example of Christ, he presented himself as a pilgrim <a id="op2-31"></a>

<p id="61">
[1] <ref to="Luke 2:41">It happened one Easter day</ref> that the brothers in the hermitage of Greccio set out their table more carefully than was their custom, with white linens and glassware. [2] But the father, coming down from his cell, approached the table, beheld it set on high and vainly adorned; yet upon that smiling table he by no means smiled. [3] Stealthily and step by step he withdrew his feet, set upon his own head the hat of a certain poor man who happened to be there, <ref to="Ps 40:7">and, carrying a staff in his hand, went out of doors</ref>. [4] <ref to="John 18:16">He waited outside at the door</ref> until the brothers should begin to eat; for they were accustomed not to wait for him whenever he did not come at the signal. [5] As they began to eat, <ref to="Ps 33:7">the true poor man cried out</ref> at the door: "For the love of the Lord God," he said, "give <ref to="Matt 6:2">alms</ref> to this poor pilgrim, sick and a stranger." [6] The brothers answered: "Come in here, man, for the love of him whom you have invoked." [7] At once, then, he entered, and presented himself to those who were eating. [8] But how great an astonishment, do you suppose, this pilgrim brought upon those who dwelt there? [9] A bowl was given to the one who asked, and, sitting alone upon the ground, he set his dish in the ashes. [10] "Now I sit," he said, "as a Friar Minor"; and to the brothers: <ref to="John 5:25">"The examples of the poverty of the Son of God</ref> ought to constrain us more than other religious. [11] I saw the table prepared and adorned, and I knew it was not that of poor men going from door to door." [12] That this man was like that pilgrim <ref to="Luke 24:18">who alone was in Jerusalem</ref> on that same day, the unfolding of the event proves. [13] No less did he make the heart of the disciples burn <ref to="Luke 24:32">while he spoke</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter XXXII — Against an excessive curiosity for books <a id="op2-32"></a>

<p id="62">
[1] He taught that in books one should seek the testimony of the Lord, not their value; edification, not beauty. [2] Yet he wished only a few to be kept, and these ready to meet the needs of brothers in want. [3] Hence, when a certain minister sought his permission to retain books that were costly and of great worth, he heard from him: "The book of the Gospel which I have promised, I am unwilling to lose for the sake of your books. [4] <ref to="2 Kgs 24:12">You indeed shall do what you will</ref>; but my permission shall not become a snare."

## Chapter XXXIII - The Example of the Lord of Ostia, and His Praise <a id="op2-33"></a>

<p id="63">
[1] In their bedding and couches, so generous was their poverty that whoever had upon the straw a few half-sound rags reckoned it a marriage-chamber. [2] Hence it happened, at the time when the chapter was being held at Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, that the lord of Ostia came thither with a throng of knights and clerics to visit the brothers. [3] <ref to="Jdt 14:16">Seeing how the brothers lay upon the ground</ref> and considering their beds, which you would have believed to be the lairs of wild beasts, he wept most bitterly and said before them all: "Behold, here sleep the brothers." [4] And he added: <ref to="Matt 19:27">"But what shall become of us wretches,</ref> who abuse so great a superfluity?" [5] All who were present, pricked even to tears and greatly edified, departed. [6] This was that lord of Ostia who, <ref to="1 Cor 16:9">having at length become a very great door</ref> in the Church, ever withstood her enemies, until he poured back into heaven that holy victim, that blessed soul. [7] O devout heart, O bowels of charity! [8] Set on high, he grieved that he did not possess high merits, though in truth he was loftier in virtue than in seat.
</p>

## Chapter XXXIV - What befell him one night on account of a feather pillow <a id="op2-34"></a>

<p id="64">
[1] Since we have made mention of beds, there comes to mind another matter perhaps worth the telling. [2] <ref to="Acts 11:21">From the time when this holy man, converted to Christ,</ref> <ref to="Lam 2:6">consigned to oblivion</ref> <ref to="1 Cor 7:33">the things that are of the world,</ref> he refused to lie upon a mattress, nor to have beneath his head a cushion of feathers. [3] Neither sickness nor lodging away from home could break through the bar of this strictness. [4] Yet it happened in the hermitage of Greccio that, being weighed down by an infirmity of the eyes worse than usual, he was compelled against his will to make use of a small pillow. [5] So in the early watch of the first night the holy man called his companion and said to him: "Brother, this night I have not been able to sleep, nor to remain upright at prayer. [6] My head trembles, my knees give way, and the whole frame of my body is shaken, as though I had eaten bread made of cockle. [7] I believe," he said, "that the devil is lodging in this pillow I have beneath my head. [8] Take it away, for I will have the devil at my head no longer." [9] The brother, with sympathetic murmur, felt for the father, and taking up the pillow flung toward him, he made to carry it off. [10] But as he went out, he suddenly lost his speech, and was pressed down and bound by so great a horror that he could neither move his feet from the spot nor draw his arms in any direction. [11] After a little while, called by the saint who had perceived this, he was set free, <ref to="Heb 5:8">and returned and told what he had suffered.</ref> [12] To him the saint said: "This evening, while I was saying Compline, I plainly knew that the devil was coming to my cell." [13] And again: "Very crafty and subtle of wit is this enemy of ours, who, when he cannot do harm within the soul, at least supplies the body with occasion for murmuring." [14] <ref to="Ezek 13:18">Let them hear who prepare little cushions under every elbow,</ref> so that wherever they fall they may be received upon soft things. [15] Gladly does the devil pursue abundance of possessions; he delights to attend upon costly beds, especially where necessity does not compel and one's profession forbids. [16] <ref to="Rev 12:9">No less does the ancient serpent</ref> flee from a naked man, whether scorning the fellowship of the poor or dreading the loftiness of poverty. [17] If a brother takes heed that the devil lurks beneath feathers, his head will be content with straw.
</p>

## Chapter XXXV - The harsh correction of a brother who touched her with his hands <a id="op2-35"></a>

<p id="65">
[1] Yet, though the friend of God <ref to="1 Cor 7:33"> scorned all the things that are of the world </ref> with the utmost zeal, money above all else he held in abomination. [2] Hence, from the very beginning of his conversion, he despised it especially, and ever signified to those who followed him that it was to be fled as the devil himself. [3] This was the discernment he imparted to his own: that they should weigh dung and money at the selfsame price of love. [4] <ref to="Gen 39:11"> It happened, then, on a certain day </ref> that a certain worldly man entered the church of Saint Mary of the Portiuncula to pray, and for the sake of an offering he laid money beside the cross. [5] When he had departed, one of the friars, simply touching it with his hand, cast it onto the windowsill. [6] What the friar had done reached the saint; and seeing himself found out, the friar ran to seek pardon, and prostrate upon the ground offered himself for stripes. [7] The saint rebuked him and most harshly upbraided him for having touched the money. [8] He commanded him to lift the money from the windowsill with his own mouth, and outside the bounds of the place to set it down with his mouth upon a heap of asses' dung. [9] And while that friar gladly fulfilled the command, fear filled the hearts of all who heard of it. [10] From then on they all the more held in contempt a thing thus likened to dung, and were daily stirred to its contempt by ever new examples.

## Chapter XXXVI - The Punishment of the Brother Who Once Collected Money <a id="op2-36"></a>

<p id="66">
[1] Two friars, journeying once together, drew near to a certain hospital for lepers. [2] On the road they came upon a coin; they halted their steps and fell to debating what ought to be done with this filth. [3] One of them, mocking his brother's scruples, made to pick up the coin, that it might be offered to the lepers, those servants of money. [4] His companion forbade him, calling him deceived by a false piety, and pressed upon the rash man the word of the Rule, by which it shines clearly enough that a coin so found ought to be trampled like dust. [5] But that one hardened his mind against the admonition, <ref to="Exod 32:9">for he was ever, by his habit, of a stiff neck</ref>. [6] He spurned the Rule, stooped down, and took up the money; yet he did not escape the divine judgment. [7] On the instant he lost his speech, <ref to="Ps 34:16">he gnashed his teeth</ref>, and could not prevail to speak. [8] Thus the punishment betrayed the madman, thus the vengeance taught the proud man to obey his father's laws. [9] At length, when the foul thing had been cast away, <ref to="Isa 6:5">his polluted lips</ref>, washed clean by the waters of repentance, were loosed into praise. [10] There is an old proverb: Correct the fool, and he will be your friend.
</p>

## Chapter XXXVII — The Rebuke of a Friar Who, Under the Pretext of Necessity, Wished to Keep Money <a id="op2-37"></a>

<p id="67">
[1] When the saint's vicar, Brother Peter Cathani, once saw that Saint Mary of the Portiuncula was thronged by crowds of friars from abroad, and that the alms were not abundant enough to provide for their necessities, he said to Saint Francis: "I do not know, brother, <ref to="John 15:15">what I am to do</ref>, since friars are flocking together from every side in such droves and I have no means of providing for them sufficiently. [2] May it please you, I beg you, that certain goods of the novices entering the order be set aside, <ref to="Ps 144:15">so that we may have recourse to spending them when the time is right</ref>." [3] The saint replied: "Far be such piety from us, dearest brother, that for any man's sake we should act impiously against the Rule." [4] And he said: <ref to="John 15:15">"What then am I to do?"</ref> "The altar," said Francis, "strip the Virgin's altar and take away its varied adornment, when you cannot otherwise come to the aid of those in need. [5] Believe me, she will hold it more pleasing that the Gospel of her Son be kept and her altar stripped, than that the altar be vested and her Son despised. [6] The Lord will send someone to restore to the Mother what he has lent to us."
</p>

## Chapter XXXVIII - How money was turned into a serpent <a id="op2-38"></a>

<p id="68">
[1] <ref to="Judg 13:6">As the man of God was once passing</ref> with a companion through Apulia near Bari, he found upon the road a great purse, swollen with coins, of the kind that in the parlance of merchants is called a moneybag. [2] The saint was urged by his companion, and earnestly pressed, to take the purse up from the ground and distribute the money to the poor. [3] Pity for the needy was held aloft, and in the giving away of that sum mercy was commended. [4] The saint utterly refused that he would do such a thing, and declared it to be a contrivance of the devil. [5] "It is not lawful, my son," he said, "to carry off what belongs to another; to give away what is another's is the penalty of sin, not the glory of merit." [6] They departed from the place and <ref to="Judg 19:14">hastened to complete the journey they had begun</ref>. But the brother was not yet at rest, deluded by an empty pity; <ref to="2 Kgs 5:22">he went on still</ref> to urge transgression. [7] The saint consented to return to the place, <ref to="Num 15:8">not to fulfill the brother's wish</ref>, <ref to="Dan 2:29">but to reveal a divine mystery to the foolish one</ref>. [8] He summoned a certain young man, <ref to="John 4:6">who was sitting beside a well</ref> along the road, <ref to="Matt 18:16">so that in the mouth of two or three witnesses</ref> the sacrament of the Trinity might shine forth. [9] When the three had now returned to the moneybag, they saw it swollen with coins. [10] The saint forbade any of them to draw near, so that by the power of prayer the demon's deceit might be exposed. [11] Withdrawing from there, <ref to="Luke 22:41">about a stone's throw</ref>, he gave himself to holy prayer. [12] Returning from prayer, he ordered the brother to lift up the purse which, as he prayed, held in place of money a serpent. [13] The brother trembled and was astonished, and now sensing I know not what, he turned over thoughts other than his wont. [14] At last, driving the doubt from his heart through fear of holy obedience, he took the purse in his hands. [15] And behold, a serpent of no small size, leaping out of the purse, showed the brother the diabolical deception. [16] And the saint said to him: <ref to="Gen 50:17">"To the servants of God, money</ref>, O brother, is nothing else than the devil and a venomous serpent."
</p>

## Chapter XXXIX - How the Saint rebuked, by word and example, those clothed in soft and luxurious garments <a id="op2-39"></a>

<p id="69">
[1] <ref to="Luke 24:49"> Clothed with power from on high </ref>, this man burned more fiercely within with the divine fire than he was covered without by any bodily garment. [2] He abhorred those clad in triple garments, <ref to="Matt 11:8">and those who, beyond necessity, made use of soft things </ref> within the order. [3] As for that necessity which is paraded not by reason but by self-indulgence, he declared it <ref to="1 Thess 5:19">a sign of the spirit extinguished </ref>. [4] "When the spirit grows tepid," he said, "and little by little cools away from grace, <ref to="Phil 2:21">it is inevitable that flesh and blood should seek the things that are their own </ref>. [5] For what remains," he said, "when the soul finds no delights, save that the flesh turn to its own? [6] Then the animal appetite cloaks itself under the pretext of necessity; <ref to="Col 2:18">then the mind of the flesh </ref> shapes the conscience to its liking." [7] And he would add: "Suppose true necessity should beset my brother, and some real want touch him: if he hastens to satisfy it and to drive it far from himself, <ref to="Gen 29:15">what reward shall he receive </ref>? [8] An occasion for merit had indeed fallen to him, but he proved that diligence displeased him." [9] With these sayings and the like he pierced those who knew nothing of true privations, since not to bear those privations patiently is nothing other than to long again for Egypt. [10] In short, he willed that on no pretext should the brothers have more than two tunics, which nonetheless he permitted them to mend with patches stitched on. [11] He bade them shrink in horror from costly cloth, and most sharply did he reproach before all those who acted otherwise; and that he might shame such men by his own example, <ref to="Job 16:16">he sewed a rough sackcloth upon his own tunic </ref>; even in death he asked that his funeral tunic be covered with a cheap sack. [12] To brothers, however, whom infirmity or some other necessity pressed hard, he allowed a soft tunic next to the flesh, yet on this condition: that outwardly in their habit the roughness and meanness be preserved. [13] For he used to say: "So far shall strictness yet be relaxed, and tepidity hold sway, that the sons of a poor father will not in the least scruple to wear even scarlet, with only the color changed." [14] Not from this, O father, do <ref to="Ps 17:46">we, your sons, prove false to you as strangers </ref>; rather <ref to="Ps 27:12">our own iniquity lies against itself </ref>. [15] For behold, clearer than light it grows known, and the day advances.
</p>

## Chapter XL - He declares that those who depart from poverty must be corrected by necessity <a id="op2-40"></a>

<p id="70">
[1] At times the saint would repeat this saying as well: "As far as the brothers turn aside from poverty, so far will the world turn aside from them, and they shall seek," he said, <ref to="Rev 9:6">"and shall not find."</ref> [2] But if they embrace my lady Poverty, the world will nourish them, for they have been given to the world for its salvation." [3] And again: "There is a commerce between the world and the brothers: they owe the world a good example, and the world owes them the provision of their needs. [4] When they withdraw the good example through faithlessness of mind, the world will withdraw its hand with just reckoning." [5] <ref to="4 Kgs 1:9">Guarding poverty, the man of God</ref> feared a multitude, for though not in fact, yet in appearance it shows one to be rich. [6] Hence he would say: <ref to="Mark 14:35">"O if it could but happen</ref>—it can happen, I say—that the world, beholding the Friars Minor but rarely, might marvel at their fewness!" [7] Bound therefore by an indissoluble bond to his lady Poverty, he awaited not her present but her future dowry. [8] The psalms that sing of poverty—such as that one, <ref to="Ps 9:19">The patience of the poor shall not perish in the end</ref>, and, <ref to="Ps 68:33">Let the poor see and be glad</ref>—he would chant with a more fervent affection and a more joyful jubilation.
</p>

On the asking of alms.

## Chapter XLI - On the Commendation of the Man Himself <a id="op2-41"></a>

<p id="71">
[1] The holy father far preferred to make use of alms begged from door to door than of those freely offered. [2] He used to say that shame in begging was the enemy of salvation, while affirming that the modesty shown in the act of begging—the kind that does not draw back the foot—was holy. [3] He praised the blush rising on a tender brow, but not the man overwhelmed by bashfulness. [4] Sometimes, urging his brothers to go and seek alms, <ref to="Sir 20:8">he would use these words</ref>: [5] "Go forth," he said, <ref to="1 John 2:18">"for in this last hour</ref><ref to="1 Kgs 1:28"> the Friars Minor have been lent</ref> to the world, so that the elect may fulfill in them that for which they shall be commended by the Judge: 'Whatever you did to one of these my least brothers, <ref to="Matt 25:40">you did to me</ref>.'" [6] <ref to="Luke 7:16">Hence he called the order privileged by the Great Prophet</ref>, <ref to="2 Kgs 18:18">who set forth the title of its name</ref> so plainly. [7] And for this reason he wished the brothers to dwell not only in cities but in the wilderness as well, <ref to="John 15:20; 2 Cor 3:16">where occasion for merit might be granted to all, and the veil of excuse</ref> taken away from the reprobate.
</p>

## Chapter XLII - The Saint's Example concerning the Seeking of Alms <a id="op2-42"></a>

<p id="72">
[1] Lest he should offend that holy bride even once, this servant of God Most High was accustomed to do as follows: [2] if he were ever invited by lords and was to be honored at lavish tables, he would first go seeking <ref to="Matt 15:36-37">scraps of bread through the neighboring houses round about </ref>, <ref to="2 Cor 8:9">and then, thus enriched by want </ref>, he would hasten to take his place at table. [3] Being once asked why he did this, he would answer that he was unwilling to forsake a lasting inheritance for the sake of a fief granted but for an hour. [4] "Poverty," he said, <ref to="Matt 5:3; Jas 2:5">"is what makes men heirs and kings of the kingdom of heaven </ref>, not those false riches of yours."
</p>

## Chapter XLIII — An example which he set in the court of the Lord of Ostia, and concerning his reply to the bishop <a id="op2-43"></a>

<p id="73">
[1] On one occasion, when Saint Francis was visiting the pope of venerable memory, Gregory—who was then still established in a lesser office—and the hour of the meal was already pressing, he went out for alms, and returning he set the scraps of black bread upon the bishop's table. [2] At this sight the bishop was somewhat ashamed, especially on account of the guests who had been newly invited. [3] But the father, with cheerful countenance, distributed the alms he had received among the knights and chaplains dining with him, and all of them received them with wondrous devotion, some eating them, others keeping them out of reverence. [4] When the meal was finished, the bishop rose and, drawing the man of God aside within, <ref to="Gen 33:4">he lifted up his arms and embraced him</ref>. [5] "My brother," he said, "why have you brought shame upon me in the house, which is yours and your brothers', by going out for alms?" [6] To him the saint replied: "Rather have I shown you honor, in that I have honored a greater Lord. [7] <ref to="Ps 67:17">For it is well pleasing to the Lord in</ref> poverty, and most of all in that which is voluntary begging. [8] As for me, I hold a royal dignity and a singular nobility: <ref to="Matt 19:21">to follow that Lord</ref> who, though he was rich, <ref to="2 Cor 8:9">for our sake became poor</ref>." [9] And he added: "I take more delight from a poor table that is laid with little alms than from great tables on which the dishes are <ref to="Ps 39:13">of which there is scarcely any number</ref>." [10] Thereupon the bishop, greatly edified, said to the saint: "Son, do what is good in your eyes, <ref to="1 Kgs 3:18; Josh 1:9">for the Lord is with you</ref>."
</p>

## Chapter XLIV — His exhortation to the seeking of alms, both by example and by word <a id="op2-44"></a>

<p id="74">
[1] Often, indeed, training himself and sparing the modesty of the brothers, he alone in the beginning would go about to beg for alms. [2] But seeing that many were not duly heeding their calling, he once said on a certain occasion: "Dearest brothers, the <ref to="Phil 4:1; Matt 4:3">Son of God</ref> was nobler than we, who 'made himself poor for our sake in this world.' [3] <ref to="Ps 118:30">For love of him we have chosen the way of poverty</ref>; we ought not to be ashamed to go for alms. [4] It is in no way fitting for the heirs of the kingdom to blush at <ref to="Jas 2:5">the pledge of the heavenly inheritance</ref>. [5] <ref to="Matt 5:22">I say to you</ref>, that <ref to="1 Cor 1:26">many noble and wise men</ref> shall be joined to our congregation, who will count it an honor to beg for alms. [6] You therefore, who are their firstfruits, <ref to="Ps 31:11; Matt 5:12">be glad and rejoice</ref>, and do not refuse to do what you hand on as a task to be done by those holy ones."
</p>

## Chapter XLV - The Rebuke of a Friar Unwilling to Beg <a id="op2-45"></a>

<p id="75">
[1] Blessed Francis often said that a true Friar Minor ought not to remain long without going forth to beg alms. [2] "And the nobler my son is," he said, "the readier let him be to go, for in this way his merits are heaped up the more." [3] In a certain place there was a friar who was no help when it came to begging, but a great help at table. [4] Observing him to be a friend of his belly, a sharer in the fruit but <ref to="Heb 5:13">untried by labor</ref>, the saint once said to him: "Go your way, brother fly, <ref to="1 Cor 15:58">for you wish to devour the sweat of your brothers and to be idle in the work of God</ref>. [5] You are like brother drone, who, unwilling to bear the toil of the bees, wants to be the first to eat the honey." [6] The carnal man, caught out and recognizing his own gluttony, returned to the world he had not yet truly forsaken. [7] For he left religion behind; and he who had been no help in begging was now no friar at all, and he who had been so much help at table became a most ample demon.
</p>

## Chapter XLVI — How, meeting a brother who was carrying alms, he kissed his shoulder <a id="op2-46"></a>

<p id="76">
[1] On another occasion at the Portiuncula, when a certain brother was returning from Assisi with the alms he had gathered, he drew near to the place and began to break forth into song, <ref to="Ps 134:1">praising the Lord with a loud voice</ref>. [2] Hearing this, the saint suddenly sprang up, ran out, and, kissing the brother's shoulder, laid the little sack upon his own: [3] "Blessed," he said, "be my brother, who sets out readily, seeks humbly, and <ref to="Luke 10:17">returns rejoicing</ref>."
</p>

## Chapter XLVII - How he prevailed upon worldly knights to beg for alms <a id="op2-47"></a>

<p id="77">
[1] It happened that blessed Francis, full of infirmities and now drawing near to his end, was sought out at the place of Nocera by the people of Assisi, who had dispatched their official envoys, <ref to="Isa 42:8">lest they yield their glory to another</ref> <ref to="3 Kgs 13:29">in the matter of the body of the man of God</ref>. [2] And as the knights reverently bore him along on horseback, they came to a certain very poor village named Satriano. [3] There, when hunger and the hour demanded food, <ref to="Matt 21:19">going about and finding nothing for sale</ref>, the knights returned to blessed Francis and said: "You must give us something from your alms, <ref to="2 Cor 6:10">since here we can have nothing</ref> to buy." [4] <ref to="John 4:13">The saint answered and said</ref>: "You find nothing for this reason, <ref to="Matt 27:43">because you put more trust in your flies than in God</ref>." For he called coins "flies." [5] "But go back," he said, "through the houses you have visited, and offering the love of God in place of coins, humbly beg for alms! [6] Do not be ashamed, for after the fall all things have been granted as alms, and that great Almsgiver bestows them with merciful tenderness upon the worthy and the unworthy alike." [7] The knights laid aside their shame, and promptly asking for alms, they purchased more for the love of God than they would have for coins. [8] Indeed, all the people gave cheerfully, vying with one another, and hunger did not prevail, <ref to="Gen 12:10">where opulent poverty held sway</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter XLVIII - Concerning a Portion of Capon at Alexandria Turned into Fish <a id="op2-48"></a>

<p id="78">
[1] In the giving of alms he sought the profit of souls rather than the relief of the flesh, and <ref to="Sir 4:36">no less in giving than in receiving</ref> he set himself before others as an example (cf. Na 3:6). [2] <ref to="Acts 13:5">For when, intending to preach the word of God, he was approaching Alexandria in Lombardy</ref>, <ref to="Job 1:1; Job 2:3">and had been devoutly received into lodging by a certain man who feared God</ref> <ref to="1 Tim 5:10">and was of praiseworthy reputation</ref>, being asked by him to eat, out of regard for the observance of the holy Gospel, of all that was set before him, he graciously consented, overcome by his host's devotion. [3] The man hastened off and zealously prepared a seven-year-old capon for the man of God to eat. [4] As the patriarch sat at the table of the poor and the household made merry, <ref to="1 Kgs 25:17">suddenly there appeared at the door a son of Belial</ref>, a beggar destitute of all grace, feigning a poverty of the things he needed. [5] Craftily he put forward the love of God by begging an alms, and in a tearful voice he demanded that, for God's sake, relief be given him. [6] The saint recognized that name which is <ref to="Rom 9:5">blessed above all things</ref> and <ref to="Judg 14:18">sweeter to him than honey</ref>; most gladly he took up a portion of the bird set before him and, laying it upon some bread, passed it to the one who asked. [7] Need more be said? The wretch kept back the gift, that he might bring reproach upon the saint.
</p>

<p id="79">
[1] <ref to="John 1:43; John 12:12"> On the following day, with the people gathered together, </ref><ref to="Acts 13:5"> the holy man preached the word of God after his custom </ref>. [2] Suddenly that wicked fellow let out a roar, <ref to="Acts 4:10">and strove to display a capon's leg to all the populace</ref>. [3] "Behold," he gabbled, "what sort of man this Francis is, this man who preaches, whom you honor as a saint: look at the meat he gave me yesterday evening while he was eating!" [4] Everyone rebuked that most depraved man, and all reviled him as one filled with a demon. [5] For what he was striving to assert was a capon's leg appeared in truth to all to be a fish. [6] Indeed, the wretch himself, dumbfounded by the miracle, was compelled to confess what the others were declaring. [7] At last the unhappy man blushed, <ref to="Josh 7:15">and with penitence he washed away the crime that had been detected</ref>. [8] Before them all he begged the saint's pardon, laying bare the wicked intent he had harbored. [9] The meat returned to its own appearance, <ref to="Isa 46:8">after the transgressor returned to his right mind</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter XLIX - An example of a certain man who gave his goods to his kinsfolk rather than to the poor, whom the saint rebuked <a id="op2-49"></a>

<p id="80">
[1] The saint taught those coming to the Order <ref to="Matt 5:31">first to hand the world a writ of divorce</ref>, to give away their outward possessions first, and <ref to="Heb 9:14">afterward to offer themselves inwardly to God</ref>. [2] He would admit none to the Order save those who had stripped themselves of everything and retained absolutely nothing, <ref to="Matt 19:21">both on account of the word of the holy Gospel</ref>, <ref to="Ps 105:36">and lest the purses kept back become an occasion of scandal</ref>.
</p>

<p id="81">
[1] It happened in the March of Ancona, after the saint's preaching, that a certain man came to him, humbly asking to enter the Order. [2] To him the saint replied: <ref to="Jas 2:5">"If you wish to be joined to God's poor,</ref> first <ref to="1 Cor 13:3">distribute your goods to the world's poor</ref>." [3] When he heard this, the man set off, but, led by carnal affection, he scattered his possessions among his own kin <ref to="Ps 111:9">and gave nothing to the poor</ref>. [4] And when he returned and reported his generous munificence to the saint, the father, mocking him, said: "Go your way, brother fly, <ref to="Gen 12:1">for you have not yet gone forth from your house and your kindred</ref>. [5] You have given your goods to your blood relations <ref to="Sir 34:25">and defrauded the poor</ref>; <ref to="Matt 8:8">you are not worthy</ref> of the holy poor. [6] You have begun from the flesh; <ref to="Wis 4:3">you have laid a crumbling foundation for a spiritual edifice</ref>." [7] <ref to="1 Cor 2:14">The natural man returned</ref> <ref to="Luke 6:30">to his own and reclaimed what was his</ref>, and being unwilling to leave these things to the poor, he all the more swiftly abandoned his resolve of virtue. [8] Today such a pitiable distribution deceives many who seek the blessed life by beginning with temporal things. [9] <ref to="Judg 16:17">For no one consecrates himself to God for this purpose,</ref> <ref to="Prov 10:22">that he may make his kin rich,</ref> <ref to="Exod 13:13">but that, redeeming his sins by the price of mercy,</ref> <ref to="Rom 2:7; Phil 1:22">he may gain life by the fruit of good works</ref>. [10] Often too, 'should the brothers be in need,' he taught them to have recourse rather to others than to those entering the Order—first indeed for the sake of example, and then to avoid all appearance of shameful advantage.
</p>

## Chapter 50 — Concerning a Certain Vision That Bears Upon Poverty <a id="op2-50"></a>

<p id="82">
[1] It is fitting here to recount a vision of the saint that deserves remembrance. [2] One night, when at last a long prayer had been concluded, he slowly drifted off and fell asleep. [3] <ref to="Ps 72:17">That holy soul was led into the sanctuary of God</ref>, and <ref to="Gen 20:3">in his sleep he saw</ref>, among other things, a certain lady of this fashion: her head appeared golden, her breast and arms silver, her belly of crystal, and from there downward, in the lowest parts, of iron; <ref to="Dan 2:32-33">she was of lofty stature</ref>, fitted together with delicate framework and proportion. [4] <ref to="Ezek 23:23">Yet this lady, though of surpassing beauty</ref>, was draped in a filthy mantle. [5] <ref to="Gen 24:54">Rising in the morning</ref>, the blessed father recounted the vision to that holy man, Brother Pacificus, yet without making clear what it might portend. [6] And although many have interpreted it as they pleased, I think it not amiss to hold to the interpretation of the aforesaid Pacificus, <ref to="John 14:26">which the Holy Spirit suggested to him in the very hearing of it</ref>. [7] "This lady," he said, <ref to="Ezek 23:23">"of surpassing beauty</ref>, is the lovely soul of holy Francis. [8] The golden head is the contemplation and wisdom of things eternal; <ref to="Dan 2:32">the breast and arms of silver</ref> are <ref to="Ps 11:7">the words of the Lord</ref>, <ref to="Ps 118:11">pondered in his heart</ref> <ref to="Gen 11:6">and fulfilled in deed</ref>; [9] the hard crystal signifies sobriety, the splendid crystal chastity; the iron is steadfast perseverance; [10] and as for the filthy mantle, take it to be his despised little body, <ref to="Prov 6:26">by which the precious soul</ref> is covered." [11] Many, however, <ref to="Dan 4:5; 1 Cor 7:40">having the spirit of God</ref>, understand this lady, as the bride of the father, to be poverty. [12] "This poverty," they say, "the reward of glory made golden, the proclamation of fame made silvery, the single profession—without purses, the same without and within—made crystalline, and final perseverance made of iron. [13] <ref to="1 Cor 2:14">But for this most illustrious lady the filthy mantle was woven by the esteem of animal men</ref>." [14] Many others apply this oracle to the religious order, following the succession of the ages along Daniel's course. [15] But that it pertains to the father is here made most clear, since, avoiding all arrogance, he refused outright to interpret it. [16] And indeed, had it concerned the order, he would not have passed it over in mute silence.
</p>

## Chapter LI - On the compassion he had toward the poor, and how he envied those poorer than himself <a id="op2-51"></a>

<p id="83">
[1] What tongue could possibly recount how great was this man's compassion for the poor? [2] Truly he possessed an inborn gentleness, which a piety poured into him from above redoubled. [3] And so <ref to="Song 5:6">the soul of Francis melted</ref> toward the poor, and to those upon whom he could not bestow his hand he offered his affection. [4] Whatever want, whatever poverty he perceived in anyone, with mind drawn back and swift turning he referred it to Christ. [5] Thus in all the poor he read the Son of the poor Lady, bearing in his heart, naked, the One whom she bore naked in her hands. [6] But although <ref to="1 Pet 2:1">he had banished all envy from himself</ref>, of one envy alone he could not be free—the envy of poverty. [7] If ever he beheld anyone poorer than himself, at once he grew envious, and contending in rivalry of poverty, he feared to be outdone by that man.
</p>

<p id="84">
[1] <ref to="Gen 39:11"> It happened one day </ref>, <ref to="1 Kgs 2:27">while the man of God was going about preaching </ref>, that he met a poor little man upon the road. [2] When he beheld the fellow's nakedness, he was pierced to the heart and turned to his companion, saying: "This man's want has put us greatly to shame, and it sharply rebukes our poverty." [3] His companion answered him: "For what reason, brother?" And the saint replied in a mournful voice: "In place of my riches, in place of my lady, I chose poverty, and behold, it shines forth more brightly in this man. [4] Do you not know that it has resounded throughout the whole world that we, for Christ's sake, are the poorest of all? But this poor man proves that the matter stands otherwise!" [5] O envy that is no envy! O rivalry to be emulated by sons! [6] This is not that envy which is tormented by the goods of others; not that which is darkened by the rays of the sun; not that which sets itself against tenderness; not that which is wracked by spite. [7] Do you suppose that evangelical poverty has nothing to be envied? It possesses Christ, <ref to="1 Cor 12:6">and through him all things in all </ref>. [8] Why do you gape after revenues, you cleric of today? Tomorrow you shall know that Francis was rich, when you find in your own hand the revenues of torments.
</p>

## Chapter LII — How he corrected a brother who spoke disparagingly of a certain poor man <a id="op2-52"></a>

<p id="85">
[1] On another day of his preaching, a certain poor and sickly man came to the place. [2] Pitying his twofold affliction—his poverty, that is, and his frailty—he began to speak with his companion about poverty. [3] <ref to="Ps 72:7">And when, suffering with the sufferer, he had already passed into the very feeling of that man's heart</ref>, the saint's companion said to him: "Brother, it is true that this man is poor, but perhaps in the whole province there is none richer in will." [4] At once the saint rebuked him, and to the one who had spoken he declared his fault: "Hasten quickly, <ref to="Isa 5:19; Bar 5:1">and strip off your</ref> tunic, and casting yourself at the poor man's feet proclaim yourself guilty! [5] And do not merely beg pardon—nay, implore his prayers!" [6] He obeyed and went to make satisfaction and returned. [7] To him the saint said: "When you see a poor man, O brother, there is set before you a mirror of the Lord and of his poor Mother. [8] In the sick likewise consider <ref to="Matt 8:17; Isa 53:4">the infirmities which</ref> he took upon himself <ref to="Matt 8:17; Isa 53:4">for us</ref>!" [9] Behold, <ref to="Song 1:12">a little bundle of myrrh ever abode</ref> with Francis, <ref to="Ps 83:10">ever he gazes upon the face of his Christ</ref>, <ref to="Isa 53:3">ever he touches the man of sorrows and one acquainted with infirmities</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter LIII - Concerning the cloak given to an old woman at Celano <a id="op2-53"></a>

<p id="86">
[1] It happened at Celano during the winter season that Saint Francis had folded about him a cloth in the manner of a mantle, which a certain man of Tibur, a friend of the brothers, had lent to him. [2] And while he was in the palace of the Bishop of the Marsi, <ref to="Acts 3:2">a certain old woman met him, begging for alms</ref>. [3] At once he loosed the cloth from his neck and, though it was another's, gave it to the poor old woman, saying: "Go, make yourself a tunic, for you are in great want." [4] The old woman smiled, and astonished—I know not whether from fear or from joy—took the cloth from his hands. [5] She ran off swiftly, and lest delay should bring the danger of his asking it back, cut it with her shears. [6] But when she found that the cloth she had cut would not suffice for a tunic, having already tasted his first kindness, she returned to the saint, pointing out the shortfall in the cloth. [7] The saint turned his eyes to his companion, who bore upon his back just as much of the same cloth: "Do you hear," he said, "brother, what this poor little woman says? [8] For the love of God let us bear the cold, and give the poor little woman the cloth, that she may complete her tunic." [9] He himself had given, and his companion gave likewise, and both remained naked, that the old woman might be clothed.
</p>

## Chapter LIV - Concerning another poor man to whom he gave another cloak <a id="op2-54"></a>

<p id="87">
[1] On another occasion, as he was returning from Siena, he met a certain poor man, and the saint said to his companion: "We must, brother, give back the cloak to the poor little man whose it is. [2] We have <ref to="Luke 6:34"> received it as a loan </ref>, until it should happen that we find someone poorer." [3] The companion, mindful of the holy father's own need, stubbornly resisted, lest he provide for another while neglecting himself. [4] To him the saint replied: <ref to="John 12:6">"I have no wish to be a thief </ref>; it would be reckoned to us as theft, were we not to give it to one more needy." [5] The companion gave way, and the saint handed over the cloak.
</p>

## Chapter LV - He did the like for another poor man <a id="op2-55"></a>

<p id="88">
[1] Something similar occurred at the Hermitage of Cortona. [2] Blessed Francis was wearing a new cloak, which the brothers had diligently sought out on his behalf. [3] A poor man came to the place, <ref to="Ps 10:14">weeping for his dead wife and the destitute little family he had been left</ref>. [4] To him the saint said: "For the love of God I hand over this cloak to you, on this condition: that you give it up to no one unless he pays a fair price for it." [5] The brothers came running at once to take the cloak away and to thwart this gift. [6] But the poor man, drawing boldness from the face of the holy father, clutched it with hooked hands and defended it as his own. [7] <ref to="2 Macc 5:5">In the end</ref> the brothers redeemed the cloak, and the poor man, having received the price, went off.
</p>

## Chapter LVI - How he gave his cloak to a certain man, lest he should hate his lord <a id="op2-56"></a>

<p id="89">
[1] On one occasion, at Colle in the county of Perugia, Saint Francis came upon a certain poor little man whom he had known earlier in the world. [2] And he said to him: "Brother, how are you faring?" [3] <ref to="Num 5:19">But the man, with an evil disposition, began to heap up curses against his lord</ref>, who had stripped him of all that he possessed: [4] "By the grace of my lord," he said, <ref to="Rev 1:8; Gen 5:29">"upon whom may the Almighty Lord pour His curse</ref>, <ref to="Matt 4:24">I fare nothing but ill</ref>." [5] Pitying his soul more than his body, since the man persisted in deadly hatred, blessed Francis said to him: "Brother, forgive your lord for the love of God, <ref to="Esth 4:13">that you may set your soul free</ref>, <ref to="Exod 22:12">and it may yet come to pass that he himself will restore to you what was taken away</ref>. [6] <ref to="Luke 9:24">But if you do not, then, having lost your goods, you will lose your soul as well</ref>." [7] And the man said: "I cannot forgive at all, unless he first gives back what he took." [8] Blessed Francis, having a certain mantle upon his back, said to him: "Behold, I give you this mantle, and I beg you <ref to="Isa 42:5">to forgive your lord for the love of the Lord God</ref>." [9] Sweetened and won over by the kindness, the man, once he had received the gift, let go of his grievances.
</p>

## Chapter LVII - How he gave part of his tunic to a certain poor man <a id="op2-57"></a>

<p id="90">
[1] On one occasion, when a poor man begged of him and he had nothing in his hands, he tore off a piece of his own tunic and gave it to the poor man. [2] Not infrequently, too, for a like work he drew off his breeches. <ref to="Col 3:12">[3] With such bowels of compassion did he overflow toward the poor</ref>, and with such affections did he <ref to="1 Pet 2:21">follow the footsteps of the poor Christ</ref>.

## Chapter LVIII - How he caused the first New Testament that existed in the Order to be given to the poor mother of two brothers <a id="op2-58"></a>

<p id="91">
[1] The mother of two brothers once came to the saint, <ref to="Acts 3:2">confidently begging alms</ref>. [2] The holy father, taking pity on her, said to his vicar, Brother Peter Cathanii: <ref to="Matt 6:2">"Can we give some alms</ref> to our mother?" [3] For he called the mother of any one of the brothers both his own mother and the mother of them all. [4] Brother Peter answered him: "Nothing remains in the house <ref to="Luke 11:41">that could be given</ref> to her." [5] And he added: "We have a single New Testament, in which—lacking a breviary—we read the lessons at matins." [6] To him blessed Francis said: "Give our mother the New Testament, that she may sell it to meet her need, since through that very book we are admonished to come to the aid of the poor. [7] Indeed, I believe that the gift will please God more than the reading of it." [8] So the book was given to the woman, and the first Testament there ever was in the Order was thus surrendered out of holy devotion.
</p>

## Chapter LIX — How he gave his mantle to a certain poor woman who was suffering in her eyes <a id="op2-59"></a>

<p id="92">
[1] During the time when Saint Francis was staying in the palace of the bishop of Rieti in order to be treated for his eye affliction, a certain poor little woman from Machilone came to the physician, suffering from an ailment much like the saint's own.
[2] The saint, then, speaking familiarly with his guardian, let fall words such as these: "Brother guardian, we must give back what belongs to another."
[3] He answered him: "Let it be given back, father, if there is anything of the sort in our keeping."
[4] "That cloak," he said, "which we received on loan from that poor little woman—let us restore it to her, for she has nothing in her purse to cover her expenses."
[5] The guardian replied: "Brother, this cloak is mine, and it was lent to me by no one.
[6] Use it as long as it pleases you; and when you no longer wish to use it, hand it back to me."
[7] For in truth the guardian had bought it a little while before for Saint Francis's need.
[8] The saint said to him: "Brother guardian, you have always been courteous to me; now, I beg you, show your courtesy once more."
[9] The guardian answered: "Do as you will, father, whatever the spirit suggests to you!"
[10] So, calling a certain very devout layman, he said to him:
[11] "Take this cloak and twelve loaves of bread, and going to that poor little woman, speak thus:
[12] The poor man to whom you lent the cloak thanks you for the loan; but now take back what is yours!"
[13] The man went and said just as he had heard.
[14] The woman, thinking she was being mocked, said to him with a blush: "Leave me in peace with your cloak! I have no idea what you are talking about."
[15] The man pressed on, and placed everything in her hands.
[16] When she saw that there was no deception in the matter, and fearing lest so easy a gain be taken from her, she rose by night and, giving no thought to the cure of her eyes, returned home with the cloak.
</p>

## Chapter LX — How three women appeared to him along the road, and after a strange greeting vanished from sight <a id="op2-60"></a>

<p id="93">
[1] Let me recount briefly something marvelous, doubtful in its interpretation, yet most certain in its truth. [2] When Francis, the poor man of Christ, was hastening from Rieti to Siena to seek a remedy for his eyes, he was making his way across the plain near the rock of Campiglia, having as his <ref to="Gen 33:12">companion on the journey</ref> a certain physician bound to the Order. [3] And behold, three poor little women appeared beside the road as Saint Francis passed. [4] So alike were they in stature, in age, and in countenance, that you would have believed a triple substance perfected in a single form. [5] As Saint Francis drew near, they bowed their heads reverently and extolled him with an unheard-of greeting, saying, "Welcome, Lady Poverty!" [6] <ref to="Ps 125:2">At once the saint was filled with unspeakable joy</ref>, for there was nothing in himself that he would more gladly have had men salute than the very thing they had chosen. [7] And at first, supposing them to be in truth poor little women, he turned to the physician who accompanied him and said, "I beg you, for God's sake, allow me to give something to those poor women." [8] Most promptly the physician produced the money, and leaping from his horse he gave coins to each of them. [9] So they went a little way along the path they had begun, when suddenly the friars and the physician, turning their eyes back, beheld that whole plain bare of any women. [10] <ref to="Ps 106:8">Greatly astonished at the wonders of the Lord</ref>, they reckoned the event among them, knowing that they had not been women, <ref to="Wis 5:11">since they had flown away swifter than birds</ref>.

## Chapter 61 - On the Time, Place, and Disposition of One at Prayer <a id="op2-61"></a>

<p id="94">
[1] <ref to="2 Cor 5:6">Absent in body from the Lord</ref>, Francis, the <ref to="1 Kgs 2:27">man of God</ref>, strove to carry his <ref to="1 Cor 5:3">present spirit</ref> heavenward, and only the wall of his flesh kept him apart, now already made a citizen among the angels. [2] His whole <ref to="Ps 62:2">soul thirsted for his Christ</ref>; to Him he dedicated not only his heart but his body entire. [3] Of the wonders of his prayer, <ref to="Sir 17:11">so far as we have beheld them with our eyes</ref> and <ref to="1 Cor 2:9">insofar as it is possible to convey them to human ears</ref>, we set down a few things for posterity to imitate. [4] Out of all his time he made a holy leisure, <ref to="Sir 45:31">in which he might inscribe wisdom upon his heart</ref>, lest, if he were not always advancing, he should be seen to be slipping back. [5] If ever the visits of worldly folk or any business at all pressed upon him, he would break them off rather than finish them, and hasten back to his inmost things. [6] Indeed the world was tasteless to one fed on heavenly sweetness, and the divine delights had made him fastidious toward the coarse fare of men. [7] He <ref to="Matt 6:4">always sought out a hidden place</ref>, where he might fit not only his spirit but each of his limbs to his God. [8] When in public he was suddenly moved, <ref to="Luke 1:68">being visited by the Lord</ref>, lest he should be without a cell he would make a little cell of his mantle. [9] At times, when he had no mantle, he would cover his face with his sleeve, <ref to="Rev 2:17">that he might not betray the hidden manna</ref>. [10] He always interposed something between himself and bystanders, lest they should perceive <ref to="Song 5:4">the touch of the Bridegroom</ref>, so that even pressed among many in the narrow space of a ship he prayed unseen. [11] Finally, when he could do none of these things, he made a temple of his breast. [12] Forgetfulness of self took away all groans and exclamations, and absorption in God removed the heavy breathing and the outward gestures.
</p>

<p id="95">
[1] Such was his manner at home. But when he prayed in the woods and solitary places, he filled the groves with groans, bedewed the ground with tears, beat his breast with his hand, and there, as though he had found a more hidden sanctuary, he would often converse in words with his Lord. [2] There he answered the judge, there he entreated the father, there he spoke with the friend, there he made merry with the bridegroom. [3] Indeed, <ref to="Ps 65:15">that he might make of all the marrow of his heart a manifold burnt-offering,</ref> <ref to="Wis 7:22; Ps 100:3">he set before his eyes, in manifold guise, the One who is supremely simple.</ref> [4] <ref to="Song 7:9">Often with motionless lips he would ruminate</ref> within, and drawing what was without into himself, he raised his spirit to the realms above. [5] <ref to="Ps 26:4">Thus he directed his every gaze and affection toward the one thing he asked of the Lord,</ref> become wholly not so much one who prays as prayer itself. [6] With what sweetness, do you suppose, was a man accustomed to such things bathed? <ref to="Job 28:23">He himself knows it,</ref> for I rather marvel at it. [7] To the one who experiences it the knowing will be given; to the inexperienced it is not granted. [8] <ref to="Job 41:22; Wis 7:22">Thus, boiling and keen with fervor of spirit,</ref> <ref to="Song 5:6">his every glance and his whole soul utterly melted,</ref> he now <ref to="2 Tim 4:18">dwelt within the highest commonwealth of the heavenly kingdom.</ref> [9] The blessed father was wont to let no visitation of the Spirit pass with negligence; for when one was offered he followed it, <ref to="1 Cor 16:7">and for as long as the Lord permitted</ref> he enjoyed the sweetness thus bestowed. [10] And so, whenever he was pressed by some business, or intent upon a journey, perceiving little by little certain touches of grace, he would taste at intervals and often that most sweet manna. [11] For even on the road, while his companions went ahead, he would halt his step, and turning the fresh inspiration to its enjoyment, <ref to="2 Cor 6:1">he did not receive grace in vain.</ref>
</p>

## Chapter LXII - On the Devout Recitation of the Canonical Hours <a id="op2-62"></a>

<p id="96">
[1] He recited the canonical hours with no less reverence than devotion. [2] For although he suffered from ailments of the eyes, stomach, spleen, and liver, he refused to lean against a wall or partition while he chanted the psalms; rather, he always discharged the hours standing upright and without his hood, neither letting his eyes wander nor cutting any portion short. [3] When he traveled on foot through the world, he would always halt his step to say his hours; but when he was mounted, he would dismount to the ground. [4] Hence one day, as he was returning from the City and the rain fell without ceasing, he climbed down from his horse to say the office, and standing there a long while, he was thoroughly drenched by the rain. [5] For he would sometimes say: "If the body takes its food in peace—food destined to become, with itself, the worms' fare—with how much greater peace and tranquility ought the soul to receive its food, which is its own God."
</p>

## Chapter LXIII - How by prayer he drove away the phantoms of the heart <a id="op2-63"></a>

<p id="97">
[1] He believed himself to be gravely at fault if ever, when given over to prayer, he was disturbed by empty fancies. [2] Whenever something of that kind befell him, he did not spare confession, but at once expiated it. [3] This zeal he had so turned into a habit that he very rarely suffered such gnats to trouble him. [4] During one Lent he had fashioned a certain little vessel, around which he occupied the spare moments of his time, lest they should slip entirely away. [5] One day, then, as he was devoutly reciting Terce, his eyes happening to fall upon a contemplation of the vessel, <ref to="Rom 7:22">he felt the inner man hindered </ref> from his fervor. [6] Grieving therefore that the voice of his heart toward the divine ears had been interrupted, when Terce was finished he said to the brothers who were listening: "Alas for this trifling work, which had such power over me that it twisted my soul toward itself! [7] <ref to="Ps 53:8">I will sacrifice it to the Lord </ref>, whose sacrifice it impeded." 8 Having said this, he seized the little vessel and <ref to="Ezek 22:20">melted it down in the fire </ref>. [9] "Let us be ashamed," he said, "to be snatched away into trifling wanderings, <ref to="Ps 94:3">when at the time of prayer we address the Great King </ref>."
</p>

## Chapter 64 - On the Rapture of the Mind <a id="op2-64"></a>

<p id="98">
[1] He was so often held suspended in the sweetness of contemplation that, rapt above himself, he would reveal to no one what he had experienced beyond the reach of human sense. [2] Yet by one instance that once became known, it is clear to us how frequently he was absorbed in that heavenly sweetness. [3] At one time he was being carried upon a donkey, since it was necessary for him to pass through Borgo San Sepolcro. [4] And while he wished to rest in a certain dwelling of lepers, <ref to="4 Kgs 5:14">the passing of the man of God became known to many</ref>. [5] From every side men and women came running to see him, longing to touch him with their accustomed devotion. [6] And what then? They handle him and pull at him, and they cut little pieces from his tunic and keep them. [7] The man seems insensible to it all, and like a lifeless body he takes no notice of the things that are being done. [8] At last they arrive at the place, and when Borgo had long since been left behind, as though returning from somewhere else, <ref to="Deut 13:14">this beholder of heavenly things anxiously inquires</ref> when they would be drawing near to Borgo.
</p>

## Chapter LXV - In What State He Showed Himself After Prayer <a id="op2-65"></a>

<p id="99">
[1] When he returned from his private prayers, <ref to="1 Kgs 10:6">by which he was almost changed into another man</ref>, he took the greatest care to make himself like the rest, lest, appearing aflame, he should lose by <ref to="Matt 25:16">the breath of human favor what he had gained</ref>.
[2] Often, too, he spoke to his intimates in this fashion: <ref to="2 Chr 24:9">"When a servant of God</ref><ref to="Luke 1:68"> at prayer is visited by the Lord with some new consolation</ref>, before he leaves his prayer <ref to="Luke 18:13; John 6:5">he ought to lift his eyes to heaven</ref>, and with hands joined say to the Lord:
[3] <ref to="Luke 18:13">'This consolation and sweetness you have sent from heaven to me, a sinner</ref><ref to="1 Pet 1:12"> and one unworthy</ref>, O Lord, and I give it back to you, <ref to="Gen 27:36">that you may keep it in reserve for me</ref>, for I am a thief of your treasure.'
[4] And again: 'Lord, take your good from me in this world, and keep it in reserve for me in the world to come' (cf. Eph 1:21)."
[5] "So," he said, "ought one to do; that when he leaves his prayer, he may show himself to others as poor and sinful, just as though he had attained no new grace."
[6] For he used to say: "It can happen that for a paltry price one loses a thing beyond all price, and easily provokes him who gave it never to give again."
[7] Finally, it was his custom to rise to prayer so stealthily, so softly, that none of his companions perceived him either rising or praying.
[8] In the evening, however, when he came to his bed, he would make a noise, almost a clatter, so that his lying down might be noticed by everyone.
</p>

## Chapter LXVI - How a bishop, finding him at prayer, was deprived of the power of speech <a id="op2-66"></a>

<p id="100">
[1] As Saint Francis was at prayer in the place of the Portiuncula, it happened that the bishop of Assisi came to visit him in friendly fashion, as was his custom. [2] No sooner had he entered the place than, with too little reverence and unbidden, he approached the saint's cell, and having pushed open the little door, he thrust himself in to enter. [3] And behold, just as he put his head inside and beheld the saint at prayer, <ref to="Job 21:6">a trembling suddenly shook him</ref>, and as his limbs grew stiff he even lost the power of speech. [4] Suddenly, by the will of the Lord (cf. Acts 21:14; Ps 50:20), he was driven forth by force, and with backward step was carried far away. [5] I believe that either he himself was unworthy to look upon that mystery, or that the other was worthy to hold longer what he had held. [6] The bishop, dumbfounded, returned to the brothers, and at his very first word, confessing his fault, he recovered his speech.
</p>

## Chapter LXVII - How a certain abbot felt the power of his prayer <a id="op2-67"></a>

<p id="101">
[1] On another occasion it happened that the abbot of the monastery of Saint Justin in the diocese of Perugia met Saint Francis. Swiftly dismounting from his horse, he conferred a little while with him concerning the salvation of his soul. [2] At last, as he was departing, he humbly begged that prayers be offered for him. [3] Saint Francis answered him: "Gladly, my lord, I will pray." [4] And so, when the abbot had gone but a little way from Saint Francis, the saint said to his companion: "Wait a moment, brother, for I wish to pay the debt that I promised." [5] For this was always his custom, <ref to="Isa 38:17">that when asked for a prayer he would not cast it behind his back</ref>, <ref to="Prov 25:14">but would quickly fulfill such a promise</ref>. [6] So while the saint was making his supplication to God, <ref to="Rom 8:5">that abbot suddenly felt in his spirit an unwonted warmth and a sweetness until then never experienced</ref>, <ref to="Acts 11:5">so that, caught up in a rapture of mind</ref>, he seemed to swoon utterly away from himself. [7] He remained thus a little while, <ref to="Luke 15:17">and when he had come back to himself</ref> he recognized the power of Saint Francis's prayer. [8] Thereafter he ever burned with a greater love for the Order, and recounted to many what had happened as a miracle. [9] Such are the little gifts that befit the servants of God to bestow upon one another; <ref to="Phil 4:15">such is the fitting fellowship of giving and receiving among them</ref>. [10] That holy love, which is sometimes called spiritual, is content with the fruit of prayer; charity sets little store by earthly gifts. [11] To help and to be helped in the spiritual warfare, <ref to="2 Cor 5:10">to commend and to be commended before the tribunal of Christ</ref>, I believe to be the proper mark of holy love. [12] But how high do you suppose he himself ascends in prayer, who by his own merits was able thus to lift up another?
</p>

## Chapter LXVIII - Of what learning and of what memory he was possessed <a id="op2-68"></a>

<p id="102">
[1] Although this blessed man had been nourished by no formal studies of learning, yet, <ref to="Col 3:2; Jas 1:17">learning that wisdom which is from above, from God,</ref> and bathed in the splendors of eternal light, he held no lowly understanding of the Scriptures. [2] <ref to="Col 1:26">For his intellect, pure from every stain, penetrated the hidden things of the mysteries,</ref> and where the learning of the masters remains outside, the affection of one who loves found its way in. [3] At times he read in the sacred books, and whatever he had once impressed upon his mind he <ref to="Rom 2:15">wrote indelibly upon his heart.</ref> [4] 'He kept his memory in place of books,' for not in vain did his hearing once grasp what his affection chewed over with unceasing devotion. [5] This he called the fruitful way of learning and reading: not to wander off through a thousand treatises. [6] He held that man to be the true philosopher who set nothing before the desire for eternal life. [7] <ref to="Prov 2:5">He maintained that from knowledge of himself a man would readily come to the knowledge of God</ref> if, attending to Scripture, he sought it humbly and not presumptuously. [8] He frequently untangled the difficulties of questions in mere words, and, <ref to="2 Cor 11:6">though unskilled in speech,</ref> he brought forth meaning and power with brilliant clarity.
</p>

## Chapter LXIX - On the prophetic word which he expounded at the request of a certain Friar Preacher <a id="op2-69"></a>

<p id="103">
[1] While he was staying at Siena, it happened that a certain man of the Order of Preachers came thither, <ref to="Hos 9:7">a spiritual man indeed</ref> and a doctor of sacred theology. [2] When he had visited blessed Francis, <ref to="John 3:34">together they, he and the saint, long enjoyed a most sweet conversation about the words of the Lord</ref>. [3] Now the said master questioned him concerning that saying of Ezekiel: If you do not announce to the wicked man his wickedness, <ref to="Ezek 3:18">I will require his soul at your hand</ref>. [4] For he said: "Many men, good father, I myself know, whom, though I know them to be in mortal sin, I do not always warn of their wickedness. [5] <ref to="Ezek 3:18">Shall the souls of such be required at my hand</ref>?" [6] When blessed Francis called himself an unlettered man, and therefore one who ought rather to be taught by him than to give an answer upon the meaning of Scripture, that humble master added: [7] "Brother, although I have heard the exposition of this saying from certain wise men, I would gladly receive your understanding upon it." [8] Blessed Francis said to him: "If this saying is to be understood in a universal sense, I take it in this way, <ref to="Dan 6:20">that a servant of God</ref> ought so to burn within himself with life and holiness, <ref to="John 5:35">that by the light</ref><ref to="1 Tim 4:12"> of his example and the speech of his manner of life</ref> he may rebuke all the wicked. [9] Thus, I say, <ref to="Ezek 3:19">the splendor of his life and the fragrance of his good name shall announce to all their iniquity</ref>." [10] And so that man, greatly edified, departing, said to the companions of blessed Francis: "My brothers, the theology of this man, sustained by purity and contemplation, <ref to="Job 9:26">is a flying eagle</ref>; <ref to="Gen 1:20; Gen 3:14">but our knowledge crawls upon its belly over the earth</ref>."
</p>

## Chapter LXX — Concerning the things he disclosed when questioned by a certain Cardinal <a id="op2-70"></a>

<p id="104">
[1] On another occasion, while he was in Rome, in the house of a certain cardinal, <ref to="Wis 6:24">when he was questioned on obscure matters he so brought profound things into the light</ref> that you would have thought he had always dwelt within the Scriptures. [2] And the lord cardinal said to him: "I do not question you as a learned man, <ref to="1 Cor 7:40">but as a man having the Spirit of God</ref>, and I therefore gladly receive the meaning of your answer, <ref to="John 15:26; Matt 4:4">because I know that it proceeds from God alone</ref>."

## Chapter LXXI - What he replied concerning his own knowledge to a certain brother who was urging him to the study of reading <a id="op2-71"></a>

<p id="105">
[1] As he lay ill, beset on every side by pains, his companion once said to him: "Father, you have always taken refuge in the Scriptures; they have always offered you remedies for your sufferings.
[2] Do this now, I pray: let something be read to you from the prophets; <ref to="Luke 1:47">perhaps your spirit will rejoice in the Lord</ref>."
[3] The saint replied: "It is good to read the testimonies of Scripture, good to seek out the Lord our God within them;
[4] but I have already drawn so much from the Scriptures into myself that it is more than enough for my meditation and reflection.
[5] I need no more, my son: <ref to="1 Cor 2:2">I know Christ poor and crucified</ref>."

## Chapter 72 - Concerning the swords which Brother Pacificus saw flashing in the mouth of the saint <a id="op2-72"></a>

<p id="106">
[1] There was in the March of Ancona a certain man of the world, forgetful of himself and ignorant of God, who had wholly given himself over to vanity. [2] His name was the King of Verses, for he was the prince of those who sang lascivious songs and the inventor of worldly ballads. [3] To put it briefly, the glory of the world had so exalted the man that he had been crowned by the emperor with the utmost pomp. [4] <ref to="John 8:12"> While he thus walked in darkness </ref><ref to="Isa 5:18"> and dragged iniquity along by the cords of vanity </ref>, divine mercy took pity and resolved to call back the wretch, <ref to="2 Kgs 14:14">lest he perish who had been cast off </ref>. [5] <ref to="Wis 5:17"> By divine providence </ref> blessed Francis and the man himself <ref to="Wis 5:17">met one another </ref> at a certain monastery of poor enclosed women. [6] The blessed father had come there to his daughters with his companions; the man had come to a kinswoman of his with many comrades. [7] But <ref to="Ps 79:18"> when the hand of God was laid upon </ref> him, he saw with his bodily eyes holy Francis marked in the form of a cross with two crossed swords gleaming brightly, one of which stretched from head to foot, the other transversely across the breast from hand to hand. [8] He had not known blessed Francis before, but once so great a miracle was shown him, he at once recognized him. [9] Suddenly amazed at the sight, he began to propose better things for himself, even for the long term. [10] Now when the blessed father first preached to all in common, he turned <ref to="Heb 4:12">the sword of the word of God </ref> upon the man. [11] For drawing him aside, he sweetly admonished him concerning the vanity of the age and contempt of the world, and then pierced through his heart by threatening the divine judgments. [12] He at once replied: "What need is there to sow more words? Let us come to deeds. Take me away from men and restore me to the Great Emperor!" [13] The next day the saint clothed him, and, <ref to="Isa 26:12; Ps 84:9">having led him back to the peace of the Lord </ref>, named him Brother Pacificus. [14] His conversion was the more edifying to many, in proportion as the crowd of his vain comrades had been the wider. [15] Rejoicing in the company of the blessed father, Brother Pacificus began to feel anointings he had never felt before. [16] For once again he was permitted to see what was veiled from others. [17] A little later <ref to="Ezek 9:4; Rev 7:3"> he beheld the great sign of the Thau upon the forehead </ref> of blessed Francis, which in its rings of varied colors displayed the beauty of a peacock.
</p>

<p id="107">
[1] Although Francis the evangelist preached to the unlearned by means of homely and unpolished matters, knowing as he did that virtue is more needful than words, yet among those who were spiritual and more capable of receiving it he brought forth life-giving and profound discourses. ## Chapter LXXIV — How, through Brother Silvester, he drove out the demons from Arezzo by the power of his word <a id="op2-74"></a>

<p id="108">
[1] Not only when present were the words of Francis charged with power, <ref to="Isa 55:11">but when sent at times through others they did not return empty</ref>. [2] It happened that he once came to the city of Arezzo when, behold, <ref to="Matt 8:34">the whole city</ref>, shaken by civil strife, was threatening its own imminent ruin. [3] And so the man of God, having found lodging in a hamlet outside the city, beheld demons exulting above that land and inflaming the citizens to the destruction of fellow citizens. [4] Calling, then, a brother named Silvester, <ref to="1 Kgs 9:7">a man of God</ref> of worthy simplicity, <ref to="Gen 28:1">he commanded him, saying</ref>: "Go before the gate of the city, <ref to="Wis 7:25">and in the name of almighty God</ref> command the demons <ref to="Matt 10:11">to depart from the city at once</ref>!" [5] His holy simplicity made haste to carry out the obedience, <ref to="Ps 94:2">and coming before the face of the Lord with praise</ref>, <ref to="Dan 3:4">he cried out mightily before the gate</ref>: "In the name of God and by the command of our father Francis, depart far from here (cf. 1 Esd 6:6), all you demons!" [6] A little while after, the city returned to peace, and its citizens preserved the laws of civic order in great tranquility. [7] Whence afterward, when blessed Francis was preaching to them, he said at the beginning of his sermon: [8] "I speak to you as to those once subjected and bound to the devil and to demons, yet whom I know to have been freed by the prayers of a certain poor man."

## Chapter LXXV - On the conversion of the same Brother Sylvester, and on a certain vision of his <a id="op2-75"></a>

<p id="109">
[1] I think it not unworthy to join to these present matters the conversion of the aforesaid Silvester, and how the Spirit moved him to enter the Order. [2] Now Silvester had been a secular priest of the city of Assisi, <ref to="4 Kgs 1:10">from whom the man of God had once bought stones for the repair of a church</ref>. [3] When this man saw, at that time, that brother Bernard—<ref to="Luke 2:34">who after the holy one of God</ref> was the first sprout of the Order of Minors—<ref to="Matt 19:21">perfectly forsake his goods and give them to the poor</ref>, inflamed with devouring greed he brought a complaint against the man of God concerning the stones once sold to him, as though the full price had not been paid him. [4] Francis smiled, perceiving that the priest's mind was tainted with the poison of avarice. [5] Yet, wishing somehow to provide some relief for that accursed burning, he filled the priest's hands with money unnumbered. [6] The priest Silvester rejoiced in what was given, but he marveled still more at the liberality of the giver; and returning home, he turned the deed over again and again in his mind, murmuring with a blessed murmur <ref to="1 John 2:15">that he, now growing old, still loved the world</ref>, and he was astonished that this young man so despised all things. [7] Thereupon, now full <ref to="2 Cor 2:15">of a sweet odor</ref>, Christ opened to him the bosom of his mercy. [8] <ref to="Acts 18:9">He showed him in a vision</ref> how great a power the works of Francis possessed, with what eminence they shone in his sight, and how magnificently they filled the whole frame of the world. [9] For he saw in his sleep a golden cross issuing from the mouth of Francis, <ref to="Gen 28:12">'whose top touched the heavens,'</ref> whose arms, stretched out wide, encircled and embraced both parts of the world. [10] <ref to="Sir 40:7">The priest, stung with compunction at the sight</ref>, shook off all ruinous delay, <ref to="John 16:28">forsook the world</ref>, and became a perfect imitator <ref to="4 Kgs 5:14">of the man of God</ref>. [11] He began to live perfectly in the Order, and most perfectly, by the grace of Christ, he made his end. [12] But what wonder, if Francis appeared crucified to one for whom there was always so much fellowship with the cross? [13] With the marvelous cross thus rooted within him, what marvel is it, <ref to="Matt 13:8">if, springing from good ground</ref>, he brought forth flowers, foliage, and conspicuous fruits? [14] Nothing of any other kind could be born in that soil which from the beginning that wondrous cross had so wholly claimed for itself. [15] But now we must return to our subject.
</p>

<p id="111">

At once the wicked sow began to sicken, and, paying out the torments of her punishment for three days, she at last suffered an avenging death. ## Chapter LXXVIII - On the avoidance of familiarity with women, and how he spoke with them <a id="op2-78"></a>

<p id="112">
[1] He commanded that the honeyed poisons—namely, intimacies with women—<ref to="Matt 24:24">which lead even </ref> holy men into error, be utterly shunned. [2] For he feared that by this the delicate soul might quickly be broken, <ref to="Ezek 21:7">and the strong spirit often weakened </ref>. [3] He said that for one who keeps company with such women to escape their contagion—<ref to="Jas 1:12">unless he be a man most thoroughly proven </ref>—was as easy as, according to Scripture, <ref to="Prov 6:28">to walk in fire and not burn the soles of one's feet </ref>. [4] And that he might speak by his own hand, <ref to="Titus 2:7">he offered himself as the very pattern of all </ref> virtue. [5] Indeed, womankind was so troublesome to him that you would believe it to be not mere caution or example, but dread and horror. [6] When their importunate talkativeness occasioned a clash in conversation, with <ref to="Rom 9:28">a word cut short </ref> and humble, with <ref to="Dan 10:15">downcast countenance </ref>, he summoned silence. [7] <ref to="Acts 7:55">Sometimes indeed, with eyes fixed upon heaven </ref>, he seemed <ref to="Isa 29:4">to draw from there what he answered to those murmuring from the earth </ref>. [8] But those women, in whose minds the urgency of holy devotion had set up a dwelling place for wisdom, he instructed with wondrous yet brief discourses. [9] When he spoke with a woman, he uttered what he had to say in a clear voice, so that he might be heard by all. [10] Once he said to a companion: "I confess the truth, dearest one: if I were to look, I should recognize none by the face save two. [11] Hers," he said, "and hers—their countenance is known to me; of any other I know nothing." [12] How excellent, father, since the sight of them sanctifies no one; how excellent, I say, since there is in it no gain at all, but much loss—even loss of time. [13] These things are a hindrance to those who wish to press on along the steep path, <ref to="Esth 15:17">and to behold the face full of graces </ref>.
</p>

## Chapter LXXIX - A Warning Against the Sight of Women <a id="op2-79"></a>

<p id="113">
[1] <ref to="1 Cor 13:12; Ps 118:120">He was accustomed to transfix unchaste eyes with the following riddle</ref>: [2] "A mighty king sent two messengers in turn to a queen. [3] The first returns, and he brings back only word for word. [4] For <ref to="Eccl 2:14">the wise man's eyes were in his head</ref>, and they had not darted out anywhere. [5] The second returns, and after the brief words he relates, he weaves a long account of the lady's beauty: 'Truly, my lord, I have seen a most beautiful woman. Happy is the man who shall enjoy her!' [6] But the king replied: <ref to="Matt 18:32">'Wicked servant</ref>, have you cast shameless eyes upon my bride? It is plain that you wished to buy something you had inspected with such subtlety.' [7] He orders the first to be recalled and says: 'What is your impression of the queen?' [8] And he answered: 'A most excellent one indeed, for she listened in silence and responded with wisdom.' [9] 'And is there nothing,' said the king, 'of beauty in her?' [10] 'My lord,' he replied, 'to look upon that was your concern; mine was to deliver the words.' [11] The king pronounces sentence: 'You,' he says, 'who are chaste of eye, shall be in my chamber, chaster still in body! But let this other go forth from the house, lest he defile the marriage bed!' [12] Now the blessed father used to say: "Out of too great a sense of security, the enemy is the less guarded against. [13] If the devil can get but a single hair of his own upon a man, he quickly makes it grow into a beam. [14] Nor, if for many years he has failed to cast down the one he tempts, does he complain of the delay, provided that the man yields to him in the end. [15] For this is his work, and day and night he is anxious about nothing else."
</p>

## Chapter LXXX — The Saint's Example Against Excessive Familiarity <a id="op2-80"></a>

<p id="114">
[1] It once happened, as holy Francis was making his way to Mevania, that he was unable to reach the town for the weakness brought on by fasting. [2] His companion, therefore, sending a messenger to a certain spiritual lady, humbly begged bread and wine for the saint. [3] As soon as she heard, she ran to the saint together with her virgin daughter, who was devoted to God, carrying what was needful. [4] And the saint, refreshed and somewhat strengthened, refreshed in turn <ref to="Luke 4:4">with the word of God </ref><ref to="Esth 9:1"> both mother and daughter</ref>. [5] And when he had preached to them, <ref to="Ps 83:10">he looked upon no one's face</ref>. [6] As they were departing, his companion said to him: "Why, brother, did you not look upon the holy virgin, who came to you with such great devotion?" [7] To whom the father replied: "Who ought not to fear to gaze upon the bride of Christ? [8] For if there is any preaching by the eyes and the face, let it be she who has seen me, and not I who have seen her." [9] Speaking often of such matters, he maintained that all conversation with a woman is frivolous, save only confession, or, as is customary, the briefest admonition. [10] For he would say: "What dealings has a Friar Minor to transact with a woman, unless when in religious entreaty she begs for holy penance or counsel toward a better life?"

## Chapter LXXXI — On the temptations of the saint, and how he overcame temptation <a id="op2-81"></a>

<p id="115">
[1] As the merits of Saint Francis increased, <ref to="Rev 12:9">so too did his strife with the ancient serpent grow </ref>. [2] <ref to="1 Cor 12:31">For the greater this man's gifts of grace </ref>, the more subtle were the other's temptations, and the heavier the battles he stirred up. [3] And although the serpent had often proved him <ref to="Isa 3:2">a man of war </ref><ref to="Gal 2:5">and valiant, and one who had never for an hour yielded </ref> in the contest, still he strives ever to assail one who is always victorious. [4] For at a certain time a most grievous temptation of the spirit was sent upon the holy father, surely for the increase of his crown. [5] He was straitened thereby and filled with sorrows; he <ref to="Heb 11:37; Job 7:4">afflicted </ref> and wasted his body, he prayed and wept most bitterly. [6] Assailed in this manner for several years, while he was praying one day at Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, he heard a voice in his spirit: "Francis, if you have faith like a grain of mustard seed, you shall say to the mountain that it remove, <ref to="Matt 17:19">and it shall remove" </ref>. [7] The saint answered: "Lord, what is the mountain that I would wish to remove?" And again he heard: "The mountain is your temptation." [8] And he, weeping, said: "Be it done to me, <ref to="Luke 1:38">Lord </ref>, as you have said!" [9] At once, with every temptation driven off, he was made free and wholly stilled in his innermost being.

<p id="116">
[1] In the hermitage of the brothers at Sarteano, that malign one, <ref to="Rom 5:2">who is forever envious of the progress of the sons of God</ref>, dared to attempt something of this kind against the saint. [3] And he answered, saying: <ref to="Mark 9:37; Matt 20:21">"What do you want?"</ref> [4] And the other replied: "There is no sinner in the world to whom, <ref to="Ezek 33:9">if he be converted</ref>, the Lord does not show mercy; but whoever kills himself by harsh penance <ref to="Dan 3:39">shall find no mercy forever."</ref> [5] <ref to="Gal 1:12; Sir 1:6">At once the saint recognized through revelation the enemy's cunning</ref>, how he had striven to recall him to lukewarmness. [6] What then? The enemy did not cease to bring on another struggle. [7] For seeing that he could not conceal his snare in this way, he prepared <ref to="Ps 139:6">another snare</ref>, namely the goad of the flesh. [8] Yet in vain, since he who had detected the spirit's craft could not be deceived through the flesh. [9] So the devil cast upon him a most grievous temptation of lust. [10] But the blessed father, as soon as he perceived it, laying aside his garment, scourged himself most harshly with his cord, saying: "Come now, brother ass, thus it befits you to remain, thus to submit to the lash. <p id="117">
[1] But seeing that the temptation would not depart for all his scourging, though he had already painted every limb with bruises, he opened his cell and <ref to="Mark 14:68">went out </ref> into the garden, and plunged himself, naked, into the deep snow. [2] Taking up the snow with both hands full, <ref to="Job 38:38">he packed seven mounds of it into the shape of balls </ref>. [6] At once the devil withdrew in confusion, <ref to="Luke 2:20">and the saint returned into his cell glorifying God </ref>. [7] A certain spiritual brother, <ref to="1 Cor 7:5">who at that time was given to prayer </ref>, <ref to="Job 31:36">as the moon shone bright </ref>, beheld the whole thing. [8] But the saint, having afterward learned that the man had seen him in the night, grieved much, and charged him to disclose the matter to no one <ref to="Titus 2:12">so long as he lived in this world </ref>.

<p id="118">
[1] On one occasion, when a certain brother who was being tempted sat alone with the saint, he said to him: <ref to="3 Kgs 13:6; 1 Thess 5:25">"Pray for me</ref>, kind father, for I truly believe <ref to="Luke 22:28">that I shall at once be freed of my temptations</ref> if you would deign to pray for me.
[2] Indeed I am afflicted beyond my strength, and I know that this is not hidden from you."
[3] Saint Francis said to him: <ref to="John 4:21">"Believe me</ref>, my son, <ref to="Acts 16:17">that for this very reason I believe you the more to be a servant of God</ref>, and the more you are tempted, the more you may know yourself to be loved by me."

[5] A temptation overcome," he said, "is, as it were, a ring <ref to="Ps 85:4">by which the Lord betroths to himself the soul of his servant</ref>.
[6] Many flatter themselves on the merits of their years, yet rejoice that they have endured no temptations.
[7] But since the mere terror before the contest would crush them, let them know that the Lord has reckoned the weakness of their spirit.
[8] For hard struggles are scarcely set before anyone except where virtue has been made perfect." How the demons beat him.

## Chapter 84 - How the demons beat him, and that the courts of the great are to be shunned <a id="op2-84"></a>

<p id="119">
[1] <ref to="Luke 22:31"> This man was not only assailed by Satan with temptations </ref>, but contended with him hand to hand. [2] Once, when he had been asked by Lord Leo, cardinal of the Holy Cross, to remain with him a little while in the City, he chose a certain remote tower, which, vaulted with nine arches, presented as it were the appearance of tiny hermits' cells. [3] <ref to="2 Chr 6:19"> And so on the first night, when after pouring out his prayer to God </ref> he wished to take his rest, <ref to="Luke 4:34">demons came and stirred up hostile struggles against the holy one of God </ref>. [4] Beating him long and most harshly, <ref to="Luke 10:30">at last they left him half dead </ref>. [5] When they had departed, his breath at length recovered, the holy man called to his companion who was sleeping beneath another arch, and as he came he said to him: "Brother, I wish that you would stay beside me, for I dread to be alone. [6] For demons beat me a little while ago." [7] And the holy man trembled and shook in his limbs, like one suffering a most violent fever.
</p>

<p id="120">
[1] As they passed the whole night sleepless, Saint Francis said to his companion: "The demons are the stewards of our Lord, whom He sends to punish transgressions. [2] It is a sign of greater grace that He leaves nothing unpunished in His servant while he yet lives in the world. [3] For my part, I recall no offense <ref to="Rom 12:1">which, through the mercy of God,</ref> I have not washed away by atonement; [4] indeed, in His fatherly graciousness He has always dealt with me in such a way as to reveal to me, when I prayed and meditated, what was pleasing and what displeasing to Him. [5] But it may be that He has permitted His stewards to fall upon me for this reason: that my dwelling in the court of the great makes no good impression upon others. [6] My brothers, who linger in their poor little dwellings, hearing that I am in the company of cardinals, <ref to="Luke 7:25">might perhaps suspect that I abound in luxuries.</ref> [7] Therefore, brother, <ref to="Job 17:6">I judge it better that he who is set forth as an example</ref> should flee the courts, <ref to="Prov 28:27">and that he should strengthen those who endure want</ref> by enduring the like himself." [8] <ref to="Mark 16:2">So they come in the morning,</ref> and when all had been recounted, they bid the cardinal farewell. [9] Let those of the palaces take note of these things, and let them know that they are untimely births, <ref to="Luke 1:15">drawn forth from their mother's womb.</ref> [10] I do not condemn obedience, but I rebuke ambition, idleness, and luxury; [11] and finally, I set Francis altogether above all obediences. [12] Yet let whatever displeases God be endured, <ref to="Eccl 5:3; Ps 52:3">since it pleases men.</ref>

## Chapter LXXXVII - Concerning a Certain Brother Delivered from Temptation <a id="op2-87"></a>

<p id="124">
[1] A certain friar, a man of the spirit and of long standing in the Order, was afflicted by a <ref to="1 Cor 7:28">grievous tribulation of the flesh</ref>, and seemed <ref to="Ps 68:16">all but swallowed up in an abyss of despair</ref>. [6] <ref to="Gen 15:1; Matt 1:20">And do not be afraid</ref>, for what befalls you, and which you yourself do not bring about, shall turn to your crown and not to your guilt! [8] Amazed at how the saint could have known this, and <ref to="Prov 15:3">filled with the greatest gladness</ref>, after a short while he was wholly delivered from his tribulation.

## Chapter LXXXVIII — On spiritual joy and the praise thereof, and on the evil of spiritual sloth <a id="op2-88"></a>

<p id="125">
[1] <ref to="Eph 6:11; 2 Cor 11:3">As the surest remedy against the thousand snares and stratagems of the enemy</ref>, this holy man held fast to spiritual joy. [2] For he used to say: "The devil exults most of all when he can steal away <ref to="Gal 5:22">the joy of the spirit</ref> from a servant of God. [3] He carries dust which, through however small the openings of conscience, he might cast in to befoul the brightness of the mind and the cleanness of life. [4] But when spiritual joy," he said, "fills the heart, <ref to="Prov 23:32">in vain does the serpent pour out his deadly venom</ref>. [5] The demons cannot harm the servant of Christ <ref to="Acts 2:28">when they see him filled with holy gladness</ref>. [6] But when the soul is tearful, desolate, and grieving, <ref to="2 Cor 2:7">it is either easily swallowed up by sorrow</ref> or led astray to empty delights." [7] He strove therefore always to abide in jubilation of heart, <ref to="Ps 44:8">to preserve the anointing of the spirit and the oil of gladness</ref>. [8] With the utmost care he shunned that most baneful sickness of sloth, so that whenever he felt even a little of it creeping into his mind, he would run with all haste to prayer. [9] He used to say: <ref to="Dan 6:20">"When the servant of God</ref> is troubled by something, as commonly happens, he ought to rise at once to prayer and to persist before the most high Father for as long as it takes, <ref to="Ps 50:14">until He restore to him the joy of His salvation</ref>. [10] For if he lingers in sadness (cfr. Hab 2,3), that Babylonian sickness will grow, and at last, unless it be roused away through tears, will breed an abiding rust in the heart."
</p>

## Chapter LXXXIX - Concerning the Angelic Harp Which He Heard <a id="op2-89"></a>

<p id="126">
[1] During the days when he was staying at Rieti for the treatment of his eyes, he summoned one of his companions who in the world had been a player of the lyre, and said: "Brother, <ref to="Luke 16:8">the children of this world </ref><ref to="Wis 2:22"> do not understand the divine mysteries </ref>.
[2] For musical instruments, once appointed for the praises of God, human lust has turned into a delight for the ears.
[3] I would wish, then, brother, that you might secretly borrow a lyre and bring it, so that, composing some seemly verse upon it, you might bring solace to your brother body, which is full of pains."
[4] The brother answered him: "I am not a little ashamed, father, fearing lest by such lightness men should suspect that I have been tempted."
[5] To him the saint replied: "Then let us drop the matter, brother! It is good to forgo many things, lest our good name be wounded."
[6] On the following night, while the holy man kept vigil and meditated upon God, <ref to="Sir 40:21">suddenly there sounded forth a certain lyre of wondrous harmony and most sweet melody </ref>.
[7] No one was to be seen, <ref to="Ezek 10:13">but the very nimbleness of the sound, heard now here and now there, signified the coming and going of the lyre-player </ref>.
[8] <ref to="Job 34:14"> At length, his spirit being directed toward God </ref>, the holy father took such delight in that sweet-sounding song that he thought himself to have passed over into another world.
[9] <ref to="Gen 28:18"> Rising in the morning </ref>, the saint called the aforesaid brother, <ref to="Esth 15:9">and, recounting to him everything in order </ref>, added: "The Lord, who comforts the afflicted, <ref to="2 Cor 1:4">has never left me without consolation </ref>.
[10] For behold, I who could not hear the lyres of men have heard a sweeter lyre."
</p>

## Chapter 90 - How the saint, made joyful in spirit, would sing in French <a id="op2-90"></a>

<p id="127">
[1] At times, indeed, he would do things such as these. [2] When the sweetest melody of the spirit bubbled up within him, it would pour forth outwardly in a French strain, and the vein of divine whispering, <ref to="Job 4:12">which his ear had secretly received</ref>, would break out into a French song of jubilation. [3] Sometimes, as I saw with my own eyes, he would gather up a stick from the ground, and laying it across his left arm, he would hold in his right hand a little bow bent with a string, drawing it across the stick as though over a viol, and miming the appropriate gestures, <ref to="Ps 12:6">he would sing in French of the Lord</ref>. [4] All these raptures of his often ended in tears, and this jubilation would dissolve into compassion for the Passion of Christ. [5] Thereupon this holy man would draw unbroken sighs, and with redoubled groans, forgetting the lower things he held in his hands, he would be lifted up toward heaven.
</p>

## Chapter XCI - How he rebuked a certain brother who was sad, and how he admonished him to conduct himself <a id="op2-91"></a>

<p id="128">
[1] Once he saw one of his companions wearing a sullen and sorrowful face, and, not taking it lightly, he said to him: <ref to="Dan 6:20">"It does not befit a servant of God </ref><ref to="Isa 42:4"> to show himself </ref> sad and troubled before men, but always serene.
[2] <ref to="Eccl 10:20"> In your own chamber </ref> examine your faults, <ref to="Gen 6:8">and before your God </ref> weep and groan.
[3] When you return to the brothers, lay aside your grief and conform yourself to the rest."
[4] And shortly afterward: "The enemies of human salvation greatly envy me, and they always strive," he said, "to stir up in my companions what they cannot stir up in me."
[5] Indeed, he so loved a man filled with spiritual joy that, by way of a general admonition, he had these words written down in a certain chapter:
[6] "Let the brothers take care not to show themselves outwardly gloomy and sad like hypocrites, <ref to="Isa 61:10">but let them show themselves rejoicing in the Lord </ref>, cheerful and glad, and becomingly gracious."
</p>

## Chapter XCII - How the body is to be handled, so that it may not murmur <a id="op2-92"></a>

<p id="129">
[1] The saint also once said: "Provision must be made for brother body with discretion, lest a storm of sloth be stirred up by it.
[2] <ref to="Tob 3:11">For that it may not weary of keeping vigil and persisting reverently in prayer</ref>, let the occasion for grumbling be taken from it.
[3] For it might say: <ref to="Lam 2:19">'I am fainting with hunger</ref>, I am unable to bear the burden of your discipline.'

[7] For the ardor of his spirit had by then so refined his body that, while his soul thirsted for God, <ref to="Ps 62:2">that most holy flesh thirsted too, and in how many ways</ref>.

## Chapter XCIII - Against Vainglory and Hypocrisy <a id="op2-93"></a>

<p id="130">
[1] Yet while he embraced spiritual gladness, he diligently shunned the foolish kind, knowing that what perfects must be ardently loved, and that what corrupts must be fled with no less vigilance. [2] <ref to="Gal 5:26"> Empty glory </ref> he strove to crush in the very seed, <ref to="1 Kgs 29:7">not allowing anything that might offend his Lord's eyes </ref> to abide for even a moment. [3] For many times, when he was lifted up by lavish praises, grieving and groaning he straightway exchanged such feeling for sorrow. [4] In the wintertime, when his holy little body was covered by but a single tunic—and that patched with cloth scraps exceedingly mean—his guardian, who was also his companion, having obtained a piece of fox fur and brought it to him, said: [5] "Father, you suffer an infirmity of the spleen and the stomach; I beg your charity in the Lord that you suffer this fur to be sewn beneath your tunic. [6] But if the whole of it does not please you, at least permit it to be set over the stomach." [7] To him blessed Francis replied: "If you wish me to endure this beneath my tunic, have a piece of the same size applied to the outside, so that, sewn on the exterior, it may show men the fur hidden within." [8] The brother listens, but does not approve; he presses, yet obtains nothing else. [9] At last, the guardian yielding, piece is sewn upon piece, lest Francis should be shown to be other on the outside than within. [10] O the same in tongue and in life! the same without and within! the same as subject and as prelate! [11] Nothing of borrowed glory, nothing of private glory did you love, <ref to="1 Cor 1:31">you who always gloried in the Lord </ref>. [12] But lest I should give offense to those who wear furs, I beg pardon <ref to="Job 2:4">if I have spoken of skin set in place for skin </ref>; <ref to="Gen 3:21">for we know that, stripped of innocence, men had need of tunics of skin </ref>.

## Chapter 94 — Concerning a Certain Confession of His Against Hypocrisy <a id="op2-94"></a>

<p id="131">
[1] On one occasion, at the hermitage of Poggio, around the feast of the Lord's Nativity, when a great crowd had been summoned to a sermon, he began with this prologue: [2] "You believe me to be a holy man, and for that reason you have come devoutly. But I confess to you," he said, "that throughout this entire Lent I have eaten food seasoned with lard." [3] And so he frequently charged to self-indulgence what he had earlier granted to infirmity.
</p>

## Chapter XCV — On Confession as a Remedy Against Vainglory <a id="op2-95"></a>

<p id="132">
[1] With like fervor, whenever his spirit was stirred toward vainglory, he would at once lay it bare before all in open confession. [2] For on one occasion, as he was walking through the city of Assisi, <ref to="Matt 20:20">a certain old woman came up to him, asking something of him</ref>. [3] Yet <ref to="2 Cor 6:10">having nothing</ref> but his mantle, he bestowed it upon her with ready generosity. [4] But sensing the stirrings of empty self-satisfaction creeping in, he straightway confessed before all that he had felt vainglory.
</p>

## Chapter XCVI — His Words Against Those Who Praised Him <a id="op2-96"></a>

<p id="133">
[1] He strove <ref to="Ps 26:13">to keep the good things of his Lord</ref> hidden in the secret recesses of his heart, unwilling to set forth before the eyes of glory what might become the occasion of his downfall. [2] For often, when many called him blessed, he would answer with <ref to="Gen 39:10">words of this kind:</ref> "I may yet still come to have sons and daughters; do not praise me as one already safe! No man is to be praised whose end is still uncertain. [3] Whenever he who has granted a loan should wish to take back what he granted," he said, "only the body and the soul will remain, which even an unbeliever likewise possesses." [4] Such were his words to those who praised him. [5] But to himself he spoke thus: "Had the Most High bestowed so much upon a thief, he would be more grateful to you than you are, Francis!"
</p>

## Chapter XCVII - Words Against Those Who Praised Him <a id="op2-97"></a>

<p id="134">
[1] He often said to the brothers: "Of everything that a sinner is capable of, no one ought to flatter himself with unwarranted applause.
[2] A sinner," he said, "can fast, can pray, can weep, can mortify his own flesh.
[3] But this one thing he cannot do: remain faithful to his Lord.
[4] Therefore in this alone is there cause for boasting, <ref to="Sir 35:10; John 9:24">if we render to God his own glory</ref>, if, serving faithfully, we ascribe to him whatever he bestows.
[5] Man's greater enemy is the flesh; it knows not how to reflect on anything so as to grieve, nor to foresee anything so as to fear.
[6] Its whole concern is to abuse the things at hand.
[7] And what is worse," he said, "the flesh usurps for itself, the flesh turns to its own glory, that which was given not to it but to the soul.
[8] From the virtues it snatches praise, from vigils and prayers it plucks outward favor.
[9] Leaving nothing to the soul, it seeks to claim even a coin from its tears."
</p>

<p id="135">
[1] Yet it is not lawful to pass over in silence with what a great veil he covered, and with what zealous care he concealed, those marks of the Crucified, which are venerable even to the highest of spirits. [2] <ref to="Isa 9:1">At the first season</ref>, <ref to="2 Cor 3:18">when the true love of Christ had transformed the lover into that very image</ref>, he began to hide and to keep secret this treasure with such caution <ref to="Wis 4:13">that for a long while</ref> not even his own intimates were aware of it. <p id="136">

## Chapter XCIX - How a certain man beheld those things through a pious deception <a id="op2-99"></a>

<p id="137">
[1] <ref to="Judg 13:6">While the man of God was staying at Siena</ref>, it happened that a certain friar from Brescia came there, who, longing greatly to behold the stigmata of the holy father, begged for this earnestly through the insistent help of Friar Pacificus. [2] And that one said: "When I am about to depart from this place, I shall ask him to let me kiss his hands; and when he gives them, <ref to="Prov 10:10">I will signal to you with my eyes</ref>, and you shall see." [3] Ready to depart, the two of them went to the saint, and <ref to="Eph 3:14">bending their knees</ref>, Pacificus said to Saint Francis: "Bless us, dearest mother, and give me your hand to kiss!" [4] He kissed the hand held out to him, though not willingly, and gave the sign for the friar to look. [5] Asking for the other hand also, he kissed it, and showed it to the same man. [6] As they were departing, the father suspected that there had been a holy deception there—which indeed there had been. [7] Judging the pious curiosity to be impious, he straightway called Friar Pacificus back and said to him: "May the Lord forgive you, brother, <ref to="2 Cor 2:4">for you cause me much tribulation</ref> at times." [8] <ref to="Jdt 9:1">Pacificus at once prostrated himself</ref> <ref to="Matt 22:2">and humbly asked, saying</ref>: "What tribulation have I caused you, dearest mother?" ## Chapter 100 - Concerning the wound in his side, beheld by a certain man <a id="op2-100"></a>

<p id="138">
[1] Although the wounds in his hands and feet made those exposed places of his limbs evident to some, the wound in his side no one was worthy to behold during his lifetime, save one alone, and that only once. [2] For whenever he had his tunic shaken out, he would cover the wound in his side with his right arm. [3] At other times he would lay his left hand over the pierced side, concealing that blessed gash. [4] Now a certain companion of his, while massaging him, let his hand slip onto the wound and caused him great pain. [5] Another of the brothers, striving by curious inquiry to see what was hidden from the others, said one day to the holy father: "Would it please you, father, that we shake out your tunic?" [6] To him the saint replied: <ref to="Ps 17:21">"May the Lord reward you</ref>, brother, since indeed I have need of it." [7] And so, as he undressed himself, the brother, looking <ref to="Lam 4:17">with attentive eyes</ref>, plainly saw the wound in his side. [8] This man alone saw it during his lifetime; of all the rest, none did until after his death.
</p>

## Chapter 101 - On the Concealment of Virtues <a id="op2-101"></a>

<p id="139">
[1] In this way that man had renounced all glory <ref to="Phil 3:19">that did not savor of Christ</ref>; in this way he had laid an eternal curse upon human favors. [2] He knew that the price of fame diminishes the secret treasure of conscience, and that it is far more ruinous to misuse virtues than to lack them. [3] He knew that to guard what has been won is no lesser virtue than to seek it. [4] Alas! vanity stirs us to more than charity does, and the world's favor prevails over the love of Christ. [5] We do not discern our affections, <ref to="1 John 4:1">we do not test the spirits</ref>, and when vain glory has driven us to some deed, we suppose it was drawn forth by charity. [6] Moreover, if we have done even a little good, <ref to="Job 31:23">we cannot bear its weight</ref>: whatever it may be, we unburden ourselves of it while we yet live, and lose it on the farthest shore. [7] We endure patiently not to be good; but not to seem so, not to be believed so—that we cannot suffer. [8] <ref to="Rom 2:29">Thus we live wholly upon the praises of men</ref>, <ref to="Matt 8:9">because we are nothing other than men</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter 102 — The humility of Saint Francis in his dress, his understanding, and his manner of life, and against trusting in one's own judgment <a id="op2-102"></a>

<p id="140">
[1] Humility is the guardian and the grace of all the virtues. [2] When it is withdrawn from the spiritual edifice, the structure, even as it seems to rise, advances only toward its own ruin. [3] Lest anything be wanting to a man adorned with so many gifts, this virtue <ref to="Ps 64:12">had filled him with a more abundant plenty</ref>. [4] In his own estimation, indeed, he was nothing but a sinner, though he was the very grace and splendor of holiness in every form. [5] Upon this virtue he labored to build himself up, <ref to="Heb 6:1">that he might lay the foundation</ref> he had learned from Christ. [6] Forgetful of <ref to="Matt 25:16">the things he had gained</ref>, he <ref to="Ps 100:3">set before his eyes</ref> only his failings, reckoning that more was lacking to him than was present. [7] One desire alone burned strong within him: to become better, so that, not content with his first attainments, he might add new virtues to them. [8] Humble in his bearing, humbler in his judgment, most humble in his own esteem. [9] <ref to="Gen 23:6">This prince of God could not be told</ref> to be a prelate save by this most brilliant gem: that among the lesser brothers he was present as the least. [10] This virtue, this title, this insignia marked him out to be the minister general. [11] All loftiness was absent from his speech, all pomp from his gestures, all arrogance from his deeds. [12] <ref to="Sir 16:24; Wis 9:18">In many matters he had learned his understanding by revelation</ref>, yet, bringing these into common counsel, he set the judgments of others before his own. [13] He believed the counsel of his companions to be the safer course, and another's view seemed to him better than his own. [14] He said that the man who held on to the strongbox of his own opinion had <ref to="Matt 19:27">not left all things for the Lord</ref>. [15] He <ref to="Ps 30:14">preferred to hear blame</ref> of himself rather than praise, since the one compelled him to amend himself, while the other drove him toward a fall.
</p>

## Chapter 103 - On His Humility toward the Bishop of Terni and toward a Certain Peasant <a id="op2-103"></a>

<p id="141">
[1] Once, when he was preaching to the people of Terni, the bishop of the city, commending him before all when the sermon was finished, spoke thus: <ref to="1 John 2:18">"In this last hour </ref><ref to="Isa 53:3; Isa 66:2">God has illumined His Church through this poor little despised man </ref>, simple and unlettered; [2] <ref to="Ps 146:1">for which reason we are bound ever to praise the Lord </ref>, <ref to="Ps 147:20">knowing that He has not done so for every nation </ref>." [3] <ref to="Gen 50:17">When the saint had heard these words </ref>, he received with wondrous delight that the bishop had pronounced him contemptible in terms so plain. [4] And as they entered the church, <ref to="Mark 5:22">he fell at the feet </ref> of the bishop, saying: <ref to="Ps 110:8">"In truth </ref>, my lord bishop, you have done me great honor, for while others strip away what is mine, you alone have kept it unharmed. [5] You have separated, I say, <ref to="Jer 15:19">the precious from the vile </ref>, like a man of discernment, giving praise to God and <ref to="Luke 18:43">rendering to me the baseness that is my due </ref>."
</p>

<p id="142">
[1] <ref to="Judg 13:6">Now the man of God showed himself humble</ref> not only toward his superiors but also toward his equals and the lowly, readier to be admonished and corrected than to admonish.
[2] Thus one day, as he was riding on a little donkey—for, weak and infirm, he could not go on foot—and was passing through the field of a certain peasant who was then at his labor, that peasant ran up to him and anxiously asked whether he was Brother Francis.
[3] When <ref to="Judg 13:6">the man of God</ref> had humbly answered that he was indeed the very man about whom he inquired, the peasant said: "Strive to be as good as men say you are, <ref to="Heb 6:9">for many place their trust in you</ref>.
[4] Therefore I admonish you never to be other than what is hoped of you."
[5] But when <ref to="Judg 13:6">Francis, the man of God</ref>, heard this, he flung himself down from the donkey to the ground, and prostrating himself before the peasant, <ref to="Luke 7:38">he humbly kissed his feet</ref>, <ref to="Tob 11:7">giving him thanks</ref> for deigning to admonish him.
[6] And so, though he was of such widespread renown that he was held by very many to be a saint, <ref to="Rom 12:17">he reckoned himself worthless before God and men</ref>,
[7] taking pride neither in his celebrated fame, nor in the holiness in which he flourished, <ref to="2 Cor 6:13">nor even in the many holy brothers and sons given to him as the beginning of the reward</ref> of his merits.

## Chapter 104 — How He Resigned His Office of Prelacy in the Chapter, and Concerning a Certain Prayer <a id="op2-104"></a>

<p id="143">
[1] To preserve the holy virtue of humility, a few years after his conversion, in a certain chapter, before all the brothers of the order he resigned the office of superior, saying: [2] "From this moment I am dead to you. But behold," he said, "brother Peter Cathanii, whom both I and all of you shall obey." [3] And bowing himself at once before him, he promised to that man 'obedience and reverence.' [4] The brothers therefore wept, and grief wrung from them deep groans, as they saw themselves in a manner made orphans by so great a father. [5] Rising up, blessed Francis, <ref to="John 17:1">with hands joined and eyes lifted to heaven</ref>, said: "Lord, to you I commend the family which until now you have entrusted to me. [6] And now, on account of the infirmities which you know, most sweet Lord, being unable <ref to="Luke 10:35">to have care of it</ref>, I commend it to the ministers. [7] Let them be bound on the day of judgment, before you, Lord, <ref to="Matt 12:30">to render an account</ref>, if any brother of theirs should perish through negligence, or through bad example, or even through harsh correction." [8] From then on he remained a subject until his death, conducting himself more humbly than any of the others.
</p>

## Chapter 105 - How he resigned his companions <a id="op2-105"></a>

<p id="144">
[1] On another occasion he surrendered all his companions to his vicar, saying: "I do not wish to seem to enjoy this singular privilege of liberty; rather, let the brothers attend me from place to place, just as 'the Lord shall inspire them.'" [2] And he added: "I have indeed seen a blind man who had a little dog to guide him on his way." [3] This, then, was his glory: that, every appearance of singularity and boasting being banished, <ref to="2 Cor 12:9">the power of Christ might dwell in him</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter 106 - His Words Against Those Who Covet Office, and a Portrait of the True Friar Minor <a id="op2-106"></a>

<p id="145">
[1] Seeing certain men hungering after positions of authority—men whom, among other faults, the very ambition to preside rendered unworthy—he declared that they were no longer Friars Minor, but had <ref to="Eph 4:1">forgotten the calling to which they had been called </ref> and had <ref to="Gal 5:4">fallen away from glory </ref>. [2] As for certain wretched men who bore it ill to be removed from their offices—since they sought not the burden but the honor—he confounded them with many a discourse. [3] And once he said to his companion: "I do not seem to myself a Friar Minor unless I were in the state which I shall now describe to you." [4] And he said: "Behold, being prelate of the brothers, I go to the chapter, I preach, I admonish the brothers, and at the end it is said against me: [5] 'An unlettered and contemptible man does not suit us; <ref to="Luke 19:4">therefore we will not have you reign over us </ref>, for you are tongue-tied, <ref to="Acts 4:13">simple and unlearned.</ref>' [6] At last I am cast out with reproach, despised by all. [7] I tell you, unless with the same countenance, <ref to="Ps 15:10">the same gladness of mind </ref>, the same resolve of holiness I hear these words, I am in no way a Friar Minor." [8] And he would add: "In prelacy there is a fall, in praise a headlong plunge, but in the humility of one who is subject there is gain for the soul. [9] Why then do we attend more to perils than to gains, when we have received the time for gaining?"
</p>

## Chapter 107 - Concerning the submission he wished the brothers to show toward the clergy, and the reason for it <a id="op2-107"></a>

<p id="146">
[1] Now though he wished his sons to <ref to="Rom 12:18"> be at peace with all men </ref> and to show themselves as little ones to everyone, he nonetheless taught them, by word and demonstrated by example, that they should be especially humble toward the clergy. [2] For he would say: <ref to="Ps 69:2; Dan 10:13">"We have been sent in support </ref><ref to="1 Pet 1:9"> of the clergy, for the salvation of souls </ref>, so that whatever is found wanting in them may be supplied by us. [3] Each one shall receive his reward not according to his authority, <ref to="1 Cor 3:8">but according to his labor </ref>. [4] Know, brothers," he said, "that <ref to="Wis 3:13">the harvest of souls </ref> is most pleasing to God, and that it can better be gathered by the peace of the clergy than by their discord. [5] But if they themselves hinder the salvation of the peoples, <ref to="Deut 32:35">vengeance belongs to God, and He will repay them in due time </ref>. [6] <ref to="1 Pet 2:13"> Therefore be subject </ref> to prelates, <ref to="Rom 18:18">so that, as far as it lies in you </ref>, no rivalry may arise. [7] <ref to="Luke 10:6"> If you are sons of peace </ref>, you will win both clergy and people for the Lord, <ref to="1 Pet 2:5">which the Lord judges more acceptable </ref> than to win the people alone while the clergy is scandalized. [8] Cover up," he said, "their failings, make good their many shortcomings, <ref to="Luke 17:10">and when you have done these things </ref>, be the more humble for it."
</p>

## Chapter 108 - Of the reverence which he showed to the Bishop of Imola <a id="op2-108"></a>

<p id="147">
[1] On a certain occasion, when Saint Francis came to Imola, a city of the Romagna, he presented himself to the bishop of that region, asking of him permission to preach. [2] To him the bishop replied: "It is enough, brother, that I myself preach to my own people." ## Chapter 111 - That for the sake of true obedience he always kept a guardian <a id="op2-111"></a>

<p id="151">
[1] That this most prudent merchant might gain in many ways, and convert all the present time into merit, he chose to be guided by the reins of obedience, and to submit himself to another's governance.
[2] Indeed, he not only resigned the general office, but, for the greater good of obedience, sought out a particular guardian, whom he might cultivate especially as his superior.
[3] For he said to Brother Peter Cathanii, to whom he had earlier promised holy obedience: "I beg you, for God's sake, to entrust your authority over me to one of my companions, that I may obey him as devotedly as I obey you.
[4] I know," he said, "the fruit of obedience, <ref to="Sir 51:34">and that for him who has bowed his neck beneath another's yoke no moment of time passes without profit</ref>."
[5] His insistence being granted, then, he remained everywhere a subject <ref to="Phil 2:8">even unto death</ref>, always reverently obeying his own guardian.
[6] On one occasion he said to his companions: "Among the other things which the divine mercy has graciously bestowed upon me, it has granted me this grace: that I would obey as diligently a novice of one hour, were he given to me as guardian, as I would obey the most senior or most discerning of men.
[7] A subject," he said, "ought not to regard his superior as a man, but as Him for whose love he is made subject.
[8] And the more contemptible the one who presides, the more pleasing is the humility of the one who obeys."

## Chapter 112 - How he described the truly obedient man, and concerning the three kinds of obedience <a id="op2-112"></a>

<p id="152">
[1] On another occasion, as he sat with his companions, blessed Francis heaved a sigh of this kind: "There is scarcely a religious in the whole world who perfectly obeys his superior."
[2] His companions, much moved, said to him: "Tell us, father, what perfect and supreme obedience may be."
[3] And he, describing the true obedient man under the figure of a dead body, replied: "Take up a lifeless body, and set it down wherever you please.
[4] You will see that it does not resist being moved, nor murmur at its position, nor protest at being left behind.
[5] If it be set upon a chair, it will look not upward but downward; if it be clothed in purple, it will grow but twice as pale.
[6] This," he said, "is the truly obedient man: he does not judge why he is moved, he does not care where he is placed, he does not press to be transferred.
[7] Raised to an office, he keeps his accustomed humility; the more he is honored, the more he reckons himself unworthy."
[8] On another occasion, speaking of such matters, he called those permissions granted after a request 'one's own licenses,' but those duties imposed without being sought he named 'sacred obediences.'
[9] He said that both were good, yet the one was safer.
[10] <ref to="Matt 16:17"> But the highest, in which flesh and blood would have no part </ref>, he believed to be that by which one goes 'by divine inspiration among the infidels,' whether for the gain of one's neighbors or out of a desire for martyrdom.
[11] <ref to="Phil 4:18"> And he judged that to seek this was greatly acceptable to God </ref>.
</p>

## Chapter 113 - That commands are not to be given lightly under obedience <a id="op2-113"></a>

<p id="153">
[1] He judged, therefore, that commands under obedience should be issued but rarely, and that the bolt which ought to be the last resort should not be the first to be hurled. [2] "One must not be quick," he said, "to lay hand to the sword." [3] But as for the man who would not hasten to obey a command given under obedience, he held that such a one <ref to="Luke 18:4">neither feared God nor respected man</ref>. [4] Nothing could be truer than these words. For what is the authority to command in a rash superior but a sword in the hand of a madman? And what is more hopeless than a religious who holds obedience in contempt?
</p>

## Chapter 114 - Concerning the brother whose hood he cast into the fire, because he had come without permission, even though drawn by devotion <a id="op2-114"></a>

<p id="154">
[1] On one occasion a hood was taken from a brother who had come alone without obedience, and he ordered it to be cast into a great fire. [2] But when no one snatched the hood out—for they feared the somewhat troubled countenance of their father—the saint ordered it to be drawn from the flames, and it had suffered no harm. [3] Although the merits of the saint may have accomplished this, perhaps the merit of that brother too was not wanting. [4] For the devotion of seeing his most holy father had bound him fast, though discretion—the sole charioteer of the virtues—was not present.

## Chapter 115 - On the example of a certain good brother, and on the custom of the brothers of old <a id="op2-115"></a>

<p id="155">
[1] He used to affirm that the Friars Minor had been sent by the Lord in these last days (cf. <ref to="John 1:6">Jude 18</ref>) for this very purpose: that to those wrapped in the gloom of sin they might display examples of the light. [2] He said that he was filled with the sweetest fragrances <ref to="Exod 29:18; John 12:3"></ref> and anointed with the virtue of a precious ointment <ref to="Matt 26:7"></ref> whenever he heard <ref to="Acts 2:11">the mighty deeds of the holy brothers scattered throughout the world</ref>. [3] It happened that a certain brother named Barbarus, in the presence of a certain nobleman from the island of Cyprus, <ref to="Job 18:2">once hurled a word of insult against another brother</ref>. [4] When he perceived that the brother had been somewhat wounded by the clash of words, kindled with zeal against himself, he took some donkey's dung and thrust it into his own mouth to be ground up, saying: [5] "Let the tongue chew dung, <ref to="Prov 23:32">the tongue that poured out the venom of anger against my brother</ref>." [6] Beholding this, the knight, struck with astonishment and greatly edified, departed, and from that time forth freely placed himself and his possessions at the disposal of the brothers. [7] All the brothers infallibly kept this as their custom: that if any of them should at any time utter a word of disturbance to another, <ref to="2 Macc 10:4">he would at once prostrate himself upon the ground</ref> and, with blessed kisses, soothe the foot of the wounded brother, even against the latter's will. [8] The saint exulted in such things, when he heard that his sons drew examples of holiness from their very selves, heaping upon those brothers <ref to="1 Tim 1:15">blessings most worthy of all acceptance</ref> who <ref to="Col 3:17">by word or deed</ref> led sinners to the love of Christ. [9] By the zeal for souls <ref to="Acts 5:17">with which he was perfectly filled</ref>, he wished his sons to correspond to him in true likeness.
</p>

<p id="156">
[1] <ref to="2 Pet 2:8">In the same way, whoever should violate the holy Order by wicked works </ref> or examples incurred through his curses a most grievous sentence.
[2] For when one day it was reported to him that Bishop Fundanus had said to two brothers passing before him—men who, under the guise of greater contempt of self, were growing their beards longer—"Take heed, lest the beauty of religious life be disfigured by the presumption of novelties of this kind":
[3] the saint at once rose up, <ref to="2 Macc 14:34">and with hands stretched out toward heaven </ref>, bathed in tears, burst forth into these words of prayer, or rather of imprecation:
[4] "Lord Jesus Christ, who chose the company of twelve Apostles—and though one of them fell away, <ref to="Eph 2:18">the rest, cleaving to you and filled with one spirit </ref>, preached the holy Gospel—
[5] you, O Lord, <ref to="1 John 2:18">in this last hour </ref><ref to="Ps 88:50; Sir 51:11">mindful of your ancient mercy </ref>, have planted the Order of the brothers as a support of faith in you, and that the mystery of your Gospel might be fulfilled through them.
[6] Who, then, shall make satisfaction for them before you, if, in that for which they were sent, they not only fail to display to all the examples of light, <ref to="Rom 13:12">but rather reveal the works of darkness </ref>?

[8] Where are those who proclaim themselves blessed by his blessing, and boast that they enjoyed his familiarity to their heart's desire?
[9] If—God forbid—they should be found, without repentance, to have displayed in themselves the works of darkness to the peril of others, woe to them (cf. Jude 11), woe of eternal damnation!

## Chapter 120 - How he, being himself a tireless worker, hated the idle <a id="op2-120"></a>

<p id="161">
[1] He used to say that the lukewarm, who applied themselves familiarly to no useful undertaking, would <ref to="Rev 3:16">soon be spewed out of the mouth of the Lord</ref>. [2] No one could appear before him in idleness without his rebuking him with a biting reproach. [3] Indeed, that very pattern of all perfection <ref to="1 Cor 4:12">laboured and worked with his own hands</ref>, allowing nothing to slip away of that most precious gift of time. [4] He once declared: "I want all my brothers to labour and be kept busy, and those who know no craft to learn some skill." ## Chapter 121 — A Lament Addressed to Him Concerning the Idle and the Gluttonous <a id="op2-121"></a>

<p id="162">
[1] Suffer it, holy father, that on behalf of those who are called yours I should this day raise my lament to the heights. [2] The exercises of virtue are hateful to many, who, wishing to rest before they labor, prove themselves the sons not of Francis but of Lucifer. [3] We abound more in the sickly than in soldiers, though <ref to="Job 5:7">men are born to labor</ref> and ought to reckon their <ref to="Job 7:1">life a warfare</ref>. [4] It does not please them to advance by action; by contemplation they cannot. [5] Since they have disturbed everyone by their singularity, laboring more with the gullet than with the hands, they hate the one who reproves in the gate (cf. [6] Yet, according to the word of blessed Francis, I marvel the more at the shamelessness of those who at home <ref to="Gen 3:19">would have lived only by the sweat</ref> of their brow, and now without labor are fed by the sweat of the poor. [8] They know the hours for the banquet, and if ever hunger presses them, they plead that the sun has overslept. [9] Could I believe, O good father, that such monsters of men are worthy of your glory? Why, not even of the habit! [10] You always taught that in this slippery and fleeting time the riches of merit should be sought, lest in the time to come it should fall to one's lot to beg. [12] This disease thrives among subjects because the prelates connive at it, as though it were possible that those whose vice they uphold should not earn their own punishment.

## Chapter CXXII - What manner of man a preacher ought to be <a id="op2-122"></a>

<p id="163">
[1] <ref to="Acts 6:4; Acts 17:13">Ministers of the word of God</ref> he wished to be such men as, intent upon spiritual pursuits, would be hindered by no other duties. [2] For he said that these had been chosen by a certain great king to deliver to the peoples the edicts which they received from his mouth. [3] And he would say: "The preacher must first draw in by secret prayers what he is afterward to pour out in sacred sermons; he must first grow warm within before he utters cold words without." [4] He called this office worthy of reverence, and those who administered it worthy of veneration from all. [5] "These," he said, "are the life of the body, these the assailers of demons, <ref to="Matt 5:14">these the lamp of the world</ref>." [6] The doctors of sacred theology, indeed, he judged worthy of still greater honors. [7] For he once had it written for all in general: "All theologians, and those who minister to us the divine words, we ought to honor and venerate, <ref to="John 6:64">as those who minister to us spirit and life</ref>." [8] And when he once wrote to blessed Anthony, he had it set thus at the beginning of the letter: "To Brother Anthony, my bishop."

## Chapter 124 — The Saint's Love for Creatures Both Sentient and Insensible <a id="op2-124"></a>

<p id="165">
[1] Hastening to depart from the world as from the exile of a pilgrimage, this blessed wayfarer was helped not a little, indeed, by <ref to="John 17:11">those things which are in the world</ref>. [2] For toward <ref to="Eph 6:12">the princes of darkness</ref> he used it as a field of combat, but toward God as a most brilliant mirror of His goodness (cf. Wis 7:26). [3] In every work of craftsmanship he commends the Craftsman; whatever he finds in things made he refers back to their Maker. [4] <ref to="Ps 91:5; Ps 8:7">He exults in all the works of the Lord's hands</ref>, and through these spectacles of delight he beholds the life-giving reason and cause behind them. [5] In beautiful things he recognizes the most Beautiful; <ref to="Gen 1:31">all things are good to him</ref>: "<ref to="Ps 99:3">He who made us</ref> is the best of all," they cry out. [6] <ref to="Job 23:11; Song 5:17">By the traces impressed upon things he pursues the Beloved everywhere</ref>, making of all things a ladder for himself, <ref to="Job 23:3">by which he might come to the throne</ref>. [7] With an affection of unheard-of devotion he embraces all things, speaking to them of the Lord and urging them on to His praise. [8] He spares lamps, lanterns, and candles, unwilling to extinguish with his own hand a brightness <ref to="Wis 7:26">that was a token of the eternal light</ref>. [9] He walks reverently upon stones, out of regard for Him who is called the Rock. [10] When he had occasion for that verse, "<ref to="Ps 60:3">Upon a rock You have exalted me</ref>," in order to say something more reverent he would say, "<ref to="Ps 17:39">beneath the feet</ref> of the Rock <ref to="Ps 60:3">You have exalted me</ref>." [11] When the brothers cut wood, he forbids them to fell the whole tree, so that it may have hope of sprouting again. [12] He bids the gardener leave undug borders around the garden, <ref to="Eph 4:6">so that in their proper seasons the greenness of the herbs and the loveliness of the flowers may proclaim the beautiful Father of all things</ref>. [13] He directs that within the garden a little plot be set apart for fragrant and flowering herbs, so that they may call those who gaze upon them to the remembrance of eternal sweetness. [14] He gathers up little worms from the road, lest they be trodden under foot, and he orders honey and the best wines to be set out for the bees, lest they perish from hunger in the winter's frost. ## Chapter 125 — How those very creatures repaid him love in turn, and concerning the fire that did not harm him <a id="op2-125"></a>

<p id="166">
[1] Therefore all creatures strive to repay the saint's love in kind, and to answer with their gratitude his deserving; they smile upon him who coaxes them, they assent to him who asks, they obey him who commands. [2] Let the account of a few suffice. [3] During an affliction of his eyes, when he had been pressed to allow himself to be treated, a surgeon was summoned to the place. [4] Coming therefore, he brought an iron instrument for making cauteries, and ordered it to be placed in the fire until it too should be made to glow. [5] But the blessed father, comforting his body already shaken with dread, addressed the fire thus: "My brother fire, surpassing all other things in beauty, full of strength, <ref to="Sir 1:8">beautiful and useful the Most High created you</ref>. [7] <ref to="Ps 47:2; Deut 32:6">I beseech the great Lord who created you</ref>, that he may now temper your heat, so that I may be able to endure your gentle burning." [8] His prayer finished, he marked the fire with the sign of the cross, and thereafter stood undaunted. [9] The glowing and scorching iron was taken in hand; the brothers, overcome by their human feeling, fled; joyful and eager, the saint offered himself to the iron. [10] The hissing iron sank into the tender flesh, and the cautery was drawn slowly from the ear all the way to the eyebrow. [11] How great a pain that fire inflicted, the words of the saint, who knew it better, bear witness. [15] The experienced physician, who in similar cases had met with unlike outcomes, extolled this as a divine miracle, saying: "I tell you, brothers, <ref to="Luke 5:26">I have seen wondrous things today</ref>." [16] I believe he had returned to that first innocence, for whom, when he willed it, harsh things were made gentle.

<aside>
He set forth an example of perfection.
</aside>

<p id="172">
[1] Since the power of love had made him brother to all other creatures, it is no wonder that the charity of Christ rendered him a still closer brother to those <ref to="2 Cor 5:14">stamped with the image of the Creator</ref>. [4] <ref to="John 15:14">He did not reckon himself a friend of Christ</ref> <ref to="1 John 4:21">unless he cherished the souls that Christ himself had cherished</ref>. ## Chapter 133 - On his compassion toward the sick <a id="op2-133"></a>

<p id="175">
[1] His compassion toward the sick was great, and great too his solicitude for their needs. [2] If ever the kindness of laypeople sent him some restorative confection, though he himself stood in greater need than the others, he would give it to the other sick brethren. [3] He took upon himself the feelings of all who suffered, offering words of compassion where he could not offer relief. [4] He himself would eat on days of fasting, lest the sick be afraid to eat; nor was he ashamed to go about the public places of the cities seeking meat for a sick brother. [5] Yet he admonished the infirm to bear their want patiently, and not to rise up in scandal when they were not satisfied in every particular. [6] Hence in a certain rule he caused these words to be written: "I beg all my sick brothers that in their infirmities they grow neither angry nor troubled against God or against the brethren. [7] Let them not too anxiously demand medicines, nor too eagerly desire to deliver from suffering a flesh soon to die, which is the soul's enemy. [8] <ref to="1 Thess 5:18"> Let them in all things give thanks </ref>, so that, whatever God wills them to be, that they may desire to be. [9] For <ref to="Acts 13:48"> those whom God has foreordained to eternal life </ref> He instructs with the goads of scourges and infirmities, just as He Himself has said: Such as I love, <ref to="Rev 3:19; Heb 12:6">I correct and chastise </ref>."

<p id="176">
[1] Once he led into a vineyard a certain sick man, whom he knew to be possessed by a craving to eat grapes, and <ref to="Mic 4:4">sitting beneath a vine</ref>, that he might give him the boldness to eat, he himself ate first.

## Chapter 134 — On the compassion he bore toward those weak in spirit, and on those who act contrary to this <a id="op2-134"></a>

<p id="177">
[1] He cherished the gravely ill with tenderness and bore with them in patience, for he knew them to be <ref to="Eph 4:14">like little ones tossed about</ref>, <ref to="Ps 76:4">driven by temptations and failing in spirit</ref>. [2] Hence, avoiding harsh corrections wherever he saw no danger, he <ref to="1 Macc 13:5">spared the rod</ref> <ref to="Prov 13:24">that he might spare the soul</ref>. [3] To forestall the occasion of wrongdoing, <ref to="Ps 144:14">and not to allow one who, once cast down, could only with difficulty be raised again, to slip away</ref>—this, he said, belongs to a prelate who is a father, not a tyrant. [4] Alas for the pitiable folly of our age! [5] Not only do we fail to raise up or restrain those who stumble, but at times we even thrust them into falling. [6] We count it as nothing to draw away from that supreme Shepherd a single little sheep, <ref to="Heb 5:7">for which He offered upon the cross a mighty cry with tears</ref>. [7] Far otherwise were you, holy father, who chose rather to amend those who erred than to lose them. [8] Yet we know that in some the diseases of self-will are rooted more deeply, and that for these there is need of the cautery, not of the salve. [9] <ref to="Ps 2:9">For it is plain that for very many it is more wholesome to be broken with a rod of iron</ref> than to be soothed by gentle hands. [10] But <ref to="Luke 10:34">oil and wine</ref>, <ref to="Ps 22:4">the rod and the staff</ref>, zeal and tenderness, the searing and the anointing, the prison and the bosom—<ref to="Eccl 3:1">all things have their season</ref>. ## Chapter 138 — How He Wished That Slanderers Be Punished <a id="op2-138"></a>

<p id="182">
[1] In short, since a soul filled with charity hates <ref to="Rom 1:30">those hateful to God</ref>, this trait flourished in holy Francis. [2] For, abominating detractors with horror above every other kind of vicious person, he used to say that they <ref to="Jas 3:8">carry poison upon their tongue</ref> and infect others with their venom. [3] And so he shunned tale-bearers and biting fleas; if ever they began to speak, he would, as we have seen, turn his ears away, lest they be defiled by such a hearing. [4] For hearing on one occasion a certain friar blackening the reputation of another, he turned to Brother Peter of Cattaneo, his vicar, and uttered this terrible word: "Perils press upon the Order, unless detractors are withstood. [5] <ref to="Lev 1:13; Exod 5:21">Soon the most pleasant fragrance of many will stink</ref>, <ref to="Esth 13:17">unless the mouths of the foul are shut</ref>. [6] Rise, rise, examine the matter diligently, and if you find the accused friar innocent, render the accuser conspicuous to all by a harsh correction! [7] Hand him over," he said, "into the fist of the Florentine boxer, if you yourself cannot punish him!" [8] (Now he called Brother John of Florence, <ref to="Bar 3:26">a man great in stature</ref> and of great strength, the boxer.) [9] "I want you," he said, "with the utmost foresight—you and all the ministers—to take care that this pestilent disease does not spread itself more widely." [10] And sometimes he declared that he who robbed his brother of the glory of his good name ought to be stripped of his tunic, <ref to="Luke 18:13">and could not lift his eyes to God</ref> until he had first restored what he had taken away. [11] Hence it was that the friars of that time, abjuring this vice as though it were something singular, had established among themselves by a firm decree that they would studiously avoid whatever might detract from another's honor or sound like a reproach. [12] And rightly and most excellently so! For what is a detractor but the gall of men, the leaven of wickedness, the disgrace of the world? [13] And what is a double-tongued man but a scandal to religion, the poison of the cloister, the rending of unity? [14] Alas, <ref to="Exod 5:10">the surface of the earth abounds with venomous creatures</ref>, nor can it come about that any upright man should escape the bites of his rivals. [15] Rewards are set before informers, and, innocence overthrown, the palm is sometimes awarded to falsehood. [16] Behold, where each cannot live by his own integrity, by laying waste the integrity of others he earns his food and clothing.
</p>

<p id="183">
[1] In keeping with this Saint Francis would often say: "This is the voice of the slanderer:
[2] 'I lack perfection of life, I have no abundant gift of learning or of singular grace, and on this account I find no place either with God or among men.
[3] I know what I shall do: <ref to="Luke 16:4; Sir 11:33">I will set a stain upon the elect</ref>, and so I shall win favor with my superiors.
[4] I know my prelate to be a man, and one who at times exercises the same office as I; so that, once the cedars are felled, <ref to="Judg 9:15">the bramble alone may be seen</ref> in the forest.'
[5] Come now, wretch! Feed upon human flesh, and since you can live no otherwise, gnaw at the bowels of your brothers!
[6] Such men strive to seem good rather than to become so, accusing vices yet not laying their own vices aside.
[7] They praise only those by whose authority they desire to be cherished, keeping silent about praises they reckon will not redound to their own credit.
[8] They sell the pallor of a <ref to="Matt 6:16">fasting face</ref> for ruinous praises, <ref to="1 Cor 14:37; Matt 6:18">so that they may seem spiritual</ref> men who judge all things, <ref to="1 Cor 2:15">and yet are themselves judged by no one</ref>.
[9] They take delight in the reputation of holiness, not in its work, in an angelic name, not in its virtue." A general description of the minister and of the other ministers.
</p>

## Chapter CXXXIX - What manner of man he ought to be among his companions <a id="op2-139"></a>

<p id="184">
[1] Near the end of his calling to the Lord, a certain friar—ever solicitous for things divine and moved by devotion to the Order—asked him, saying: "Father, you will pass on, <ref to="Ps 83:7">and the household that has followed you is left behind in the valley of tears.</ref> [2] Point out someone, if you know of any in the Order on whom your mind may rest, to whom the burden of the general ministry may safely be entrusted." [3] Saint Francis replied, clothing all his words in sighs: "I see no one, my son, sufficient to be the commander of so manifold an army, the shepherd of so vast a flock. [4] But I wish to portray one for you, and, as the proverb says, to fashion him with my own hand, in whom may shine forth what manner of man the father of this household ought to be."
</p>

<p id="185">
[1] "Such a man," he said, "ought to be of the most austere life, of great discernment, of praiseworthy reputation.
[2] A man who is free of private affections, lest, while he loves one part more, he engender scandal in the whole.
[3] A man to whom the pursuit of holy prayer is a friend, who apportions certain hours to his soul, and certain hours to the flock entrusted to him.
[4] For <ref to="Matt 20:1">in the early morning</ref> he ought first to attend to the sacraments of the Mass, and with long devotion to commend both himself and the flock to divine protection.
[5] But after prayer," he said, "let him set himself in public, to be plucked at by all, to answer to all, to provide for all with gentleness.
[6] He ought to be a man who makes no corner sordid <ref to="Rom 2:11">by respect of persons</ref>, with whom care for the lesser and simpler is no less alive than for the wise or the greater.
[7] A man who, though it has been granted him to excel in the gift of learning, nonetheless bears in his conduct rather the image of devout simplicity, and cherishes virtue.
[8] A man who abhors money, the chief corruption of our profession and perfection, and who, being the head of a poor religion and offering himself for the rest to imitate, never misuses any purse.
[9] For himself," he said, "a habit and a little book ought to suffice, but for the brethren a pen-case and a seal.
[10] Let him not be a collector of books, nor too intent upon reading, lest he detract from his office what he grants beforehand to study.
[11] A man who consoles the afflicted, <ref to="Ps 31:7; Ps 45:2">since he is the last refuge for the troubled</ref>, lest, if the remedies of healing should fail in him, the sickness of despair prevail among the infirm.
[12] That he may bend the insolent to gentleness, let him prostrate himself, and relax something of his own right, <ref to="Phil 3:8">that he may win the soul for Christ</ref>.
[13] To those who flee the Order, <ref to="Luke 15:4">as to the sheep that had perished</ref>, <ref to="1 John 3:17">let him not close the bowels of compassion</ref>, knowing that the temptations are most powerful which can drive one to so great a fall."
</p>

<p id="186">
[1] "I would wish him to be honored by all in Christ's stead, and to be provided for in all necessities with every kindness. [2] Yet he ought not to smile upon honors, nor to take more delight in favors than in injuries. [3] If ever, being weak or weary, he should require richer food, let him take it not in hidden but in public places, so that others might be freed from any shame in providing for their own weak bodies. [4] It belongs to him above all to discern hidden consciences, to draw forth the truth from concealed veins, and not to lend his ears to the talkative. [5] Such, in short, ought he to be, that out of an eagerness to retain his honor he in no way undermine the manly form of justice, and that he feel so great an office to be more a burden to him than an honor. [6] Yet let no sluggishness be born from excessive gentleness, nor any dissolution of discipline from lax indulgence, so that, while he is a delight to all, <ref to="Prov 10:29">he may be no less a terror to those who work evil</ref>. [7] Moreover, I would wish him to have companions endowed with integrity, who, like himself, would <ref to="Titus 2:7">offer themselves as an example of all good things</ref>: [8] stern against pleasures, strong against hardships, and so fittingly affable that they would receive all who came with holy cheerfulness. [9] Behold," he said, "such ought the general minister of the Order to be."

## Chapter 140 - On the Provincial Ministers <a id="op2-140"></a>

<p id="187">
[1] All these qualities the blessed father sought in the provincial ministers, though in the minister general each ought singularly to stand preeminent. [2] He wished them to be approachable to their subjects, and so gracious in their kindness that offenders would not be afraid to entrust themselves to their goodwill. [3] He wished them to be temperate in their commands, merciful toward offenses, readier to endure wrongs than to repay them, enemies to vices yet physicians to the vicious. [4] In short, he wished them to be men whose life should be a mirror of discipline to the rest. [5] Yet these same men he wished to be honored with every honor and to be loved, <ref to="Matt 20:12">as those who bore the weight </ref> of cares and labors. [6] He declared that they were worthy of the highest rewards in the sight of God who governed the souls entrusted to them by such a model and such a law.
</p>

## Chapter 141 - What the saint replied when questioned about the ministers <a id="op2-141"></a>

<p id="188">
[1] When once a certain brother asked him why, having thus cast off all the brothers from his own care, he had handed them over into the hands of others, as though they belonged to him in no way at all, he replied: [2] "My son, I love the brothers as much as I can; <ref to="1 Pet 2:21">but if they would follow in my footsteps</ref>, I would assuredly love them all the more, nor would I make myself a stranger to them. [3] For there are some among the prelates who draw them off to other things, setting before them the examples of the ancients and reckoning little of my admonitions. [4] But what they do shall be seen in the end." [5] And a little later, when he was weighed down by an exceedingly grievous infirmity, <ref to="Ps 47:8">in vehemence of spirit</ref> he raised himself up in his little bed: "Who are these," he said, <ref to="John 10:28">"who have snatched my religion and the brothers out of my hands</ref>? [6] If I come to the general chapter, then I shall show them what manner of will I have." [7] And that brother added: "Will you not also change those provincial ministers who have so long abused their liberty?" [8] And the father, groaning, gave back a terrible word: "Let them live as they please, for the ruin of a few is of lesser harm than the ruin of many!" [9] He spoke this not on account of all but on account of certain ones, who by the too-long span of time seemed to have laid claim to their prelacy as if by hereditary right. [10] Indeed, in every kind of regular prelate he chiefly commended this: not to change their ways except for the better, not to seek out cultivated favors, not to wield power but to fulfill the office.
</p>

## Chapter 142 - What True Simplicity Is <a id="op2-142"></a>

<p id="189">
[1] Holy simplicity, daughter of grace, sister of wisdom, mother of justice, the saint cultivated within himself with a certain more attentive zeal, <ref to="Wis 1:1">and loved </ref> in others. [2] Yet not every simplicity won his approval, but only that which, content with its God, holds all else of little worth. [3] This is the simplicity <ref to="Sir 9:22">that glories in the fear of God </ref>, that knows not how to do or speak evil. [4] This is the simplicity <ref to="John 8:10">that, examining itself, condemns no one by its own judgment </ref>, that, rendering due authority to one better, seeks no authority for itself. [5] This is the simplicity <ref to="2 Macc 4:15">that, deeming the glories of the Greeks far from the best </ref>, <ref to="Acts 1:1">chooses rather to do than to learn or teach </ref>. [6] This is the simplicity that, in all the divine laws, leaving wordy circumlocutions, ornaments and trappings, displays and curiosities to those things doomed to perish, seeks not the bark but the marrow, not the shell but the kernel, not many things but much, the highest and abiding good. [7] This the most holy father sought in his brethren, both the learned and the lay, believing it not contrary to wisdom but truly her sister, though for the poor in learning easier to attain and readier to put to use. [8] Hence in the Praises which he composed concerning the virtues, he says thus: "Hail, Queen Wisdom! May the Lord keep you, with your sister, pure holy Simplicity!"
</p>

## Chapter 143 - Concerning Brother John the Simple <a id="op2-143"></a>

<p id="190">
[1] As Saint Francis was walking past a certain hamlet near Assisi, a man named John, of the most artless simplicity, who was ploughing in a field, ran up to him and said: "I want you to make me a brother, <ref to="Acts 14:3; Matt 6:24">for I have long desired to serve God</ref>." [2] The saint rejoiced, considering the man's simplicity, and answered him agreeably: "If you wish, brother," he said, "to become our companion, give to the poor <ref to="Matt 19:21">whatever you have</ref>, and dispossessed I will receive you." [3] At once he unyoked his oxen and offered one to Saint Francis. [4] "Let us give this ox," he said, <ref to="Luke 15:12">"to the poor! For I deserve to receive so large a portion of my father's goods.</ref>" [5] The saint smiled, but warmly approved his disposition of simplicity. [6] When his parents and his little brothers heard this, they came running in tears, grieving more that the ox was being taken from them than the man. [7] To them the saint said: <ref to="Bar 4:27">"Take heart!</ref> behold, I give you back the ox; I take away the brother." [8] So he led the man away with him and, clothing him in the garments of religion, made him on account of the grace of his simplicity a special companion. [9] Now whenever Saint Francis stood in some place to meditate, whatever gestures or movements he made, simple John would at once copy and reproduce in himself. [10] For when the saint spat, he spat; when he coughed, he coughed; pairing sigh with sigh and joining weeping to weeping; <ref to="Deut 32:40">when the saint raised his hands to heaven</ref>, he too raised his, watching him carefully as a model and transforming everything into himself. [11] The saint noticed this and one day asked why he was doing such things. [12] He replied: "I have promised," he said, "to do everything that you do; it is dangerous for me to omit anything." [13] The saint rejoiced at his pure simplicity, yet gently forbade him to do so any longer. [14] And so, not long afterward, the simple man passed to the Lord in this purity. [15] Frequently setting forth his life as one to be imitated, the saint most delightedly called him not Brother John but Saint John. [16] Observe that it is proper to holy simplicity to live by the rules of one's superiors and always to rely upon the examples and instructions of the saints. [17] <ref to="Job 6:8; 1 Cor 2:4">Who will grant to human wisdom</ref> to follow with such zeal even Him who reigns in heaven, as holy simplicity conformed itself to him on earth? [18] What more? Having followed the saint in life, it went before the saint into life.
</p>

## Chapter 144 — How he fostered unity among his sons, of which he spoke under the veil of a riddle <a id="op2-144"></a>

<p id="191">
[1] It was his constant prayer and watchful endeavor always to preserve among his sons the bond of unity (cf. Eph 4:3), <ref to="Job 34:14">so that those whom one and the same Spirit had drawn</ref>, <ref to="Prov 23:22">and whom one and the same father had begotten</ref>, might be cherished peacefully in the bosom of a single mother. [2] He wished the greater to be united with the lesser, the wise to be joined to the simple by brotherly affection, and the far distant to be bound to one another by the glue of love. [3] Once he set forth a moral parable that held no small instruction: "Behold," he said, "let there be made of all the religious who are in the Church one general chapter! [4] <ref to="Acts 4:13">Since, therefore, there are present both lettered men and those without letters</ref>, <ref to="Heb 11:6">the learned and those who without learning know how to please God</ref>, let a sermon be appointed for one of the wise and one of the simple. [5] The wise man deliberates, for he is wise, <ref to="Matt 16:7">and he reasons within himself</ref>: 'This is no place for parading knowledge, where men are perfect in knowledge, nor is it fitting that I, speaking subtle things among the most subtle, should make myself remarkable for vain curiosity. [6] To speak simply will perhaps be more fruitful.' [7] <ref to="Esth 10:11">The appointed day dawns</ref>, <ref to="Esth 8:11; Ps 110:1">the assemblies of the saints are gathered into one</ref>, and they thirst to hear the sermon. [8] The wise man comes forward clad in sackcloth (cf. <ref to="Lam 2:10">Jon 3:5) and with his head sprinkled with ashes</ref>, and to the wonder of all, preaching more by deed than by word, <ref to="Rom 9:28">he cuts his words short</ref>: [9] 'Great things,' he says, 'have we promised; greater things have been promised to us. Let us keep these, let us sigh for those. Pleasure is brief, punishment everlasting; the suffering slight, the glory infinite. Many are called, few chosen, all repaid.' [10] <ref to="Gen 43:30">The hearts of the hearers, pricked with compunction, burst into tears</ref><ref to="Ps 108:17"></ref>, and they truly venerate the wise man as a saint. [11] 'Is this so?' <ref to="Ps 13:1">says the simple man in his heart</ref>: 'The wise man has snatched from me everything I had resolved to do or say. <ref to="Luke 16:4">But I know what I shall do</ref>. [12] I know certain verses of the psalms; I will adopt the manner of the wise man, since he has adopted the manner of the simple.' [13] The next day's session arrives; the simple man rises and sets a psalm as his theme. [14] Then, breathed upon by the divine Spirit, he delivers his discourse so fervently, so subtly, so sweetly, out of an inspired gift of God, <ref to="Acts 3:10">that all, filled with amazement</ref>, truly say: <ref to="Prov 3:32">His conversation is with the simple</ref>."
</p>

<p id="192">
[1] This moral parable, which he proposed in this way, <ref to="3 Kgs 13:1">the man of God expounded thus</ref>:
[2] "Our Order," he said, "is a vast assembly, a kind of universal synod, which from every part of the world gathers together under one form of living.
[3] In it the wise turn to their own profit what belongs to the simple, when they behold unlettered men seeking heavenly things with fiery vigor, <ref to="Ps 93:10">and the untaught, by man's instruction</ref>, <ref to="Acts 11:28; Matt 16:23">savoring spiritual things through the Spirit</ref>.
[4] In it, too, the simple convert to their own fruit what pertains to the wise, when they see illustrious men humbled to the same level as themselves, <ref to="Sir 44:1">men who might live anywhere in the world in glory</ref>.
[5] "Hence," he said, "shines forth the beauty of this blessed family, whose manifold adornment is no small delight to the Father of the household."
</p>

<p id="193">
[1] When Saint Francis was being shaved, he would often say to the barber: "Take care not to make my tonsure too large!

[3] He desired, in the end, that the Order be open in common to the poor and the unlettered, and not to the rich and the learned alone.
[4] "With God," he said, <ref to="Rom 2:11">"there is no respect of persons</ref>, and the general minister of the Order, the Holy Spirit, <ref to="Isa 11:2">rests upon the poor and the simple no less</ref>."

## Chapter 146 - How he wished that great clerics coming into the Order should divest themselves of their possessions <a id="op2-146"></a>

<p id="194">
[1] He once said that even a great scholar ought in some measure to renounce his learning when he came to the Order, so that, stripped of such a possession, he might offer himself naked into the arms of the Crucified. [2] "Knowledge," he said, "renders many unteachable, allowing a certain rigidity in them that will not bend to humble disciplines. [3] Wherefore," he said, <ref to="Heb 5:7">"I should wish that a lettered man would first offer me this prayer</ref>: 'Behold, brother, <ref to="Titus 2:12">long have I lived in the world</ref>, <ref to="John 1:10">and have not truly known my God</ref>. [4] I beg you, grant me a place set apart from the din of the world, <ref to="Isa 38:15">where I may recount my years in</ref> sorrow, <ref to="Ps 146:2; Luke 1:51">and where, gathering up again the scatterings of my heart</ref>, I may reform my spirit toward better things.' [5] What sort of man," he said, "would you believe such a one would become, who began in this way? [6] Surely he would go forth like a lion unchained, robust for all things, and the blessed sap which he had drunk in at the beginning would grow within him through continual progress." [7] <ref to="Acts 6:4">At last he would be entrusted with confidence to the true ministry of the word</ref>, for he would pour forth that with which he boiled over." [8] Truly a holy teaching! For what is so necessary for one returning from the region of unlikeness as to root out and wipe away, by humble exercises, the worldly affections so long forged and stamped upon him? [9] Whoever should enter would swiftly attain perfection in the school of perfection.
</p>

## Chapter 147 - How he wished to learn them, and how he appeared to a companion who was intent upon preaching <a id="op2-147"></a>

<p id="195">
[1] He grieved if learning was pursued while virtue was neglected, and especially if anyone failed to persevere in that vocation <ref to="1 Cor 7:20">in which he had been called from the beginning</ref>. [2] "My brothers," he would say, "who are led on by a curiosity for learning will find their hands empty <ref to="Hos 9:7">on the day of recompense</ref>. [3] I should wish them rather to be strengthened by virtues, so that, <ref to="Ps 36:39">when the times of tribulation</ref> should come, <ref to="2 Chr 15:4">they might have the Lord with them in their distress</ref>. [4] For there is coming," he said, "a <ref to="Ps 21:12; Prov 1:27">tribulation</ref> in which books useful for nothing will be flung into the window-recesses and hiding places." [5] He said this not because the study of the Scriptures was displeasing to him, but in order to draw all back from an excessive eagerness for learning, and because he preferred that everyone be good through charity rather than knowing little through curiosity. [6] He had a foreknowledge, too, of times not far off, in which he knew that knowledge would become an occasion of ruin, while the spirit would find its support in things spiritual. [7] To a lay brother who wished to possess a psalter and asked his leave for it, he offered ashes in place of the psalter. [8] Appearing in a vision after his death to one of his companions who was at one time devoting himself to preaching, he forbade it and commanded him to walk the road of simplicity. [9] <ref to="Rom 1:9">God is his witness</ref> that after this vision he felt such sweetness that for many days the dew-laden words of the father seemed to be instilled, as if present, into his very ears.

## Chapter 148 — How he was moved upon hearing of the love of God <a id="op2-148"></a>

<p id="196">
[1] To touch briefly upon the special devotions of Saint Francis will perhaps be neither useless nor unworthy.
[2] For though this man was devout in all things, <ref to="Luke 4:18">being one who enjoyed the anointing of the Spirit</ref>, yet by a particular affection he was moved toward certain particular things.
[3] Among the other words that found their place in common speech, he could not hear <ref to="Job 14:14">the love of God without a certain transformation of himself</ref>.
[4] For at once, upon hearing of the love of God, he was roused, stirred, set aflame, as though the inner string of his heart were touched by the plectrum of an outward voice.
[5] He used to say that to offer such a sum as alms was a noble extravagance, and that those who reckoned it of less worth than coins were the most foolish of all.
[6] As for himself, the resolve he had made while still mingled among worldly affairs—never to refuse any poor man who asked of him for the love of God—he kept unfailingly until his death.
[7] For when a poor man once begged of him for the love of God, and he had nothing at hand, he secretly took up a pair of shears and hastened to divide his tunic.
[8] And he would have done so, had he not been caught by the brothers, who then by some other compensation saw that provision was made for the poor man.
[9] "His love," he said, "who loved us so greatly, is greatly to be loved."

## Chapter 149 - Of his devotion to the angels, and what he used to do out of love for Saint Michael <a id="op2-149"></a>

<p id="197">
[1] He held in the deepest affection the angels who stand with us in the battle-line, <ref to="Ps 22:4; Isa 9:2">and who walk with us in the midst of the shadow of death</ref>. [2] He used to say that such companions are everywhere to be revered, and that such guardians are no less to be invoked. [3] He taught that men should keep their gaze untainted before these angels, <ref to="Rom 12:17">and presume to do nothing in their sight that would not be done in the sight of men</ref>. [4] <ref to="Ps 137:1">Because in the choir the psalms are sung in the presence of the angels</ref>, he wished that all who were able should gather in the oratory <ref to="Ps 46:8">and there sing the psalms with wisdom</ref>. [5] As for the blessed Michael, because he holds the office of presenting souls, he often declared that he ought to be honored with special excellence. [6] For in honor of Saint Michael, between the feast of the Assumption and his own feast, he kept a most devout fast of forty days. [7] For he used to say: <ref to="Matt 5:23">"Everyone, for the honor of so great a prince, ought to offer to God some token of praise or some special gift"</ref>.

## Chapter 150 - On his devotion to Our Lady, to whom he in a special way entrusted the Order <a id="op2-150"></a>

<p id="198">
[1] He embraced the Mother of Jesus with unspeakable love, <ref to="Ps 28:3">because she had made the Lord of majesty </ref> our brother.
[2] To her he rendered special praises, poured forth prayers, and offered devotions of such number and such quality as no human tongue could utter.

## Chapter 151 - On his devotion to the Lord's Nativity, and how he wished that all should be relieved at that time <a id="op2-151"></a>

<p id="199">
[1] He kept the Nativity of the child Jesus with an inexpressible eagerness above all other solemnities, declaring it the feast of feasts, the day on which God, made a tiny infant, hung upon human breasts. [2] With hungering thought he would lick over and over the images of those infant limbs, and the compassion for the child, melting within him, made him even babble words of sweetness after the manner of infants. [3] And this name was for him <ref to="Prov 16:24">like honey and the honeycomb</ref> in his mouth. [4] When a discussion arose about not eating meat, because it was a Friday, he replied to Brother Morico, saying: "You sin, brother, in calling it Friday, the day on which <ref to="Isa 9:6">a Child has been born to us</ref>. [5] I wish," he said, "that on such a day even the walls might eat meat, and if they cannot, that at least they might be smeared with it on the outside!"

<p id="200">
[1] <ref to="1 Kgs 2:5"> On this day he wished the poor and the hungry to be filled by the rich </ref>, and that to the oxen and asses fodder and hay be granted more liberally than was usual. [3] He would recall, not without tears, how great a want encircled the poor little Virgin on that day. [6] Hence he would say that this was a royal virtue, which had shone forth so excellently in the King and the Queen. [7] For indeed, when the brothers were deliberating in their gathering as to which virtue would render one more dear to Christ, he answered, as though unveiling the secret of his heart: "Know, my sons, that poverty is the special way of salvation, whose fruit is manifold and best known to but few."

## Chapter 152 - On Devotion to the Body of the Lord <a id="op2-152"></a>

<p id="201">
[1] He burned toward the sacrament of the Lord's Body with the ardor of his whole inmost being, holding in the most profound amazement that dear condescension and most worthy charity. [2] To miss even a single Mass each day, when there was leisure for it, he reckoned no small contempt. He communicated often, and so devoutly that he made others devout. [3] For pursuing that reverend mystery with all reverence, he offered the sacrifice of all his limbs, and receiving <ref to="1 Pet 1:19">the slain Lamb</ref>, with <ref to="Lev 6:12; Sir 23:22">that fire which ever burned upon the altar of his heart</ref>, he offered up his spirit. [4] For this reason he loved France as a friend of the Body of the Lord, and he longed to die there out of reverence for the sacred mysteries. [6] He wished that great reverence be shown to priestly hands, upon which so divine an authority has been conferred for the confecting of that very mystery. [7] He often said: <ref to="John 3:31">"If it should befall me to meet at once some saint coming down from heaven</ref> and some poor little priest, I would first do honor to the priest, and would hasten the more swiftly to kiss his hands. ## Chapter 153 - On Devotion to the Relics of the Saints <a id="op2-153"></a>

<p id="202">
[1] <ref to="Sir 45:1">Showing himself most devoted to divine worship, this man beloved of God</ref> left nothing <ref to="Matt 22:21">that is God's</ref> neglected through carelessness or want of honor. [2] While he was at Monte Casale, in the province of Massa, he commanded the brothers to bear the holy relics from a certain church, abandoned by all, to the brothers' dwelling with the utmost reverence. [5] Now on a certain day, when the brothers wished to celebrate Mass and had removed the cloth from the altar, as is the custom, they found bones most beautiful and exceedingly fragrant. [6] They were utterly astounded, gazing upon what they had never seen before. [7] <ref to="Luke 4:34">When a little later the holy one of God returned</ref>, he carefully inquired whether what he had ordered concerning the relics had been carried out. ## Chapter 154 - On Devotion to the Cross and on a Certain Hidden Mystery <a id="op2-154"></a>

## Chapter 155 - How he wished the brothers to conduct themselves among them <a id="op2-155"></a>

<p id="204">
[1] It would not be fitting to pass over in silence the memory of a spiritual edifice, far nobler than that earthly one which the blessed father, after the repair of that material church and <ref to="Isa 63:14">under the guidance of the Holy Spirit </ref>, established in that place for the increase of the heavenly city. [2] One must not believe that Christ, from the wood of the cross, spoke to him in a manner so utterly astonishing—he <ref to="2 Macc 12:22">who strikes fear into those who hear </ref> and sends in sorrow—merely for the repairing of a work doomed to perish and crumble away. [3] Rather, <ref to="Acts 1:16">as the Holy Spirit had long before foretold </ref>, an Order of holy virgins was to be established there, a polished heap, <ref to="1 Pet 2:5">as it were, of living stones </ref>, that was one day to be raised up for the restoration of the heavenly house. ## Chapter CLXVII — The Prayer of the Saint's Companions to Him <a id="op2-167"></a>

<p id="221">
[1] Behold, our blessed father, the efforts of our simplicity have striven to praise, as best they might, your magnificent deeds, and to set forth in public, for your glory, at least a few of the countless virtues of your holiness. [2] We know that our words have stripped away much of the splendor from your distinctions, since they were found unequal to the task of proclaiming the great wonders of so great a perfection. [3] We beg of you, and of those who read, that affection be weighed as much as effort, and that you take joy that human pens are surpassed by the heights of your wondrous character. [4] For who, O most excellent of saints, could shape within himself, or imprint upon others, <ref to="Isa 4:4">the ardor of your spirit</ref>; and who would be able to conceive those ineffable affections that flowed from you unceasingly toward God? [5] Yet we have written these things, delighting in your sweet memory, which—so long as we live—we strive to pour forth to others, even if only stammeringly. [6] You are fed now <ref to="Ps 80:17">with the richness of wheat</ref>, you who once were hungry; now you are given drink from the torrent of delight, you who until now thirsted. [7] We do not believe you to be so <ref to="Ps 35:9">made drunk with the abundance of the house of God</ref> <ref to="Hos 4:6">that you have forgotten your sons</ref>, since even He whom you drink <ref to="Ps 113:12">is mindful of us</ref>. [8] Draw us, then, to yourself, worthy father, <ref to="Song 1:3">that we may run after the fragrance of your ointments</ref>—us whom you surely see lukewarm with sloth, languid with laziness, half-alive with negligence! [9] <ref to="Luke 12:32">A little flock follows you</ref>, but now with wavering step; the keenness of our feeble eyes, beaten back, cannot bear the radiance of your perfection. [10] Renew our days <ref to="Lam 5:21">as at the beginning</ref>, O mirror and pattern of the perfect, and do not allow those who are conformed to you by the same profession to be unlike you in life!
</p>

<p id="222">
[1] Behold, <ref to="Dan 9:18">we now lay down </ref><ref to="Ps 71:19"> our humble entreaties before the mercy of the eternal Majesty </ref> on behalf of Christ's servant, our Minister, the heir of your holy humility and a zealous follower of true poverty, <ref to="Gen 46:32">who bears the watchful care of your sheep </ref>, <ref to="Rom 8:35; Ps 83:10">with sweet affection for the love of your Christ </ref>; [2] him we beseech you, O holy one, so to advance and embrace, that, ever clinging to your very footsteps, he may forever attain that <ref to="Phil 1:11">praise and glory </ref><ref to="1 Tim 4:6"> which you have obtained </ref>.
</p>

<p id="223">
[1] With all the love of our hearts we likewise beseech you, most kindly father, on behalf of that son of yours who now, as in days past, has devoutly set down your praises. [2] This little work, though not worthy of your merits, yet gathered in piety according to his strength, he offers and dedicates to you together with us. [3] Deign to keep and deliver him from every evil, increasing holy merits within him, and joining him forever by your prayers to the fellowship of the saints.
</p>

<p id="224">
[1] Remember, O father, the whole company of your sons, who, beset by perils beyond all telling, follow your footsteps from afar — and you, most holy one, know perfectly <ref to="Gen 33:14">how far behind they tread in your steps</ref>. [2] Grant them strength to stand firm; purify them, that they may shine; gladden them, that they may rejoice in fruitfulness. [3] Obtain that there be <ref to="Zech 12:10">poured out upon them the spirit of grace and of supplication</ref>, that they may have the true humility which you had, that they may keep the poverty which you held fast, that they may merit <ref to="1 Cor 1:23">the charity with which you ever loved Christ crucified</ref>. [4] Who with the Father and the Holy Spirit lives and reigns world without end. <ref to="Rev 11:15">Amen</ref>.
