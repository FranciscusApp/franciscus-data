# MAJOR LEGEND OF SAINT FRANCIS

<aside>
THE FIRST LITTLE WORK
</aside>

## PROLOGUE <a id="prolog"></a>

<aside>
HERE BEGINS THE PROLOGUE TO THE LIFE OF BLESSED FRANCIS.
</aside>

<p id="prolog-1">
[1] In these last days the grace of God our Savior has appeared in his servant Francis to all the truly humble and the friends of holy poverty, who, venerating the superabounding mercy of God in him, are instructed by his example utterly to renounce impiety and worldly desires, <ref to="Titus 2:11-13; Heb 1:2">to live in conformity with Christ, and with unwearied longing to thirst after the blessed hope</ref>. [2] For upon this man, as one truly poor and contrite, <ref to="Isa 66:2; Job 36:22">God on high looked with such condescension of kindness </ref> <ref to="1 Kgs 2:8">that he not only raised up the needy one from the dust of worldly converse</ref>, but also made of him a professor of evangelical perfection, and <ref to="Isa 49:6">gave him as leader and herald, set as a light for believers</ref>, so that, bearing witness to the light, <ref to="John 1:7; Luke 1:76">he might prepare for the Lord a way of light and peace in the hearts of the faithful</ref>. [3] For this man, <ref to="Sir 50:6">like the morning star in the midst of a cloud</ref>, gleaming with the bright radiance of his life and teaching, by his preeminent brilliance guided into the light <ref to="Luke 1:79">those who sat in darkness and the shadow of death</ref>, [4] and <ref to="Sir 50:8">like a rainbow shining amid the clouds of glory</ref>, displaying in himself <ref to="Gen 9:13">the sign of the Lord's covenant</ref>, <ref to="Rom 10:15">he proclaimed the gospel of peace and salvation</ref> to men, [5] being himself also <ref to="Isa 33:7">an Angel of true peace</ref>, appointed by God after the imitative likeness of the Forerunner, so that, <ref to="Mark 1:3; Luke 3:4">preparing in the wilderness a way </ref> of most exalted poverty, <ref to="Isa 40:3; Luke 24:47">he might preach penance both by example and by word</ref>. [6] First anticipated by the gifts of heavenly grace, then enriched by the merits of unconquered virtue, <ref to="Luke 1:67">filled likewise with the prophetic spirit</ref>, <ref to="4 Kgs 2:11">assigned moreover to an angelic office, wholly aflame with the seraphic fire, and, like a hierarchic man borne aloft in a fiery chariot</ref>, he is reasonably shown—as clearly appears from the course of his life—to have come <ref to="Luke 1:17">in the spirit and power of Elijah</ref>. [7] Hence <ref to="John 3:29">by the other friend of the Bridegroom</ref>, the Apostle and Evangelist John, in his truthful prophecy under the likeness of an Angel ascending from the rising of the sun and bearing the sign of the living God, he is not without merit asserted to have been designated. [8] "For under the opening of the sixth seal," says John in the Apocalypse, "I saw another Angel ascending from the rising of the sun, <ref to="Rev 6:12; Rev 7:2">having the sign of the living God</ref>."
</p>

<p id="prolog-2">
[1] We gather with unshakable faith that Francis was this messenger of God—beloved of Christ, a model for us, and a marvel to the world—when we mark in him the height of surpassing sanctity, by which, while still dwelling among men, he was an imitator of angelic purity, and by which he was set as an example for the perfect followers of Christ.
[2] To hold this faithfully and devoutly we are drawn not only by the office that was his, of summoning men to weeping and lamentation, <ref to="Isa 22:12; Ezek 9:4">to baldness and the girding of sackcloth, and of marking the Thau upon the foreheads of those who groan and grieve</ref>, through the sign of the penitential cross and a habit conformed to the cross;
[3] but it is also confirmed by the irrefutable testimony of truth, <ref to="Ezek 28:12">namely the seal of the likeness</ref> of the living God—<ref to="1 Cor 2:2">that is, of Christ crucified</ref>—
[4] which was imprinted upon his very body, not by the power of nature or the craft of art, <ref to="2 Cor 3:3">but rather by the wondrous power of the Spirit of the living God</ref>.
</p>

<p id="prolog-3">
[1] Sensing myself unworthy and unequal to the task of recording the life of so venerable a man—a life supremely worthy of all imitation—I should by no means have ventured upon it, had not the ardent affection of the brethren urged me on, [2] had not the harmonious insistence of the General Chapter prevailed upon me, and had not that devotion which I am bound to bear toward our holy father compelled me; [3] for since, through his invocation and merits, I was in my boyhood—as I hold in fresh remembrance—snatched from the very jaws of death, I fear I should be charged with the crime of ingratitude were I to keep silent about the heralds of his praise. [4] And this is for me the chief reason for taking up this labor: that I, who acknowledge that the life of body and soul has been preserved for me by God through him, and who have come to know his power tested within my very self, [5] might gather up after my fashion—however imperfectly—the virtues, the deeds, and the words of that life, as it were certain fragments partly neglected and partly scattered, lest, with the passing of those who had lived alongside the servant of God, they should <ref to="John 6:12">be lost</ref>.
</p>

<p id="prolog-4">
[1] Therefore, that the truth of his life, to be handed down to posterity, might rest upon me more surely and more clearly, I went to the place of the holy man's birth, his manner of living, and his passing, and I held careful conference about these matters with those familiar with him who still survive, [2] and especially with certain ones who were both privy to his sanctity and its foremost followers, to whom, on account of the truth they had recognized and the virtue they had tested, unhesitating credence must be given. [3] In describing those things which God graciously wrought through His servant, I judged that the fastidious ornament of style should be set aside, since the devotion of the reader profits more from plain speech than from a gaudy one. [4] Nor have I always woven the history according to the order of time, so as to avoid confusion; rather I have striven to keep the order of a more fitting arrangement, joining together such deeds as, though accomplished at the same time, seemed to suit diverse subjects, or, though wrought at diverse times, seemed to belong to one and the same subject.
</p>

<p id="prolog-5">
[1] The beginning of his life, its progress, and its consummation are described below in fifteen distinct chapters, set down as follows. The first treats of his manner of life while still in the garb of the world. [2] The second, of his perfect conversion to God and of the restoration of three churches. [3] The third, of the founding of his religious order and the approval of the rule. [4] The fourth, of the growth of the Order under his hand and the confirmation of the rule previously approved. [5] The fifth, of the austerity of his life, and how created things afforded him consolation. [6] The sixth, of his humility and obedience, and of the divine condescensions granted to him at his beck. [7] The seventh, of his love of poverty and the wondrous supplying of want. [8] The eighth, of the tenderness of his devotion, and how creatures lacking reason seemed moved with affection toward him. [9] The ninth, of the fervor of his charity and his longing for martyrdom. [10] The tenth, of his zeal for prayer and its power. [11] The eleventh, <ref to="Rev 19:10">of his understanding of the Scriptures and the spirit of prophecy</ref>. [12] The twelfth, of the efficacy of his preaching and his grace of healing. [13] The thirteenth, of the sacred stigmata. [14] The fourteenth, of his patience and his passing in death. [15] The fifteenth, of his canonization and translation. [16] Finally, certain things are appended concerning the miracles displayed after his blessed passing.
</p>

<aside>
Here ends the prologue.
</aside>

<aside>
HERE BEGINS THE LIFE OF BLESSED FRANCIS.
</aside>

## Chapter I - On the Manner of Life of Saint Francis While Yet in Secular Garb <a id="cap1"></a>

<p id="cap1-1">
[1] There was a man in the city of Assisi, Francis by name, <ref to="Job 1:1; Sir 45:1">whose memory is held in benediction</ref>, inasmuch as God, <ref to="Ps 20:4">graciously going before him with the blessings of his sweetness</ref>, both mercifully snatched him from the perils of the present life and abundantly filled him with the gifts of heavenly grace.
[2] For indeed, <ref to="Ps 61:10">though he was reared amid vanities in his youthful years among the vain sons of men</ref>, and, after some acquaintance with letters, was assigned to the lucrative business of trade, yet, with help from on high attending him, neither did he go astray after the wantonness of the flesh among the licentious youths—however given over to merriment he was—nor, among the covetous merchants, however bent upon gain, did he <ref to="Sir 31:8">place his hope in money and treasures</ref>.
[3] For there dwelt in the heart of the young Francis a certain generous compassion toward the poor, divinely kindled, <ref to="Job 31:18">which, growing up with him from his infancy</ref>, had so filled his heart with kindness that, being now no deaf hearer of the Gospel, <ref to="Luke 6:30">he resolved to give to everyone who asked of him</ref>, most of all if anyone should plead the love of God.
[4] But once, while caught up in the bustle of his business, he had, contrary to his accustomed manner, sent away empty a certain poor man who was begging alms for the love of God; immediately, returning to his heart, he ran after the man, and, having graciously bestowed alms upon him, he promised the Lord God that never from that time, while the possibility was at hand, would he refuse those who asked of him for the love of the Lord;
[5] and observing this with unwearied devotion until death, he merited bountiful increases of love and grace toward God.
[6] For afterward he used to say, <ref to="Gal 3:27">when he had now perfectly put on Christ</ref>, that even while he was still in secular dress he could scarcely ever hear a voice expressive of divine love without his heart being moved.
[7] Moreover, the gentleness of his mildness together with the elegance of his manners, his patience and tractability beyond the human measure, the largeness of his generosity beyond the means of his resources—qualities by which the youth of good character was seen to flourish with sure signs—
[8] seemed to be certain preludes that a more copious abundance of divine blessing was thereafter to be poured out upon him.
[9] Indeed a certain man of Assisi, very simple, as is believed, taught by God, when at one time he met Francis going through the city, would lay down his cloak,
[10] <ref to="Luke 19:36">spreading his garment beneath his feet</ref>, declaring that Francis would be worthy of all reverence, as one who was soon to accomplish great things and on that account to be magnificently honored by the whole company of the faithful.
</p>

<p id="cap1-2">
[1] <ref to="Job 15:8"> But as yet Francis knew not the counsel of God concerning him </ref>, for, drawn outward by his father's bidding as much as he was pressed downward by the corruption of his natural origin, he had not yet learned to contemplate things heavenly nor grown accustomed to taste of things divine. [2] And because <ref to="Isa 28:19"> the affliction laid upon a man gives understanding to the spiritual hearing </ref>, <ref to="Ezek 1:3">the hand of the Lord came upon him </ref><ref to="Ps 76:11"> and the change wrought by the right hand of the Most High </ref>, afflicting his body with lingering illness, that it might fit his soul for the anointing of the Holy Spirit. [3] And when, his bodily strength restored, he had after his wonted manner made ready for himself becoming garments, he chanced to meet a certain knight, of noble birth indeed, yet poor and meanly clad, [4] whose poverty he pitied with tender affection; and at once, stripping himself, he clothed the man, that in a single deed he might fulfill a twofold office of compassion, both covering the shame of the noble knight and relieving the want of the poor man.
</p>

<p id="cap1-3">
[1] On the night that followed, when he had given himself over to sleep, the divine mercy showed him a fair and spacious palace filled with soldierly arms emblazoned with the sign of Christ's cross, that it might foreshow to him how the kindness he had displayed toward a poor man for the love of the most high King was to be repaid with a recompense beyond all comparison. [2] And so, when he asked whose these things might be, the answer came by an assurance from above that all of them would be his and his soldiers'. [3] Waking therefore in the morning, since he did not yet have a mind trained to search out divine mysteries, nor knew how to pass through the appearances of visible things to behold the truth of things invisible, he reckoned that the unwonted vision was a token of great prosperity to come. [4] And so, still ignorant of the divine purpose, he resolved to betake himself into Apulia to a certain generous count, hoping in his service to win the honor of knighthood, as the vision shown to him seemed to promise. [5] And when, having set out a little later, he had come as far as the nearest city, he heard in the night the Lord addressing him in familiar speech, saying: "Francis, who can do better for you, the master or the servant, the rich man or the poor?" [6] And when Francis had answered that the master, no less than the rich man, can do better, the Lord at once pressed him: "Why then do you forsake the Master for the servant, and for a poor man God who is rich?" [7] And Francis: "What do you wish me to do, Lord?" <ref to="Acts 9:6">And the Lord said to him</ref><ref to="Gen 32:9">: "Return into your own land</ref>, for the vision you have seen prefigures a spiritual outcome, to be brought to fulfillment in you not by human but by divine disposing." [8] <ref to="John 21:4">And so, when morning had come</ref>, he turned back in haste toward Assisi, secure and rejoicing, and now, made a pattern of obedience, he awaited the will of the Lord.
</p>

<p id="cap1-4">
[1] From that time on, withdrawing himself from the tumult of public commerce, he devoutly entreated the clemency of heaven to deign to show him what he ought henceforth to do. [2] But as, through the frequent practice of prayer, the flame of heavenly longing grew mightily within him, <ref to="Song 8:7">and now, for love of the homeland above, he despised all earthly things as though they were nothing</ref>, he felt that he had discovered a hidden treasure, [3] <ref to="Matt 13:44-46">and, like a prudent merchant who has found a pearl, he resolved to acquire it by selling all that he had</ref>. [4] Yet still he did not know how he might accomplish this, save that it was suggested to his spirit that spiritual trafficking takes its beginning from contempt of the world, and that the warfare of Christ must be begun with the victory over oneself.
</p>

<p id="cap1-5">
[1] One day, then, as he was riding across the plain that lies below the city of Assisi, he came upon a certain leper, whose unexpected appearance struck him with no small horror. [2] But recalling his resolve toward the perfection already conceived in his mind, and remembering that he must first conquer himself <ref to="2 Tim 2:3">if he wished to become a soldier of Christ</ref>, he leapt down from his horse and hastened to kiss the man. [3] <ref to="Esth 8:4; Acts 3:5">And when the leper stretched out his hand as though to receive something</ref>, <ref to="Gen 43:21">he handed over money together with a kiss</ref>. [4] Then at once mounting his horse and turning himself about on every side, though the field lay open and unobstructed all around, he could not see the leper anywhere. [5] Filled therefore with wonder and joy, he began devoutly to sing praises to the Lord, resolving from this moment ever to ascend toward greater things. [6] Thereafter he sought out solitary places, well suited to sorrows, <ref to="Rom 8:26">in which, while he ceaselessly applied himself with unutterable groanings</ref>, after a long and persistent course of prayer he was found worthy to be heard by the Lord. [7] For one day, while he was praying in such seclusion and was wholly absorbed in God through the very excess of his fervor, Christ Jesus appeared to him as though fastened to the cross. [8] <ref to="Song 5:6">At the sight of him his soul melted within him</ref>, and the memory of Christ's passion was so deeply imprinted upon the very marrow of his heart that, from that hour onward, whenever Christ's crucifixion came to mind, he could scarcely outwardly restrain himself from tears and groans, as he himself afterward told in familiar confidence as he drew near to his end. [9] By this indeed the man of God understood that the words of the Gospel were spoken to him: If you wish to come after me, <ref to="Matt 16:24">deny yourself and take up your cross and follow me</ref>!
</p>

<p id="cap1-6">
[1] From that time he put on the spirit of poverty, the sense of humility, and the affection of an inward devotion. [2] For although before he had recoiled with vehement loathing not only from the company of lepers but even from the distant sight of them, now, for the sake of Christ crucified, <ref to="Isa 53:4">who, according to the prophet's word, appeared contemptible as a leper,</ref> in order that he might wholly despise himself, he rendered to the lepers the services of humility and humane kindness with merciful generosity. [3] He often visited their dwellings, freely bestowed alms upon them, and with deep feeling of compassion kissed their hands and their faces. [4] To the poor who begged he longed to give not only what was his but his very self, at times stripping off his garments, at times unstitching them, at times tearing them to bestow upon them, when he had nothing else at hand. [5] He also came to the aid of poor priests reverently and devoutly, especially in the furnishings of the altar, that he might both share in divine worship and supply the needs of those who served it. [6] Now when at that time he was visiting with religious devotion the threshold of the Apostle Peter, and beheld a throng of poor folk before the doors of the church, drawn partly by the sweetness of his piety and partly enticed by the love of poverty, he gave his own clothes to one of them who was more needy than the rest, [7] and, covered with that man's tattered garments, he passed that day in the midst of the poor with an unaccustomed gladness of spirit, that he might both spurn worldly glory and, ascending step by step, attain to evangelical perfection. [8] He kept watch all the more attentively over the mortification of his flesh, so that he might also bear outwardly upon his body the cross of Christ, which he bore within his heart. Yet all these things the man of God Francis did while not yet set apart from the world either in habit or in manner of life.
</p>

## Chapter II - Concerning His Perfect Conversion to God and the Restoration of Three Churches <a id="cap2"></a>

<p id="cap2-1">
[1] Now since the servant of the Most High had no teacher in matters of this kind save Christ alone, His clemency yet again deigned to visit him with the sweetness of grace. [2] For one day, having <ref to="Gen 24:63">gone out to meditate in the field</ref>, as he was walking near the church of Saint Damian—which by reason of its great age threatened to fall into ruin—and had entered it for the sake of prayer, urged on by the spirit, he prostrated himself before the image of the Crucified and was filled in his prayer with no small consolation of spirit. [3] And while with tearful eyes he gazed intently upon the Lord's cross, with his bodily ears he heard <ref to="1 Pet 1:17">a voice descending from that very cross</ref>, saying to him three times: "Francis, go and repair my house, which, as you see, is wholly falling to ruin!" [4] Trembling, since he was alone in the church, Francis stood amazed at the sound of so wondrous a voice, and perceiving in his heart the power of the divine utterance, he was carried beyond himself in an ecstasy of mind. [5] Coming at last to himself, he prepared to obey and gathered all his strength to fulfill the command concerning the repair of the material church—although the principal intention of the word was directed toward that Church <ref to="Acts 20:28">which Christ purchased with His own blood</ref>, as the Holy Spirit afterward taught him, and as he himself later revealed to the brothers. [6] He therefore arose, fortifying himself with the sign of the cross, and taking up some cloth he had for sale, he hastened to the city called Foligno; and there, having sold what he had brought, this happy merchant left behind even the horse on which he had been riding, taking up its price as well. [7] Returning to Assisi, he reverently entered the church for whose repair he had received the command, and finding there a poor little priest, he showed him fitting reverence, offered the money for the repair of the church and for the use of the poor, and humbly asked that he might be allowed to remain with him for a time. [8] The priest consented to his staying, but for fear of his parents would not accept the money; whereupon this true despiser of riches, casting it onto a certain window, held it of as little worth as dust thrown away.
</p>

<p id="cap2-2">
[1] <ref to="Matt 25:5">While the servant of God lingered</ref> with the aforesaid priest, his father, learning of it, was deeply troubled in mind and hastened to the place. [2] But the young man, being a fresh champion of Christ, when he heard the threats of his pursuers and sensed their coming, wished <ref to="Rom 12:19">to give place to wrath</ref> and hid himself in a certain secret pit. [3] Lying concealed there for several days, drenched in a shower of tears, he begged the Lord without ceasing <ref to="Ps 30:16; Ps 108:31">to deliver his soul from the hands of his pursuers</ref>, and to fulfill with kindly favor the holy vows He had inspired. [4] Then, filled with a certain overflowing gladness, he began to reproach himself for the cowardice of his faintheartedness; and, leaving the pit and casting off his dread, he set out upon the road toward the city of Assisi. [5] When the townsfolk caught sight of him, squalid of face and changed in mind, and on this account thought him out of his senses, they pelted him with <ref to="2 Kgs 22:43">the mud of the streets</ref> and with stones, and assailed him with clamorous shouts as though he were a madman bereft of reason. [6] But the servant of the Lord, neither broken nor altered by any injury, passed through it all as one deaf. [7] And when his father heard this uproar, he ran up at once—not to free him, but rather to destroy him: with all compassion withdrawn, he dragged him home and tormented him, first with words, then with blows and chains. [8] Yet by this very thing he was rendered the readier and the stronger to carry out what he had begun, recalling that word of the Gospel: [9] Blessed are they who suffer persecution for justice' sake, <ref to="Matt 5:10">for theirs is the kingdom of heaven</ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap2-3">
[1] After a little while, however, when his father had departed from the homeland, his mother—not approving of her husband's deed, and having no hope that her son's unbending constancy might be softened—loosed him from his bonds and let him go free. [2] And he, giving thanks to the almighty Lord, returned to the place where he had been before. [3] But when the father came back and did not find him at home, he heaped reproaches upon his wife and, raging, ran to the place, so that, if he could not call his son back, he might at least drive him out of the province. [4] Francis, however, strengthened by God, of his own accord went out to meet his furious father, crying aloud in a fearless voice that he counted his bonds and blows as nothing, and moreover declaring that for the name of Christ he would gladly undergo every evil. [5] And so the father, seeing that he could not call him back, turned to extorting his money; [6] which, when at last he had found it in a certain little window-niche, his fury was somewhat assuaged, the thirst of his greed being in some measure tempered by the draught of money.
</p>

<p id="cap2-4">
[1] Then the father of his flesh, finding the son of grace already stripped of his money, sought to bring him before the bishop of the city, that into his hands he might renounce his paternal possessions and surrender all that he had. [2] To do this the true lover of poverty showed himself ready, and coming before the bishop he brooks no delay nor hesitates over anything, neither awaits nor speaks any words; but at once, having laid aside all his clothing, he gave it back to his father. [3] And then the man of God was found to be wearing a hair-shirt next to his flesh beneath his fine garments. [4] Moreover, drunk in spirit with a wondrous fervor, casting off even his undergarments, he stood wholly stripped before all, saying to his father: "Until now I called you my father on earth, but from this hour I can safely say: Our Father, <ref to="Matt 6:9">who art in heaven</ref>, in whom I have laid up all my treasure and placed all the trust of my hope." [5] Seeing this, and marveling at so surpassing a fervor in the man of God, the bishop straightway rose up, and gathering him into his arms with tears—for he was a pious and good man—he covered him with the cloak he himself was wearing, commanding his attendants to give him something to clothe the limbs of his body. [6] There was offered to him the poor and worthless mantle of a certain peasant in the bishop's service, which he received gladly, and with a piece of plaster that came to hand he marked it with his own hand in the shape of a cross, fashioning out of it a covering for a crucified and half-naked man of poverty. [7] Thus, then, the servant of the most high King was left naked, that he might follow naked the crucified Lord whom he loved; [8] thus indeed was he fortified by the cross, that he might entrust his soul to the wood of salvation, by which he might come forth safe from the shipwreck of the world.
</p>

<p id="cap2-5">
[1] Loosed thereafter from the bonds of worldly desires, the despiser of the world left the city behind and, secure and free, sought the seclusion of solitude, that alone and in silence he might hear the secret of heaven's address. [2] And while he was journeying through a certain wood, Francis, the man of God, was singing the praises of the Lord in the French tongue with rejoicing, when robbers rushed upon him from their hiding places. [3] When with savage temper they demanded who he was, the man of God, full of confidence, answered with a prophetic voice, saying: "I am the herald <ref to="Ps 47:3; Matt 27:4">of the great King</ref>." [4] But they, striking him, cast him into a pit filled with snow, saying: "Lie there, you peasant herald of God!" [5] Yet when they had withdrawn, he leapt out of the hollow, and gladdened by a great joy, he began with louder voice to sing the praises of the Creator of all things throughout the groves.
</p>

<p id="cap2-6">
[1] Coming to a certain neighboring monastery, he begged alms as a beggar and was received as one unknown and despised. [2] Going on from there, he came to Gubbio, where he was recognized and taken in by a former friend, and clothed in a poor little tunic as one of Christ's paupers. [3] From there, this lover of all humility betook himself to the lepers and dwelt among them, serving all of them most diligently for God's sake. [4] He washed their feet, bound up their sores, drew out the putrefaction of their wounds, and wiped away the pus; [5] he even kissed their ulcerous sores out of wondrous devotion, soon to become the physician of the Gospel. [6] On account of this he obtained from the Lord such great power that he gained a marvelous efficacy in wondrously cleansing diseases both spiritual and bodily. [7] I shall relate one instance among many, which occurred when the fame of the man of God afterward shone forth more widely. [8] For when a certain horrible disease was gnawing away by its devouring at both the mouth and jaw of a certain man from the county of Spoleto, and he could be helped by no remedy of medicine; [9] it happened that, returning from a pilgrimage—having visited the thresholds of the Apostles to implore the merits of the saints—he came upon the servant of God; [10] and when out of devotion he wished to kiss his footprints, the humble man, not bearing this, gave the kiss of his own mouth to the one who wished to kiss his feet. [11] But while Francis, the servant of the lepers, touched that horrible wound with his sacred mouth in wondrous tenderness, the whole disease was put to flight, and the sick man suddenly recovered the health he had longed for. [12] I do not know which of these is the more deserving of admiration: the depth of humility in so kindly a kiss, or the brilliance of power in so astounding a miracle.
</p>

<p id="cap2-7">
[1] Now grounded in the humility of Christ, Francis recalled the obedience laid upon him from the cross concerning the repair of the church of San Damiano, and like a true obedient son he returned to Assisi, that he might at least by begging comply with the divine voice. [2] Laying aside all shame for love of the poor Crucified, he begged among those among whom he had once been wont to abound, subjecting his frail body, worn down by fasting, to the burden of stones. [3] When therefore the aforesaid church had been restored, the Lord aiding him and the devotion of the citizens lending its help, lest after his labor his body should grow sluggish with idleness, he betook himself to repairing a certain church of the blessed Peter set some distance from the city, on account of the special devotion which, in the purity of sincere faith, he bore toward the prince of the Apostles.
</p>

<p id="cap2-8">
[1] When at last this church was completed, he came to the place called the Portiuncula, where a church of the most blessed Virgin, the Mother of God, had been built in ancient times, but which then lay deserted and uncared for by anyone. [2] When the man of God beheld it thus abandoned, out of the fervent devotion he bore toward the Lady of the world, he began to dwell there continually for its restoration. [3] And perceiving, in keeping with the very name of that church—for from of old it was called Saint Mary of the Angels—that angelic visitations were frequent there, he fixed his abode in that place out of reverence for the angels and his singular love for the Mother of Christ. [4] This place the holy man loved above all other places in the world; for here he humbly began, here he made virtuous progress, here he came to a blessed end, and at his death he commended it to the brothers as most dear to the Virgin. [5] Concerning this place a certain brother devoted to God had, before his conversion, seen a vision worthy of being recounted. [6] He beheld <ref to="Gen 19:11">countless men stricken with blindness</ref>, <ref to="2 Chr 6:13">their faces turned toward heaven and their knees bent</ref>, standing about the circuit of this church. [7] All of them, <ref to="2 Macc 14:34">with hands stretched out on</ref> high, <ref to="Ps 54:17">cried out tearfully to God</ref>, begging for mercy and for light. [8] And behold, a vast splendor came down from heaven, spreading itself over them all, which granted light to each one and bestowed the salvation they longed for. [9] This is the place in which the Order of Friars Minor was begun by Saint Francis through the prompting of divine revelation. [10] For by the bidding of that divine providence, by which the servant of Christ was directed in all things, he raised up three material churches before, in inaugurating the Order, he preached the Gospel, [11] so that he might not only ascend by an ordered progression from things sensible to things intelligible, from lesser to greater, but might also foreshadow mystically, in an outward and sensible work, what he was to accomplish in time to come. [12] For after the likeness of that threefold edifice restored under this holy man's leadership, according to the form, rule, and teaching of Christ given by him, the Church was to be renewed in a threefold manner, and the threefold army of those to be saved was to triumph—just as we now behold to have been fulfilled.

## Chapter III - On the Founding of the Order and the Approval of the Rule <a id="cap3"></a>

<p id="cap3-1">

<p id="cap4-1">
[1] Thereafter Francis, supported by grace from on high and by the authority of the pope, set out with great confidence toward the valley of Spoleto, <ref to="Acts 1:1">to do and to teach the Gospel of Christ</ref>. [2] And while along the way he was conferring with his companions—how they might sincerely keep the rule they had undertaken, <ref to="Luke 1:75">how they might walk in all holiness and righteousness before</ref> God, how they might make progress within themselves and be an example to others—the conversation was drawn out at length, and the hour slipped by. [3] And when at last they were wearied by the long exertion, they halted, hungry, in a certain solitary place. [4] Yet though there was no means whatever by which they might provide themselves with necessary food, at once the providence of God was at hand. [5] For suddenly a man appeared, bearing bread in his hand, which he gave to Christ's little poor ones, and just as suddenly he vanished, no one knowing whence he had come or whither he went. [6] Now the poor brothers, recognizing by this that heavenly protection accompanied the man of God in their company, were refreshed more by the gift of divine generosity than by the food of their own flesh. [6] On account of this, the holy man, seeing him perfectly converted from the restlessness of the world to the peace of Christ, called him Brother Pacificus. [7] This man, afterward advancing in all holiness, before he became Minister in France — for he was the first there to exercise the office of ministry — was granted to see once again the great Thau upon the brow of Francis, which, distinguished by a variety of colors, adorned his face with a wondrous beauty. [8] This sign indeed the holy man venerated with great affection, commended in frequent discourse, [9] and inscribed with his own hand upon the little letters he sent, as though it were his whole endeavor to mark the Thau, <ref to="Ezek 9:4">according to the prophetic word, upon the foreheads of the men who groan and grieve</ref>, of those truly converted to Christ Jesus.
</p>

<p id="cap4-10">
[1] In the course of time, when the brothers had now multiplied, the watchful shepherd began to summon them to a general chapter at the hearth of Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, <ref to="Ps 77:54; Gen 41:52">that by the measuring-line of divine apportionment in the land of their poverty</ref> he might assign to each his share of obedience.
[2] There, although there was a want of all necessities, and the multitude of brothers sometimes exceeded five thousand, yet by the aid of divine mercy a sufficiency of sustenance was at hand, bodily health attended them, and spiritual gladness flowed abundantly.
[3] As for the provincial chapters, since he could not display his bodily presence, he was present in spirit through the anxious care of his governance, through the urgency of his prayer, and through the efficacy of his blessing\u2014though sometimes, by the wondrous power of God working, he appeared visibly.
[4] For while that distinguished preacher\u2014who now too is Christ's illustrious confessor, Anthony, preaching under the heading of the cross: Jesus of Nazareth, <ref to="John 19:19">king of the Jews</ref>\u2014was preaching to the brothers in the chapter at Arles, a certain brother of proven virtue, Monaldus by name, looking toward the door of the chapter at a divine prompting, beheld with his bodily eyes blessed Francis raised aloft in the air, his hands extended as on a cross, blessing the brothers.
[5] <ref to="Acts 9:31">All the brothers felt themselves filled with so great and so unaccustomed a consolation of spirit</ref> <ref to="John 1:7">that the Spirit bore certain witness within them of the holy father's true presence</ref>, though afterward this was established not only by clear signs but also by the outward attestation of the holy father's own words.
[6] It is surely to be believed that the almighty power of God, which granted Ambrose, the holy prelate, to be present at the burial of the glorious Martin, that he might honor the holy pontiff with a holy office,
[7] likewise presented His servant Francis at the preaching of His truthful herald Anthony, that he might give his approval to the words of truth, and above all to the cross of Christ, of which Francis was both the bearer and the minister.
</p>

<p id="cap4-11">
[1] When, the Order having now grown wide, he was arranging that the rule of life approved by the lord Innocent should be confirmed in perpetuity through his successor Honorius, he was admonished by God through a revelation of this kind. [2] It seemed to him that he had gathered from the ground the tiniest crumbs of bread, and that he must distribute them to the many famished brothers standing about him. [3] And when he was afraid to give out crumbs so fine, lest perchance they should slip from between his hands, a voice from above said to him: "Francis, make of all the crumbs a single host, and give it to those who wish to eat." [4] When he did this, whoever did not receive it devoutly, or, having received the gift, despised it, was soon infected with leprosy and stood out plainly to view. [5] In the morning the holy man recounted all these things to his companions, grieving <ref to="Dan 2:19">that he did not grasp the mystery of the vision</ref>. [3] But on the following day, <ref to="Tob 3:11">while he persevered watchful in prayer</ref>, he heard <ref to="2 Pet 1:17">a voice of this kind that came down from heaven</ref>: "Francis, the crumbs of last night are the words of the Gospel, the host is the rule, the leprosy is iniquity." [7] Wishing, therefore, to reduce the Rule—handed down at fuller length out of a gathering together of the words of the Gospel—into a more compendious form, to be then confirmed, according to what the vision shown to him had dictated, he climbed, with two companions and led by the Holy Spirit, onto a certain mountain, where, content with bread and water alone and fasting, he had it written down according to what the divine Spirit suggested to him as he prayed. [8] When, descending from the mountain, he had entrusted it to his vicar to be kept, and that man, after a few days had passed, declared it lost through carelessness, the holy man returned a second time to that place of solitude and at once restored it just as it had been before, as though he were receiving the words from the mouth of God; and through the aforesaid lord Pope Honorius, in the eighth year of his pontificate, he obtained, as he had desired, its confirmation. [9] Fervently urging the brothers to its observance, he used to say that he had put nothing there of his own contriving, but had caused everything to be written down just as it had been divinely revealed to him. [10] And that this might be established more surely by the testimony of God, after only a very few days had passed, <ref to="Gal 6:17">the stigmata of the Lord Jesus were impressed upon him</ref> <ref to="Rev 7:2">by the finger of the living God</ref>, as it were the seal of Christ the supreme Pontiff, for the complete confirmation of the rule and the commendation of its author, as will be described below in its proper place, after the account of his virtues.
</p>

## Chapter V - On the Austerity of His Life, and How Creatures Afforded Him Comfort <a id="cap5"></a>

<p id="cap5-1">
[1] Now when the man of God, Francis, saw that by his example a great many were being roused with fervent spirit to bear the cross of Christ, he too was stirred, like a good captain of Christ's army, to attain the palm of victory by scaling the heights of unconquered virtue. [2] For he gave heed to that word of the Apostle: <ref to="Gal 5:24">But they who are Christ's have crucified their flesh with its vices and concupiscences</ref>; and so that he might bear the armor of the cross upon his body, he so reined in his sensual appetites with rigorous discipline that he scarcely took even what was needful for the sustenance of nature. [3] For he used to say that it was a difficult thing to satisfy the body's necessity and yet not yield to the inclination of the senses. [4] On this account, even in times of health, he scarcely and only rarely allowed himself cooked food; and when he did allow it, he would either mix it with ashes, or, more often by adding water, render the savor of its seasoning tasteless. [5] And what shall I say of the drinking of wine, when even of water, while thirst burned within him, he scarcely drank his fill? [6] He devised ever more potent modes of abstinence, and grew daily by exercise; and although he had now reached the summit of perfection, yet, as though forever beginning anew, he would take up some fresh practice, chastising lust by the afflictions of the flesh. [7] But when he went abroad for the sake of the Gospel word, he would conform himself, in the kind of food he took, to those who received him; yet once returned to his own retirement, he strictly kept the rigid sparingness of his abstinence. [8] And thus, making himself austere toward himself, gracious toward his neighbor, and in all things subject to the Gospel of Christ, he offered an example of edification not only by abstaining but also by eating. [9] The bare ground was, more often than not, the bed for his wearied little body, and he frequently slept sitting up, with a piece of wood or a stone placed beneath his head; [10] clad in a single poor tunic, he served the Lord <ref to="2 Cor 11:27">in nakedness and in cold</ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap5-2">
[1] Asked once how he could shield himself from the harsh bite of winter's cold with so thin a garment, he answered in fervor of spirit: "If inwardly we were touched by the flame of our heavenly homeland through longing, we would easily bear that outward cold." [2] He shrank from softness in clothing and loved roughness, declaring that for this very reason John the Baptist had been praised by the lips of God. [3] But whenever he chanced to feel some smoothness in a tunic given to him, he would weave little cords into its inner side, for he used to say that softness of garments is to be sought not in the cottages of the poor but in the palaces of princes, according to the word of Truth. [4] For by sure experience he had learned that demons are terrified by roughness, while by what is delicate and soft they are spurred all the more boldly to tempt. [5] Hence, when one night, on account of an infirmity of his head and eyes, he had a pillow of feathers placed under his head contrary to his usual custom, a demon entered into it and harassed him in many ways until the hour of matins, disturbing him from the pursuit of holy prayer, until at last, calling his companion, he had the cushion together with its demon carried far away outside the cell. [6] But the brother, having gone out of the cell with the cushion, lost the strength and use of all his limbs, until, at the voice of the holy father who perceived this in spirit, the former vigor of heart and body was fully restored to him.
</p>

<p id="cap5-3">
[1] Rigorous in self-discipline, <ref to="Isa 21:8">he stood firm upon his watchtower </ref>, taking the utmost care to preserve the purity of both the inner and the outer man; [2] and therefore, in the early days of his conversion, during the winter season he would often plunge himself into a pit filled with ice, that he might both perfectly subdue the household enemy within him and guard the white garment of chastity from the fire of pleasure. [3] He used to declare that it was incomparably more bearable for a spiritual man to endure great cold in the flesh than to feel even the slightest ardor of carnal lust in the mind.
</p>

<p id="cap5-4">
[1] One night, while he was at the hermitage of Sarteano, given over to prayer in his cell, the ancient enemy called to him, saying three times: "Francis, Francis, Francis!"
[2] When Francis answered, asking what he wanted, that deceiver cunningly added: "There is no sinner in the world to whom God will not show mercy, <ref to="Ezek 33:9">if he shall be converted</ref>; but whoever shall destroy himself by harsh penance will find no mercy in eternity.
[3] <ref to="Gal 1:12; Sir 1:6">At once the man of God knew through revelation</ref> the deceit of the enemy, and how he had striven to recall him to lukewarmness.
[4] Indeed, the outcome that followed bore this out.
[5] For immediately after this, at the breathing of him <ref to="Job 41:12">whose breath kindles coals to burn</ref>, a grievous temptation of the flesh laid hold of him.
[6] When this lover of chastity sensed it coming, he cast off his garment and began to beat himself most violently with his cord, saying: "Come now, brother ass, thus it befits you to remain, thus to submit to the lash.
[7] The tunic belongs to religion; it bears the seal of holiness, and it is not lawful for one given to lust to steal it away. If you wish to go somewhere, <ref to="Isa 40:4; 1 Kgs 30:13">go!"</ref>
[8] Moreover, animated by a wondrous fervor of spirit, he opened his cell, <ref to="Mark 14:6">went out</ref> into the garden, and plunging his little body, now stripped bare, into the deep snow, he began to pack from it seven heaps with his full hands.
[9] Setting these before himself, he thus spoke to his outer man: "Behold," he said, "this larger one is your wife; those four are your two sons and two daughters; the remaining two are a manservant and a maidservant, whom you must keep to serve you.
[10] Hurry, then, and clothe them all, for they are dying of cold.
[11] But if their manifold care troubles you, <ref to="Matt 4:10">serve the one Lord with care!"</ref>
[12] Thereupon the tempter withdrew, vanquished, and the holy man returned to his cell in victory; for, while he shivered well and painfully outside, he so extinguished within the burning of lust that thereafter he felt no such thing at all.
[13] Now a certain brother, <ref to="1 Cor 7:5">who was then given over to prayer</ref>, beheld all these things, <ref to="Job 31:16">while the moon shone clear</ref>.
[14] When the man of God learned that this brother had seen these things in the night, he disclosed to him the course of the temptation and commanded that, as long as he himself should live, he should reveal what he had seen to no living person.
</p>

<p id="cap5-5">
[1] He taught that not only must the vices of the flesh be mortified and their enticements bridled, but also that the outward senses, through which death enters into the soul, must be guarded with the utmost vigilance. [2] He commanded that familiarity with women, conversation with them, and the very sight of them—which for many are the occasion of ruin—be shunned with anxious care, declaring that <ref to="Ezek 21:7">by such things the weak is broken and the strong spirit is often enfeebled</ref>. [3] For one who keeps company with them, he said—unless he be a man of the most proven virtue—to escape their contagion is as easy as, according to Scripture, <ref to="Prov 6:28">to walk in fire and not scorch the soles of one's feet</ref>. [4] Indeed he himself had so turned away his eyes <ref to="Ps 118:37">that they might not behold such vanity</ref>, that, as he once told a companion, he recognized scarcely any woman by her face. [5] For he did not think it safe to draw inward the images of those forms, which can either rekindle the smoldering ember of the subdued flesh or stain the brightness of a chaste mind. [6] He maintained also that conversation with a woman is a frivolous thing, save for confession alone or the briefest instruction, in the measure that befits salvation and accords with decency. [7] "What business," he said, "has a religious to transact with a woman, except when with devout petition she begs for holy penance or counsel for a better life? [8] From too great a sense of security the enemy is less guarded against, and the devil, if he can have so much as a single hair of his own upon a man, soon makes it grow into a beam."
</p>

<p id="cap5-6">
[1] He taught that idleness, the bilge-water of all wicked thoughts, must be shunned above all things, demonstrating by his own example that the rebellious and sluggish flesh must be tamed by unbroken discipline and fruitful labors. [2] Hence he would call his body Brother Ass, as one to be laden with toilsome burdens, beaten with frequent lashes, and sustained on mean fodder. [3] But if he saw anyone idle and wandering about, wishing to eat the labors of others, he judged that such a one should be named Brother Fly, since by doing no good himself, yet spoiling the good deeds of others, he renders himself vile and abominable to all. [4] On account of which he once said: "I wish my brothers to labor and to keep themselves occupied, lest, given over to idleness, they go wandering through forbidden things in heart or tongue." [5] For he desired the brothers to observe the silence of the Gospel, namely that at all times they should anxiously refrain from every idle word, <ref to="Matt 12:36">as those who must render an account of such things on the day of judgment</ref>. [6] And if he found any brother accustomed to empty words, he rebuked him sharply, [7] affirming that a modest taciturnity is the guardian of a pure heart and no slight virtue, <ref to="Prov 18:21">for the reason that death and life are said to be in the hands of the tongue</ref>, not so much by reason of taste as by reason of speech.
</p>

<p id="cap5-7">
[1] Yet although he urged the brothers with all his might toward an austere life, the severity of strictness did not please him <ref to="Col 3:12">when it does not clothe itself in the bowels of compassion </ref><ref to="Col 4:6"> nor is seasoned with the salt of discretion </ref>. [2] For when on a certain night one of the brothers, sorely tormented by hunger through excessive abstinence, could find no rest, the loving shepherd, perceiving that danger threatened his sheep, summoned the brother, set bread before him, and—to spare him any shame—himself began to eat first and gently invited him to take food. [3] The brother laid aside his embarrassment and took the food, rejoicing greatly that through the shepherd's prudent condescension he had both escaped harm to his body and received no small example of edification. [4] When morning came, the man of God called the brothers together as one, and recounting what had happened in the night, he added this with a thoughtful admonition: "Let it be charity, brothers, and not food, that serves you as an example." [5] He taught them, moreover, to follow discretion as the charioteer of the virtues—not the discretion that the flesh counsels, but that which Christ has taught, whose most sacred life is acknowledged to be the very pattern of perfection set forth for us.
</p>

<p id="cap5-8">
[1] And since no man, hemmed about by the frailty of the flesh, can follow so perfectly the crucified Lamb without spot as not to gather some defilement, he therefore laid it down as a sure teaching that those who keep watch over the perfection of life must cleanse themselves daily in the streams of tears. [2] For although he had already attained a wondrous purity of heart and body, yet he ceased not to cleanse continually the eyes of his mind with showers of tears, taking no account of the loss of his bodily sight. [3] For when from his unceasing weeping he had incurred a most grievous infirmity of the eyes, and his physician urged him to refrain from tears if he wished to escape the blindness of bodily vision, the holy man answered: [4] "Brother physician, the visitation of the eternal light must not be put off, even a little, for the love of that light which we hold in common with the flies; for it is not the spirit that has received the benefit of light for the flesh's sake, but the flesh for the spirit's sake." [5] He chose rather to lose the light of bodily vision than, by checking the devotion of his spirit, to hinder those tears by which the inner eye is cleansed, that it may be able to see God.
</p>

<p id="cap5-9">
[1] When at one time the physicians gave counsel, and the brothers urgently pressed him to permit relief to be brought to him through the remedy of cauterization, the man of God humbly consented, for he perceived that this measure was at once health-bringing and harsh.
[2] And so the surgeon was summoned and came, thrusting an iron instrument into the fire to perform the cauterization.
[3] But the servant of Christ, steadying a body already shaken with dread, began to address the fire as a friend, saying: "My brother fire, surpassing all other things in beauty, the Most High created you <ref to="Sir 1:8">strong, fair, and useful</ref>.
[4] <ref to="Gen 33:10">Be favorable to me in this hour</ref>, be gentle!
[5] I entreat the great Lord, <ref to="Ps 47:2">who created you</ref>, that he may temper your heat for me, so that I may be able to endure your gentle burning."
[6] When the prayer was finished, he made the sign of the cross over the iron instrument glowing white-hot, and thereafter stood his ground undaunted.
[7] The sizzling iron was sunk deep into the tender flesh, and the cauterization was drawn out from the ear all the way to the eyebrow.
[8] How much pain that fire inflicted the holy man himself made plain: "Praise," he said to the brothers, "the Most High, for truly I tell you, I felt neither the burning of the fire nor any pain of the flesh."
[9] And turning to the physician he said: "If the flesh is not well cauterized, apply it again!"
[10] The experienced physician, astonished at so mighty a strength of spirit in such frail flesh, extolled this divine miracle, saying: "I tell you, brothers, <ref to="Luke 5:26">I have seen wonders today</ref>."
[11] For because he had attained to such purity that flesh accorded with spirit and spirit with God in a marvelous harmony, it came to pass by divine ordinance <ref to="Wis 16:24">that the creature, serving its Maker</ref>, was wondrously subject to his will and command.
</p>

<p id="cap5-10">
[1] On another occasion, while the servant of God lay gravely ill at the hermitage of San Urbano, and feeling his nature failing he had asked for a cup of wine, the reply came that there was no wine to be had that might be given him; so he ordered water to be brought, and when it was brought he blessed it by making the sign of the cross. [2] At once what had been pure water became excellent wine, and what the poverty of that desert place could not supply, the purity of the holy man obtained. [3] Upon tasting it he so swiftly recovered his strength that the newness of the flavor and the renewal of his health, supernaturally renewing both the thing tasted and the one who tasted, confirmed by a twofold testimony <ref to="Col 3:8">the perfect stripping away of the old man within him and the putting on of the new</ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap5-11">
[1] Not only did the creation serve the servant of God at his bidding, but the providence of the Creator itself everywhere bent down to grant him his pleasure. [2] For when on a certain occasion, his body being weighed down by an onset of many infirmities, he had conceived a desire to hear some harmonious sound to rouse the gladness of his spirit, and the seemliness of propriety did not allow this to be accomplished through human service, the ministry of Angels came forth to fulfill the wish of the holy man. [3] For on a certain night, while he himself was keeping watch and meditating upon the Lord, <ref to="Sir 40:21">suddenly there sounded forth a lyre of wondrous harmony and most sweet melody</ref>. [4] No one was seen, <ref to="Ezek 10:3">but the very wavering of the sound, now here, now there, suggested the coming and going of the lyre-player</ref>. [5] <ref to="Job 34:14">With his spirit directed toward God</ref>, he so reveled in the sweetness of that mellifluous strain that he supposed he had exchanged this world for another. [6] Nor did this escape the friars who were close to him, who frequently perceived through sure tokens <ref to="Luke 1:68">that he was visited by the Lord with such surpassing and frequent consolations</ref> that he could not altogether conceal them.
</p>

<p id="cap5-12">
[1] At another time, as the man of God was making his way between Lombardy and the March of Treviso for the sake of preaching, traveling with a brother companion alongside the Po, the gloomy darkness of night came upon them. [2] And since the road lay open to great and many perils on account of the darkness, the river, and the marshes, his companion said to the holy man: "Pray, Father, that we may be delivered from the dangers that press upon us!" [3] To him the man of God answered with great confidence: <ref to="Luke 3:8">"God is able</ref>, if it please his sweetness, to scatter the gloom of darkness and bestow upon us the gift of light." [4] Scarcely had he finished speaking when, behold, so great a light at once began to shine round about them by power from on high that, though the night was elsewhere dark, they themselves by that clear light could see not only the road but indeed very many things all around. [6] Guided bodily by the leading of this light and strengthened in spirit, they came safe and sound, with divine hymns and praises, over no small stretch of road all the way to their lodging-place. [6] Consider, then, of how wondrous a purity and of how great a power this man must have been, at whose bidding fire tempers its own burning, water changes its savor, angelic melody offers solace, and divine light affords guidance—so that thus, the senses of the holy man being sanctified, the whole frame of the world is proven to serve him.
</p>

## Chapter VI - On his humility and obedience, and on the divine condescensions granted to him at his bidding <a id="cap6"></a>

<p id="cap6-1">
[1] Humility, the guardian and ornament of all the virtues, had filled the man of God with rich abundance. [2] In his own estimation he was nothing but a sinner, though in truth he was the mirror and splendor of holiness in every form. [3] Upon this foundation he strove to build himself up, <ref to="1 Cor 3:10">like a wise master-builder laying the foundation first</ref>, as he had learned from Christ. [4] He used to say that for this very reason the Son of God had descended from the height of the Father's bosom to our despicable lot, that the Lord and Master might teach humility by deed no less than by word. [5] Therefore he strove, as a disciple of Christ, to become worthless in his own eyes and in those of others, recalling what had been spoken by the supreme Master: That which is lofty among men <ref to="Luke 16:15">is an abomination in the sight of God</ref>. [6] And he was wont to repeat this saying: "As much as a man is in the eyes of God, so much is he and no more." [7] Judging it folly, accordingly, to be exalted by worldly favors, he rejoiced in reproaches and was saddened by praises. [3] He would rather hear blame of himself than praise, knowing that the one led him to amend himself, while the other drove him toward a fall. [9] And so often, when the people extolled in him the merits of holiness, he would charge some brother to do the opposite and to thrust into his ears words that disparaged him. [10] And when that brother, however unwilling, called him a boor and a hireling, ignorant and useless, he would answer, made joyful both in mind and in face: [11] <ref to="Num 6:24; Ps 127:5">"May the Lord bless you</ref>, dearest son, for you speak the truest things, and such words befit the son of Pietro Bernardone to hear."
</p>

<p id="cap6-2">
[1] That he might render himself contemptible to others, he did not spare his own blushes, but in his preaching, before all the people, would lay bare his own faults. [2] It happened once that, weighed down by illness, he had for the sake of recovering his health relaxed the strictness of his abstinence a little. [3] But when his bodily strength had somewhat returned, this true despiser of himself, moved to reproach his own flesh, said: "It is not fitting that the people should believe me a man of abstinence, while I on the contrary refresh myself with meat in secret." [4] Therefore, inflamed with the spirit of holy humility, he rose up; and having summoned the people in the public square of the city of Assisi, he entered the principal church in solemn procession with the many brothers he had brought with him; and with a rope tied about his neck, naked save for his drawers, he commanded that he be dragged before the eyes of all as far as the stone upon which evildoers who were to be punished were wont to be placed. [5] Mounting upon it, though he was sick with a quartan fever and feeble, in a season of bitter cold he preached with great vigor of soul, and declared to all who listened that he ought not to be honored as a spiritual man, but rather to be despised by everyone as a carnal man and a glutton. [6] And so those who had gathered, beholding so great a spectacle, were filled with wonder; and because they already knew his austerity, they were pierced with devout hearts and proclaimed such humility to be more to be marveled at than imitated. [7] <ref to="Isa 20:3">Yet though this may seem to have been rather a portent</ref> after the manner of a prophetic foretelling than an example, it nonetheless truly stood as a lesson in perfect humility, [8] by which the follower of Christ is taught that he must despise the heralding of fleeting praise, must also crush the arrogance of swelling boastfulness, and must refute the lie of fraudulent pretense.
</p>

<p id="cap6-3">
[1] Many things indeed he often did in this manner, so that outwardly he might become <ref to="Ps 30:13">like a vessel that is lost</ref>, while inwardly he possessed the spirit of sanctification. [2] He <ref to="Ps 26:13">strove to hide the good things of his Lord</ref> within the secret recesses of his breast, unwilling that what might be the occasion of ruin should lie open to glory. [3] For often, when he was called blessed by many, he would utter a <ref to="Gen 39:10">saying of this sort</ref>: "I might yet have sons and daughters; do not praise one who is still secure! [4] No one is to be praised whose end is uncertain." [5] Such words indeed he addressed to those who praised him; but to himself he spoke thus: "If the Most High had bestowed so much upon a thief, he would be more pleasing than you, Francis!" [6] He often said to the brothers: "For all that a sinner is able to do, no one ought to flatter himself with unjust applause. [7] A sinner," he said, "can fast, can pray, can weep, and can mortify his own flesh. [8] This alone he cannot do: namely, to be faithful to his Lord. [9] In this, therefore, are we to glory, if we render his glory back to the Lord (Sir 35,10; ioa 9,24), if, serving faithfully, we ascribe to him whatever he gives."
</p>

<p id="cap6-4">
[1] Now, that this merchant of the Gospel might gain by many means and forge the whole of present time into merit, he chose not so much to govern as to be governed, not so much to command as to obey. [2] And so, yielding up the office of minister general, he asked for a guardian, to whose will he might be subject in all things. [3] For he declared the fruit of holy obedience to be so abundant <ref to="Sir 51:34">that for those who bowed their necks beneath its yoke</ref> no moment of time passed without gain. [4] Hence he was accustomed always to promise and to keep obedience even to the brother with whom he was wont to travel. [5] He once said to his companions: "Among the other things which the divine mercy has graciously granted me, it has bestowed this grace, that I would obey as diligently a novice of a single hour, were he given me as my guardian, as I would the most senior and most discreet of brothers. [6] A subject," he said, "ought not to regard in his prelate the man, but Him for whose love he is subject. [7] And the more contemptible the one who presides, the more pleasing is the humility of him who obeys." [8] When once he was asked who ought truly to be judged obedient, he set forth as an example the likeness of a dead body. [9] "Take," he said, "a lifeless body, and place it wherever you please! [10] You will see that it does not resist being moved, does not murmur at where it is set, does not protest at being cast aside. [11] If it be enthroned in a chair, it will gaze not upward but downward; if it be clothed in purple, it grows doubly pale. [12] This," he said, "is the man truly obedient, who does not question why he is moved, who cares not where he is set, who does not press to be transferred; [13] raised to office, he keeps his accustomed humility; the more he is honored, the more he reckons himself unworthy."
</p>

<p id="cap6-5">
[1] He once said to his companion: "I do not think myself a Friar Minor unless I am in the state which I shall now describe to you.
[2] Behold, being a prelate of the brothers, I go to the chapter, I preach and admonish the brethren, and at the end it is said against me: You are not fit for us, because you are unlettered, halting of speech, <ref to="Acts 4:13">an unlearned and </ref> simple man;
[3] at last I am cast out with reproach, despised by all.
[4] I tell you, unless I hear these words with the same countenance, the same gladness of mind, and the same resolve of holiness, I am in no way a Friar Minor."
[5] And he would add: "In prelacy there is a fall, in praise a precipice, in the humility of a subject there is gain to the soul.
[6] Why then do we heed perils more than gains, since we have received this time for gain?"
[7] For this reason, then, Francis, as a model of humility, wished his brothers to be called Minors, and the prelates of his Order to be called ministers, both that he might use the words of the Gospel which he had promised to observe, and that from the very name his disciples might learn that they had come to the school of the humble Christ to learn humility.
[8] For Christ Jesus, the master of humility, in order to form his disciples to perfect humility, said: Whosoever among you will be made greater, let him be your minister, and whosoever among you will be first, <ref to="Matt 20:26-27">shall be your servant </ref>.
[9] But when the lord of Ostia—protector and chief promoter of the Order of Friars Minor, who afterward, just as that same holy man had foretold, was raised to the honor of the supreme pontificate and called Gregory the Ninth—asked him whether it would please him that his brothers be advanced to ecclesiastical dignities, he replied: "My lord, my brothers are therefore called Minors, that they may not presume to become greater.
[10] If you wish," he said, "that they bear fruit in the Church of God, keep them and preserve them in the state of their vocation, and by no means allow them to rise to ecclesiastical prelacies."
</p>

<p id="cap6-6">
[1] And because he prized humility, both in himself and in all his subordinates, above every honor, God, who loves the humble, judged him worthy of loftier heights—as a vision shown from heaven made plain to one of the brothers, a man of singular virtue and devotion. [2] For when he was in the company of the man of God, and was praying together with him in a certain deserted church with fervent affection, he was caught up in ecstasy and saw among the many thrones in heaven one more worthy than the rest, adorned with precious stones and gleaming with every glory. [3] Marveling within himself at the radiance of that exalted throne, he began to inquire with anxious thought who would be raised up to it. [4] <ref to="Acts 9:4"> In the midst of this he heard a voice saying to him </ref>: "This throne belonged to one of those who fell, and now it is reserved for the humble Francis." [5] At length the brother, returning to himself from the rapture of prayer, followed the blessed man as he went out, as was his custom. [6] And as they walked along the way, speaking together of God, that brother, not forgetful of his vision, shrewdly asked him what he thought of himself. [7] To him the humble servant of Christ replied: "I seem to myself the greatest of sinners." [8] When the brother answered to the contrary, that he could neither say nor believe this with a sound conscience, Francis added: "If Christ had pursued with such great mercy any man, however wicked, I am quite certain that he would be far more pleasing to God than I." [9] The brother was confirmed, by hearing of such wondrous humility, in the truth of the vision shown to him, recognizing—with the holy Gospel as witness—that to the excellence of glory, from which the proud man is cast out, the truly humble is exalted.
</p>

<p id="cap6-7">
[1] On another occasion, when he was praying in a certain deserted church in the province of Massa near Monte Casale, he understood by the spirit that sacred relics had remained there. [2] And since he beheld, not without sorrow, that they had now for a long time been cheated of their due honor, he commanded the brothers to carry them to the friary with reverence. [3] But when, called away by some matter, he had departed from them, the sons, forgetful of their father's command, neglected the merit of obedience. [4] Yet one day, when they wished to celebrate the sacred mysteries, having removed the upper covering of the altar, they discovered—not without astonishment—bones most beautiful and exceedingly fragrant, gazing upon relics that no human hand but the power of God had brought there. [5] Returning a little while afterward, the man devoted to God began carefully to inquire whether what he had commanded concerning the relics had been carried out. [6] But the brothers, humbly confessing the fault of their neglected obedience, earned pardon along with their penalty. [7] And the holy man said: <ref to="Ps 17:47">"Blessed be the Lord my God,</ref> who through himself has fulfilled what you ought to have done!" [8] Consider attentively the care of divine providence concerning our dust, and weigh how excellent in the eyes of God was the virtue of the humble Francis! [9] For God obeyed the prayers of the man whose commands a man had failed to obey.
</p>

<p id="cap6-8">
[1] On one occasion, having come to Imola, he approached the bishop of the city and humbly requested that, with his good pleasure, he might be permitted to summon the people to a sermon. [2] To him the bishop replied harshly: "It is enough, brother," he said, "that I preach to my own people." [3] The truly humble man bowed his head and went outside, and after a brief hour he returned within. [4] When the bishop, somewhat troubled, asked him what he could possibly wish to request a second time, he answered with a heart as humble as his voice: "My lord, if a father drives his son out by one door, the son must come back in by another." [5] Overcome by such humility, the bishop embraced him with a cheerful countenance, saying: "You and all your brothers shall henceforth preach throughout my diocese by my general license, for holy humility has earned it."
</p>

<p id="cap6-9">
[1] It happened that he once came to Arezzo, when the whole city, shaken by civil war, was threatened with imminent destruction. [2] Lodging in the suburb, he saw demons exulting above the city and inflaming the disturbed citizens to mutual slaughter. [3] In order to put to flight those seditious powers of the air, he sent ahead, as a herald, Brother Silvester, a man of dove-like simplicity, saying: "Go before the gate of the city and, on behalf of almighty God, command the demons in the power of obedience to depart at once." [4] The truly obedient man hastened to carry out his father's command, and <ref to="Ps 94:2">coming before the face of the Lord with praises,</ref> he began to cry out mightily before the gate of the city: "On behalf of almighty God and by the command of his servant Francis, depart far from here, all you demons!" [5] At once the city returned to peace, and all the citizens restored among themselves the laws of civic order with great tranquillity. [6] For once the raging pride of the demons—which had hemmed in that city as though with a siege—was driven out, the wisdom of the poor man, namely the humility of Francis, came upon it, restored peace, and saved the city. [7] For by the meritorious and lofty virtue of humble obedience he had obtained so commanding an authority over those rebellious and insolent spirits that he both crushed their ferocious insolence and drove back their importunate violence.
</p>

<p id="cap6-10">
[1] Indeed, the proud demons flee before the lofty virtues of the humble, save when at times divine mercy permits them to buffet the humble for the safeguarding of their humility, <ref to="2 Cor 12:7">as the Apostle Paul writes of himself</ref>, and as Francis proved by experience. [2] For when he was asked by the lord Leo, cardinal of the Holy Cross, to abide with him a little while in the City, he humbly consented out of reverence and love for the man. [3] On the very first night, then, when after prayer he wished to take his rest, the demons came upon him, rising savagely against the soldier of Christ. [4] And after they had long and harshly beaten him, <ref to="Luke 10:30">at last they left him half-dead</ref>. [5] When they had departed, his companion, being summoned, came to him; and when the man of God had recounted to him what had happened, he added these words: "I believe, brother, that the demons—who can do nothing except insofar as the providence on high disposes—have now assailed me so ferociously for this reason: that my dwelling in the court of the great makes a poor showing. [6] My brothers, who abide in lowly little places, hearing that I am among cardinals, will perhaps suspect that I am entangled in worldly affairs, <ref to="Luke 7:25">puffed up with honors and abounding in delights</ref>. [7] Therefore I judge it better that he <ref to="Job 17:6">who is set forth as an example</ref> should flee the courts and dwell humbly among the humble in humble places, so that, bearing want himself, he may make others strong to bear the like." [8] <ref to="Mark 16:2">So they came in the morning</ref>, and, offering a humble excuse, they bade the cardinal farewell.
</p>

<p id="cap6-11">
[1] The holy man indeed loathed pride, the origin of all evils, and disobedience, that worst offspring of pride; yet he no less embraced the humility of penance. [2] It happened once that a certain brother was brought before him who had done something contrary to the law of obedience, and who was to be corrected by the discipline of justice. [3] But when the man of God saw, by clear signs, that this brother was truly stricken with remorse, he was moved by love of humility to grant him pardon. [4] Lest, however, the readiness of forgiveness should become an incitement to others to transgress, he ordered that the cowl be taken from the brother and cast into the midst of the flames, so that all might perceive with how great and what kind of penalty the offense of disobedience must be struck. [5] And when the cowl had remained for a while in the midst of the fire, he commanded that it be drawn out of the flames and restored to the humbly penitent brother. [6] Wonderful to tell! The cowl was drawn out from the midst of the flames, bearing not the least trace of burning. [7] And thus it came to pass that by this single miracle God commended both the holiness of the man and the humility of penance. [8] Worthily, therefore, is the humility of Francis to be followed, which obtained on earth so marvelous a dignity that it bent God to his desire and changed the disposition of man, that at his command it drove off the insolence of demons and by a mere nod restrained the voracity of the flames. [9] Truly this is the humility which, exalting those who possess it, while it shows reverence to all, earns honor from all.
</p>

## Chapter VII - On the Love of Poverty and the Wondrous Supplying of His Needs <a id="cap7"></a>

<p id="cap7-1">
[1] Among the other gifts of grace that Francis received from the bounteous Giver, he merited by a certain special prerogative to grow rich in the riches of simplicity through his love of the loftiest poverty. [2] The holy man, observing that this poverty had been the close companion of the Son of God and was now spurned, as it were, by the whole world, strove <ref to="Jer 31:3">to wed her with everlasting love</ref>, so that <ref to="Gen 2:24; Mark 10:7">not only for her sake did he leave father and mother</ref>, but he even scattered abroad all that he could possess. [3] No one was so greedy for gold as he was for poverty, nor was anyone more anxious in guarding a treasure than he was in guarding this gospel pearl. [4] In this above all was his gaze offended, if he should see anything among the brothers that did not in every respect accord with poverty. [5] Indeed, from the beginning of his religious life until his death he was rich in a tunic, a little cord, and breeches, and with these he was content. [6] He frequently, and with tears, called to mind the poverty of Christ Jesus and of his Mother; [7] and thence he declared this to be the queen of the virtues, <ref to="1 Tim 6:15">because in the King of kings</ref> and in his royal Mother it had shone forth so preeminently. [8] For when the brothers, gathered in private, asked which virtue would render one a greater friend of Christ, he replied, as though laying open the secret of his heart: [9] "Know, brothers, that poverty is the special way of salvation, being as it were the nurse of humility and the root of perfection, whose fruit is manifold, though hidden. [10] <ref to="Matt 13:44">For this is the treasure hidden in the gospel field</ref>, for the purchase of which all things must be sold, and whatever cannot be sold must, in comparison with it, be despised."
</p>

<p id="cap7-2">
[1] "Whoever desires to reach the summit of this virtue," he said, "must in some fashion renounce not only worldly prudence but even skill in letters, so that, dispossessed of such a holding, he may <ref to="Ps 70:16">enter into the powers of the Lord</ref> and offer himself naked into the arms of the Crucified. [2] For he by no means renounces the world perfectly who keeps the purse of his own judgment hidden within the secret places of his heart." [3] Often, when he discoursed upon poverty, he pressed upon the brothers that saying of the Gospel: "The foxes have their dens and the birds of heaven their nests, <ref to="Matt 8:20; Luke 9:58">but the Son of Man has nowhere to lay his head</ref>." [4] On this account he taught the brothers that, after the manner of the poor, they should raise up little poor dwellings, which they were to inhabit not as their own, <ref to="1 Pet 2:11">but as pilgrims and strangers</ref> in another's house. [5] For he said it is the <ref to="Exod 12:49">law of pilgrims</ref> to be gathered beneath another's roof, to thirst for their homeland, and to pass through in peace. [6] Sometimes he ordered houses already built to be torn down, or the brothers to be removed from them, if he perceived in them anything that, by reason of ownership or lavishness, was contrary to evangelical poverty. [7] He called this poverty the foundation of his Order, upon which, laid down first of all, the whole structure of the religious life so rests that by its firmness it is made firm, and by its overthrow it is utterly overthrown.
</p>

<p id="cap7-3">
[1] He taught, moreover, as he had learned by revelation, that the entrance into holy religion ought to be begun from that word of the Gospel: If thou wilt be perfect, go, sell all that thou hast, <ref to="Matt 19:21">and give to the poor</ref>;[2] and so he admitted to the Order none but those who had stripped themselves of all things and retained nothing whatsoever, both because of the word of the holy Gospel, and lest reserved purses should become an occasion of scandal.[3] Hence in the March of Ancona, to one who begged to be received into the Order, the true patriarch of the poor replied: "If thou wouldst be joined to the poor of Christ, distribute thy goods to the poor of the world!"[4] Hearing this, the man went off, and, drawn by carnal affection, left his goods to his own kin, and nothing to the poor.[5] But when the holy man, hearing this from the fellow's own account, learned of it, he struck him with a harsh rebuke, saying: "Go thy way, brother fly, <ref to="Gen 12:1">for thou hast not yet departed from thy house and thy kindred</ref>.[6] Thou hast given thy goods to thy blood relations and defrauded the poor; thou art not worthy of the holy poor.[7] Thou hast begun from the flesh, and laid a crumbling foundation for a spiritual edifice."[8] <ref to="1 Cor 2:14">The natural man returned</ref> to his own people and took back his goods, and, being unwilling to leave them to the poor, he soon abandoned his resolve of virtue.
</p>

<p id="cap7-4">
[1] On another occasion, when there was such want at the place of Saint Mary of the Portiuncula that the brothers arriving as guests could not be provided for as their need required, his vicar approached the man of God, [2] setting forth the destitution of the brothers and asking that it might be permitted to keep back certain goods of the novices who entered, <ref to="Ps 144:15">so that the brothers might have recourse to spending them in time of need</ref>. [3] To him the man, not unschooled in counsel from on high, replied: "Far be it from us, dearest brother, that for any man's sake we should act impiously against the Rule. [4] I would rather strip bare the altar of the glorious Virgin, when need demands it, than attempt anything, even the least thing, against the vow of poverty and the observance of the Gospel. [5] For the blessed Virgin will hold it more pleasing that her altar be uncovered, the counsel of the holy Gospel being perfectly kept, than that, with the altar adorned, the promised counsel of her Son be passed over."
</p>

<p id="cap7-5">
[1] As the man of God was once passing through Apulia near Bari with a companion, he found upon the road a great purse, swollen as though it were full of coins — the kind commonly called a money-bag. [2] The poor man of Christ is urged by his companion and pressed insistently to lift the purse from the ground and distribute the money to the poor. [3] But the man of God refused, declaring that what had been found in the purse was a contrivance of the devil, and that the brother was counseling not a deed of merit but of sin — namely, to filch and give away what belonged to another. [4] They departed from the place and hastened to complete the journey they had begun. [5] Yet the brother did not yet rest, deluded by an empty piety, vexing the man of God as though he cared nothing for relieving the want of the poor. [6] At last the gentle man consented to return to the place — not to carry out the brother's wish, but to lay bare the devil's deceit. [7] So, having gone back to the money-bag with the brother and a certain young man who happened to be on the road, he first offered a prayer and then bade his companion lift it up. [8] The brother, trembling, stood aghast, already sensing some diabolical portent; yet, for the sake of the command of holy obedience, driving the doubt from his heart, he stretched out his hand to the purse. [9] And behold, a serpent of no small size leaped out from the bag, and together with the bag itself suddenly vanished, revealing to the brother the deception of the devil. [10] Thus, with the cunning falsehood of the enemy laid bare, the holy man said to his companion: "Money, O brother, is to the servants of God nothing other than the devil and a venomous snake."
</p>

<p id="cap7-6">
[1] After this a marvelous thing befell the holy man, while he was making his way to the city of Siena as some matter required. [2] On a certain broad plain between Campiglia and San Quirico, three poor little women, alike in every respect—in stature, in age, and in countenance—met him, offering him a strange little gift of greeting: "Welcome," they said, "Lady Poverty!" [3] Hearing this, the true lover of poverty was filled with ineffable joy, since there was nothing in himself that he would so gladly have men greet as that which they had chosen to acclaim. [4] When they suddenly vanished, his companions, the friars, weighing the wondrous novelty in them—their likeness, their greeting, their encounter, and their disappearance—judged, not without reason, that something mystical was signified concerning the holy man. [5] Indeed, through those three poor little women, as it seemed—meeting him with so uniform a countenance, leaping about so unusually, vanishing so suddenly—the beauty of evangelical perfection, that is, in chastity, obedience, and poverty, is fittingly enough shown to have shone forth perfectly in the man of God in equal measure, though he chose to glory above all in the privilege of poverty, which he was wont to call now his mother, now his bride, <ref to="Matt 12:50; 2 Cor 11:2">now his lady.</ref> [6] In this he longed to surpass all others, he who had learned from poverty itself to reckon himself the least of all. [7] If ever, therefore, he perceived someone poorer than himself in outward dress, he at once reproached himself and roused himself to do the like, as though, contending in rivalry of poverty, he feared to be outdone by that man. [8] For it happened that he met a certain poor man on the road, and when he beheld his nakedness, pierced to the heart, he said to his companion in a mournful voice: [9] "This man's want has brought us great shame, for we have chosen poverty in place of great riches, and behold, it shines more brightly in him."
</p>

<p id="cap7-7">
[1] For love of holy poverty the servant of Almighty God made use, far more gladly, of alms begged from door to door than of those freely offered. [2] For whenever he was invited by persons of rank and was to be honored at their more lavish tables, he would first go through the neighboring houses round about, begging for fragments of bread, and then, thus enriched by want, he would take his place at the meal. [3] And when on one occasion he had done this, having been invited by the Lord of Ostia, who embraced the poor man of Christ with a singular affection, the bishop complained that he had slighted his honor, seeing that he, who was to dine in his lodging, had gone out begging for alms; whereupon the servant of God replied: "Great honor, my lord, have I shown you, in that I have honored a greater Lord. [4] For poverty is pleasing to the Lord, and that above all which is a begging undertaken willingly for Christ. [5] This royal dignity, which the Lord Jesus, made needy for our sake, <ref to="2 Cor 8:9; Matt 5:3">took upon himself, that by his want he might enrich us and might establish us, truly poor in spirit, as kings and heirs of the kingdom of heaven</ref>, I will not forsake for the fief of false riches granted to you but for an hour."
</p>

<p id="cap7-8">
[1] Now and again, when urging the brothers to go and beg for alms, he would use words such as these:
[2] "Go," <ref to="1 John 2:18">he said, "for in this last hour</ref> the Friars Minor have been lent to the world, that in them the elect may fulfill that for which they shall be commended by the Judge, hearing that most sweet word: As long as you did it to one of these my least brothers, <ref to="Matt 25:40">you did it to me</ref>."
[3] He therefore called it a delight to beg under the title of Friars Minor, a title which the Master of evangelical truth set forth with His own lips so pointedly in the recompense of the just.
[4] On the chief feast days too, where occasion offered, he was wont to beg, saying that in the holy poor that prophetic saying was fulfilled: <ref to="Ps 77:25">Man ate the bread of Angels</ref>.
[5] For he said that bread to be truly angelic which holy poverty gathers from door to door, sought for the love of God and, at the prompting of the blessed Angels, bestowed for love of Him.
</p>

<p id="cap7-9">
[1] Hence, when one Easter Day he was staying in a certain hermitage so far removed from any human dwelling that he could not conveniently go out to beg, <ref to="Luke 24:13">mindful of him who on that very day appeared in the guise of a pilgrim to the disciples journeying to Emmaus</ref>, he sought alms from his own brethren as though a pilgrim and a poor man. [2] And when he had humbly received it, he instructed them with sacred discourse, that, <ref to="1 Pet 2:11">passing through the desert of this world as pilgrims and strangers</ref> and as true Hebrews, they should continually celebrate the Lord's Pasch in poverty of spirit—<ref to="John 13:1">that is, the passing from this world to the Father</ref>. [3] And since in the seeking of alms he was moved not by any greed for gain but by liberty of spirit, God, the <ref to="Job 29:16">Father of the poor</ref>, seemed to take a special care of him.
</p>

<p id="cap7-10">
[1] It happened once that the servant of the Lord, weighed down by sickness, was being carried back from the place of Nocera to Assisi by solemn envoys, dispatched for this purpose through the devotion of the people of Assisi. [2] As they conducted Christ's servant along, they came to a certain poor little hamlet called Satriano, where, when hunger and the hour demanded food, they went out and, finding nothing for sale, returned empty-handed. [3] To them the holy man said: "You found nothing for this reason, <ref to="Matt 27:43">because you trust more in your flies than in God</ref>." [4] For he called coins "flies." [5] "But go back," he said, "through the houses you have already visited, and offering the love of God in place of payment, humbly beg for alms! [6] And do not, by some false reckoning, think this shameful or base, for after sin that great Almsgiver granted all things as alms, with overflowing tenderness, to the worthy and the unworthy alike." [7] The soldiers laid aside their embarrassment and, asking willingly for alms, purchased more for the love of God than they could have for coins. [8] Indeed, pricked in their hearts by a divine prompting, the poor inhabitants offered not only their possessions but their very selves with open generosity. [9] And so it came to pass that the want which money could not relieve was made good by Francis's opulent poverty.
</p>

<p id="cap7-11">
[1] During the time when, lying ill, he dwelt in a hermitage near Rieti, a certain physician attended upon him with timely care. [2] But since this poor man of Christ had no means to repay the worthy wages of such labor, the most generous God, lest He should send the physician away without a present reward, recompensed his devoted service on the poor man's behalf with this single benefit. [3] For this physician's house, which he had at that very time newly built from all his earnings, was so threatened with imminent collapse, owing to a <ref to="Matt 27:51">gaping crack in its walls running from top to bottom</ref>, that it seemed impossible by any skill of human craft to forestall its fall. [4] He, however, trusting wholly in the merits of the holy man, with the devotion of a great faith asked his companions to grant him something that this same man of God had handled with his own hands. [5] And so, having obtained, after much insistent prayer, a small portion of his hair, he placed it one evening within the crack of the wall; and rising in the morning, he found that opening so firmly closed up that he could neither draw out the relics set there, <ref to="Wis 5:10">nor find any trace of the former crack</ref>; [6] and so it came to pass that he who had diligently ministered to the frail and ruinous little body of the servant of God was guarded against the peril of his own house's ruin.
</p>

<p id="cap7-12">
[1] At another time, the man of God, wishing to withdraw to a certain hermitage that he might there devote himself more freely to contemplation, was being carried—since he was frail—upon the donkey of a certain poor man.
[2] <ref to="Josh 2:16">And when, in the days of summer, that man climbed the mountains in following Christ's servant</ref>, <ref to="John 4:6">wearied by the journey along a rougher and longer road</ref> and failing from the excessive burning of thirst, he began to cry out urgently after the saint: "See," he said, "I am dying of thirst, unless I am at once revived by the benefit of some draught."
[3] Without delay the man of God leapt down from the donkey, and, fixing his knees upon the ground, stretched out his palms to heaven, <ref to="2 Chr 6:13; Col 1:9">not ceasing to pray </ref> until he understood that he had been heard.
[4] When at last his prayer was finished, he said to the man: "Hasten to the rock, and there you will find living water, <ref to="Isa 48">which Christ has mercifully drawn forth from the stone for you to drink at this very hour</ref>."
[5] O the astonishing condescension of God, who bends himself so readily to his servants!
[6] <ref to="Ps 77:16; Deut 32:13">The thirsting man drank water from the rock by the power of the one praying, and drew his draught from the hardest stone</ref>.
[7] <ref to="Ps 1:3">No running of waters</ref> had been there before, nor, though it was diligently sought, could any be found there afterward.
</p>

<p id="cap7-13">
[1] How Christ, through the merits of His poor servant, multiplied food upon the sea must be noted below in its proper place; here let it suffice to recall only this: that from the slender alms bestowed upon him he delivered the sailors from the peril of famine and death for very many days, [2] <ref to="Ps 77:16">so that from this it may plainly be perceived that the servant of Almighty God, just as in drawing forth water from the rock</ref> he proved a match for Moses, so in the multiplication of provisions he proved a match for Elisha. [3] Let every distrust, therefore, depart far from the poor of Christ. [4] For if the poverty of Francis was of such abundant sufficiency that it could supply the wants of those who came to his aid with so wondrous a power that neither food nor drink nor shelter was lacking, even when the resources of money, of craft, and of nature had failed, [5] how much more shall that poverty deserve those things which, by the customary order of divine providence, are commonly granted to all. [6] If, I say, the dryness of the rock at the poor man's word offered an abundant draught to the thirsting little pauper, surely now nothing among all things will deny its service to those who, for the Author of all things, have forsaken all.

## Chapter VIII — On His Affectionate Devotion, and How Even Creatures Lacking Reason Seemed to Be Drawn in Affection Toward Him <a id="cap8"></a>

<p id="cap8-1">
[1] True piety, which according to the Apostle <ref to="1 Tim 4:8">is profitable unto all things</ref>, had so filled the heart of Francis and penetrated his inmost being that it seemed to have claimed the whole man of God as its rightful possession. [2] It was this that bore him aloft toward God through devotion, that transformed him into Christ through compassion, that bent him down toward his neighbor through condescension, and that, through a universal reconciliation with all things, refashioned him into the state of innocence. [3] And though through this piety he was moved tenderly toward all creatures, yet in a special way, when he perceived souls redeemed by the precious blood of Christ Jesus to be defiled with any stain of sin, he lamented them with such tenderness of mercy that, like a mother, he travailed over them daily in Christ. [4] And this was for him the chief reason for venerating the ministers of God's word: that by the conversion of sinners they raise up offspring to their dead brother — namely, to Christ crucified for sinners — <ref to="Deut 25:5">and through their conversion and devout solicitude raise up</ref> and govern him with anxious piety. [5] <ref to="2 Cor 1:3">Such an office of mercy he held to be more acceptable to the Father of mercies</ref> than any sacrifice, especially if it were carried out in the pursuit of perfect charity, so that one labor at it more by example than by word, more by tearful prayer than by talkative discourse.
</p>

<p id="cap8-2">
[1] He used to say, therefore, that a preacher must be mourned over as one deprived of true devotion, whether he who seeks in his preaching not the salvation of souls but his own praise, or he who destroys by the depravity of his life what he builds up by the truth of his doctrine. [2] He used to say that a simple and tongue-tied friar, who by his good example spurred others on to good, was to be preferred to such a man. [3] That saying too: <ref to="1 Kgs 2:5">Until the barren woman has borne many </ref>, he expounded in this fashion: "The barren one," he said, "is the poor little friar, who has not the office of begetting children in the Church. [4] He shall bring forth many at the judgment, because those whom he now converts to Christ by private prayers, the Judge will then reckon to his glory. [5] <ref to="1 Kgs 2:5">She who has many children shall grow feeble</ref>, because the empty and talkative preacher, who now rejoices over the many begotten as it were by his own power, shall then recognize that he has nothing of his own in them."
</p>

<p id="cap8-3">
[1] Since, then, he longed for the salvation of souls with heartfelt tenderness and burned for it with fervent zeal, he used to say that he was filled <ref to="Exod 29:18">with the sweetest fragrances</ref> and anointed <ref to="John 112:3">as with a precious ointment</ref> whenever he heard that, through the sweet-smelling renown of holy brothers scattered across the world, many were being led onto the path of truth. [2] At the report of such men he exulted in spirit, heaping upon those brothers <ref to="Titus 1:15">blessings most worthy of every acceptance</ref>—the brothers who by word or deed drew sinners to the love of Christ. [3] Likewise those who by wicked deeds violated the sacred Order incurred the most grievous sentence of his curse: [4] "By you," he said, "most holy Lord, and by the whole heavenly court, and by me, your little one, let them be cursed who by their bad example confound and tear down what you have built—and do not cease to build—through the holy brothers of this Order!" [5] He was often afflicted with such sorrow over the scandal given to the little ones that he thought he would faint, had he not been upheld by the consolation of divine mercy. [6] But once, troubled by evil examples, when with anxious spirit he was pleading with the merciful Father on behalf of his sons, he received from the Lord this answer: [7] "Why are you so distressed, poor little man? Have I set you up as a shepherd over my religion in such a way that you do not know that I am its chief patron? [8] I appointed you, a simple man, to this very end: that what I shall accomplish in you be ascribed not to human ingenuity but to grace from above. [9] I have called; <ref to="Isa 48:15; Rev 3:10">I will preserve and feed</ref>, and as some fall away I will set lofty ones in their place, so that, <ref to="Matt 26:24">if they had not been born</ref>, I will cause them to be born; and however great the blows by which this poor little religion is shaken, it shall by my gift always remain safe."
</p>

<p id="cap8-4">
[1] He likewise abhorred the vice of detraction, the enemy of the fountain of piety and grace, as though it were a serpent's bite, and he affirmed it to be a most savage plague and an abomination to the most loving God, since the detractor feeds upon the blood of souls, <ref to="Ps 56:5">which he slays with the sword of his tongue</ref>. [2] Hearing on one occasion a certain brother blackening the reputation of another, he turned to his vicar and said: [3] "Rise up, rise up, examine the matter diligently, and if you find the accused brother to be innocent, mark out the accuser before all with a stern correction!" [4] Indeed, more than once he declared that the man who would strip his brother of the glory of his good name ought himself to be stripped of his habit, <ref to="Luke 18:13">and could not lift his eyes to God</ref> unless he first took care to restore, so far as he was able, what he had taken away. [5] "So much the greater," he would say, "is the impiety of detractors than that of robbers, inasmuch as the law of Christ, which is fulfilled in the keeping of piety, binds us to desire the salvation of souls rather than of bodies."
</p>

<p id="cap8-5">
[1] Stooping with the wondrous tenderness of compassion to all who were afflicted by any bodily hardship, whenever he discerned in anyone some want or some lack, with the sweetness of his devout heart he referred it back to Christ. [2] Indeed he possessed an inborn clemency, which the piety of Christ poured upon him from above only doubled. [3] And so his soul would melt toward the poor and the sick, and to those for whom he could not provide a helping hand he offered his tender affection. [4] It happened once that one of the brothers answered rather harshly a certain poor man who was importunately begging for alms. [5] Hearing this, the devout lover of the poor commanded the brother to strip himself and cast himself down at that poor man's feet, to declare himself guilty, and to ask the support of his prayers and his pardon. [6] When the brother had done this humbly, the father gently added: "When you behold a poor man, O brother, there is set before you a mirror of the Lord and of His poor Mother. [7] In the sick likewise consider the infirmities which He took upon Himself!" [8] And since he too, that most Christian of poor men, beheld in all the poor the very image of Christ, even when the necessaries of life had been bestowed upon him, he not only gave them away freely to those he met, but judged that they ought to be restored, as though they were the others' own property. [9] It happened once, as he was returning from Siena, that a certain poor man came up to him, who because of an illness was wrapped in a little cloak over his habit. [10] When his wretchedness was beheld with a merciful eye, he said to his companion: "We must give the mantle back to this poor little man, for it is his. [11] <ref to="Luke 6:34">For we received it only on loan</ref>, until we should chance to find someone poorer." [12] But the companion, considering the devout father's own need, stubbornly resisted, lest he provide for another while neglecting himself. [13] Yet he replied: "I reckon it a theft to be charged against me by the great Almsgiver, if I do not give what I am carrying to one more needy." [14] For this reason, of all the things that were given to him for the relief of his body's needs, he was accustomed to ask leave of the givers, so that he might lawfully bestow them should one more needy come his way. [15] He spared absolutely nothing—neither mantles nor tunics nor books nor even the altar furnishings—but rather, while he was able, he lavished all these things upon the needy, that he might fulfill the office of piety. [16] Many a time, when he met poor men laden with burdens on the road, he set their loads upon his own feeble shoulders.
</p>

<p id="cap8-6">
[1] Filled, too, with an abundant tenderness born of contemplating the first origin of all things, he would call creatures, however small, by the names of brother or sister, because he knew that they shared with him a single beginning. [2] Yet those he embraced more inwardly and with greater sweetness which by a natural resemblance set forth the gentle meekness of Christ and which figure Him in the symbolism of Scripture. [3] Often he bought back lambs that were being led to slaughter, mindful of that most gentle Lamb <ref to="Isa 53:7">who willed to be led to the killing</ref> for the redemption of sinners. [4] On one occasion, when the servant of God was lodging at the monastery of Saint Verecundus in the diocese of Gubbio, a certain little ewe gave birth that night to a tiny lamb. [5] There was nearby a most ferocious sow which, sparing not the innocent creature's life, slew it with a ravenous bite. [6] When he heard of this, the loving father, moved with wondrous compassion and remembering the Lamb without blemish, lamented before all for the death of the little lamb, [7] <ref to="Gal 2:14">saying</ref>: "Alas for me, brother lamb, innocent animal, that representest Christ to men, <ref to="Job 24:18">cursed be</ref> the impious beast that killed thee, <ref to="Gen 3:17">and let no</ref> man or beast <ref to="Gen 3:17">eat of her</ref>." [8] Wonderful to tell! At once the wicked sow began to sicken, and after paying a bodily penalty for three days, at last endured an avenging death. [9] Cast into the moat of the monastery, where it lay for a long time, dried up like a board, it became food for no hungry creature. [10] Let human impiety therefore take note with what punishment it is to be smitten in the end, if a beast's ferocity was struck down by so dreadful a death; [11] and let faithful devotion weigh well how great in the servant of God was the tenderness of wondrous power and abundant sweetness, so that even the nature of brute beasts applauded him in its own fashion.
</p>

<p id="cap8-7">
[1] Once, as he was making his way near the city of Siena, he came upon a great flock of sheep grazing in the pastures. [2] When he had greeted them kindly, as was his custom, they all left off feeding and ran to him, lifting up their heads and fixing their gaze upon him with eyes raised. [3] Indeed they made such joyful display before him that the shepherds marveled, and so too the brothers, who beheld both the lambs and the very rams gathered about him, leaping for joy in so wondrous a fashion. [4] At another time, at Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, a certain ewe was offered to the man of God, and he received it gladly for love of that innocence and simplicity which the nature of the sheep displays. [5] The holy man would admonish the little ewe to give heed to the divine praises and to guard against giving any offense to the brothers; and the ewe, as though she perceived the holiness of the man of God, carefully observed his instruction. [6] For when she heard the brothers chanting in choir, she too would enter the church and, with no one teaching her, bend her knees and let out the sound of her bleating before the altar of the Virgin, the Mother of the Lamb, as if she longed to greet her. [7] Moreover, when the most sacred body of Christ was elevated amid the solemnities of the Mass, she would bow down upon her bent knees, as though that reverent beast were reproaching the undevout for their irreverence and inviting those devoted to Christ to reverence for the Sacrament. [8] At one time he had kept a little lamb with him in the City, out of reverence for that most gentle Lamb, and upon his departure he entrusted it to a noble matron, namely the lady Jacoba of the Seven Suns, to be cared for. [9] The lamb, indeed, as though schooled by the saint in spiritual things, clung to the lady with inseparable companionship as she went to church, stood there, and returned. [10] If at the hour of matins the lady was slow to rise, the lamb would get up and nudge her with its little horns and rouse her with its bleating, urging her by its gestures and signs to hasten to the church. [11] On account of this the lamb, the disciple of Francis and now made a master of devotion, was kept by the lady as something wondrous and beloved.
</p>

<p id="cap8-8">
[1] On another occasion, at Greccio, a little hare was offered alive to the man of God; and when it had been set free upon the ground, though it might have fled wherever it wished, at the call of the gentle father it sprang in a swift bound into his bosom. [2] Cherishing it with the tender affection of his heart, he seemed to take pity on it as a mother would, and admonishing it with sweet words not to let itself be caught again, he allowed it to go away free. [3] But though it was set upon the ground many times that it might depart, it always returned to the father's bosom, as if by some hidden sense it perceived the tenderness of his heart; until at last, by the father's command, it was carried off by the brothers to safer places of solitude. [4] In like manner, on an island in the lake of Perugia, a certain rabbit was caught and offered to the man of God; and though it would flee from all others, it entrusted itself to his hands and bosom with the trust of a household pet. [5] As he was hastening across the lake of Rieti to the hermitage of Greccio, a fisherman, out of devotion, presented him with a waterfowl. [6] Receiving it gladly, he invited it with open hands to depart; but when it would not go away, he raised his eyes to heaven and remained long in prayer, and as though returning to himself from some far place after a long while, he sweetly commanded the little bird once more to depart and praise the Lord. [7] So, having received leave with a blessing, the bird, displaying a kind of joy in the movement of its body, flew away. [8] On the same lake there was likewise offered to him a great living fish, which, calling it by the name of brother as was his custom, he placed back into the water beside the boat. [9] But the fish played in the water before the man of God, and as if drawn by love of him, would in no way withdraw from the boat until leave had first been given it by him with a blessing.
</p>

<p id="cap8-9">
[1] On another occasion, as he was walking with a certain brother through the marshes of Venice, he came upon a vast multitude of birds settled and singing among the thickets. [2] Seeing them, he said to his companion: "Our sisters the birds are praising their Creator; let us therefore go into their midst and sing to the Lord his praises and the canonical hours." [3] And when they had entered among them, the birds were not stirred from the spot; [4] and since, because of their chattering, the two could not hear one another as they recited the hours, the holy man turned to the birds and said: "Sister birds, cease your song until we have rendered to God the praises due to him!" [5] At once they fell silent, and they remained in that silence for as long as it took the saint of God—the hours recited unhurriedly and the praises rendered—to grant them leave to sing again. [6] And when the man of God gave them leave, they immediately took up their song in their accustomed manner. [7] At Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, beside the cell of the man of God, a cicada that perched and sang upon a fig tree, since by its song it often roused the servant of the Lord—who had learned to admire the magnificence of the Creator even in small things—to the divine praises, was one day called by him, and, as though taught from heaven, it flew upon his hand. [8] When he had said to it, "Sing, my sister cicada, and with your jubilant song praise the Lord your Creator!", it obeyed without delay and began to sing, nor did it cease until, at the father's command, it flew back to its own place. [9] And it remained there for eight days, each day fulfilling his commands by coming, singing, and departing. [10] At last the man of God said to his companions: "Let us now give leave to our sister the cicada; for it has gladdened us enough with its song and has roused us to the praises of God over the span of eight days." [11] And at once, dismissed by him, it departed and appeared there no more, as if it would not dare in any way to overstep his command.
</p>

<p id="cap8-10">
[1] To the saint, while he lay ill at Siena, a certain pheasant, lately taken, was sent alive by a certain nobleman. [2] No sooner had the bird heard and seen the holy man than it clung to him with such affectionate fondness that it would in no way endure to be parted from him. [3] For when, on many occasions, it was set down outside the friars' little dwelling in the vineyard, that it might go off if it wished, it always came back at a swift run to the father, as though it had been reared by him from its earliest days. [4] Then, when it was given to a certain man who out of devotion was wont to visit the servant of God, it refused all food, as if it grieved to be away from the sight of the holy father. [5] At last, when it was brought back to God's servant, the instant it caught sight of him, with certain gestures betokening its delight, it ate eagerly. [6] When he had come to the hermitage of La Verna to keep a Lent in honor of the Archangel Michael, birds of various kinds, flitting about his cell with melodious harmony and joyful movements, seemed, as though rejoicing at his arrival, to invite and entice the holy father to remain. [7] Seeing this, he said to his companion: "I perceive, brother, that it is God's will we should tarry here for a while; so greatly do our sisters the little birds appear to be consoled by our presence." [8] And so, when he had settled there to stay, a falcon nesting in that place bound itself to him in a great bond of friendship. [9] For it always, in the night hours at which the holy man was accustomed to rise for the divine offices, went before him with its song and its cry. [10] This was most pleasing to the servant of God, for the great solicitude the bird showed toward him shook off from him all the sluggishness of sloth. [11] But when the servant of Christ was burdened more than usual by his infirmity, the falcon would spare him, and would not announce vigils so untimely early. [12] Indeed, <ref to="2 Tim 3:17">as though instructed by God</ref>, near daybreak it would sound the bell of its voice with a gentle touch. [13] Surely it seems to have been a divine portent, both in the rejoicing of so many kinds of birds and in the song of the falcon, that he who praised and worshipped God, borne aloft on the wings of contemplation, was there to be exalted by a seraphic apparition.
</p>

<p id="cap8-11">
[1] While he was staying for a time in the hermitage of Greccio, the inhabitants of that place were afflicted by manifold evils. [2] For a multitude of ravening wolves was devouring not only beasts but men as well, and each year hail in its season laid waste the grain fields and the vineyards. [3] When, therefore, the herald of the sacred Gospel preached to them in their affliction, he said: [4] "To the honor and praise of almighty God I pledge to you that all this pestilence will depart, and that the Lord, looking upon you, will multiply your temporal goods, if, believing me, you take pity on yourselves, so that—having first made true confession—you may <ref to="Matt 3:8">bring forth worthy fruits of penance</ref>. [5] Yet again I announce this to you: that if, ungrateful for these benefits, <ref to="Prov 26:11">you return to your vomit </ref>, the plague will be renewed, the punishment doubled, <ref to="Josh 22:18">and a greater wrath will rage against you</ref>." [6] And so, from that very hour, as they did penance at his exhortation, the disasters ceased, the perils vanished, <ref to="Dan 3:50">and neither wolves nor hailstorms inflicted any further harm</ref>. [7] Indeed—and this is greater still—whenever hail swept across the fields of their neighbors, on drawing near to the borders of these people it either stopped there or turned aside in another direction. [8] The hail kept the pact, and so did the wolves, the covenant of the servant of God, nor did they presume any longer to rage against the law of mercy upon men now converted to mercy, so long as these, according to the agreement, did not act impiously against the most merciful laws of God. [9] Devoutly, then, must we ponder the devotion of the blessed man, which was of such wondrous sweetness and power that it tamed savagery, domesticated the wild, made the gentle obedient, and bent to its own command the nature of brute beasts, rebellious to fallen man. [10] Truly this is that devotion which, drawing all creatures into alliance with itself, avails for all things, holding the promise of the life <ref to="1 Tim 4:8">that now is and that is to come</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter IX - On the Fervor of His Charity and His Longing for Martyrdom <a id="cap9"></a>

<p id="cap9-1">
[1] As for the fervent charity with which Francis, the friend of the Bridegroom, burned, <ref to="Sir 18:2">who could suffice to recount it</ref>? [2] For he seemed wholly absorbed, like some glowing coal, in the flame of divine love. [3] At the mere mention of the Lord's love he would suddenly be roused, moved, set ablaze, as if the inner string of his heart were touched by the plectrum of a voice from without. [4] He called it a noble extravagance to offer such a tribute in exchange for alms, and declared those most foolish who valued love less than pennies, [5] since the priceless price of divine love alone suffices to purchase the kingdom of heaven, and the love of Him who loved us so greatly is greatly to be loved in return. [6] And that he might be roused to divine love by all things, <ref to="Ps 91:5">he exulted in all the works of the hands</ref> of the Lord, and through the spectacle of their loveliness he rose up to their life-giving meaning and cause. [7] <ref to="Job 23:11">In beautiful things he beheld the Most Beautiful, and through the traces imprinted upon things he everywhere pursued</ref> his Beloved, making of all things a ladder for himself, <ref to="Song 5:16; Gen 28:12">by which he might climb up to lay hold of Him who is wholly to be desired</ref>. [8] For with an affection of unheard-of devotion he tasted in each single creature, as in so many little streams, that fountainhead of goodness; [9] and as though he perceived a heavenly harmony in the concord of the powers and acts bestowed upon them by God, he sweetly exhorted them, after the manner of the prophet David, <ref to="Ps 148:1">to the praise of the Lord</ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap9-2">
[1] <ref to="Song 1:12">Christ Jesus crucified abode unceasingly within the bosom of his mind like a bundle of myrrh</ref>, into whom he longed to be wholly transformed through the burning blaze of an overflowing love. [2] As a special prerogative of his particular devotion to him, from the feast of the Epiphany through forty continuous days—that very season, namely, in which Christ lay hidden in the desert—he would withdraw to places of solitude and, shut up in his cell, give himself without interruption to fasting, prayer, and the praises of God, with all the rigor of food and drink that he could endure. [3] So fervent indeed was the affection with which he was borne toward Christ; yet the Beloved repaid him with so intimate a love that it seemed to the servant of God that he felt as it were the unbroken presence of the Savior before his eyes, as he once familiarly disclosed to his companions. [4] He blazed toward the Sacrament of the Lord's Body with a fervor reaching to the very marrow, marveling in utmost wonder at that most precious condescension and that most worthy charity. [5] Often he communicated, and so devoutly that he made others devout, for <ref to="1 Pet 1:19">while at the sweet tasting of the spotless Lamb</ref>, as though drunk in spirit, he was very often caught up into an ecstasy of mind.
</p>

<p id="cap9-3">
[1] He embraced the Mother of the Lord Jesus with unspeakable love, because she had made the Lord of majesty our brother, <ref to="1 Pet 2:10">and through her we have obtained mercy</ref>. [2] Placing his trust in her above all things after Christ, he made her the advocate of himself and of his brethren, and in her honor he fasted most devoutly from the feast of the Apostles Peter and Paul until the feast of the Assumption. [3] <ref to="Matt 4:2">To the angelic spirits, who burn with a wondrous fire to soar into God and to inflame the souls of the elect, he was joined by an inseparable bond of love, and out of devotion to them he fasted for forty days from the Assumption of the glorious Virgin,</ref> giving himself unceasingly to prayer. [4] To the blessed Archangel Michael, because he holds the office of presenting souls, he was bound by a more particular and devoted love, on account of the ardent zeal he bore for the salvation of all who are to be saved. [5] <ref to="Ezek 28:14">From the remembrance of all the saints, as of stones of fire,</ref> he grew warm again into a godlike blaze, embracing with the highest devotion all the Apostles, and above all Peter and Paul, because of the fervent charity they had toward Christ; [6] and out of reverence and love for them he dedicated to the Lord a special Lenten fast. [7] <ref to="Mark 12:42">Christ's poor man had nothing but two mites,</ref> namely his body and his soul, which he could bestow with generous charity. [8] But these he so continually offered up through the love of Christ that, as it were at every time, he sacrificed his body through the rigor of fasting and his spirit through the ardor of longing, <ref to="Exod 30:27">sacrificing the burnt offering outwardly in the court, and inwardly within the temple burning the incense</ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap9-4">
[1] In such a way did the surpassing devotion of charity bear him aloft into things divine, that his loving kindness might also reach out to those who shared with him in nature and in grace. [2] For since tenderness of heart had made him a brother to all other creatures, it is no wonder <ref to="2 Cor 5:14">that the charity of Christ made him a still nearer brother</ref> to those marked <ref to="Rev 5:9">with the image of the Creator and redeemed by the blood of their Author</ref>. [3] He did not reckon himself a friend of Christ unless he cherished the souls whom Christ had redeemed. [4] He used to say that nothing was to be set before the salvation of souls, proving it above all by this, <ref to="John 3:18">that the Only-Begotten of God</ref> had deigned to hang upon the cross for souls. [5] Hence came his wrestling in prayer, his journeying about in preaching, and his excess in the giving of examples. [6] And so, whenever excessive austerity was found fault with in him, he would answer that he had been given to others as an example. [7] For although his innocent flesh, which already submitted itself willingly to the spirit, had no need of any scourge on account of offenses, yet for the sake of example he kept renewing its punishments and burdens, <ref to="Ps 16:4">holding to hard ways for the sake of others</ref>. [8] For he would say: "If I should speak with the tongues of men and of angels, yet have not charity within myself and show not to my neighbors examples of the virtues, I profit others but little, <ref to="1 Cor 13:1">and myself nothing</ref>."
</p>

<p id="cap9-5">
[1] With the burning fire of charity he likewise emulated the glorious triumph of the holy Martyrs, in whom neither could the flame of love be quenched nor their fortitude be weakened. [2] He longed therefore, kindled by that perfect love <ref to="1 John 4:18">which casts out fear</ref>, <ref to="Rom 12:1">to offer himself a living sacrifice to the Lord through the flame of martyrdom</ref>, that he might repay Christ in turn, who died for us, and stir up others to divine love. [3] For in the sixth year of his conversion, blazing with the desire for martyrdom, he resolved to sail across to the regions of Syria in order to preach the Christian faith and penance to the Saracens and other unbelievers. [4] And when he had boarded a certain ship to make his way thither, driven by the blowing of <ref to="Matt 14:24; Mark 6:48">contrary winds</ref> he was forced to land in the territory of Slavonia. [5] Having thus tarried there for some while, and being unable to find any ship setting sail at that time, he felt himself cheated of his desire and earnestly begged certain sailors bound for Ancona to take him along with them for the love of God. [6] But when they obstinately refused on account of the lack of provisions, the man of God, trusting greatly in the goodness of the Lord, secretly boarded the ship with his companion. [7] There was present a certain man—sent, as is believed, by God on behalf of his poor one—who, bearing with him the necessaries of sustenance, called to himself <ref to="Job 1:1">a certain man who feared God</ref> from among the ship's crew and addressed him thus: [8] "Keep all these things faithfully for the poor brothers hidden in the ship, <ref to="Sir 8:12">and in time of need </ref> supply them kindly!" [9] And so it came to pass that, while the sailors for many days were unable to land anywhere because of the force of the winds, all their own provisions were consumed, and only the alms bestowed from above upon poor Francis remained. [10] And though this was very slight, it received by divine power so great an increase that, throughout the many days they were delayed at sea by the continual tempest, it fully met the needs of all the way to the port of Ancona. [11] The sailors therefore, seeing that through the servant of God they had escaped many perils of death, <ref to="Ps 106:24">like those who had felt the dreadful dangers of the sea and beheld the wondrous works of the Lord in the deep</ref>, <ref to="Sir 50:19">gave thanks to almighty God</ref>, who ever shows himself wondrous and worthy of love among his friends and servants.
</p>

<p id="cap9-6">
[1] When, having left the sea behind, he began to journey across the land, casting upon it the seed of salvation, he brought back fruitful sheaves. [2] But because the fruit of martyrdom had so captivated his heart that he longed for a precious death for Christ above all the merits of the virtues, he set out on the road toward Morocco, that he might preach the Gospel of Christ to the Miramolin and his people, in the hope that he might in some way attain the palm he so desired. [3] For he was borne by so great a longing that, although he was feeble in body, he outran <ref to="2 Cor 8:19">the companion of his pilgrimage </ref> and, eager to carry out his purpose, flew onward as though drunk with the Spirit. [4] But when he had already advanced as far as Spain, by divine arrangement, which was reserving him for other things, a most grievous illness came upon him, by which he was hindered and could not accomplish what he desired. [5] And so the man of God, perceiving that his life in the flesh was still necessary for the offspring he had begotten—though he reckoned death to be gain for himself—<ref to="John 21:17">returned to feed the sheep </ref> entrusted to his care.
</p>

<p id="cap9-7">
[1] But since the ardor of charity urged his spirit on toward martyrdom, he attempted a third time to set out toward the unbelievers, that the faith of the Trinity might be spread abroad through the shedding of his own blood. [2] For in the thirteenth year of his conversion, journeying to the regions of Syria, he steadfastly exposed himself to many perils, that he might come into the presence of the Sultan of Babylon. [3] For at that time there was a war so implacable between the Christians and the Saracens—the camps of the armies pitched here and there in the field, drawn up close at hand and facing one another—that the road of passage between them could not be crossed without the peril of death. [4] Indeed, a cruel edict had gone forth from the Sultan, that whoever should bring the head of any Christian would receive a gold bezant as his reward. [5] Yet Francis, the fearless soldier of Christ, hoping that he could soon attain his purpose, resolved to seize the road—not terrified by the dread of death, but spurred on by desire. [6] For when he had first offered prayer, <ref to="1 Kgs 30:6">strengthened by the Lord</ref>, he confidently sang aloud that prophetic verse: For though I should walk in the midst of the shadow of death, I will fear no evils, <ref to="Ps 22:4">for thou art with me</ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap9-8">
[1] And so, having taken as his companion a friar named Illuminato — a man indeed of light and virtue — when he had set out upon the journey, he met two little sheep coming toward him; [2] and at the sight of them the holy man was filled with gladness and said to his companion: "Take heart, brother, <ref to="Sir 11">in the Lord </ref>, for in us is fulfilled that word of the Gospel: <ref to="Matt 10:16">Behold, I send you forth as sheep in the midst of wolves </ref>." [3] But when they had gone on further, they were met by Saracen guards who, rushing upon them as swiftly as wolves upon sheep, seized the servants of God savagely and handled them cruelly and contemptuously, heaping insults upon them, beating them with blows, and binding them in chains. [4] At last, after they had been afflicted and worn down in manifold ways, by the ordering of divine providence and in accordance with the desire of the man of God, they were brought before the Sultan. [5] And so, when that prince inquired by whom and to what end and in what manner they had been sent, and how they had come, Christ's servant Francis answered with undaunted heart that he had been sent not by man but by God Most High, that he might show to him and to his people the way of salvation and proclaim the Gospel of truth. [6] Indeed, with such steadfastness of mind, such strength of soul, and such fervor of spirit did he preach to that same Sultan the God who is three and one, and Jesus Christ the Savior of all, that the saying of the Gospel was shown to be truly fulfilled in him: I will give you a mouth and wisdom, <ref to="Luke 21:15">which all your adversaries shall not be able to resist and gainsay </ref>. [7] For the Sultan too, beholding in the man of God so wondrous a fervor of spirit and such virtue, <ref to="Mark 6:10">heard him gladly </ref> and pressed him ever more urgently to prolong his stay with him. [8] But the servant of Christ, enlightened by a heavenly oracle, said: "If you are willing to be converted, you together with your people, to Christ, then for the love of Him I will most gladly remain with you. [9] But if you hesitate to abandon the law of Mahomet for the faith of Christ, command that a very great fire be kindled, and I will enter the fire together with your priests, so that in this way at least you may learn which faith is the more certain and the more holy, and not undeservedly to be held." [10] To him the Sultan replied: "I do not believe that any of my priests would be willing to expose himself to the fire in defense of his faith, or to undergo any kind of torment." [11] For he had just seen one of his priests, a man of standing and great age, who upon hearing this word had fled from his presence. [12] To him the holy man said: "If you are willing to promise, for yourself and your people, that you will come over to the worship of Christ should I emerge from the fire unharmed, then I will enter the fire alone; [13] and if I am burned, let it be charged to my sins; but if the divine power protects me, then acknowledge Christ, the power and wisdom of God, <ref to="1 Cor 1:24; John 17:3; John 4:42">the true God and Lord and Savior </ref> of all." [14] The Sultan, however, replied that he dared not accept this proposal, for he feared an uprising of the people. [15] Nevertheless he offered him many precious gifts, which the man of God — greedy not for worldly things but for the salvation of souls — scorned all as so much mire. [16] The Sultan, seeing the holy man so perfect a despiser of worldly things, was moved with admiration and conceived a still greater devotion toward him. [17] And although he was unwilling, or perhaps did not dare, to pass over to the Christian faith, he nevertheless devoutly begged the servant of Christ to accept the aforesaid gifts, for his own salvation, to be distributed among poor Christians or churches. [13] But he, because he shunned the burden of money and saw in the Sultan's soul no root of true piety, in no way consented.
</p>

<p id="cap9-9">
[1] Perceiving, moreover, <ref to="2 Tim 3:10">that he was making no progress in the conversion of that people, nor could he attain what he had purposed</ref>, he returned to the lands of the faithful, forewarned by a divine revelation. [2] And thus, by the ordaining mercy of God and through the deserving virtue of the holy man, it came to pass, both mercifully and marvelously, that this friend of Christ should seek death for His sake with all his strength, and yet by no means find it, so that he might neither lack the merit of his longed-for martyrdom nor be denied the honor of being marked, in time to come, with a singular privilege. [3] So indeed it came about that this divine fire blazed all the more perfectly within his heart, that afterward it might more openly burst forth in his flesh. [4] O man truly blessed, whose flesh, though not cut down by the tyrant's sword, is yet not deprived of likeness to <ref to="Rev 5:12">the slain Lamb</ref>! [5] O, I say, man truly and fully blessed, whose soul, "though the persecutor's sword did not bear it away, yet did not lose the palm of martyrdom!"
</p>

## Chapter X - On the Practice and Power of Prayer <a id="cap10"></a>

<p id="cap10-1">
[1] <ref to="2 Cor 5:6">Christ's servant Francis, sensing that in the body he was a pilgrim away from the Lord</ref>, having by the love of Christ <ref to="2 Cor 5:14">already been rendered wholly insensible to outward, earthly desires</ref>, strove—lest he should be without the consolation of his Beloved—to present his spirit before God by <ref to="1 Thess 5:17">praying without ceasing</ref>.
[2] For prayer was the solace of one contemplating, while, already made a fellow citizen of the angels amid the circuit of the heavenly mansions, he <ref to="Song 3:1-2">sought with burning desire the Beloved</ref> from whom only the wall of his flesh kept him apart.
[3] And it was a safeguard to one at work, for in all that he did, distrusting his own diligence and trusting in the mercy from on high, <ref to="Ps 54:23">through his persistence in prayer he cast his whole thought upon the Lord</ref>.
[4] He firmly maintained that the grace of prayer was to be desired above all things by a religious man, believing that without it none could prosper in the service of God; and by whatever means he could, he stirred up his brothers to its pursuit.
[5] For walking and sitting, within and without, laboring and at leisure, he was so intent upon prayer that he seemed to have dedicated to it not only whatever of heart and body was in him, but indeed his every work and all his time.
</p>

<p id="cap10-2">
[1] He was accustomed to let pass no visitation of the Spirit through negligence. [2] Indeed, when one was offered to him, he followed after it, and for as long as the Lord granted, he savored to the full the sweetness set before him. [3] But when, intent upon a journey, he felt some breathings of the divine Spirit, he would halt his step while his companions went on ahead, and, turning the new inspiration into fruition, <ref to="2 Cor 6:1">he did not receive the grace in vain</ref>. [4] Many times he was lifted up in so great an excess of contemplation that, rapt above himself and perceiving something beyond human sense, he was unaware of what was happening around him in the outward world. [5] For once, while he was passing through Borgo San Sepolcro, a town indeed thronged with people, riding upon a little donkey on account of the weakness of his body, he met crowds rushing upon him out of devotion. [6] Drawn and held back by them, pressed in upon and handled this way and that, he seemed insensible to it all, and like a lifeless body he took in nothing whatever of the things being done about him. [7] Hence, when the town had long since been passed and the crowds left behind, and he had come to a certain dwelling of lepers, the contemplator of heavenly things, as though returning from elsewhere, anxiously inquired when they would be drawing near to the Borgo. [8] His mind indeed, fixed upon the heavenly splendors, had not perceived the changes of places, nor of times, nor of the persons who met him. [9] That this happened to him quite frequently the manifold experience of his companions confirmed.
</p>

<p id="cap10-3">
[1] And because he had perceived in prayer that the longed-for presence of the Holy Spirit offers itself the more intimately to those who pray the more it finds them withdrawn from the clamor of worldly things, he therefore sought out solitary places, and would go by night to lonely spots and abandoned churches to pray; [2] there he often endured the dreadful assaults of demons, who, contending with him in tangible form, strove to disturb him in his earnest devotion to prayer. [3] But he, armed with celestial weapons, the more fiercely he was attacked by his enemies, the stronger he became in virtue and the more fervent in prayer, [4] saying with confidence to Christ: "Under the shadow of your wings protect me from the face of the wicked, <ref to="Ps 16:8-9">who have afflicted me</ref>." [5] And to the demons: "Do whatever you can against me, malicious and deceitful spirits! [6] For you can do nothing except so far as the hand from on high permits you; [7] and I stand ready, with all joyfulness, to bear all that that hand shall decree to be inflicted upon me." [8] The proud demons, unable to endure such steadfastness of mind, withdrew in confusion.
</p>

<p id="cap10-4">
[1] But the man of God, remaining alone and at peace, filled the groves with his groans, drenched the ground with his tears, beat his breast with his hand, and, as though having found a more hidden sanctuary, conversed with his Lord. [2] There he answered the Judge, there he entreated the Father, there he spoke with the Friend; there too he was sometimes overheard by the brothers who watched over him devoutly, as with clamorous groans he interceded before the divine clemency on behalf of sinners, and as he lamented in a loud voice the Lord's Passion, as though it were set before his very eyes. [3] There he was seen praying by night, his hands stretched out in the manner of a cross, his whole body lifted up from the earth and surrounded by a kind of shining little cloud, so that the marvelous illumination about his body might bear witness to a wondrous enlightenment within his mind. [4] There also, as has been proven by sure tokens, <ref to="Ps 50:8">the uncertain and hidden things of the divine wisdom </ref> were laid open to him, though he did not divulge them outwardly, <ref to="2 Cor 5:14">except insofar as the charity of Christ urged him </ref> and the benefit of his neighbors required. [5] For he used to say: "For a paltry price it happens that a priceless thing is lost, and that He who gave it is easily provoked never to give it again." [6] When he returned from his private prayers, <ref to="1 Kgs 10:6">by which he was almost changed into another man </ref>, he took the greatest care to make himself like the rest, <ref to="Sir 2:8">lest perchance the breath of favor that he showed without should empty within him of its reward </ref>. [7] When in public he was suddenly moved, being visited by the Lord, he always cast something before the bystanders, lest the intimate touches of the Bridegroom be made known abroad. [8] As he prayed among the brothers he wholly avoided coughing, groans, hard breathing, and outward gestures, whether because he loved secrecy, or because, returning into his inner self, he was carried wholly into God. [9] Often he said such things as these to those close to him: "When the servant of God is visited from on high while praying, he ought to say: [10] <ref to="Luke 18:13">'This consolation You have sent down from heaven to me, a sinner </ref><ref to="1 Pet 1:12">and unworthy one </ref>, O Lord, and I commit it to Your keeping, for I feel myself to be a thief of Your treasures.' [11] But when he returns from prayer, he ought to show himself so poor and so much a sinner, as though he had obtained no new grace at all."
</p>

<p id="cap10-5">
[1] While the man of God was at prayer in the place of the Portiuncula, it happened that the Bishop of Assisi came to visit him, as was his custom. [2] As soon as he had entered the place, he approached the cell in which the servant of Christ was praying with more boldness than was fitting, and having knocked at the little door, he pressed forward as though about to enter; but when he had thrust his head within and beheld the saint at prayer, <ref to="Job 21:6">he was shaken by a sudden trembling</ref>, his limbs grew stiff, he even lost the power of speech, and at once, by the divine will, he was driven out by force and led far away, stepping backward. [3] Astonished, the bishop hastened to the friars as best he could, and when God had restored his speech to him, his very first words were a confession of his fault. [4] It happened at a certain time that the abbot of the monastery of Saint Justin, in the diocese of Perugia, met the servant of Christ. [5] On seeing him, the devout abbot quickly dismounted from his horse, both to show reverence to the man of God and to confer with him on certain matters touching the soul's salvation. [6] At length, after a sweet conversation, the abbot as he departed humbly begged to be remembered in prayer; and the man dear to God answered him: "I will pray gladly." [7] So when the abbot had gone but a little way, the faithful Francis said to his companion: "Wait a moment, brother, for I wish to discharge the debt I promised." [8] And while he prayed, <ref to="Rom 8:5">the abbot suddenly felt in his spirit an unaccustomed warmth and a sweetness never before experienced</ref>, <ref to="Acts 11:5">so that, caught up in an ecstasy of mind</ref>, he passed wholly out of himself into God. [9] He remained thus but a brief while, <ref to="Luke 15:17; Acts 12:11">and when he had come to himself again</ref>, he recognized the power of holy Francis's prayer. [10] Thereafter he burned ever with a greater love for the Order, and recounted to many what had befallen him as a miracle.
</p>

<p id="cap10-6">
[1] The holy man was accustomed to render the canonical hours with no less reverent fear of God than devotion. [2] For although he suffered from ailments of the eyes, the stomach, the spleen, and the liver, he would nevertheless not lean upon wall or partition while he chanted, but always performed the hours standing upright and without his hood, neither letting his eyes wander nor cutting any verse short. [3] If he chanced to be on a journey, he would halt his steps for the time, not omitting this reverent and sacred custom even amid the downpour of rains. [4] For he would say: "If the body eats its food in repose—the body that is one day to be the food of worms—with how much greater peace and tranquility ought the soul to receive the food of life?" [5] He also reckoned that he gravely offended whenever, given over to prayer, his mind strayed inwardly amid idle fancies. [6] When anything of the kind befell him, he did not spare himself confession, but at once expiated it. [7] He had so turned this zeal into habit that he very rarely endured such flitting distractions. [8] During one Lent he had fashioned a little vessel, so as to occupy the small fragments of time lest they slip away entirely. [9] But when, as he was saying Terce, the memory of it came to mind and drew his spirit somewhat aside, moved by fervor of spirit he consumed the little vessel in the fire, saying: <ref to="Ps 53:8">"I will sacrifice it to the Lord</ref>, whose sacrifice it has hindered." [10] He recited the psalms with such attentiveness of mind and spirit, as though he had God present before him; [11] and when the name of the Lord occurred within them, for the sweetness of its delight he seemed to lick his very lips. [12] Wishing to honor with especial reverence the Lord's name—not only as thought, but also as spoken and written—he once urged the brothers to gather up all written scraps of paper wherever they were found and to set them in a clean place, lest perchance that sacred name written there should chance to be trodden underfoot. [13] And when he uttered or heard the name of Jesus, filled inwardly with a certain jubilation, he seemed wholly transformed outwardly, as if a honey-sweet savor had altered his taste, or a harmonious sound had changed his hearing.
</p>

<p id="cap10-7">
[1] Now it came to pass in the third year before his death that, at the village of Greccio, he resolved to keep the memory of the birth of the child Jesus with all the greater solemnity he could muster, in order to kindle devotion. [2] But lest this might be charged with novelty, having sought and obtained license from the Supreme Pontiff, he had a manger prepared, hay brought in, and an ox and an ass led to the place. [3] The brothers are summoned, the people come, the woodland rings with voices, and that venerable night is made splendid and solemn with lights abundant and bright and with songs of praise sonorous and harmonious. [4] The man of God stood before the manger, filled with piety, bathed in tears, and overflowing with joy. [5] The solemnities of the Mass are celebrated over the manger, with Francis, the levite of Christ, chanting the holy Gospel. [6] He then preaches to the people standing about concerning the birth of the poor King, whom, when he wished to name him, he would call the child of Bethlehem out of the tenderness of his love. [7] Now a certain knight, virtuous and truthful, who for the love of Christ had abandoned worldly soldiery and was joined to the man of God in great intimacy, the lord John of Greccio, declared that he had seen a little child of surpassing beauty sleeping in that manger, whom the blessed father Francis, embracing him in both arms, seemed to rouse from sleep. [8] This vision of the devout knight is rendered credible not only by the holiness of the one who saw it, but it is also confirmed by the truth it signified and corroborated by the miracles that followed. [9] For the example of Francis, when pondered by the world, has power to awaken hearts grown sluggish in the faith of Christ, [10] and the hay of the manger, kept by the people, proved wondrously to heal sick beasts and to ward off divers other plagues, as God in all things glorified his servant and demonstrated by the manifest wonders of miracles the efficacy of his holy prayer.
</p>

## Chapter XI - On His Understanding of the Scriptures and His Spirit of Prophecy <a id="cap11"></a>

<p id="cap11-1">
[1] Tireless devotion to prayer, joined with the unceasing practice of virtue, had brought the man of God to such serenity of mind that, though he had no skill in sacred letters acquired through formal teaching, yet, illumined by the radiance of the eternal light, <ref to="Job 28:11">he probed the depths of the Scriptures with a wondrous keenness of understanding</ref>. [2] For his genius, pure of every stain, penetrated the hidden secrets of the mysteries, and where the learning of the schoolmen stands without, the affection of one who loves entered in. [3] Now and then he would read in the sacred books, and whatever he had once admitted into his soul he stamped tenaciously upon his memory, for it was not in vain that he grasped by the hearing of mental attention what he chewed over with the affection of continual devotion. [4] When the brothers once asked him whether it pleased him that learned men, now received into the Order, should apply themselves to the study of sacred Scripture, he answered: [6] "It pleases me well, provided that, after the example of Christ, who we read prayed more than he read, they do not neglect the study of prayer, and provided they do not study merely to know how they ought to speak, but to do what they have heard, and, having done it, to set before others what is to be done. [6] I wish," he said, "my brothers to be disciples of the Gospel, and so to advance in the knowledge of truth that they grow likewise in the purity of simplicity, <ref to="Matt 10:16">so as not to separate the simplicity of the dove from the prudence of the serpent</ref>, which the supreme Master joined together with his own blessed lips."
</p>

<p id="cap11-2">
[1] When he was questioned at Siena by a certain religious man, a doctor of sacred theology, concerning certain matters difficult to grasp, he laid open the secrets of divine wisdom with such clarity of holy teaching that the learned man was utterly astonished and declared in wonder:
[2] "Truly the theology of this holy father, borne aloft as if on the wings of purity and contemplation, is an eagle in flight; <ref to="Gen 1:20; Gen 3:14">but our own knowledge crawls upon its belly along the ground</ref>."
[3] <ref to="2 Cor 11:6">For although he was unskilled in speech</ref>, <ref to="Job 28:11">yet, full of knowledge, he untangled the perplexities of questions and brought hidden things into the light</ref>.
[4] Nor is it strange that the holy man should have received understanding of the Scriptures from God, since through the imitation of Christ he carried their perfect truth written out in his very deeds, and through the fullness of the Holy Spirit's anointing he had within his heart their very teacher.
</p>

<p id="cap11-3">
[1] So brightly, too, did the spirit of prophecy shine forth in him that he foresaw things to come and beheld the hidden depths of hearts, perceived things absent as though they were present, and marvelously showed himself present to those who were far away. [2] For at the time when the army of the Christians was besieging Damietta, the man of God was present, armed not with weapons but with faith. [3] When, therefore, on the day of battle the Christians were making ready for the fight, the servant of Christ, upon hearing this, groaned grievously, [4] and said to his companion: "If the engagement of war be attempted, <ref to="4 Kgs 8:10">the Lord has shown me that it will not turn out prosperously</ref> for the Christians; [5] yet if I say this, I shall be reckoned a fool, and if I keep silent I shall not escape my conscience. What, then, seems best to you?" [6] His companion answered, saying: "Brother, <ref to="1 Cor 4:3">let it be the least of your concerns to be judged by men</ref>, for it is not only now that you begin to be reckoned a fool. [7] Unburden your conscience, <ref to="Acts 5:29">and fear God rather than men </ref>." [8] Hearing this, the herald of Christ leapt up and approached the Christians with saving admonitions; he forbade the war and foretold disaster. [9] But the truth was turned into a jest: <ref to="Tob 3:4; John 12:40">they hardened their heart</ref> and would not turn back. [10] They went forth, joined battle, and fought, and the whole Christian army was put to flight, bringing back as the end of the war disgrace, not triumph. [11] Indeed, by so great a slaughter the number of the Christians was so diminished that about six thousand lay among the dead and the captives. [12] In this it was clearly made known that the wisdom of the poor man was not to be despised; for the soul of a just man at times declares things true, <ref to="Sir 37:18">more than seven watchmen seated on high to keep watch</ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap11-4">
[1] On another occasion, when after his return from overseas he was making his way to Celano to preach, a certain knight, with humble devotion and great insistence, invited him to dine. [2] And so he came to the knight's house, and the whole household rejoiced at the coming of their poor guests. [3] But before they took food, the man, devout in spirit, after his accustomed manner offered prayers and praises to God, <ref to="John 17:1">standing with his eyes lifted up to heaven</ref>. [4] When the prayer was finished, he drew his kindly host aside in friendly fashion and spoke to him thus: "Behold, brother host, overcome by your entreaties I have entered your house in order to eat. [5] Now quickly heed my counsel, for you shall eat not here but elsewhere. [6] Confess now your sins, contrite with the sorrow of true penance, and let nothing remain in you that you do not lay open in a truthful confession. [7] Today the Lord will repay you in turn, because you have received His poor with such great devotion." [8] That man at once assented to the words of the saint, and disclosing all his sins in confession to the saint's companion, he <ref to="Isa 38:1">set his house in order</ref> and prepared himself, as best he could, to meet death. [9] At length they sat down to table, and as the others were beginning to eat, the host suddenly breathed out his spirit, carried off by a sudden death according to the word of the man of God. [10] And so it came to pass, through the deserving mercy of his hospitality, that, according to the word of Truth, by receiving a prophet he <ref to="Matt 10:41">received a prophet's reward</ref>; [11] for, through the holy man's prophetic forewarning, that devout knight provided for himself against the suddenness of death, so that, fortified beforehand with the arms of penance, he might <ref to="Luke 16:9">escape everlasting damnation and enter into the eternal dwellings</ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap11-5">
[1] At the time when the holy man lay ill at Rieti, a certain prebendary named Gideon, a slippery and worldly fellow, was seized by a grave sickness and confined to his bed. When he had been carried to him, he begged tearfully, together with those standing around, that he might be marked by him with the sign of the cross. [2] To him the saint said: <ref to="Gal 5:16">"When once you lived according to the desires of the flesh</ref>, <ref to="Sir 17:24">having no fear of the judgments of God</ref>, how shall I mark you with the cross? [3] Yet on account of the devout prayers of those who intercede for you, I will sign you with the sign of the cross in the name of the Lord. [4] Know this, however: that you shall suffer worse things <ref to="Prov 26:11">if, once delivered, you return to your vomit</ref>. <ref to="Matt 12:45">For because of the sin of ingratitude, things ever worse than the former</ref> are visited upon a man." [5] And so, when the sign of the cross had been made over him, the man who had been lying crippled at once rose up healed, and bursting forth in praise of God said, "I am delivered!" [6] His loins' bones resounded, in the hearing of many, just as dry sticks crack when broken by hand. [7] But after a little while had passed, <ref to="Judg 3:7">forgetful of God</ref>, he gave his body back to unchastity; and when one evening he had dined in the house of a certain canon and was sleeping there that night, suddenly the roof of the house collapsed upon them all. [8] But while the others escaped death, that wretched man alone was caught and crushed to death. [9] <ref to="Matt 12:45">By the just judgment of God, therefore, the last things of that man became worse than the first</ref>, because of the vice of ingratitude and contempt of God; for one ought to be thankful for the pardon received, and a crime repeated displeases doubly.
</p>

<p id="cap11-6">
[1] On another occasion a certain noblewoman, devoted to God, came to the saint to lay bare her sorrow before him and to beg for a remedy.
[2] She had a husband of great cruelty, whom she endured as an adversary in the service of Christ; and so she asked the saint to pray for him, that God might deign in His mercy to soften his heart.
[3] He, hearing this, said to her: "Go in peace, and await without doubt the consolation that will soon come to you from your husband."
[4] And he added: "You shall tell him, on behalf of God and of me, that now is the time of mercy, and afterward the time of justice."
[5] Having received his blessing, the woman returned, found her husband, and delivered the word.
[6] <ref to="Acts 10:44">The Holy Spirit fell upon him,</ref> <ref to="Eph 4:2">and, made new out of the old, he answered thus with all meekness:</ref> "My lady, <ref to="Josh 22:27; Gen 19:19">let us serve the Lord and save our souls."</ref>
[7] And so, at the urging of his holy wife, they led a celibate life for many years, and on one and the same day they both passed to the Lord.
[8] Truly to be marveled at in the man of God was the power of the prophetic spirit, by which he both restored vigor to limbs already withered and imprinted piety upon hardened hearts,
[9] though no less to be wondered at is the clarity of that same spirit, by which he so foreknew the outcome of things to come that he even searched out the hidden depths of consciences, as though, like a second Elisha, <ref to="4 Kgs 2:9">he had obtained the double spirit of Elijah </ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap11-7">
[1] For when at Siena he had foretold to a certain man dear to him certain things that were finally to come to pass, that learned man, of whom mention was made above—he who sometimes conferred with him concerning the Scriptures—having heard these words, sought to inquire of the same holy father, in doubt, whether he himself had spoken those things which he had learned from that man's report; [2] not only did he affirm that he had spoken them, but to the questioner he even foretold by prophecy, concerning another's fate, his own end. [3] And that he might imprint this the more surely upon his heart, he both wondrously laid bare, by revealing it, a certain secret scruple of that man's conscience, which the aforesaid man had disclosed to no living soul, and wholesomely cut it away by his counsel. [4] To the confirmation of all these things it is added that this religious man came to his final end just as the servant of Christ had foretold to him.
</p>

<p id="cap11-8">
[1] At that same time, when he was returning from across the sea, having as his companion Brother Leonard of Assisi, it happened that, worn out and weary, he mounted a little donkey for a while. [2] His companion, following behind and himself not a little tired, began to say within himself, suffering a touch of merely human feeling: [3] "His parents and mine were not playing as equals. Behold, he rides, while I on foot lead his donkey." [4] <ref to="Matt 1:20">While he was thinking these things</ref>, the holy man at once dismounted from the donkey and said: "It is not fitting, brother, that I should ride and you should come on foot, for you have been nobler and more powerful in the world than I." [5] At this the brother was struck with amazement, and, flushed with shame, recognizing that he had been found out, <ref to="Esth 8:3; Mark 5:22">he fell at his feet</ref> and, bathed in tears, laid bare his hidden thought and begged forgiveness.
</p>

<p id="cap11-9">
[1] A certain brother, devoted to God and to the servant of Christ, turned over often in the depths of his heart a single thought: that the man whom the holy man embraced with intimate affection must surely be worthy of grace from on high, while the one he regarded as a stranger ought to be counted by God among those outside the number of the elect. [2] Driven again and again by the goad of this very reflection, he longed intensely for the familiar friendship of the man of God, yet revealed the secret of his heart to no one; whereupon the loving father, gently summoning him to his side, addressed him thus: [3] "Let no thought trouble you, my son, for holding you most dear among those especially dear to me, I gladly bestow upon you the gift of my friendship and love." [4] The brother, astonished at this and grown from devout to more devout still, not only increased in his love for the holy man but was also heaped with greater gifts through the bounty of the grace of the Holy Spirit. [5] Now while he was staying enclosed in his cell on Mount Alverna, one of his companions earnestly desired to have some writing concerning the words of the Lord, briefly noted down in his own hand. [6] For he believed that by this means he might escape a grievous temptation that beset him — not of the flesh but of the spirit — or at the very least bear it more lightly. [7] Wasting away under such longing, he was inwardly anguished, since, overcome by bashfulness, he dared not disclose the matter to the reverend father. [8] But what the man did not tell him, the Spirit revealed. [9] For he ordered the aforesaid brother to bring him ink and paper, and with his own hand he wrote the Praises of the Lord according to the brother's desire, and at the end his blessing, saying: "Take this little paper for yourself, and guard it diligently until the day of your death." [10] The brother received that longed-for gift, and at once all that temptation was put to flight. [11] The letter was preserved, and since afterward it wrought wondrous things, it became a testimony to the powers of Francis.
</p>

[8] Foretelling likewise, in this same manner and with unshakable truth, the ruin of many <ref to="1 Cor 10:12">who seemed to stand firm</ref>, and also the conversion to Christ of very many who were perverse, he <ref to="Wis 7:26">seemed to have drawn near to the contemplation of the mirror of eternal light</ref>, by whose wondrous splendor the gaze of his mind beheld things bodily absent as though they were present.

[2] When one of them, sheltering behind a certain cloak of self-justification, refused to submit himself to discipline, the holy man, perceiving this in spirit, summoned one of the brothers and said to him: [3] "I have seen, brother, the devil upon the back of that disobedient brother, gripping his neck tightly; subdued by such a rider, having spurned the bridle of obedience, the brother followed wherever his promptings reined him. [4] And when I prayed to God for the brother, the demon, confounded, suddenly departed. [5] Go therefore and tell the brother to bow his neck without delay beneath the yoke of holy obedience!" [6] Admonished through this messenger, the brother at once turned to God and humbly threw himself at the vicar's feet.

[2] But arriving and not finding him—for he had already withdrawn from public view to his cell—they were departing in dismay. [3] And behold, as they were leaving, though by no human means could he have perceived anything of their arrival or departure, contrary to his accustomed manner he came forth from his cell, called out after them, and, just as they had wished, made the sign of the cross and blessed them in the name of Christ.
</p>

[3] And when he answered, "Quite well indeed," the father added, "Take heed, brother, lest under the guise of humility you tell a lie. For I know, I know; but wait a little while and you shall see." [4] The brother marveled greatly at how he had come to know through the spirit things so far removed. [5] <ref to="Luke 15:13">And so, not many days after,</ref> the one who had given offense to his brother, scorning the religious life, went forth abroad; nor had he sought pardon from the father, nor received the discipline of correction that was his due. [2] That this came to pass by divine ordinance must be believed, so that from the marvelous apparition of his bodily presence it might shine forth plainly that the man whose spirit, present and penetrating, stood near to the gates of eternal wisdom's light— [3] that wisdom which, more mobile than all things that move, and reaching everywhere by reason of her purity, <ref to="Wis 7:24">passes through the nations into holy souls and makes them friends of God and prophets.</ref> [4] For the exalted Teacher is wont to unfold his mysteries <ref to="Matt 11:25">to the simple and to little ones,</ref> as he appeared of old in David, the most excellent of the prophets, and afterward in Peter, the prince of the Apostles, and at last in Francis, the little poor man of Christ. ## Chapter XII - On the Power of His Preaching and the Grace of Healings <a id="cap12"></a>

<p id="cap12-1">
[1] Truly the faithful servant and minister of Christ, Francis, that he might accomplish all things faithfully and perfectly, devoted himself above all to those exercises of virtue which, at the prompting of the Holy Spirit, he had come to know were more pleasing to his God. [2] Hence it happened that he fell into a great agony of doubt, which on many days, returning from prayer, he set before the brothers who were closest to him, that it might be resolved. [3] "What," he said, "brothers, do you counsel? What do you commend? That I give myself to prayer, or that I go about preaching? [4] For I, a little one, <ref to="2 Cor 11:6">simple and unskilled in speech,</ref> have received a greater grace for praying than for speaking. [5] In prayer, moreover, there seems to be gain and an accumulation of graces; in preaching, a kind of distributing of the gifts received from heaven; [6] in prayer, too, a purification of the inner affections and a uniting to the one, true, and highest good, together with the strengthening of virtue; [7] in preaching, a dusting of the feet over spiritual things, a distraction amid many concerns, and a slackening of discipline. [8] Finally, in prayer we address God and listen to Him and, living as it were an angelic life, dwell among the Angels; [9] in preaching we must employ much condescension toward men, and living humanly among them, must think, see, say, and hear human things. [10] But there is one thing on the contrary that seems to outweigh all these before God: namely, that the only-begotten Son of God, who is the highest wisdom, <ref to="John 1:18">came down from the bosom of the Father for the salvation of souls,</ref> [11] that He might instruct the world by His own example and speak the word of salvation to men, <ref to="Eph 5:26">whom He redeemed at the price of His sacred blood and cleansed in the laver</ref> and sustained with the cup, reserving nothing at all for Himself that He did not lavishly bestow for our salvation. [12] And since we ought to do all things according to the pattern of those things <ref to="Exod 25:40">which we behold in Him as on a lofty mountain,</ref> it seems more pleasing to God that, my repose set aside, I go forth to labor." [13] And although for many days he turned over words of this kind with the brothers, he could not with certainty perceive which of these he ought to choose as truly the more acceptable to Christ. [14] <ref to="Rev 19:10">For though through the spirit of prophecy he knew wondrous things,</ref> he was unable by himself to resolve this question clearly, since God provided better, so that the merit of preaching might be made manifest through a heavenly oracle, and the humility of Christ's servant might be preserved.
</p>

<p id="cap12-2">
[1] The true Lesser Brother, who had learned great things from the supreme Master, did not blush to seek small things from lesser men. [2] For with singular zeal he was wont to inquire by what way and by what manner he might serve God more perfectly according to His own good pleasure. [3] This was his highest philosophy, this his supreme desire as long as he lived: to seek from the wise and the simple, the perfect and the imperfect, the young and the aged, how he might more virtuously attain to the summit of perfection. [4] And so, taking two of the brothers, he sent them to brother Sylvester, who had seen a cross issuing from his mouth and was then continually devoting himself to prayer on the mountain above Assisi, that he might seek out a divine answer concerning a doubt of this kind, which he was to convey to him on the Lord's behalf. [5] He charged the same upon the holy virgin Clare, that through some purer and simpler one of the virgins living under her discipline, and she herself praying together with the other sisters, <ref to="Luke 12:47">she might seek out the Lord's will concerning this matter</ref>. [6] Now the venerable priest and the virgin consecrated to God agreed wonderfully upon this very thing, the Spirit revealing it to them from on high: namely, that it was of the divine good pleasure that the herald of Christ should go forth to preach. [7] And so, when the brothers returned and made known the will of God according to what they had received, <ref to="John 21:7">rising up at once he girded himself</ref>, and with no delay whatever incurred, he set out upon the road. [8] He went, moreover, with such fervor to carry out the divine command, and ran on so swiftly, as though, <ref to="Ezek 1:3; 4 Kgs 3:15">the hand of the Lord being upon him</ref>, he had put on a new strength from heaven.
</p>

<p id="cap12-3">
[1] As he drew near to Bevagna, then, he came to a certain place where a very great multitude of birds of various kinds had gathered together. [2] When the holy man of God saw them, he ran eagerly to the spot and greeted them as though they shared in reason. [3] And as they all waited and turned themselves toward him—so that those perched in the bushes, with their heads inclined as he approached, fixed their gaze upon him in an unwonted manner—he came right up to them and earnestly urged them all to hear the word of God, [4] saying: "My brother birds, you ought greatly to praise your Creator, who has clothed you in feathers and granted you wings for flying, who has given you the purity of the air and governs you without any care on your part." [5] Now while he spoke these and similar things to them, the little birds, exulting in a marvelous fashion, began to stretch out their necks, to spread their wings, to open their beaks, and to gaze upon him intently. [6] And he, passing through their midst with wondrous fervor of spirit, touched them with his tunic, yet not one of them stirred from its place until, after he had made the sign of the cross and granted them leave with the blessing of the man of God, they all flew away together. [7] All these things his companions, waiting on the road, beheld. [8] When the simple and pure man returned to them, he began to accuse himself of negligence, because until then he had never preached to the birds.
</p>

<p id="cap12-4">
[1] Setting out from there to preach through the neighboring regions, he came to a fortified town called Alviano, where, when the people had gathered and silence had been called for, he could scarcely be heard on account of the swallows nesting in that same place and chattering with their loud din. [2] These the man of God addressed, while all listened, saying: "My sisters the swallows, now it is time <ref to="Tob 12:20">that I too should speak</ref>, for up to this moment you have said quite enough. [3] <ref to="Ezek 6:3">Hear the word of God!</ref>, <ref to="2 Chr 36:21">and keep silence until the discourse of the Lord is complete </ref>." [4] And they, as though endowed with understanding, fell silent at once, and did not stir from the spot until the whole sermon was finished. [5] Therefore all who saw it, filled with amazement, <ref to="Acts 3:10; Matt 9:8">glorified God</ref>. [6] The fame of this miracle, spreading far and wide on every side, kindled many to reverence for the Saint and to devotion in the faith.
</p>

<p id="cap12-5">
[1] In the city of Parma there was a certain student of good disposition who, diligently absorbed in his studies along with his companions, found himself harassed by the unwelcome chattering of a swallow, and so <ref to="Luke 4:21">he began to say </ref> to his companions:
[2] "This swallow is one of those that once troubled Francis, the man of God, while he was preaching, <ref to="Matt 22:34">until he imposed silence upon them </ref>."
[3] And turning to the swallow, he said with confidence: <ref to="Acts 16:18">"In the name of Francis, the servant of God, I command you </ref> to come to me at once and fall silent."
[4] And when it heard the name of Francis, the swallow, as if schooled in the discipline of the man of God, immediately grew still and entrusted itself to his hands as to a safe keeping.
[5] Astonished, the student at once restored it to freedom, and heard its chattering no more.
</p>

<p id="cap12-6">
[1] On another occasion, when the servant of God was preaching at Gaeta upon the seashore, <ref to="Luke 5:1">the crowds pressed in upon him out of devotion</ref> that they might touch him; and Christ's servant, recoiling from so great an acclaim of the people, leapt alone into a single little boat that lay close against the shore. [2] But that vessel, as though moved within by some pilot endowed with reason, without any oarsman, while all looked on in wonder, drew itself out farther from the land. [3] And when it had withdrawn some distance into the deep of the sea, it stood unmoving amid the waves for as long as the holy man preached to the crowds that awaited him on the shore. [4] And when, having heard the sermon and beheld the miracle and received the blessing, the multitude withdrew, so as to trouble him no further, the little boat returned of its own guidance to the land. [5] Who then could be of mind so obstinate and impious as to despise the preaching of Francis, by whose wondrous power it came to pass that not only creatures lacking reason received instruction, but even inanimate bodies served the preacher as though they were living things?
</p>

<p id="cap12-7">
[1] Indeed, He who had anointed and sent him was present with His servant Francis <ref to="Ruth 1:16">wherever he went</ref>, the <ref to="Isa 61:1; Luke 4:18">Spirit of the Lord</ref> and <ref to="1 Cor 1:24">Christ Himself, the power and wisdom of God</ref>, so that he overflowed with words of sound doctrine and shone forth with miracles of mighty power. [2] For his word was like a burning fire, piercing the depths of the heart, and it filled the minds of all with wonder, since it made no show of the embellishment of human invention, but breathed the fragrance of divine revelation. [3] Indeed, on one occasion, when he was about to preach before the Pope and the cardinals, at the suggestion of the lord of Ostia he had committed to memory a certain sermon carefully composed; yet when he stood in their midst to set forth words of edification, he so forgot it all that he was utterly unable to utter anything. [4] But when he had recounted this with truthful humility, betaking himself to invoking the grace of the Holy Spirit, he suddenly began to pour forth words so effective and to bend the minds of those exalted men to compunction with such mighty power, that it was made plainly clear that it was not he himself, <ref to="Acts 6:10">but the Spirit of the Lord that was speaking</ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap12-8">
[1] And because he had first persuaded himself by deed of what he urged upon others by word, he feared no critic and proclaimed the truth with the utmost confidence. [2] He knew not how to coddle the faults of any, but rather to prick them; not to indulge the life of sinners, but to strike at it with harsh rebuke. [3] With the same steadiness of mind he spoke to the great and to the small, and with the same gladness of spirit he spoke to the few and to the many. [4] Every age and every sex hastened both to behold and to hear this new man given to the world from heaven. [5] He himself, traveling through diverse regions, preached the Gospel ardently, <ref to="Mark 16:20">the Lord working with him and confirming the word with the signs that followed</ref>. [6] For by the power of His name Francis, the herald of truth, <ref to="Acts 4:7; Mark 16:17">cast out demons</ref>, <ref to="Luke 9:2">healed the sick</ref>, [7] and—what is greater—by the efficacy of his preaching he softened the minds of the obstinate to penance, and at once restored health to bodies and to hearts, as certain of his deeds attest, to be set down below by way of example.
</p>

<p id="cap12-9">
[1] In the city of Tuscanella, having been devoutly received as a guest by a certain knight, he raised up by his hand the man's only son, who had been crippled from birth, at the father's urgent entreaty, and so suddenly restored him to health that, while all looked on, <ref to="Acts 3:7">at once all the limbs of his body were made strong</ref>, and the boy, made whole and vigorous, <ref to="Acts 3:8">immediately rose up walking and leaping and praising God </ref>. [2] In the city of Narni, when at a bishop's request he had signed with the sign of the cross, from head to foot, a certain paralytic deprived of the use of all his limbs, he restored him to perfect health. [3] In the diocese of Rieti, a certain boy, so swollen for four years that he could in no way see his own legs, was presented to him with tears by his mother; and at once, as soon as the holy man touched him with his sacred hands, he was made sound. [4] At the city of Orte, a boy so doubled together that he carried his head pressed against his feet and had several broken bones, when at his parents' tearful prayers he received the sign of the cross from the saint, was suddenly stretched out straight and on the spot set free.
</p>

<p id="cap12-10">
[1] A certain woman of the city of Gubbio had both her hands so crippled and withered that she could accomplish nothing with them; but when he had made the sign of the cross over her in the name of the Lord, she obtained so perfect a recovery that, returning home at once, she prepared food with her own hands for his service and for that of the poor, like a second mother-in-law of Simon. [2] To a certain girl in the town of Bevagna, who had been deprived of the light of her eyes, he restored the longed-for sight when, in the name of the Trinity, he had three times anointed her eyes with his spittle. [3] A certain woman of the city of Narni, stricken with blindness of the eyes, received the sign of the cross from him and recovered the sight she desired. [4] At Bologna a certain boy had one of his eyes so clouded by a film that he could see nothing at all by it, nor could it be helped by any remedy; but after the servant of the Lord had made the sign of the cross over him from head to foot, he recovered sight so clear that, having afterward entered the Order of Friars Minor, he would declare that he saw far more distinctly with the eye that had once been diseased than with the one that had always been sound. [5] In the town of San Gemini the servant of God was received as a guest by a certain devout man, <ref to="Matt 15:22">whose wife was tormented by a demon</ref>; after he had offered up his prayer, he commanded the demon in the power of obedience to come out, and by divine might he put it to flight so suddenly that it became truly evident that the stubbornness of demons cannot withstand the power of holy obedience. [6] In Città di Castello a certain raging and wicked spirit was besieging a woman; receiving the command of obedience from the holy man, it departed in indignation, leaving the woman it had formerly possessed free in mind and body alike.
</p>

<p id="cap12-11">
[1] A certain brother was so dreadfully afflicted by an illness that many were convinced it was rather the harassment of a demon than any natural malady. [2] <ref to="Mark 9:19"> For often his whole body was dashed about and he rolled, foaming </ref>, the limbs of his body now contracted, now stretched out, now folded, now twisted, now made rigid and hard. [3] At times, wholly stretched out and stiff, with his feet brought level to his head, he would be lifted up on high, only to fall back at once in horrible fashion. [4] Full of mercy, the servant of Christ took pity on this man, so wretchedly and incurably sick, and sent him a small morsel of the bread of which he himself was eating. [5] And the taste of that bread bestowed upon the sick man such power that thereafter he felt no trouble from this infirmity. [6] In the district of Arezzo a certain woman had labored in childbirth for many days and was already near to death, and to one despairing of life there remained no remedy whatsoever save God alone. [7] But when the servant of Christ, carried on horseback because of his bodily weakness, was passing through those parts, it happened that the animal was led back through the village in which the woman lay in torment. [8] When the people of the place saw the horse on which the holy man had ridden, they drew off its bridle to lay it upon the woman; and at that wondrous touch, with every danger removed, the woman straightway gave birth in safety. [9] <ref to="Acts 10:2"> A certain man of Castello della Pieve, devout and God-fearing </ref>, kept by him a cord with which the holy Father had been girded. [10] And when a multitude of men and women in that town were suffering from various infirmities, he would go through the houses of the sick, and dipping the cord in water, he gave it to the afflicted to drink; and in this way very many were healed. [11] Moreover, when the sick tasted of the loaves that had been touched by the man of God, they swiftly obtained, by the working of divine power, the remedies of health.
</p>

<p id="cap12-12">
[1] As the herald of Christ shone forth in his preaching amid these and many other wondrous miracles, <ref to="Judg 2:4">heed was given to the things he spoke as though an angel of the Lord were speaking</ref>.
[2] For there was in him a surpassing prerogative of virtues, a <ref to="Rev 19:10">spirit of prophecy</ref>, an efficacy in miracles, an oracle concerning preaching given from heaven, the obedience of creatures devoid of reason, a vehement transformation of hearts at the hearing of his words, his instruction from the Holy Spirit beyond all human teaching, the authority to preach granted by the Supreme Pontiff not without a revelation,
[3] and moreover the Rule, in which the form of his preaching is set forth, confirmed by that same Vicar of Christ,
[4] and indeed the seals of the most high King, impressed upon his body in the manner of a signet—all these, as ten testimonies, affirm beyond all doubt to the whole age that Francis, the herald of Christ, was both to be revered in his office and authentic in his teaching and admirable in his holiness, and that thereby, as a true messenger of God, he preached the Gospel of Christ.

## Chapter XIII - On the Sacred Stigmata <a id="cap13"></a>

<p id="cap13-1">
[1] It was the custom of Francis, that angelic man, never to be idle from the good; rather, after the manner of the celestial spirits upon Jacob's ladder, he either ascended toward God <ref to="Gen 28:12">or descended </ref> to his neighbor. [2] For he had so prudently learned to apportion the time granted him for merit, that one part he expended in laborious gains for his neighbors, and another he consecrated to the tranquil raptures of contemplation. [3] Thus, when according to the demands of place and time he had stooped to procure the salvation of others, leaving behind the disturbances of the crowds he would seek the secret places of solitude and a spot of quiet, where, more freely given over to the Lord, he might wipe away <ref to="Luke 10:11">whatever dust might have clung to him from his dealings among men </ref>. [4] And so, two years before he rendered up his spirit to heaven, with divine providence as his guide, <ref to="Matt 17:1">after manifold labors he was led apart to a high place </ref> which is called Mount Alverna. [5] When therefore, according to his accustomed manner, he had begun there to keep the Lenten fast in honor of the holy Archangel Michael, being flooded more abundantly than usual with the sweetness of heavenly contemplation and kindled with the more ardent flame of celestial desires, he began to feel more copiously the gifts of supernal infusions. [6] He was indeed borne aloft, <ref to="Prov 25:27">not as a curious searcher of the majesty, to be crushed by its glory </ref>, <ref to="Matt 24:45">but as a faithful and prudent servant </ref>, seeking out the good pleasure of God, to whom he longed with the highest ardor to conform himself in every way.
</p>

<p id="cap13-2">
[1] It was therefore impressed upon his mind by a divine oracle that, upon the opening of the book of the Gospel, it would be revealed to him by Christ what would be most acceptable to God in him and concerning him. [2] And so, after he had first prayed with great devotion, he had a companion—a man devoted to God and holy—take up the sacred book of the Gospels from the altar and open it in the name of the holy Trinity. [3] Indeed, since at the threefold opening of the book the Passion of the Lord always presented itself, the man full of God understood that, just as he had imitated Christ in the deeds of his life, so too he must be conformed to him in the afflictions and sorrows of his Passion, <ref to="John 13:1">before he should pass out of this world</ref>. [4] And although, on account of the great austerity of his past life and the continual bearing of the Lord's cross, he was now feeble in body, he was in no way terrified, but rather more vigorously stirred to endure martyrdom. [5] For there had grown up in him an unconquerable fire of love for the good Jesus, burning as lamps of fire and flame, <ref to="Song 8:6-7">so that many waters could not</ref> avail to quench his love, so mighty was it.
</p>

<p id="cap13-3">
[1] And so, while he was being borne aloft toward God by the seraphic ardors of his longing, and was being transformed by tender compassion into Him <ref to="Eph 2:4">who out of His exceeding charity </ref> willed to be crucified, [2] one morning around the feast of the Exaltation of the holy Cross, as he was praying on the mountainside, <ref to="Isa 6:2">he saw a Seraph with six wings, </ref> as fiery as they were radiant, descending from the heights of heaven. [3] And when in swiftest flight it had reached a place in the air near the man of God, there appeared between the wings the figure of a crucified man, with hands and feet stretched out in the manner of a cross and fastened to a cross. [4] Two wings were raised above his head, two were spread for flight, <ref to="Isa 6:2">and two veiled his whole body. </ref> [5] Seeing this, he was utterly astonished, and a joy mingled with sorrow filled his heart. [6] He rejoiced indeed at the gracious gaze with which he beheld himself regarded by Christ under the appearance of a Seraph, <ref to="Luke 2:35">but the fixing to the cross pierced his soul with the sword of compassionate sorrow. </ref> [7] He marveled exceedingly at the sight of so inscrutable a vision, knowing that the weakness of suffering could in no way agree with the immortality of a seraphic spirit. [3] At last he understood from this, by the Lord's revealing, that divine providence had set such a vision before his eyes precisely so that this friend of Christ might know beforehand that he was to be wholly transformed into the likeness of the crucified Christ, not by the martyrdom of the flesh, but by the burning of the mind. [9] As the vision then vanished, it left a marvelous ardor in his heart, but it also imprinted upon his flesh no less marvelous a figure of the marks. [10] <ref to="John 20:25">For straightway there began to appear in his hands and feet the marks of the nails, </ref> just as he had a little before beheld them in that figure of the crucified man. [11] For his hands and feet seemed pierced through the very middle with nails, the heads of the nails appearing on the inner part of the hands and the upper part of the feet, <ref to="Mark 15:39">and their points standing out on the opposite side; </ref> [12] and the heads of the nails in the hands and feet were round and black, while the points themselves were elongated, bent back, and as if hammered down, rising from the flesh itself and protruding beyond the rest of the flesh. [13] His right side too, as though pierced by a lance, was covered with a red scar, which often, pouring forth sacred blood, sprinkled his tunic and undergarments.
</p>

<p id="cap13-4">
[1] Now when the servant of Christ perceived that the marks so vividly imprinted upon his flesh could not be hidden from his close companions, yet fearing nonetheless to publish abroad the Lord's hidden gift, he was set in a great wrestling of uncertainty, namely whether he should speak of what he had seen or keep silent. [2] Accordingly he called together certain of the brothers, and speaking in general terms, he laid his uncertainty before them and sought their counsel. [3] But one of the brothers, Illuminato both in grace and in name, understanding that he had seen some wondrous things—because he appeared utterly astonished—said to the holy man: [4] "Brother, know that the divine mysteries are at times shown to you not for your own sake alone, but for the sake of others as well. [5] It is therefore rightly to be feared that, if you conceal what you have received for the benefit of many, you may be judged blameworthy <ref to="Matt 25:25">for the talent hidden away</ref>." [6] Moved by these words, the holy man—though he was otherwise wont to say, <ref to="Isa 24:26">My secret is my own</ref>—nevertheless then, and with much fear, recounted the course of the aforesaid vision, [7] adding that the one who had appeared to him had spoken certain things which, so long as he lived, he would disclose to no man. [3] Indeed it is surely to be believed that the words of that sacred Seraph, wondrously appearing upon the cross, were so secret <ref to="2 Cor 12:4">that perhaps it was not lawful for men to utter them</ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap13-5">
[1] After true love of Christ had thus <ref to="2 Cor 3:18">transformed the lover into the same image</ref>, and the span of forty days had been completed in solitude, just as he had resolved, with the feast of the Archangel Michael now drawing near, the angelic man Francis came down from the mountain, [2] bearing with him the likeness of the Crucified—not fashioned by an artisan's hand upon tablets of stone or wood, <ref to="Matt 17:9; Exod 31:18">but inscribed upon his fleshly limbs by the finger of the living God</ref>. [3] And since <ref to="Tob 12:7">it is good to keep hidden the secret of the King</ref>, the man who shared in that royal secret concealed those sacred seals with all his strength. [4] Yet because it belongs to God to reveal, for His own glory, the great things that He does, the Lord Himself, who had impressed those seals in secret, openly displayed certain miracles through them, so that the hidden and wondrous power of those stigmata might be made plain by the manifest brightness of signs.
</p>

<p id="cap13-6">
[1] For in the province of Rieti a most grievous pestilence had grown strong, which so cruelly consumed all the oxen and sheep that no remedy could be applied. [2] But <ref to="Job 1:1">a certain man who feared God</ref> was warned in the night by a vision to hasten to the friars' hermitage and to take the water in which the hands and feet of God's servant Francis—who at that time was dwelling there—had been washed, and to sprinkle it over all his animals. [3] <ref to="Mark 16:9">Rising therefore in the morning</ref>, he came to the place, and having secretly obtained such washing-water through the holy man's companions, he sprinkled the ailing sheep and oxen with it. [4] Wonderful to tell! The moment the sprinkling touched, however slightly, the sickly beasts lying upon the ground, they at once recovered their former vigor, rose up immediately, and, as though they had felt nothing of their illness, hastened to the pastures. [5] And so it came to pass that, by the marvelous power of that water which had touched the sacred wounds, every affliction ceased utterly, and the pestilent disease was put to flight from the flocks.
</p>

<p id="cap13-7">
[1] In the region of the aforesaid Mount Alverna, before the holy man took up his dwelling there, a violent hailstorm—issuing from a cloud that rose up from the mountain itself—would habitually lay waste the fruits of the earth. [2] But after that blessed apparition, to the no small wonder of the inhabitants, the hail ceased, so that the very face of heaven, grown serene beyond its custom, might proclaim both the excellence of that celestial vision and the power of the stigmata impressed in that place. [3] It also happened that, in the wintertime, owing to his bodily weakness and the roughness of the roads, he was carried on the donkey of a certain poor man, and passed the night beneath the overhang of a jutting rock, that he might by some means escape the hardships of the snow and the night that had come upon them; for, hindered by these, he had been unable to reach a place of lodging. [4] Now when the holy man perceived that this man, murmuring with plaintive groans, was tossing himself this way and that—as one who, covered with only a thin wrap, could not rest because of the bitterness of the cold—being kindled with the fervor of divine love, he stretched out his hand and touched him. [5] A marvelous thing, surely! Suddenly, at the touch of that sacred hand, <ref to="Isa 6:6">which bore the burning of the seraphic coal,</ref> every chill was put to flight, and so great a warmth came upon the man within and without, as if some fiery force had issued upon him from the vent of a furnace. [6] For at once, strengthened both in mind and in body, he slept more sweetly among the rocks and the snows until morning than he had ever reposed in his own bed—as he himself afterward declared. [7] It is therefore established by sure tokens that those sacred seals were impressed by the power of Him who, by His seraphic working, purges, illumines, and inflames, [8] since outwardly, by purging away pestilence, they conferred upon bodies health, serenity, and warmth with wondrous efficacy, [9] just as was also shown more plainly after his death by marvels to be set down hereafter in their proper place.
</p>

<p id="cap13-8">
[1] He himself, though <ref to="Matt 13:44">like a treasure found in a field</ref> he strove with great diligence to conceal it, nevertheless could not so hide it but that some saw the stigmata of his hands and feet, even though he kept his hands almost always covered, and from that time onward went about with his feet shod. [2] For while he yet lived very many friars saw them, and although, by reason of the man's outstanding sanctity, they were in all things worthy of belief, yet to remove every doubt they confirmed by oath, with the most sacred objects touched, that it was so and that they had seen it. [3] Certain cardinals also saw them, by reason of the familiarity they had with the holy man, and they truthfully wove the praises of the sacred stigmata into the proses, hymns, and antiphons which they composed in his honor, <ref to="John 1:7; John 5:33">men who bore witness to the truth both in word and in writing</ref>. [4] The Supreme Pontiff, the lord Alexander, too, while preaching to the people in the presence of many friars and of myself, affirmed that, during the Saint's lifetime, he had beheld those sacred stigmata with his own eyes. [5] At his death more than fifty friars saw them, and the virgin most devoted to God, Clare, together with the rest of her Sisters, and countless lay folk, of whom—as shall be told in its proper place—<ref to="1 John 1:1">very many both kissed him out of the affection of devotion and handled him with their hands</ref>, for the confirmation of their testimony. [6] But the wound in his side he concealed so anxiously that, while he lived, no one could behold it except by stealth. [7] For one friar, who was accustomed to wait upon him assiduously, when he had with pious caution prevailed upon him to draw off his tunic to shake it out, looking attentively saw the wound, and applying three fingers to it with a swift touch, came to know the extent of the wound both by sight and by touch. [8] With like caution that friar also saw it who was at that time his Vicar. [9] But the companion friar, of admirable simplicity, while for the sake of the illness he was rubbing his ailing shoulders, with his hand passed through the hood and falling by chance upon the sacred wound, inflicted great pain on him. [10] On this account he wore from that time breeches so fashioned that they reached up to his armpits, to cover the wound in his side. [11] The friars too, who from time to time washed those garments or shook out the tunic, because they found them reddened with blood, came beyond doubt, by this evident sign, to the knowledge of the sacred wound, [12] <ref to="2 Cor 3:18">which afterward, when his face was unveiled in death</ref>, they themselves, together with very many others, at once contemplated and venerated.
</p>

<p id="cap13-9">
[1] Up now, most valiant soldier of Christ, bear the arms of that most invincible Leader; armed with them in conspicuous fashion you shall overcome every adversary! [2] Bear the banner of the most high King, at the sight of which all the warriors of the divine army may take heart! [3] Bear likewise the seal of Christ, the supreme pontiff, <ref to="Titus 2:8">by which your words and deeds, as beyond reproach </ref> and authentic, may deservedly be received by all! [4] For now, on account of the stigmata of the Lord Jesus, which you carry in your body, <ref to="Gal 6:17">let no one trouble you </ref>; rather, every servant of Christ is bound to be devoted to you with all affection. [5] Now by these most certain signs—proved not merely by two or three witnesses to satisfaction, but by very many to overflowing abundance—<ref to="Deut 19:15; Ps 92:5">the testimonies of God concerning you and through you, made exceedingly credible, </ref> strip from the faithless every veil of excuse, [6] while they establish believers in faith, lift them upward by the confidence of hope, and kindle them with the fire of charity.
</p>

<p id="cap13-10">
[1] Now indeed the first vision has been fulfilled, <ref to="Dan 9:24; Dan 4:6">which you beheld </ref>, namely that you, destined to be a leader in the warfare of Christ, were to be adorned with heavenly arms and with the badge of the sign of the cross. [2] Now at the very beginning of your conversion, the vision of the Crucified—piercing you inwardly with the sword of compassionate sorrow—and likewise the hearing of the voice proceeding from the cross as from a throne and from the sublime and hidden mercy-seat of Christ, <ref to="Luke 2:35; Num 7:89">are believed beyond doubt to have been true </ref>, just as you affirmed in your sacred testimony. [3] Now in the progress of your way of life, both the cross which Brother Silvester saw issuing marvelously from your mouth, and the swords transfixing your inmost being in the manner of a cross, which the holy Pacificus beheld; [4] and your being lifted up into the air after the figure of the cross, while Saint Anthony was preaching on the title of the cross—just as the angelic man Monaldus perceived—are truly believed and confirmed to have been seen not by a fantastical vision but by a heavenly revelation. [5] Now at last, near the end, the fact that there was shown to you at once both the sublime likeness of a Seraph and the humble image of the Crucified, kindling you within and sealing you without as another Angel ascending from the rising of the sun, <ref to="Rev 7:2">you who bear within yourself the sign of the living God </ref>, <ref to="John 5:33">both lends firmness of faith to the things foretold and receives from them the testimony of truth </ref>. [6] Behold, now that seven appearances of the cross of Christ in you and around you have been marvelously displayed and shown according to the order of time, you have arrived, as it were by six steps, at this seventh, in which you might finally come to rest. [7] For the cross of Christ—first set before you and then taken up at the beginning of your conversion, and thereafter borne in the progress of your way of life through a most exemplary existence, continually displayed in your very self and held up as an example to others—has shown with such radiant certainty that you have at last attained the summit of evangelical perfection, [8] that no one truly devout may cast aside this demonstration of Christian wisdom, engraved upon the dust of your flesh; [9] no one truly faithful may assail it, no one truly humble may make light of it, <ref to="1 Tim 1:15; 1 Tim 4:9">since it is truly set forth by divine power and worthy of all acceptance </ref>.

## Chapter XIV - Concerning His Patience and His Passage in Death <a id="cap14"></a>

<p id="cap14-1">
[1] And so, <ref to="Gal 2:19">now nailed to the cross with Christ</ref> in flesh as in spirit, Francis not only burned with seraphic love toward God, but also thirsted, together with Christ crucified, for the multitude of those to be saved. [2] Since, because of the nails grown out upon his feet, he could no longer walk, he had his deadened body carried <ref to="Luke 8:1">through cities and villages</ref>, that he might rouse the rest to take up the cross of Christ. [3] To the brothers, too, he would say: "Let us begin, brothers, to serve the Lord our God, for up to now we have made but little progress." [4] He blazed, moreover, with a great longing to return to the first beginnings of humility, to minister to lepers as he had done at the outset, and to recall his body—now broken down by toil—to its former servitude. [5] He resolved, with Christ as his leader, to accomplish mighty things, and though his limbs were failing, strong and fervent in spirit he hoped, in a fresh contest, for triumph over the enemy. [6] For neither weariness nor sloth has any place where the goad of love ever drives one on to greater things. [7] So great, indeed, was the harmony of his flesh with his spirit, so great the readiness of its obedience, that while he strove to attain to every holiness, his flesh not only offered no resistance but even endeavored to run on ahead.
</p>

<p id="cap14-2">
[1] But that the heap of his merits might increase for the man of God—merits which all attain their true perfection in patience—he began to suffer under afflictions of every kind, so grievously that scarcely a single limb remained in him free from the immense anguish of suffering. [2] At last he was brought, through various, long-drawn-out, and unceasing maladies, to such a state that, <ref to="Job 19:20; Lam 4:8">his flesh now wasted away, only skin seemed to cling to his bones</ref>. [3] And though he was wrung by the harsh pains of his body, he did not reckon those torments of his by the name of punishments, but of sisters. [4] But once, when he was pressed by the stings of his sufferings more severely than usual, a certain simple brother said to him: "Brother, <ref to="Sir 38:9">pray the Lord</ref> that He deal more gently with you, <ref to="Ps 31:4">for He seems to lay His hand upon you more heavily than is due</ref>." [5] Hearing this, the holy man cried out with a groan and said: "Were I not aware of the simple purity within you, from this moment I would shrink from your company, you who have dared to pass reproachful judgment upon the divine judgments concerning me." [6] And although he was utterly worn down by the long duration of his grave sickness, he cast himself upon the ground and dashed his feeble bones in the hard fall. [7] And kissing the earth, he said: "I give You thanks, <ref to="John 11:41">O Lord God</ref>, for all these pains of mine; and I ask You, my Lord, that, if it please You, You add a hundredfold; [8] for this will be most acceptable to me, that, afflicting me with pain, <ref to="Job 6:10">You do not spare me</ref>, since the fulfillment of Your holy will is to me a consolation more than full." [9] For this reason it seemed to the brothers that they beheld, as it were, a second Job, in whom, as the weakness of the flesh increased, the vigor of his mind increased together with it. [10] He himself, moreover, foreknew his death long beforehand, and as the day of his passing drew near, <ref to="2 Pet 1:14">he told the brothers that the tabernacle of his body must be laid down</ref> very soon, just as had been revealed to him by Christ.
</p>

<p id="cap14-3">
[1] And so, when two years had passed since the impressing of the sacred stigmata—that is, in the twentieth year from his conversion—and he had been squared by the many proving blows of afflicting infirmities, like a stone destined to be set in the building of the heavenly Jerusalem, and like wrought work brought to perfection beneath the hammer of manifold tribulation, he asked to be carried to Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, so that <ref to="Heb 10:29">where he had received the spirit of grace</ref>, <ref to="Gen 6:17">there he might render up the spirit of life</ref>.
[2] When he had been brought there, that he might show, after the example of Truth, that he had nothing in common with the world, in that grievous illness which closed in upon him with every weakness, he cast himself wholly stripped upon the bare ground in fervor of spirit, so that in that last hour, in which the enemy could yet rage, he might wrestle naked with the naked foe.
[3] Lying thus upon the earth, his sackcloth garment laid aside, <ref to="Job 11:15">he raised his face to heaven in his accustomed manner</ref>, <ref to="Acts 7:55">and intent wholly upon that glory</ref>, he covered the wound in his right side with his left hand, lest it be seen.
[4] And he said to the brothers: <ref to="3 Kgs 19:20">"I have done what is mine to do</ref>; what is yours may Christ teach you."
</p>

<p id="cap14-4">
[1] As the saint's companions wept, pierced by the wondrous dart of compassion, one of them, whom the man of God called his guardian, perceiving by divine inspiration what he desired, rose in haste and, taking up a tunic with its cord and breeches, offered it to the little poor man of Christ,
[2] saying: "I lend you this as I would to a beggar, and you shall receive it under the command of holy obedience."
[3] <ref to="Song 3:11">At this the holy man rejoiced and exulted in the gladness of his heart</ref>, for he saw that he had kept faith with his lady Poverty even unto the end, and, <ref to="2 Chr 6:13">lifting his palms to heaven</ref>, he magnified his Christ, because, unburdened of all things, he went free to Him.
[4] For he had done all this out of zeal for poverty, so that he might not even wish to own a habit unless it were lent him by another.
[5] He desired indeed to be conformed in all things to Christ crucified, who hung upon the cross poor and suffering and naked.
[6] For this reason, at the very beginning of his conversion, he had stood naked before the bishop, and at the close of his life he wished to depart from the world naked,
[7] and he enjoined upon the brothers attending him, by the obedience of charity, that when they saw him already dead they should leave him lying naked upon the ground for as long a span as one might gently walk the distance of a single mile.
[8] O man most truly Christian, who strove to be conformed by perfect imitation to the living Christ while living, to the dying Christ while dying, and to the dead Christ in death, and who merited to be adorned with His express likeness!
</p>

<p id="cap14-5">
[1] At last, as the hour of his passing drew near, he had all the brothers who were present in that place summoned to him, and, soothing them with words of consolation regarding his death, he urged them with fatherly affection toward the love of God. [2] He drew out a discourse on the keeping of patience, of poverty, and of fidelity to the holy Roman Church, setting the holy Gospel above all other rules of life. [3] Then, with all the brothers seated about him, <ref to="Gen 48:14">he stretched out his hands over them</ref> in the form of a cross, with his arms crossed over one another—because he had always loved this sign—and he blessed all the brothers, both those present and those absent, in the power and the name of the Crucified. [4] Moreover he added: "Farewell, all my sons; <ref to="Tob 2:14">remain in the fear of the Lord</ref> and abide in it always. [5] <ref to="Sir 27:6">And since a coming temptation and tribulation</ref> draws near, happy are those <ref to="Matt 10:22">who shall persevere</ref> in the things they have begun. [6] As for me, I hasten to God, to whose grace I commend you all." [7] When this gentle admonition was finished, the man most dear to God commanded that the book of the Gospels be brought to him, and he asked that the Gospel according to John be read to him, the one that begins at this place: <ref to="John 13:1">Before the festival day of the Passover</ref>. [8] And he himself, as best he could, broke forth into the words of this Psalm: With my voice I cried to the Lord, with my voice I made supplication to the Lord, and he completed it to the very end: The just, he said, wait for me, <ref to="Ps 141:2-8">until you reward me</ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap14-6">
[1] At last, when all the mysteries had been fulfilled in him, that most holy soul, released from the flesh and absorbed into the abyss of divine brightness, <ref to="Acts 7:60">the blessed man fell asleep in the Lord</ref>.
[2] Now one of his brothers and disciples beheld that blessed soul, <ref to="Rev 14:14">borne aloft upon a gleaming white little cloud in the likeness of a most radiant star</ref> <ref to="Ps 28:31">over many waters</ref>, carried straight upward into heaven on a direct path, as if shining with the brilliance of sublime holiness and at once filled with the abundance of heavenly wisdom and grace, by which the holy man merited to enter the place of light and peace, where he rests with Christ without end.
[3] At that time the minister of the brothers in the Terra di Lavoro was Brother Augustine, a man indeed holy and just, who, set in his final hour and having long since lost his power of speech, in the hearing of those who stood by, <ref to="Acts 23:4; Luke 9:39">suddenly cried out and said</ref>:
[4] "Wait for me, father, wait; behold, I am coming with you now!"
[5] When the brothers asked him, marveling greatly, to whom he was speaking so boldly, he answered: "Do you not see our father Francis, who is going to heaven?"
[6] And at once his holy soul, departing from the flesh, followed the most holy father.
[7] The bishop of Assisi had at that time gone for the sake of pilgrimage to the oratory of Saint Michael on Monte Gargano, to whom blessed Francis, appearing on the night of his passing, said: "Behold, <ref to="John 16:28">I leave the world and go to</ref> heaven."
[8] <ref to="Gen 24:54">Rising therefore in the morning</ref>, the bishop told his companions what he had seen, and returning to Assisi, after he had inquired carefully, he discovered with certainty that at that very hour, in which it had been made known to him through the vision, the blessed Father had departed from this world.
[9] The larks, birds that are friends of the light and shudder at the darkness of twilight, at the hour of the holy man's passing, when it was already the dusk of the night that was to follow, came in a great multitude above the roof of the house
[10] and, wheeling about for a long while with a certain unwonted jubilation, gave to the glory of the Saint, who had been accustomed to invite them to divine praise, <ref to="John 1:7">a testimony as joyful as it was manifest</ref>.

## Chapter XV - On His Canonization and Translation <a id="cap15"></a>

<p id="cap15-1">
[1] Francis, therefore, servant and friend of the Most High, founder and leader of the Order of Friars Minor, professor of poverty, model of penance, herald of truth, mirror of holiness, and exemplar of all evangelical perfection, was prevented by grace from on high and, by an ordered advance, rose from the lowest things to the highest. [2] This wondrous man—rich indeed in poverty, exalted in humility, vigorous in mortification, prudent in simplicity, and conspicuous for every uprightness of character—whom the Lord had made marvelously renowned in life, He made incomparably more renowned in death. [3] For when the blessed man departed from the world, <ref to="Eccl 12:5; Ps 35:10">that holy spirit, entering the house of eternity and made glorious by drinking its fill from the fountain of life,</ref> left behind <ref to="Rom 8:18">certain manifest signs of future glory upon his body,</ref> [4] so that that most holy flesh, <ref to="Gal 5:24; 2 Cor 5:17">which, crucified together with its vices, had now passed over into a new creature,</ref> might both display the likeness of Christ's Passion by a singular privilege and, by the novelty of a miracle, foreshow the appearance of the resurrection.
</p>

<p id="cap15-2">
[1] For there could be seen in those blessed members nails wondrously wrought from his own flesh by divine power, and so engrafted into that very flesh that, whenever they were pressed upon any side, they at once rebounded to the opposite side as though they were sinews continuous and hard. [2] There was also found, more plainly visible upon his body, the wound of the side—neither inflicted by human hand nor made by any blow—after the likeness of the pierced side of the Savior, which in our Redeemer brought forth the sacrament of human redemption and regeneration. [3] Now the semblance of the nails was black as iron, while the wound of the side was red and, drawn together into a certain roundness by the contraction of the flesh, appeared to be a most beautiful rose. [4] But the rest of his flesh, which before had inclined toward darkness both from infirmity and by nature, now gleaming with an exceeding whiteness, gave promise of the beauty of that second robe.
</p>

<p id="cap15-3">
[1] His limbs yielded so soft and pliant to the touch that they seemed to have been restored to the tenderness of childhood, and to certain beholders they appeared adorned with manifest signs of innocence. [2] Since, then, the nails showed dark against that flesh of dazzling whiteness, while <ref to="Sir 50:8">the wound in his side glowed red like a blossoming rose of spring</ref>, it is no wonder that so lovely and miraculous a variety of colors stirred delight and admiration in those who gazed upon it. [3] The sons wept for the loss of so beloved a Father, yet they were also suffused with no small gladness as they kissed upon him the seals of the supreme King. [4] The novelty of the miracle turned their lamentation into jubilation, and it swept their probing understanding away into wonder. [5] For so unwonted and so remarkable a spectacle was, to all who beheld it, both a confirmation of faith and a spur to love; while to those who heard of it, it was an occasion of wonder and a kindling of desire to see it.
</p>

<p id="cap15-4">
[1] When the passing of the blessed Father had been heard of, and the fame of the miracle had spread abroad, the people came hastening together to the place, that they might behold with the eyes of flesh that which would banish every doubt from reason and heap up joy for their affection. [2] And so a great many citizens of Assisi were admitted to contemplate those sacred stigmata with their eyes and to kiss them with their lips. [3] But one of them, a certain knight, lettered and prudent, Jerome by name, a man indeed renowned and celebrated, <ref to="John 20:27">since he had doubted concerning such sacred signs and was unbelieving</ref> like Thomas, more fervently and boldly, in the presence of the friars and the other citizens, moved the nails, [4] and with his own hands handled the Saint's hands, <ref to="1 John 1:1">feet, and side</ref>, so that, while he touched by palpation those true signs of Christ's wounds, he might cut away from his own heart and the hearts of all every "wound of doubt." [5] On account of which he too afterward became, among the others, a steadfast witness of this truth so certainly recognized, and, having touched the holy things, he confirmed it by oath.
</p>

<p id="cap15-5">
[1] The brethren and sons who had been summoned to the passing of their Father consecrated that night, in which the gracious confessor of Christ departed, with such divine praises, together with the whole multitude of the people, that it seemed not the funeral rites of the dead, but the watches of Angels. [2] <ref to="John 21:4; Acts 2:6"> But when morning came, the crowds that had gathered, </ref><ref to="John 12:13; Matt 21:8"> taking up branches of trees </ref> and multiplying the lights of their candles, bore the sacred body to the city of Assisi with hymns and canticles. [3] Passing also by the church of Saint Damian, in which that noble virgin Clare, now glorious in heaven, then dwelt enclosed with her virgins, they halted there a little while and offered the sacred body, marked with heavenly pearls, to those holy virgins to be gazed upon and kissed. [4] Coming at last to the city with rejoicing, they laid up the precious treasure they bore, with all reverence, in the church of Saint George. [5] For it was in that very place that as a small boy he had learned his letters, there that he afterward preached for the first time, and there at the last that he received his first resting place.
</p>

<p id="cap15-6">
[1] The venerable Father passed from the shipwreck of this world in the year of our Lord's Incarnation one thousand two hundred and twenty-six, on the fourth day before the Nones of October, on the evening of a Saturday, and was buried on the Lord's Day. [2] Yet at once the blessed man, while the regard of the divine countenance shone down upon him, began to flash forth with great and many miracles, so that the loftiness of his holiness—which, while he yet lived in the flesh, had become known to the world through examples of perfect righteousness for the amendment of conduct—might now, <ref to="Rev 20:4">while he reigns with Christ</ref>, be confirmed from heaven through the miracles of divine power unto every strengthening of the faith. [3] And when in diverse parts of the world his glorious miracles and the abundant benefits obtained through him kindled very many to devotion toward Christ and stirred them to reverence for the Saint himself—the tongues both of words and of works crying aloud—there reached the ears of the Supreme Pontiff, the lord Gregory the Ninth, those mighty works which God was accomplishing through his servant Francis.
</p>

<p id="cap15-7">
[1] Now since that same shepherd of the Church had come to know his marvelous holiness with full faith and certainty—not only from miracles heard after his death, <ref to="1 John 1:1">but also from things proven during his life, seen with his own eyes and handled with his own hands</ref>—and thus could in no way doubt that he had been glorified by the Lord in heaven, he resolved with devout deliberation, that he might act in harmony with Christ, whose Vicar he was, to render him celebrated upon the earth as one most worthy of every veneration. [2] And in order to bring full certainty to the whole world concerning the glorification of this most holy man, he had the miracles that had been discovered, recorded, and attested by suitable witnesses examined by those among the cardinals who seemed least disposed to favor the cause. [3] When these had been carefully scrutinized and approved by all, by the harmonious counsel and consent of his brethren and of all the prelates who were then at the curia, he decreed that he should be canonized. [4] And so, coming in person to the city of Assisi in the year of the Lord's Incarnation one thousand two hundred and twenty-eight, on the seventeenth day before the Kalends of August, a Sunday, amid the greatest solemnities, which it would be tedious to recount, he enrolled the blessed Father in the catalogue of the Saints.
</p>

<p id="cap15-8">
[1] In the year of our Lord one thousand two hundred and thirty, when the brothers had gathered for the General Chapter held at Assisi, that body consecrated to the Lord was translated to the basilica raised in his honor, on the eighth day before the Kalends of June. [2] Now while that sacred treasure was being carried over—sealed with the bull of the Most High King—he whose likeness it bore deigned to work many miracles, <ref to="Song 1:3">so that by his saving fragrance the affections of the faithful might be drawn to run </ref> after Christ. [3] Truly it was fitting that one whom God had found pleasing and beloved in his life, and had borne into paradise through the grace of contemplation like Enoch, [4] <ref to="4 Kgs 2:11">and had snatched up to heaven in a chariot of fire </ref> through the ardor of charity like Elijah, [5] should now—blossoming amid those heavenly flowers of the eternal planting—have those blessed bones breathe forth their fragrance from his resting place <ref to="Sir 50:8; Sir 46:14">with a wondrous flowering</ref>.
</p>

<p id="cap15-9">
[1] Moreover, just as this blessed man had shone forth in life through the wondrous tokens of his virtues, so too, from the day of his passing until the present, throughout the diverse regions of the world, he gleams with the resplendent marvels of miracles, as the divine power glorifies him. [2] For to the blind and the deaf, the mute and the lame, the dropsical and the paralytic, the demon-possessed and the leprous, the shipwrecked and the captive, remedies are bestowed through his merits, and relief is granted in every sickness, every need, and every peril. [3] But also, since through him many of the dead have been wondrously raised to life, <ref to="Luke 1:35">the magnificence of the power of the Most High</ref> <ref to="Ps 4:4">makes known to the faithful that He works wonders in His holy one</ref>, to whom be honor and glory through the infinite ages of ages. <ref to="Rom 16:27">Amen</ref>.
</p>

## I — And first concerning the virtues of the sacred stigmata <a id="mir1"></a>

<p id="mir1-1">
[1] About to set down, for the honor of Almighty God and for the glory of the Blessed Father Francis after his glorification in heaven, some of the approved miracles, I have judged that the beginning ought above all to be taken from that one in which the power of the cross of Jesus is displayed and its glory renewed. [2] Francis, then, the new man, shone forth by a new and astonishing miracle, when he appeared marked with a singular privilege not granted in ages past, namely <ref to="Phil 3:10; Rom 7:24">adorned with the sacred stigmata and conformed in the body of this death</ref> to the body of the Crucified. [3] Of which, whatever the human tongue may say, will fall short of worthy praise. [4] Indeed the whole zeal of the man of God, both public and private, turned upon the cross of the Lord; [5] and that he might mark his body outwardly with the sign of the cross, which had been imprinted upon his heart from the beginning of his conversion, enclosing himself in that very cross, he took up a habit of penance bearing the image of the cross, [6] so that, just as his mind within had put on the crucified Lord, so too his body might put on the armor of the cross, and that in the same sign by which God had vanquished the powers of the air, his army might wage war for the Lord. [7] But also from the beginning of the time when he had begun to serve the Crucified, diverse mysteries of the cross shone about him, as becomes more clearly known to one who considers the course of his life: [8] how, by the sevenfold apparition of the Lord's cross, he was, in thought as in affection and in deed, wholly transformed into the likeness of the Crucified through his own ecstatic love. [9] Worthily, therefore, the clemency of the most high King, condescending toward His lovers beyond all human reckoning, imprinted the standard of His cross to be borne upon his body, so that he who had been forestalled by a wondrous love of the cross might also be made wondrously glorious by the honor of the cross.
</p>

<p id="mir1-2">
[1] <ref to="Luke 24:39; Ps 92:5">For the unshakeable confirmation of this astonishing miracle there stand, witnessed by those who saw and touched and in every way worthy of belief</ref>, not only their testimonies, but also the wondrous apparitions and the powers that flashed forth after his death, all combining to drive every cloud of doubt from the mind. [2] For the lord Pope Gregory the Ninth, of happy memory—of whom the holy man, prophesying, had foretold that he would be raised to the apostolic dignity—before he enrolled the bearer of the cross's standard in the catalogue of the Saints, carried in his heart a certain scruple of doubt concerning the wound in the side. [3] But on a certain night, as that blessed prelate himself related with tears, the blessed Francis, his face set in a kind of stern severity, <ref to="Matt 1:20; Matt 2:13">appeared to him in his sleep</ref> [4] and, rebuking the hesitation of his heart, raised his right arm, uncovered the wound, and asked of him a vial in which to catch the welling blood that flowed from his side. [5] In the vision the Supreme Pontiff offered the vial that was asked, and it seemed to be filled to the very brim with the blood streaming from the side. [6] From that time he began to be moved with such devotion toward that sacred miracle, and to burn with such zeal, that he could in no way endure that anyone should presume to darken those most resplendent signs with proud assault, but would strike such a one with severe rebuke.
</p>

<p id="mir1-3">
[1] A certain friar of the Order of Minors, a preacher by office, eminent for the prerogative of his virtue and renown, and one to whom the Saint's stigmata had been firmly proven, began—while he was searching out within himself the rational ground of this miracle by human reasoning—to be teased by the prick of a certain doubt. [2] And so, when for many days, his carnal sense gathering strength, he was enduring a struggle of this kind, the holy Francis appeared by night to him as he slept, his feet caked with mud, humbly stern and patiently angry. [3] And he said, "What are these contests of conflict within you, <ref to="John 20:27">what is this filth of doubting? See my hands </ref>and my feet." [4] And although the friar saw the pierced hands, he did not see the stigmata of the muddied feet. [5] "Remove," he said, <ref to="John 20:25">"the mud from my feet and recognize the places of the nails </ref>." [6] And when the friar had reverently taken hold of them, <ref to="1 John 1:1">it seemed to him that he was wiping away the mud and handling with his own hands the places of the nails </ref>. [7] At once, as he awoke, he was bathed in tears, and he wiped away those former, in some sense muddied, sentiments both by the flowing of his tears and by public confession.
</p>

<p id="mir1-4">
[1] In the city of Rome there was a certain matron, noble in the brightness of her character and in the renown of her ancestry, who had chosen Saint Francis as her advocate, keeping his image painted in a private chamber, <ref to="Matt 6:6">where she would pray to the Father in secret</ref>. [2] One day, while she was devoting herself to prayer and contemplating the image of the Saint, she noticed that it lacked those sacred marks of the stigmata, and she began to grieve no little and to wonder. [3] But it was no wonder if that was absent from the painting which the painter had omitted. [4] And when for several days she had turned over in her anxious mind what the cause of such a defect might be, behold, suddenly one day those marvelous marks appeared in the painting, just as they are wont to be depicted in other images of that same Saint. [5] Trembling, she at once summoned her daughter, who was devoted to God, asking whether the image had been without the stigmata until then. [6] The daughter affirms it and swears that it had formerly stood without the stigmata, and that now it truly appeared with the stigmata. [7] Yet, because the human mind frequently drives itself to fall and calls truth back into doubt, a harmful uncertainty crept once more into the woman's heart, lest perhaps the image had been so marked from the beginning. [8] But the power of God, that the first miracle might not be despised, added a second as well. [9] For immediately those marks vanished, and the image remained stripped of its privileges, so that by the following sign there might be proof of the one that went before.
</p>

<p id="mir1-5">
[1] In Catalonia too, at Lérida, it happened that a certain man named John, devoted to blessed Francis, was walking one evening along a road on which an ambush lay hidden to inflict death—not indeed upon himself, who had no enemies, but upon another man who seemed to resemble him and was at that time in his company. [2] A certain man rising up from the ambush, supposing this man to be his enemy, struck him with so many wounds of the sword so mortally that no hope whatever remained of recovering his health. [3] For the first blow inflicted had all but severed his shoulder together with the arm, and another stroke beneath the breast had left so great an opening that the breath issuing thence would extinguish about six candles set together at once. [4] Therefore, since in the judgment of the physicians his cure was impossible—because, the wounds festering, there exhaled from them a stench so intolerable that even his own wife shrank from it in horror, and he could no longer be aided by any human remedies—he turned himself to the patronage of the blessed Father Francis, to be besought with as much devotion as he could muster, whom even amid the very blows he had most confidently invoked together with the blessed Virgin. [5] And behold, as the wretched man lay alone upon his bed of calamity, while wakeful and wailing he repeated again and again the name of Francis, there stood before him one in the habit of a Friar Minor, who had entered, as it seemed to him, through the window. [6] <ref to="Isa 40:26">Calling him by name </ref>, he said: "Because you have had confidence in me, behold, the Lord will deliver you." [7] And when the sick man asked of him who he was, he answered that he was Francis, and at once, drawing near, he loosed the bindings of his wounds and anointed him, as it seemed, with ointment over all the wounds. [8] But at once, as he felt the sweet touch of those holy hands, hands able to heal by the power of the stigmata of the Savior, the rottenness was driven out, the flesh restored, and the wounds made whole, and he was wholly restored to his former soundness. [9] When this was done, the blessed Father departed. [10] And he, feeling himself healed and bursting joyfully into a voice of divine praise and of blessed Francis, called his wife. [11] But she, running the more swiftly and now seeing standing the man whom she had believed would be buried on the morrow, was so violently terrified with amazement that she filled the whole neighborhood with her cries. [12] His people came running, and when they strove to set him back upon the bed as though he were a man out of his mind, while he on the contrary resisted, declaring and showing himself to be healed, they were struck with such astonishment that, as though all bereft of their senses, they believed what they saw to be a phantasm—for him whom a little before they had beheld torn by the most atrocious wounds and already wholly wasted away, they now saw delightful in full soundness of health. [13] To them the man who had been made whole said: "Do not be afraid; do not believe that what you behold is illusion, for Saint Francis has just now departed from this place, and by the touch of those holy hands has cured me wholly of every wound." [14] At length, as the fame of this miracle grew, all the people hastened thither, and seeing in so manifest a wonder the power of the stigmata of blessed Francis, they were filled at once with admiration and joy and extolled the standard-bearer of Christ with great proclamations of praise. [15] Worthily indeed did the blessed Father—now dead in the flesh and living with Christ—grant health to the mortally wounded man by the marvelous showing of his presence and the sweet touch of his holy hands, [16] since he bore within himself the stigmata of Him who, mercifully dying and wondrously rising, healed by the power of His own wounds the human race that had been <ref to="Luke 10:30">wounded and left half-dead </ref>.
</p>

<p id="mir1-6">
[1] At Potenza, a city of Apulia, there was a certain cleric named Roger, an honorable man and a canon of the cathedral church. [2] When, shaken by illness, he had one day entered a church to pray—a church in which there was painted an image of blessed Francis, displaying his glorious stigmata—he began to doubt the sublimity of that miracle as though it were something altogether unheard-of and impossible. [3] Suddenly, therefore, while wounded in mind he was inwardly entertaining these empty thoughts, he felt himself struck grievously in the palm of his left hand beneath his glove, hearing the sound of the blow, as when an arrow leaps forth from a crossbow. [4] At once, as much pierced by the wound as stunned by the sound, he drew the glove from his hand, that he might discern by sight what he had perceived by touch and by hearing. [5] And although there had previously been no blow upon the palm, he beheld in the middle of his hand a wound as if inflicted by the strike of an arrow, from which there proceeded such a force of burning that he seemed to be fainting away from it. [6] A marvel to tell! No trace appeared upon the glove, so that the pain of the wound answered to the hidden injury of a heart hiddenly stricken. [7] Thereafter for two days he cried out and roared, goaded by the most grievous pain, and he unfolded to all the veil of his unbelieving heart. [8] He confesses and swears that he truly believes the sacred stigmata to have been in Saint Francis, testifying that the phantasms of all doubt had departed. [9] He prays as a suppliant that the Saint of God might come to his aid through the sacred stigmata, and he enriches the many prayers of his heart with a great flood of tears. [10] A wonder indeed! With unbelief cast off, the healing of his body follows the healing of his mind. [11] All pain subsides, the burning grows cold, no trace of the blow remains, and thus it came about that, by the providing clemency of heaven, the hidden infirmity of the mind was cured through the open cautery of the flesh, and, his mind being healed, the flesh likewise was healed as well. [12] The man becomes humble, devoted to God, subject by perpetual familiarity to the Saint and to the Order of the brothers. [13] This so solemn a miracle was confirmed by oaths, and notice of it was brought to us in letters fortified with the protection of the bishop's seal. [14] Concerning the sacred stigmata, then, let there be no place for any ambiguity, and in this matter let no one's <ref to="Matt 20:15">eye be evil, because God is good </ref>, as though the bestowal of a gift of this kind were not fitting to His everlasting goodness. [15] <ref to="1 Cor 12:12; Eph 1:22">For if, by that seraphic love, many members should cleave to Christ the head </ref>, so that they might be found worthy of like armor in a like war and might be raised up to like glory in the kingdom, <ref to="Mark 5:15">no one of sound mind </ref> would say that this pertained to anything but the glory of Christ.
</p>

<p id="mir2-1">

[2] But as the clergy gathered by night to chant the funeral rites and the vigils with their Psalters, suddenly, while all looked on, the woman raised herself up upon her bed and called to one of the priests standing by—her own godfather—saying: "I wish to confess, father; hear my sin!
[3] For I, having died, was to be handed over to a harsh prison, because there was a sin that I owe to you which I had not yet confessed.
[4] But because Saint Francis," she said, "to whom, while I lived, I gave service with a devoted mind, prayed for me, it has now been granted me to return to my body, so that, with that sin disclosed, I may earn everlasting life.
[5] And behold, while you watch, once I have laid that sin bare, I shall hasten to the promised rest."
[6] Trembling, then, she made her confession to the trembling priest, and <ref to="Acts 7:60">after receiving absolution she settled herself quietly upon the bed and happily fell asleep in the Lord</ref>.
</p>

<p id="mir2-2">
[1] In the town of Pomarico, set among the mountains of Apulia, a father and mother had an only daughter, in her tender years tenderly cherished above all. [3] And so, when kinsfolk and friends had gathered for what was all too mournful a funeral, the unhappy mother lay overwhelmed by unutterable griefs and swallowed up in the deepest sorrow, taking no notice whatever of what was being done. [4] Meanwhile Saint Francis, accompanied by but a single companion, appeared and deigned to visit the desolate woman, whom he had perceived to be devoted to him, and addressing her with kindly words: [5] <ref to="Luke 7:13">"Weep not,"</ref> he said, "for the light of your lamp, which you lament as extinguished, shall by my intercession be restored to you." <p id="mir2-3">

[3] A marvelous thing! At these words the boy rose up, and forbidding their lamentation, declared that he, having died and been drawn out of his body, had been led away and brought back again through blessed Francis.
</p>

<p id="mir2-4">

[2] His mother, who had not yet gone far, suspecting at the sound of the falling that her child had plunged down, swiftly returned, and finding her son so suddenly snatched away by so pitiable a mishap, at once laid avenging hands upon herself and with sorrowful cries roused the whole neighborhood to lamentation. [3] But a certain friar named Raho, of the Order of Minors, who had betaken himself thither to preach, drew near to the boy and, full of faith, said to the father: [4] <ref to="Acts 17:31">\of God, can raise your son from the dead—do you believe it?</ref> for the sake of that love which he ever bore toward Christ, who was crucified to restore life to mankind?" [6] When this was done, the boy began to yawn a little, and, opening his eyes and lifting his arms, he raised himself up and at once walked unharmed before them all, restored at the same moment to life and to health through the wondrous power of the Saint.

<p id="mir2-5">
[1] In the city of Capua, while a certain boy was sporting with several companions along the bank of the river Volturno, he fell heedlessly into the depths, and <ref to="Ps 45:5">the rushing of the river</ref>, swiftly swallowing him, buried him dead beneath the sand. [2] At the outcry of the boys who had been playing with him beside the river, a crowded multitude gathered there. [3] And while the whole assembled people humbly and devoutly invoked the merits of blessed Francis—that, looking upon the faith of the parents who were devoted to him, he might deign to snatch the child from the peril of death—a certain swimmer who was standing at a distance, having heard the cries, drew near. [4] And after a search, when at last he had called upon the aid of blessed Francis, he found the spot where the mud had covered the boy's corpse in the manner of a tomb; and digging him out and carrying him forth, he gazed upon the dead child in grief. [6] And even the Jews who had come, moved by natural pity, were saying: "Saint Francis, restore the boy to his father!" [7] Suddenly the boy, while all rejoiced and marveled, rose up unharmed, and humbly begged to be led to the church of blessed Francis, that he might devoutly render thanks to him by whose power he knew himself to have been wondrously raised.

## III - Concerning Those Whom He Delivered from the Peril of Death <a id="mir3"></a>

<aside>

<p id="mir3-2">

[1] At Corneto, when certain devout men had gathered at the friars' dwelling for the casting of a bell, a little boy of eight years, Bartholomew by name, brought along a small gift for the friars on behalf of the laborers. [2] And behold, a sudden and violent wind, <ref to="Job 1:19">shaking the house</ref>, hurled the heavy and great door of the gate upon the little boy himself with so mighty a force that, crushed beneath so vast a weight, he was thought to have been shattered by a fatal blow. [3] For he lay so utterly entombed beneath the weight that nothing of him appeared from without. [4] All who were present came running, invoking the mighty right hand of blessed Francis. [5] His very father too, who, his limbs stiffened with grief, could not so much as move himself, offered his son to Saint Francis with vows and with his voice. [6] At length the deadly weight was lifted from off the boy, and behold, the one whom they had believed dead appeared joyful, as though roused from sleep, displaying upon himself no injury whatsoever. [7] And so, when he was fourteen years old, he became a Friar Minor, afterward a learned man and a famous preacher.
</p>

<p id="mir3-6">
[1] The men of Lentino cut a huge stone from the mountain, which was to be set upon the altar of a certain church of blessed Francis that was soon to be consecrated. [2] But when nearly forty men were striving to hoist that stone onto a wagon, and had time and again renewed their efforts, the stone fell upon one of the men and covered him over like a tomb. [3] Yet, with their minds confounded and not knowing what to do, the greater part of the men despaired and went away. [4] But ten men who remained, calling upon Saint Francis with mournful voices, beseeching him not to suffer a man to perish so horribly in his very service; [5] then, taking heart again, they shifted the stone with such ease that no one could doubt the power of Francis had been present. [6] The man rose up unharmed in all his limbs, and moreover recovered the clear light of his eyes, which before had been darkened, so that thereby it might be given to all to understand how mighty in power are the merits of blessed Francis in matters past all hope.
</p>

<p id="mir3-7">
[1] Something of the same kind happened at San Severino in the March of Ancona. [2] For while an enormous stone, brought from Constantinople, was being hauled by the strength of many men toward the basilica of blessed Francis, it suddenly broke loose and rolled down upon one of those who were drawing it. [3] And though he was believed not merely dead but utterly crushed to pieces, blessed Francis stood beside him and lifted the stone away, so that, the weight of the rock thrown off, he sprang up sound and unharmed, without any injury at all.
</p>

<p id="mir3-8">
[1] Bartholomew, a citizen of Gaeta, while toiling no little to build a certain church of blessed Francis, was gravely battered when a beam, poorly fixed, came crashing down and crushed his neck. [2] Feeling death looming over him, and being a faithful and devout man, he asked a certain friar for the viaticum. [3] But that friar, unable to fetch it so swiftly—since the man was thought to be dying at any moment—uttered to him the saying of blessed Augustine: "Believe, and you have eaten." [4] Yet on the following night blessed Francis appeared to him with eleven friars, and carrying a little lamb against his breast, he approached his bed and called him by name, saying: "Bartholomew, <ref to="Gen 4:17; Gen 15:1">do not be afraid </ref>, <ref to="Jer 1:19">for he shall not prevail against you </ref>—the enemy who sought to hinder you in my service. [5] This is the Lamb whom you begged might be given to you, whom indeed you received through your good desire, by whose power also you shall obtain the well-being of both the inner and the outer man." [6] And so, drawing his hand across the wounds, he bade him return to the work he had begun. [7] Rising very early, and appearing whole and joyful to those who had left him half-dead, he filled them with wonder and astonishment, [3] and by his own example as much as by the Saint's miracle he stirred their minds to reverence and love for the blessed Father.
</p>

<p id="mir3-9">
[1] A certain man from the town of Ceperano, Nicholas by name, <ref to="Judg 15:18">fell into the hands of cruel enemies</ref> upon a certain day. [2] With a savage, beastlike cruelty they heaped wound upon wound, hacking him down, and they raged against the wretched man until they believed him either already dead or about to die forthwith. [3] But the aforesaid Nicholas, as he received the first blows, had cried out with a loud voice: "Saint Francis, come to my aid! Saint Francis, help me!" [4] This cry many heard from afar off, though they could bring no help. [5] At last, borne home and wholly soaked in his own blood, he confidently declared <ref to="Luke 2:26">that he would not see death from those wounds</ref>, and that even then he felt no pain, for Saint Francis had come to his aid, and had obtained from the Lord <ref to="Mark 6:12">that he might do penance</ref>. [6] And the outcome that followed confirmed this; for once washed clean of his blood, he was at once, against all human hope, made well.
</p>

<p id="mir3-10">
[1] The son of a certain nobleman in the town of San Gimignano, gripped by a severe sickness and despaired of any further hope of recovery, was brought at last to his very end. [2] For a stream of blood flowed from his eyes, just as it is wont to gush from a vein in the arm; and with the other true signs of approaching death appearing throughout the rest of his body, he was reckoned as good as dead. Indeed, from the weakness of his spirit and the failure of his faculties of sense and motion, he seemed to have utterly departed this life. [3] When, according to custom, his kinsfolk and friends had gathered for the mourning, and were now occupied solely with the burial, the father himself, placing his trust in the Lord, ran with hurried step to the church of blessed Francis that had been built in that same town, and, with his belt slung about his throat, <ref to="Jdt 10:20">with all humility cast himself down upon the ground</ref>. [4] <ref to="1 Kgs 1:11; Job 40:22">And thus making vows and multiplying his prayers</ref>, with sighs and groans he won the right to have the holy Francis as his patron before Christ. [5] So the father, returning at once to his son and finding him restored to health, turned his <ref to="Lam 5:15">mourning into joy</ref>.
</p>

<p id="mir3-11">
[1] <ref to="Prov 16:4">The Lord wrought something similar through the merits of the Saint</ref> in the case of a certain girl in Catalonia, from the town named Tamarit, and another from Ancona,[2] who, when through the excess of their sickness they had been brought to their last breath, were at once restored to perfect health by blessed Francis, faithfully invoked by their parents.
</p>

<p id="mir3-12">
[1] A certain cleric of Vico Albo, Matthew by name, having <ref to="Mark 16:19">drunk a deadly poison</ref>, was so gravely afflicted that, unable to speak in any way, he awaited only his final passing. [2] A certain priest urged him to make his confession, yet could not wring a single word from him. [3] He, however, <ref to="Ps 13:1">in his heart</ref> humbly prayed to Christ that, through the merits of blessed Francis, He might deign to snatch him from the jaws of death; [4] and presently, as one strengthened by the Lord, he uttered the name of blessed Francis with faithful devotion before the witnesses who stood by, and, the poison cast forth, gave thanks to his deliverer.
</p>

## IV - On Those Delivered from Shipwreck <a id="mir4"></a>

<p id="mir4-1">
[1] Certain sailors, finding themselves in great peril upon the sea, when they were some ten miles distant from the harbor of Barletta, and as the tempest grew exceedingly violent, already despairing of their lives, let down their anchors. [2] But as the sea swelled ever more furiously beneath <ref to="Ps 10:7">the spirit of the storms,</ref> their cables broke and their anchors were abandoned, and they were carried wandering over the waters in an uncertain and erratic course. [3] At length, the sea being calmed by divine command, they prepared themselves with all their strength to recover the anchors, whose cables floated upon the surface above. [4] But since they were unable to accomplish this by their own strength, having called upon the aid of many Saints and being already drenched with much sweat, they could not recover even a single one throughout the whole day. [5] Now there was present a certain sailor, Perfectus by name but imperfect in his ways, who said to his companions with a kind of mockery: "Behold, you have invoked the help of all the Saints, and, as you see, there is none who comes to your aid. [6] Let us call upon this Francis, who is a new Saint, to see whether he might somehow plunge himself into the sea and restore the lost anchors." [7] The others assented to the urging of Perfectus, not in mockery but in earnest, and rebuking his derisive word, they made a freely-given vow to the Saint; [8] and at once, in a moment, without any assistance, the anchors floated up upon the waters, as though the very nature of iron had been turned into the lightness of wood.
</p>

<p id="mir4-2">
[1] A certain pilgrim, frail of body on account of the lingering effects of a most acute fever which he had endured before, was being carried by ship as he came from the lands beyond the sea. [2] He too was borne toward blessed Francis with a singular devotion of affection, and had chosen him as his advocate before the heavenly King. [3] And so, not yet fully freed from his illness, when he was tormented by the burning pangs of thirst, and the water had already given out, he began to cry out with a loud voice: "Go boldly, draw a drink for me, for blessed Francis has filled my little vessel with water." [4] A marvel indeed! They found the vessel filled with water, which before had been left empty. [5] But on another day, <ref to="Matt 8:24">when a storm had arisen and the ship was being covered by the waves </ref> and shaken by mighty squalls, so that they now feared shipwreck, this same sick man suddenly began to cry out aloud throughout the ship: [6] "Rise up, all of you," he said, "and go to meet blessed Francis as he comes! Behold, he is here to save us," [7] and so, falling upon his face with a great cry and with tears, he <ref to="Luke 5:12; John 9:38">worshipped </ref>. [8] At once, upon the vision of the Saint, the sick man recovered his full health, <ref to="Matt 8:26">and a calm of the sea </ref> followed.
</p>

<p id="mir4-3">
[1] Brother James of Rieti, while crossing a certain river in a small boat with other brothers, had already set his companions ashore and was at last making ready to disembark himself. [2] But when that flimsy craft was overturned by mishap, the boatman swam free, yet the brother was plunged into the depths. [3] The brothers standing on the bank called upon blessed Francis with heartfelt prayers, beseeching him with tearful groans to come to the aid of his son. [4] The brother too, sinking down within the belly of that vast and devouring whirlpool, cried out with his heart as best he could—since his mouth was unable—imploring the help of his loving Father. [5] And behold, with the presence of the blessed Father coming to his aid, he walked through the deep as if upon dry land, and seizing the sunken boat, he reached the shore together with it. [6] A marvel to tell! His garments were not soaked, nor did a single drop of water so much as touch his tunic.
</p>

<p id="mir4-4">
[1] A brother named Bonaventure, while sailing across a certain lake with two other men, found the little vessel broken open by the force of the inrushing water, and was plunged into the depths together with the ship and his companions. [2] But when, <ref to="Ps 39:3">from the lake of misery,</ref> they called with great confidence upon the merciful father Francis, the boat, though filled with water, suddenly floated up again, and with them aboard—the Saint himself providing safe conduct—it came soundly to harbor. [3] In like manner a certain brother from Ascoli, drowned in a river, was set free by the merits of Saint Francis. [4] So too on the lake of Rieti, when certain men and women had fallen into a like peril, <ref to="Ps 31:6">at the invocation of the name of Saint Francis they escaped soundly from the perilous shipwreck of the many waters.</ref>
</p>

<p id="mir4-5">
[1] Certain sailors of Ancona, tossed about by a stormy tempest, already foresaw the peril of going down to the deep. [2] And when, thus despairing of life, they suppliantly called upon Saint Francis, a great light appeared in the ship, and with that light a calm was granted from on high, as though the blessed man, by his wondrous power, could command both the winds and the sea. [3] With how many prodigies of miracles this blessed Father has shone, and continues to shine, upon the sea, how often he has there brought help to the despairing, I deem it in no way possible to recount one by one. [4] Nor is it any wonder that to one now reigning in heaven dominion over the waters has been bestowed, since to him, even while he dwelt in this mortal life, every bodily creature, restored to its first condition, marvelously rendered service.
</p>

## V — Concerning Those Set Free from Bonds and Prisons <a id="mir5"></a>

<p id="mir5-1">
[1] In Romania it befell a certain Greek lord's servant to be falsely accused of theft. [2] The lord of that land ordered him shut up in a narrow prison and bound with heavy chains; but <ref to="Matt 18:27">the lady of the house, taking pity on the servant </ref>, whom she firmly believed innocent of the fault laid upon him, pressed her husband with devout entreaties for the man's release. [3] Yet since her husband's obstinate hardness would not yield, the lady turned as a suppliant to Saint Francis, and by a vow commended the innocent man to his loving care. [4] <ref to="Matt 25:36">At once the helper of the wretched was present and mercifully visited the man set in prison </ref>. [5] He loosed the chains, broke open the prison, took the innocent man by the hands, led him outside, and said: "I am he to whom your lady devoutly entrusted you." [6] And when the man was seized with great fear, and was casting about for some way down the towering cliff over its yawning chasm, suddenly, by the power of his deliverer, he found himself on level ground, and returned to his lady; [7] and recounting in order the truth of the miracle, he kindled the devout lady more ardently still to the love of Christ and to reverence for his servant Francis.
</p>

<p id="mir5-2">
[1] In Massa San Pietro a certain poor man owed a sum of money to a knight. [2] When, through his destitution, he had no means of paying it, the debtor was seized by the knight who demanded repayment, and he begged him humbly to take pity on him, seeking a postponement for the love of blessed Francis. [3] The haughty knight scorned the entreaties offered him and emptily made light of the Saint's love as though it were some empty thing. [4] For, answering stiff-necked, he said: "I shall shut you up in such a <ref to="Gen 41:10">place and thrust you into such a prison </ref> that neither Francis nor anyone else will be able to help you." [5] He attempted what he had said. He found a dark dungeon, into which he cast the man bound in chains. [6] A little while later Saint Francis was at hand, and, having broken the prison and shattered the fetters, he led the man back unharmed to his own home. [7] Thus the might of Francis, having despoiled the proud knight, <ref to="Matt 6:13; 2 Tim 4:18">delivered from evil </ref> the captive who had commended himself to him, and by a wondrous miracle transformed the knight's insolence into meekness.
</p>

<p id="mir5-3">
[1] When Albert of Arezzo was held in the tightest of bonds on account of debts unjustly demanded of him, he humbly commended his innocence to Saint Francis. [2] Indeed, he greatly cherished the Order of the Friars Minor, and among the Saints he venerated Saint Francis with a special affection. [3] But his creditor said, with blaspheming voice, <ref to="Dan 3:17">that neither Francis nor God could deliver him out of his hands</ref>. [4] And so it came to pass on the vigil of Saint Francis that, when this prisoner had eaten nothing but, for love of the Saint, had given his own food to a certain pauper, as night drew on Saint Francis appeared to him while he was awake. [5] At his entrance the fetters fell from his feet and the chains from his hands, <ref to="Acts 12:7">the doors opened of their own accord</ref>, the planks of the floor sprang loose, and the man departed free, returning to his own home. [6] From that time he fulfilled his vow, fasting on the vigil of blessed Francis, and adding each year one ounce to the candle which he was accustomed to offer annually, as a token of his ever-increasing devotion.
</p>

<p id="mir5-4">
[1] While the lord Gregory the Ninth occupied the see of blessed Peter, a certain man named Peter, from the city of Alife, was accused of heresy, seized at Rome, and by command of that same Pontiff handed over to the bishop of Tivoli to be kept in custody. [2] The bishop, receiving him under pain of losing his bishopric, bound him in fetters and had him shut up in a dark prison, that he might not escape, <ref to="Ezek 4:16">giving him bread by weight and water by measure</ref>. [3] Now that man began to call upon blessed Francis with many prayers and tears to have mercy on him, for he had heard that the vigil of his feast was now at hand. [4] And since by the purity of his faith he had renounced every error of heretical depravity, and with his whole heart's devotion had clung to Francis, the most faithful servant of Christ, through the intercession of that saint's merits he was found worthy to be heard by the Lord. [5] For when the night of his festival was now upon them, about twilight blessed Francis came down in pity into the prison, and calling the man by his name, commanded him to rise up quickly. [6] <ref to="1 Chr 10:4">Stricken through with terror</ref>, the man asked who it might be, and heard that blessed Francis was present. [7] And when by the power of the holy man's presence he saw that the fetters of his feet had been shattered and had fallen away, that the planks of the prison had opened as the nails leapt out of their own accord, and that an open path lay before him to go out — yet, loosed and dumbfounded, he knew not how to flee, but crying out at the door, he threw all the guards into terror. [8] When they had reported to the bishop that the man had been freed from his chains, the devout pontiff, after grasping how the matter had unfolded, came to the prison, and clearly recognizing the power of God, <ref to="Mark 5:30; Gen 24:26">there adored the Lord</ref>. [9] The chains too were carried before the lord Pope and the cardinals, who, <ref to="Luke 23:47">seeing what had been done</ref>, <ref to="Luke 23:47">marveled greatly and blessed God</ref>.
</p>

<p id="mir5-5">
[1] Guidolottus of San Gimignano was falsely accused of having killed a certain man by poison, and of having resolved to destroy by the same kind of death that man's son and his entire household. [2] Seized accordingly by the local authority, weighed down with the heaviest of chains, he was shut up in a certain tower. [3] But he himself, placing his trust in the Lord on behalf of his innocence, of which he was certain, entrusted his cause to the patronage of blessed Francis, that it might be defended by him. [4] When therefore the authority was devising how to extract by torture a confession of the crime laid against him, and by what punishments to make him die once he had confessed, on that very night, when in the morning he was to be brought to torment, <ref to="Ps 125:2">he was visited by the presence of Saint Francis and encompassed until morning by an immense splendor of light, and filled with joy (</ref> and with great confidence, he received assurance of his escape. [5] In the morning the torturers came, and having led him out of prison they suspended him upon the rack, loading many weights of iron upon him. [6] He was let down many times and again raised up, so that, with one torment succeeding another, he might be driven the more swiftly to confess the crime. [7] But in the spirit of innocence he rejoiced in his countenance, displaying no sorrow amid the punishments. [8] Then a fire of no small size was kindled beneath him, and yet not a hair was harmed thereby, though his head hung down toward the ground. [9] At last, when he had been drenched with boiling oil, by the power of the patron to whom he had committed his defense he overcame all these things, and being thus released free and unharmed, he departed.
</p>

## VI - On Those Delivered from the Peril of Childbirth <a id="mir6"></a>

<p id="mir6-1">
[1] A certain countess in Slavonia, as distinguished by her nobility as she was a rival in virtue, burned with devotion toward Saint Francis, and toward the Friars with attentive piety. [2] At the time of childbirth, beset by harsh pains, she was tormented by such anguish that the imminent birth of her offspring seemed rather to be the present death of the mother. [3] It seemed that the infant could not be brought forth into life unless she breathed out her own life, and that by such striving she would not give birth but perish. [4] Into her heart came the renown of Saint Francis, his <ref to="Rev 19:1">power and glory</ref>; her faith was roused, her devotion enkindled. [5] She turned to her effective helper, her faithful friend, the solace of the devout, the refuge of the afflicted: [6] "Saint Francis," she said, <ref to="Ps 34:10">"all my bones beseech your mercy</ref>, and in my mind I vow what I cannot put into words." [7] O wondrous swiftness of mercy! The end of her speaking was the end of her suffering, the limit of her labor, the beginning of her delivery. [8] For at once, with every pang ceasing, she brought forth her child in safety. [9] She was not forgetful of her vow, nor did she shrink back from her purpose. [10] She had a certain beautiful church built, and once it was constructed she assigned it to the Friars in honor of the Saint.
</p>

<p id="mir6-2">
[1] In the region of Rome there was a certain woman named Beatrix, near to her time of delivery, who, having carried a dead child within her womb for four days, was tormented in her wretchedness by many anguishes and pressed by deadly pains. [2] The dead child was driving its mother toward death, and the stillborn one, not yet brought forth into the light, was bringing about its mother's open peril. [3] She sought the aid of physicians, <ref to="Ps 126:1">but every human remedy labored in vain</ref>. [4] Thus from the first curses something more abundant had fallen upon the wretched woman, so that, made the tomb of what she had conceived, <ref to="Jer 20:17; Job 3:21">she awaited for a certainty a tomb near at hand</ref>. [5] At last, committing herself with all devotion through messengers to the Friars Minor, she humbly and with full faith begged for some portion of the relics of Saint Francis. [6] It came to pass, by the divine will, that a small piece was found of the cord with which the Saint had once been girded. [7] Soon, as the cord was laid upon the suffering woman, all her pain was most easily relieved, the dead child that was the cause of death was brought forth, and her former health was restored.
</p>

<p id="mir6-3">
[1] The wife of a certain nobleman of Carvio, Juliana by name, was dragging out years of mourning over the death of her children and ceaselessly bewailing her unhappy lot, because all the sons whom she had borne in travail she had, after a brief interval, consigned to the grave with ever sharper grief. [2] <ref to="Matt 1:23">When therefore she had conceived in her womb a child of four months</ref>, and was more anxious, on account of past events, about the death of the offspring she had conceived than about its birth, she faithfully prayed to the blessed Father Francis for the life of the child not yet born. [3] And behold, <ref to="Matt 1:20">one night, as she slept, a certain woman appeared to her in a dream</ref>, bearing a beautiful little boy in her hands, and most joyfully offering him to her. [4] But when she refused to receive the one she at once dreaded to lose, that woman added: "Receive him without fear, for the one whom holy Francis, having compassion on your sorrow, sends to you shall live in life and rejoice in health." [5] Awaking at once, the woman understood through the vision shown her from heaven that the help of blessed Francis was at hand for her, and from then on, filled with more abundant joy, she multiplied her prayers and made promises of vows for the receiving of the offspring according to the promise. [6] At last the time of bearing was fulfilled, <ref to="Luke 1:57">and the woman brought forth</ref> a male child, who, flourishing in the vigor of youthful age, as though through the merits of blessed Francis he had received the nourishment of life, gave his parents an incitement to a more devout affection toward Christ and His Saint. [7] Something similar to this the blessed Father accomplished in the city of Tibur. [8] For when a certain woman had borne very many daughters, wearied with longing for male offspring, she redoubled her prayers and vows before holy Francis. [9] By his merits, therefore, that woman conceived, and to her who had entreated for one He granted to bear twins.
</p>

<p id="mir6-4">
[1] At Viterbo a woman near to childbirth was thought nearer still to death, tormented by anguish in her vitals and altogether wretched amid the misfortunes of women. [2] And when, the power of nature giving way, every resource of the medical art failed, upon her calling on the name of blessed Francis the woman was set free at once and brought her labor to a wholesome end; [3] but having obtained what she desired and forgetting the favor she had received, paying no honor to the Saint, <ref to="Lev 23:7">on the very day of his feast she stretched out her hand to servile works </ref><ref to="Prov 31:20"> she put forth her hand </ref>. [4] And behold, suddenly the right arm, extended for the work, remained rigid and withered. [5] When she strove to draw it back with the other hand, that one too withered away by a like vengeance. [6] Seized therefore with the fear of God, the woman renewed her vow, and the use of her limbs, which she had lost through ingratitude and contempt, she deserved to recover through the merits of the merciful and humble Saint, to whom she dedicated herself anew.
</p>

<p id="mir6-5">
[1] A certain woman from the region of Arezzo, having endured the perils of childbirth for seven days, and already turning black, was despaired of by all; so she made a vow to blessed Francis and, as she lay dying, began to call upon his aid.
[2] When the vow had been uttered, <ref to="Gen 28:12">she quickly fell asleep and saw in her dreams</ref> blessed Francis addressing her sweetly and asking whether she recognized his face, and whether she knew how to recite, in honor of that same glorious Virgin, the antiphon of the Virgin: "Hail, Queen of mercy."
[3] When she answered that she had knowledge of both, the Saint said: "Begin the sacred antiphon, and before you finish it, you shall give birth in safety."
[4] At this voice the woman awoke and, with trembling, began to say: "Hail, Queen of mercy."
[6] And as she implored those merciful eyes and called to mind the fruit of the virginal womb, she was at once freed from all her distress and brought forth a beautiful child, giving thanks to the Queen of mercy, who through the merits of blessed Francis had deigned to have mercy upon her.
</p>

## VII — Concerning the Blind Who Were Given Their Sight <a id="mir7"></a>

<p id="mir7-1">
[1] In the convent of the Friars Minor at Naples, when a certain friar named Robert had been blind for many years, an excess of flesh grew over his eyes, hindering the movement and use of his eyelids. [2] Now when many friars who travel abroad, bound for the various parts of the world, had gathered there, the blessed Father Francis, the very mirror of holy obedience, in order to spur them on their journey by the wonder of a miracle, healed the aforesaid friar in their presence in this manner. [3] One night the aforesaid friar Robert lay sick unto death, and already his soul had been commended to God, [4] when behold, the blessed Father stood beside him together with three friars perfected in all holiness, namely Saint Anthony, Friar Augustine, and Friar James of Assisi, who, as they had followed him perfectly while they lived, so too eagerly accompanied him after death. [5] Taking a small knife, Saint Francis cut away the excess flesh, restored his sight as of old, and brought him back from the very jaws of death, [6] and he said to him: "Robert, my son, the grace I have wrought with you is a sign to the friars going forth to distant nations, <ref to="Ps 39:3">that I will go before them and direct their steps</ref>. [7] Let them go," he said, "with joy, and carry out the obedience laid upon them with a cheerful heart."
</p>

<p id="mir7-2">
[1] At Thebes in Romania a blind woman, who had fasted on the vigil of Saint Francis upon bread and water alone, was led by her husband to the church of the Friars Minor in the early morning of the feast. [2] While Mass was being celebrated, at the elevation of the body of Christ she opened her eyes, saw clearly, and adored most devoutly. [3] And in that very act of adoration she cried out with a loud voice, saying: "Thanks be to God and to His Saint, for I behold the body of Christ!"—and all <ref to="Ps 46:2">who were present were turned to a voice of exultation</ref>. [4] <ref to="Luke 1:56">After the sacred rites were completed, the woman returned to her own home</ref> with joy of spirit and light in her eyes. [6] Indeed the woman rejoiced, not only because she had recovered the sight of bodily light, but also because that wondrous Sacrament, which is the true and living light of souls, she had been counted worthy—with the supporting power of faith—to gaze upon, chiefly through the merits of blessed Francis.
</p>

<p id="mir7-3">
[1] In Campania a certain boy of fourteen years, from the town of Pofi, seized by a sudden affliction, lost his left eye entirely. [2] The sharpness of the agony so thrust the eye from its place that for eight days, the nerve slackened, it hung outside the length of one finger down to his jaw, and had become almost shriveled. [3] But when nothing remained save its removal, and he had been utterly given up by the physicians, his father turned with his whole mind to the help of blessed Francis. [4] That tireless helper of the wretched did not fail the prayers of the suppliant. [5] For by a wondrous power he restored the withered eye to its place and to its former vigor, and illumined it with the rays of the longed-for light.

<p id="mir9-1">

<p id="mir10-1">
