# THE FIRST LIFE OF SAINT FRANCIS

## PROLOGUE <a id="prolog"></a>

<aside>
IN THE NAME OF THE LORD. AMEN.
HERE BEGINS THE PROLOGUE UPON THE LIFE OF BLESSED FRANCIS.
</aside>

<p id="prolog-1">
[1] Desiring to set forth in due order, with devout reverence—truth ever leading and guiding the way—the deeds and life of our most blessed father Francis, since <ref to="Acts 1:1">no man's memory holds in full all that he did and taught</ref>, I have striven, at the command of our lord and the glorious Pope Gregory, to unfold at least those things which I heard from his own mouth, or learned from faithful and approved witnesses, as best I could, though in unpolished words. [2] But would that I might deserve to be the disciple of one who ever shunned the riddles of speech and knew nothing of the trappings of words!
</p>

<p id="prolog-2">
[1] I have moreover divided into three little works all that I was able to gather concerning that blessed man, sorting every matter into its several chapters, lest the variety of times should confound the order of his deeds and cast the truth into doubt. [2] The first work, then, keeps to the order of the narrative and is devoted above all to the purity of his blessed manner of life and conduct, to his holy character and his salutary teachings. [3] Into it are also woven a few miracles out of the many which the Lord our God deigned to work through him while he yet lived in the flesh. [4] The second work recounts the deeds from the next-to-last year of his life up to his blessed passing; [5] while the third contains many miracles and passes over many more, which the most glorious Saint, reigning with Christ in heaven, works upon the earth. [6] It also relates the reverence, honor, praise, and glory which the blessed Pope Gregory, and with him all the cardinals of the holy Roman Church, most devoutly rendered to him, when they enrolled him in the catalogue of the saints. [7] Thanks be to almighty God, who ever shows himself wondrous and worthy of love in his saints.
</p>

<aside>
HERE ENDS THE PROLOGUE.
</aside>

## Chapter I - How He Conducted Himself in Worldly Dress and Worldly Spirit. <a id="op1-1"></a>

<aside>
THE FIRST LITTLE WORK
TO THE PRAISE AND GLORY OF ALMIGHTY GOD, THE FATHER AND THE SON AND THE HOLY SPIRIT. AMEN.
HERE BEGINS THE LIFE OF OUR MOST BLESSED FATHER FRANCIS
</aside>

<p id="1">
[1] There was in the city of Assisi, which lies within the borders of the Spoletan valley, a man named Francis, who from the earliest years of his life was reared by his parents in accordance with the vanity of the world, and who, having long imitated their wretched manner of life and their habits, became himself yet more vain and more dissolute.
[2] For this most evil custom has so taken root on every side among those who are reckoned by the Christian name, and this pernicious teaching has been so everywhere established and laid down, as though by public law, that they strive to raise their children, from the very cradle, all too slackly and loosely.
[3] For at first, when the little ones begin to speak or to babble, while they are scarcely yet truly born, they are taught certain shameful and utterly detestable things by gestures and by words; and when the time of weaning arrives, they are compelled to utter, and indeed to perform, things full of indulgence and wantonness.
[4] None of them dares, constrained by the fear belonging to his age, to conduct himself honorably, since for this he is subjected to harsh punishments.
[5] Well, therefore, did a secular poet say: "Because we have grown up amid the pursuits of our parents, all evils have pursued us from childhood."
[6] This testimony is true, since the wishes of parents are all the more hostile to their children the more happily they have been fulfilled.
[7] But also, when they have advanced a little further in age, of their own impulse they ever slip away to worse and worse deeds.
[8] For from a corrupted root a corrupt tree grows up, and what has once been badly perverted can scarcely be brought back to the rule of fairness.
[9] And when they begin to enter the gates of adolescence, what sort of persons do you suppose they become?
[10] Then indeed, drifting in every kind of dissipation, because they are permitted to fulfill whatever pleases them, they give themselves over with all eagerness to be the slaves of vile deeds.
[11] For thus, made by their own willing servitude the slaves of sin, they set forth all their members as weapons of iniquity, and displaying in themselves nothing of the Christian religion either in life or in conduct, they protect themselves with the name of Christianity alone.
[12] These wretches often pretend to have done worse things than they have actually done, lest they should seem the more contemptible the more innocent they are.
</p>

<p id="2">
[1] Such were the wretched beginnings amid which this man, whom today we venerate as a saint—for truly he is a saint—was caught up from his boyhood, so that until nearly the twenty-fifth year of his age he miserably squandered and consumed his time. [2] Indeed, advancing only too well in vanities beyond all his peers, he stood out all the more abundantly as a kindler of evils and an emulator of folly. [3] He was a marvel to all, and he strove to outstrip the rest in the pomp of empty glory—in jests, in idle pursuits, in scurrilous and worthless words, in ditties, in soft and flowing garments: [4] for he was exceedingly rich, not miserly but prodigal, no hoarder of money but a squanderer of his substance, a careful merchant yet a most vain spendthrift; a man nonetheless of more gracious conduct, very pleasing and affable, though to his own undoing. [5] Since for this reason above all many went trooping after him—abettors of evils and kindlers of crimes—and thus, hemmed in by throngs of the wicked, he went his way exalted and high-spirited, taking his path through the midst of the streets of Babylon, [6] until the Lord should look down from the heavens, and for His own name's sake drive His wrath far from him, and curb his mouth with His praise, lest he perish utterly. [7] And so came to pass that change wrought by the right hand of the Most High, that through him sinners might be given the confidence of breathing again into grace, and that his conversion to God might become for all an example.
</p>

## Chapter II - How God visited his heart through bodily infirmity and a vision of the night. <a id="op1-2"></a>

<p id="3">
[3] And when at last he had recovered his breath a little, and, leaning upon a staff, had begun to walk here and there about the house for the sake of regaining his health, on a certain day he went out of doors and began to gaze with more than usual curiosity upon the countryside that lay round about. [4] Yet the beauty of the fields, the loveliness of the vineyards, and whatever else is fair to the eye were powerless to delight him in any degree. [5] He marveled, therefore, at his own sudden transformation, and reckoned those who love such things to be the greatest of fools.
</p>

<p id="4">
[1] From that day forward, then, he began to count himself worthless and to hold in a certain contempt the very things he had once admired and loved. [2] Yet not fully nor truly, for he was not yet loosed from the bonds of vanity, <ref to="Gen 27:40">nor had he shaken the yoke of perverse servitude from his neck</ref>. [3] For it is a most difficult thing to abandon accustomed ways, and notions once fixed in the mind are not easily weakened; a soul long held apart hastens back to the first beginnings, and through constant repetition a vice is for the most part turned into a second nature. [4] And so Francis still attempts to flee the hand of God, and forgetting for a little while his fatherly correction, with prosperity smiling upon him, <ref to="1 Cor 7:34">he thinks on the things of this world</ref>, and ignorant of God's design, he still promises himself that he will accomplish the greatest deeds amid worldly glory and vanity. [5] For when a certain nobleman of the city of Assisi was furnishing himself in no slight measure with the arms of war, and, puffed up by the wind of empty glory, pledged that he would go into Apulia to increase his gains of money or honor, [6] Francis, hearing this—since he was light of spirit and no little daring—conspired to go along with him, unequal in nobility of birth but superior in greatness of soul, poorer in riches but more lavish in generosity.
</p>

<p id="5">
[1] One night, then, when he had given himself with total resolve to the accomplishment of these designs, and, burning with desire, panted above all to set out upon his journey, He who had struck him with the rod of justice visited him in the sweetness of grace through a nocturnal vision (cf. Job 4:13; 33:15); and because he was greedy for glory, He allured and exalted him with the very summit of glory.
[2] For it seemed to him that his whole house was filled with the arms of soldiers—saddles, that is, shields, lances, and the rest of the trappings; and rejoicing greatly, he silently wondered to himself what this might mean.
[3] For he was not accustomed to behold such things in his house, but rather heaps of cloth set out for sale.
[4] And while he stood no little amazed at this sudden turn of events, the answer was given him that all these arms would be his own and his soldiers'.
[5] Awaking, too, with a joyful heart, he rose in the morning, and reckoning the vision a portent of great prosperity, he assured himself that his journey into Apulia would prosper.
[6] <ref to="Mark 9:5"> For he knew not what he said </ref>, and he did not yet in the least understand the gift bestowed upon him from heaven.
[7] Yet in this he might have weighed that his own interpretation of the vision was not the true one: for although it did somehow contain a fair likeness of deeds of arms, nevertheless his spirit did not, as was its wont, rejoice over such matters.
[8] For he had to do a certain violence to himself in order to carry out what he had conceived and to bind his coveted journey to its effect.
[9] And indeed it is fitting enough that mention is first made of arms, and very aptly are arms handed over to a soldier about to fight against the mighty armed one, <ref to="1 Kgs 17:45">so that, like another David, in the name of the Lord God of hosts </ref> he might free Israel from the long-standing reproach of its enemies.
</p>

## Chapter III — How, changed in mind though not in body, he spoke allegorically of a treasure he had found and of a bride. <a id="op1-3"></a>

<p id="6">
[1] Transformed now, though in mind and not in body, he refused to set out for Apulia, and labored to bend his own will to the divine. [2] And so, withdrawing himself for a little while from the tumult and the affairs of the world, he strove to enshrine Jesus Christ within his inner self. [3] Like a prudent merchant, he hid the pearl he had found from the eyes of mockers, and secretly endeavored, <ref to="Matt 13:46">having sold all things</ref>, to acquire it. [4] For there was in the city of Assisi a certain man whom he loved more than the rest, since he was of the same age as himself, and a constant intimacy of mutual affection lent him the boldness to share his secrets with him; this man he would often lead off to remote places fit for confidences, declaring that he had discovered a certain treasure, precious and great. [5] That man rejoices, and being eager about what he had heard, he gladly goes along with him as often as he is summoned. [6] There was a certain cave near the city, to which they frequently went and spoke together about the treasure. [7] The man of God, who in his holy resolve was already a saint, would enter that cave while his companion waited outside, and, filled with a new and singular spirit, he would entreat <ref to="Matt 6:6">his Father in secret</ref>. [8] He was anxious that no one should know what he did within, and, wisely concealing the better thing under the pretext of the good, he took counsel about his purpose with God alone. [9] Devoutly he prayed <ref to="Ps 142:10">that the eternal and true God would direct his way and teach him to do His will</ref>. [10] He endured the greatest suffering of soul, and until he should accomplish in deed what he had conceived in his heart, he could not rest; varied thoughts followed one upon another, and their relentlessness harshly tormented him. [11] He burned within with a divine fire and could not hide outwardly the ardor kindled in his mind; <ref to="Isa 3:8">it grieved him that he had sinned so grievously and offended the eyes of His majesty</ref>, and neither past nor present evils now delighted him; yet he had not yet fully gained confidence to keep himself from those to come. [12] Therefore, when he returned outside to his companion, he was so worn out with the struggle that the one who went in seemed one man, and the one who came out another.
</p>

<p id="7">
[7] <ref to="Jas 1:27">And indeed the spotless bride of God is the true religion</ref> which he embraced, <ref to="Matt 13:44">and the hidden treasure is the kingdom of heaven</ref>, which he sought out with such great longing;
[8] for the evangelical calling had of necessity to be fulfilled wholly in him, who was to be <ref to="Eph 3:7">a minister of the Gospel</ref> <ref to="1 Tim 2:7">in faith and truth</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter IV — How, having sold all his possessions, he held in contempt the money he had received. <a id="op1-4"></a>

<p id="8">
[1] Behold, the blessed servant of the Most High, thus stirred in heart and strengthened by the Holy Spirit—<ref to="Job 12:5">for the appointed time was at hand</ref>—follows that blessed impulse of his soul, by which one presses toward the highest goods, the world's allurements trodden underfoot. [2] He could brook no further delay, for that deadly malady had now everywhere grown so great, and had so seized all the limbs of many, that, should the physician tarry even a little, it would snatch away life, the vital breath being cut off. [3] He rises up therefore, fortifying himself with the sign of the holy cross, and, his horse made ready, mounts upon it; and taking with him scarlet cloths to sell, he hastens swiftly to the city called Foligno. [4] There, having in his usual manner sold all that he carried, the fortunate merchant left behind even the very horse on which he then sat, took up the price, and, returning thence, laid down his bundles and turned over in his devout mind what he should do with the money. [5] Soon, in a marvelous way wholly given over to the work of God, feeling himself gravely burdened to carry that money for even a single hour, and reckoning all its profit as so much sand, he hastens quickly to be rid of it. [6] And as he was making his way back toward the city of Assisi, he came upon, beside the road, a certain church which in ancient times had been built in honor of Saint Damian, but which, from its exceeding age, was threatening imminent collapse.
</p>

<p id="9">
[1] <ref to="2 Tim 2:3">When this new soldier of Christ arrived there,</ref> stirred by compassion at so great a need, he entered with fear and reverence. [2] And finding there a certain poor priest, he kissed his sacred hands with great faith, offered him the money he was carrying, and recounted his purpose in order. [3] The priest, astonished and marveling beyond what can be believed at this sudden reversal of affairs, refused to believe what he was hearing. [4] And because he supposed that he was being mocked, he was unwilling to keep the money he had been offered. [5] For he had seen him, so to speak, <ref to="Luke 2:44">living riotously among his kinsmen and acquaintances only the day before,</ref> and exalting his own folly above the rest. [6] But he himself, persisting all the more stubbornly, strove to give credence to his words, praying with great earnestness and beseeching the priest to suffer him to remain with him for the Lord's sake. [7] At length the priest consented to his staying, but for fear of his parents he did not accept the money, which the true despiser of riches, throwing it into a certain window, cared for as little as if it were dust. [8] For he desired to possess wisdom, which is better than gold, <ref to="Prov 16:16">and to acquire prudence, which is more precious than silver.</ref>
</p>

## Chapter V - How his father, persecuting him, bound him. <a id="op1-5"></a>

<p id="10">
[1] While the servant of God Most High lingered in the aforementioned place, his father went prowling about on every side like a tireless spy, eager to learn what had become of his son. [2] And when he had discovered that he was dwelling in such a manner in the place already mentioned, <ref to="Gen 6:6">stricken with inward grief of heart</ref>, <ref to="Ps 6:4">he was greatly and exceedingly troubled at the sudden turn of events</ref>, and <ref to="Luke 15:6">having called together his friends and neighbors</ref>, he ran with all speed to the place where the servant of God was staying. [3] But he, being a new athlete of Christ, when he heard the threats of his pursuers and sensed their approach, unwilling <ref to="Rom 12:19">to give place to wrath</ref>, plunged himself into a certain hidden recess which he himself had prepared for this very purpose. [4] That pit was within a house, known perhaps to one man alone, in which he lay hidden thus continuously for a whole month, scarcely daring to come out even for the demands of human necessity. [5] If ever food was given him, he ate it in the secrecy of the pit, and every service was rendered to him in secret. [6] And as he prayed he prayed without ceasing, drenched in a shower of tears, <ref to="Ps 141:7">that the Lord might deliver him from the hands of those who pursued his soul</ref>, and that He might fulfill his holy desires with His gracious favor: <ref to="Joel 2:12">in fasting and weeping</ref> he implored the mercy of the Savior, and, mistrusting his own exertion, <ref to="Ps 54:23">he cast his every care upon the Lord</ref>. [7] <ref to="Dan 2:22">And although he was set within a pit and in darkness</ref>, he was nonetheless flooded with a certain unspeakable joy, until then never experienced by him, by which, wholly set aflame, he abandoned the pit and openly exposed himself to the curses of his persecutors.
</p>

<p id="11">
[1] So at once he rose up, undaunted, <ref to="Eph 6:16">swift and eager, and bearing the shield of faith to do battle for the Lord,</ref> and armed with weapons of great confidence, he set out upon the road toward the city; and, kindled with a divine ardor, he began to reproach himself bitterly for his sloth and cowardice. [2] When they saw him, all who had known him, comparing his latter state with his former, began to heap pitiable reproaches upon him, crying out that he was mad and out of his wits, and they flung <ref to="Ps 17:43">the mire of the streets</ref><ref to="John 8:59"> and stones at</ref> him. [3] They beheld him changed from his former ways and greatly wasted by the mortification of his flesh, and so they ascribed all that he did to delusion and madness. [4] <ref to="Eccl 7:9">But because the patient man is better than the arrogant,</ref> the servant of God made himself deaf to all these things, and, neither broken nor altered by any injury, he gave thanks to the Lord for them all. [5] For in vain does the wicked man persecute one who strives toward what is honorable, since the more such a one is battered, the more mightily will he triumph. A noble spirit, as a certain writer says, is made stronger by disgrace.
</p>

<p id="12">
[1] <ref to="Song 3:2"> And while for a long while such rumor and acclaim about him ran through the streets and lanes of the city </ref>, and the sound of mockers echoed here and there, the report of these things at last reached his father's ears, among the many whom it had touched. [2] When he heard his son's name, and that such an affair was being hurled against him by his fellow citizens, he rose up at once—not to free him, but rather to destroy him; [3] and observing no restraint, like a wolf swooping upon a sheep and eyeing him with a grim and savage face, he laid hands upon him and dragged him off to his own house, shamelessly and with no little dishonor. [4] Thus, withdrawing all compassion, he shut him up for several days in a dark place, and thinking to bend his son's mind to his own will, he worked upon him first with words, then with blows and chains. [5] But by this very means he was rendered the readier and the stronger to carry out his holy purpose, and neither reviled by words nor wearied by chains did he forsake his patience. [6] For he cannot be turned by scourges and chains from the right intention and constancy of his mind, nor be led away from the flock of Christ—he who is commanded to rejoice in tribulation; [7] <ref to="Ps 31:6"> nor does he tremble in the flood of many waters </ref>, whose refuge from oppression is the Son of God, who, lest our hardships should seem grievous to us, ever shows that the things He Himself endured were greater.
</p>

## Chapter VI — How his mother set him free, and how he stripped himself naked before the Bishop of Assisi. <a id="op1-6"></a>

<p id="13">
[1] Now it came to pass that when his father, pressed by some domestic affair, had departed for a little while from his own home, and the man of God remained bound in the prison of the house, his mother, who alone had stayed behind with him, not approving her husband's deed, addressed her son with coaxing words. [2] And when she saw that she could not call him back from his purpose, <ref to="3 Kgs 3:26">her maternal heart was moved with compassion toward</ref> him, and breaking his chains, she let him go away free. [3] But he, rendering thanks to almighty God, hastened back to the place where he had been before. [4] For now he enjoyed greater liberty, proven by the lessons of temptations, and through manifold conflicts he had put on a more joyful countenance; from his injuries he had received a more secure spirit, and going freely everywhere, he advanced with greater magnanimity. [5] Meanwhile the father returned, and not finding him, heaping sins upon sins, he turned to reviling his wife. [6] Then he ran to the place, raging and storming, that if he could not call him back, he might at least drive him out of the province. [7] <ref to="Prov 14:26">But because the fear of the Lord is the confidence of strength</ref>, when the son of grace heard his carnal father coming to him, secure and joyful he offered himself of his own accord, crying out with a free voice that he counted his father's chains and blows as nothing. [8] Moreover he testified that for the name of Christ he would gladly undergo all evils.
</p>

<p id="14">
[1] But the father, seeing that he could not call him back from the journey he had begun, was wholly driven to wrest the money from him. [2] The man of God had wished to spend it all upon the sustenance of the poor and the repair of that holy place, and to bestow it freely; yet he who did not love money cannot be deceived by any appearance of good that it offers, and he who was held by no affection for it is in no way disturbed by its loss. [3] And so, the money being found—which this supreme despiser of earthly things and most ardent seeker after heavenly riches had flung into the dust and onto a window-ledge—the fury of the raging father was somewhat quenched, and the thirst of his avarice was in some measure restrained by the warm vapor of its recovery. [4] Then he led him before the bishop of the city, that, renouncing all his goods into the bishop's hands, he might restore everything that he possessed. [5] At this he not only did not refuse, but, rejoicing greatly, hastened with a ready spirit to do what was demanded.
</p>

<p id="15">
[1] When he had been brought before the bishop, he brooked no delay nor hesitated over anything; indeed, he neither waited for words nor uttered any, but at once, having stripped off and cast aside all his garments, he gave them back to his father. [2] Moreover, not even keeping his undergarments, he stood wholly naked before them all. [3] But the bishop, marking the disposition of his soul and marveling exceedingly at his fervor and constancy, straightway rose up and, gathering him into his own arms, covered him with the mantle in which he himself was clad. [4] He understood plainly that this was the counsel of God, and he recognized that the deeds of the man of God which he had seen with his own eyes held a mystery within them. [5] <ref to="Ps 29:11">Thereafter, accordingly, he became his helper</ref>, and cherishing and strengthening him, he embraced him in the depths of his charity. [6] Behold, now naked he wrestles with the Naked One, and <ref to="1 Cor 7:33">having laid aside all the things that are of the world</ref>, he is mindful of divine righteousness alone. [7] Now he strives thus to hold his own life in contempt, laying down all anxiety on its behalf, that for him, a poor man, there might be peace upon the beleaguered road, and that meanwhile only the wall of the flesh might separate him from the vision of God.
</p>

## Chapter VII - How he was seized by robbers and cast into the snow, and how he ministered to the lepers. <a id="op1-7"></a>

<p id="16">
[1] For now, wrapped in cast-off rags, he who had once worn scarlet went on his way, and as he sang aloud the praises of the Lord in the French tongue through a certain wood, robbers suddenly fell upon him. [2] When they demanded with savage intent who he might be, the man of God answered confidently in a full voice, saying: <ref to="Ps 47:3; Matt 27:4">"I am the herald of the great King</ref>! What is that to you?" [3] But they, beating him, flung him into a sunken pit filled with deep snow, saying: "Lie there, you peasant herald of God!" [4] He, however, twisting himself this way and that, shook off the snow, and when they had departed leapt out of the ditch; and exhilarated with great joy, he began to sing forth in a loud voice through the groves the praises of the Creator of all. [5] At length, coming to a certain monastery of monks, he stayed there for several days clad in nothing but a paltry shirt, like a scullion in the kitchen, longing to be satisfied even with broth. [6] But when, every mercy withheld, he could not obtain so much as a worn-out garment, moved not by anger but compelled by necessity, he departed from there and came to the city of Gubbio, where from one who had once been his friend he acquired for himself a little tunic. [7] After this, when a little time had passed, <ref to="2 Chr 26:8; Luke 4:37">since the fame of the man of God was everywhere spreading and his name was being made known</ref> among the people, the prior of the aforesaid monastery, calling to mind and understanding what had been done to the man of God, came to him, and out of reverence for the Savior humbly begged his pardon for himself and his brethren.
</p>

<p id="17">
[1] Then indeed the holy lover of all humility betook himself to the lepers, and dwelt among them, most diligently serving them all for God's sake, washing away from them every foulness, and even wiping clean the corruption of their sores, just as he himself speaks in his Testament, saying: [2] "For when I was in my sins, it seemed too bitter to me to look upon lepers, and the Lord led me among them, and I showed mercy toward them." [3] For so deeply, as he used to say, did the sight of lepers at one time strike him with bitterness that, in the time of his vanity, when he beheld their dwellings from nearly two miles off, he would stop his nostrils with his own hands. [4] But when, by the grace and power of the Most High, he began to ponder <ref to="Luke 1:35; Titus 3:8">things holy and profitable </ref>, while still set in the garb of the world, he met one day a leper upon his way, and, made stronger than himself, drew near, <ref to="Mark 14:45">and kissed him </ref>. [5] From then on he began to despise himself more and more, until by the mercy of the Redeemer he should arrive at the perfect conquest of himself. [6] Of other poor folk also, while he yet remained in the world and still followed the world, he was a helper, stretching out the hand of mercy to those who had nothing, and bearing toward the afflicted an affection of compassion. [7] For when one day, contrary to his custom—since he was most courteous—he had rebuffed a certain poor man who begged an alms of him, <ref to="Matt 27:3">at once, led to repentance </ref>, <ref to="Luke 7:49">he began to say within himself </ref> that it would be a great reproach and a great disgrace to refuse what was asked of one who sought it in the name of so great a King. [8] <ref to="Ps 13:1; Acts 5:4">He therefore set it in his heart </ref> never thereafter, so far as he was able, to deny anything to one who asked it of him for God's sake. [9] And this he most diligently did and fulfilled, until in every way he gave his whole self away, becoming a doer of the evangelical counsel before he became its teacher: [10] "To him who asks of you," he says, "give; <ref to="Matt 5:42">and from him who would borrow of you do not turn away </ref>."
</p>

## Chapter VIII — How he rebuilt the church of San Damiano, and concerning the manner of life of the Ladies who dwelt in that same place. <a id="op1-8"></a>

<p id="18">
[1] The first work, then, which the blessed Francis undertakes, once he had secured his deliverance from the hand of his carnal father, is this: he builds a house for God; yet he attempts not to make it anew, but repairs the old one and mends what time had worn away. [2] He does not tear out the foundation but builds upon it, ever reserving to Christ—though unaware of it—the right of precedence: for no one can lay another foundation than that which has been laid, <ref to="1 Cor 3:11">which is Christ Jesus</ref>. [3] And when he had returned to the place where, as has been said, the church of Saint Damian had been built in ancient times, with the grace of the Most High accompanying him, in a short space of time he repaired it most diligently. [4] This is that blessed and holy place in which the glorious religious life and the most excellent order of the Poor Ladies and holy virgins took its happy beginning through the same blessed man, when nearly six years had already elapsed since the conversion of the blessed Francis; [5] in which the lady Clare, sprung from the city of Assisi, stood forth as the most precious and most steadfast stone, the foundation of the other stones set above it. [6] For when, after the founding of the Order of Friars, the said lady had been converted to God by the admonitions of the holy man, she proved an advancement to many and an example to countless souls. [7] Noble was her lineage, but nobler her grace; a virgin in the flesh, most chaste in mind; youthful in years but hoary in spirit; [8] steadfast in purpose and most ardent in the desire of divine love; endowed with wisdom and outstanding in humility: Clare by name, brighter still in life, most brilliant in her ways.
</p>

<p id="19">
[1] Upon this foundation there arose also a noble edifice of most precious pearls, <ref to="Rom 2:29">whose praise comes not from men but from God himself</ref>, since neither can a narrow meditation suffice to conceive it, nor a brief discourse to set it forth. [2] For first and above all there flourishes in them the virtue of mutual and unceasing charity, which so binds their wills into one that, though forty or fifty of them dwell together in a single place, the same willing and the same refusing make one spirit out of so many diverse persons. [3] Secondly, in each one there glows the gem of humility, which so guards the gifts conferred and the goods received from heaven that they merit the other virtues as well. [4] Thirdly, the lily of virginity and chastity sprinkles them all with so wondrous a fragrance that, forgetful of earthly thoughts, they desire to meditate on heavenly things alone; and from its perfume there springs up in their hearts so great a love of the eternal Bridegroom that the integrity of their holy affection shuts out from them every habit of their former life. [5] Fourthly, they are all so marked with the title of the most exalted poverty that scarcely ever, if at all, do they consent to satisfy even the most extreme necessity of food and clothing.
</p>

<p id="20">
[1] In the fifth place, they have so attained a singular grace of abstinence and silence that they suffer scarcely any difficulty in restraining the urges of the flesh and in bridling the tongue; indeed, some among them have grown so unaccustomed to conversation that, when necessity compels them to speak, they can hardly recall how to form words as the occasion requires. [2] In the sixth place, in all these things they are so wondrously adorned with the virtue of patience that no adversity of tribulation nor injury of vexation can break or alter their spirit. [3] In the seventh place, finally, they have so merited the very height of contemplation that within it they learn all that they must do or avoid, <ref to="2 Cor 5:13">and happily know how in mind to pass beyond themselves to God</ref>, persisting night and day in divine praises and prayers. [4] May the eternal God deign, by His holy grace, to bring so holy a beginning to a still holier end. [5] And let these things, said of the virgins consecrated to God and the most devoted handmaids of Christ, suffice for the present, since their marvelous life and glorious rule of observance—which they received from the lord pope Gregory, at that time bishop of Ostia—demand a work and a leisure all their own.
</p>

## Chapter IX - How, having changed his garb, he restored the church of Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, and how, upon hearing the Gospel, he forsook all things and devised and adopted the habit which the brothers now wear. <a id="op1-9"></a>

<p id="21">
[1] Meanwhile the holy man of God, having changed his garb and having repaired the aforesaid church, moved on to another place near the city of Assisi, where he began to rebuild a certain church that lay ruined and well-nigh overthrown; nor did he turn aside from his good beginning until he had brought the whole work to completion. [2] From there he betook himself to yet another place, called the Portiuncula, in which a church dedicated to the Blessed Virgin Mother of God had been built in ancient times, but which now lay forsaken and tended by no one. [3] When the holy man of God beheld it so ruined, he was moved with pity—for he burned with devotion toward the Mother of all goodness—and he began to dwell there continually. [4] Now it came to pass that, when he had finished repairing the said church, the third year of his conversion was running its course. [5] At that time he went about wearing what was almost a hermit's habit, girt with a belt and carrying a staff in his hand, with shoes upon his feet.
</p>

<p id="22">
[1] Now one day, when the Gospel was being read in that same church—the passage telling how the Lord sent His disciples out to preach—and the holy man of God, who was present there, had grasped the Gospel's words after a fashion, once the solemnities of the Mass were concluded he humbly begged the priest to expound that Gospel to him. [2] <ref to="Esth 15:9">When the priest had set forth the whole matter to him in order</ref>, Saint Francis, hearing that the disciples of Christ ought to possess neither gold nor silver nor money, to carry no wallet, no purse, no bread, no staff along the way, to have no shoes and no two tunics (cf. Matt 10:9-10; Mark 6:8; Luke 9:3; 10:4), <ref to="Luke 9:2; Mark 6:12">but to preach the kingdom of God and penance</ref>, <ref to="Luke 1:47">at once, exulting in the Spirit of God</ref>, cried out: [3] "This is what I want, this is what I seek, this is what I long with all the marrow of my heart to do!" [4] And so the holy father hastened, <ref to="2 Cor 7:4">overflowing with joy</ref>, to fulfill what he had so wholesomely heard, suffering no delay to pass before he devoutly set about carrying out what he had heard. [5] <ref to="Exod 3:5">Straightway he loosed the shoes from his feet</ref>, laid the staff down from his hands, and, content with a single tunic, exchanged his leather belt for a cord. [6] From that time he fashioned for himself a tunic bearing the image of the cross, that in it he might drive off all the phantoms of the demons; [7] he made it harsh and rough, <ref to="Gal 5:24">that in it he might crucify the flesh with its vices</ref> and sins; [8] he made it, in short, utterly poor and unadorned, such as the world could in no way covet. [9] And the rest of what he had heard he yearned to perform with the utmost diligence and the utmost reverence. [10] For he had been no deaf hearer of the Gospel, but, committing to praiseworthy memory all that he had heard, he took diligent care to fulfill it to the letter.
</p>

## Chapter X - On the preaching of the Gospel, the proclamation of peace, and the conversion of the first six Brothers. <a id="op1-10"></a>

<p id="23">
[1] From that time forth, with great fervor of spirit and gladness of mind, he began to preach repentance to all, edifying his hearers with simple words but with a noble heart. [2] <ref to="Sir 23:22">His word was a burning fire</ref>, piercing the innermost recesses of the heart, and filling the minds of all with wonder. [3] He seemed altogether other than he had been, and <ref to="Mark 6:41">gazing upon heaven</ref> he disdained to look down upon the earth. [4] And surely it is a marvel that he first began to preach there where, while still a little child, he had learned to read—the very place where also he was first laid to rest with honor, so that a more blessed consummation might commend a blessed beginning. [5] Where he had learned, there too he taught; and where he began, there he came to a blessed end. [6] In all his preaching, before he set forth the word of God to those gathered, he would invoke peace upon them, saying: <ref to="2 Thess 3:16">"The Lord give you peace."</ref> [7] This he proclaimed ever most devoutly to men and to women, to those he met and those who met him. [8] Therefore many <ref to="Ps 119:7">who had hated peace</ref> and salvation alike, <ref to="Mark 16:20">with the Lord working together with him</ref>, embraced peace with their whole heart, themselves becoming <ref to="Luke 10:16">children of peace</ref> and seekers after eternal salvation.
</p>

<p id="24">
[1] Among them a certain man of Assisi, bearing a devout and simple spirit, was the first to follow the man of God with devotion. [2] After him Brother Bernard, embracing the <ref to="Luke 14:32">embassy of peace</ref>, ran eagerly <ref to="Luke 4:34">after the holy one of God</ref> <ref to="Matt 13:44-46">to purchase the kingdom of heaven</ref>. [3] For he had often received the blessed father into his lodging, and having beheld and tested his life and manners, and being refreshed by the fragrance of his holiness, he conceived a holy fear and brought forth the spirit of salvation. [4] He saw him praying through the whole night, very rarely sleeping, praising God and the glorious Virgin his mother; and he marveled, saying: <ref to="Luke 23:47">"Truly this man</ref> is from God." [5] <ref to="Matt 13:46">He therefore hastened to sell all</ref> that he had, and bestowed it upon the poor, not upon his kinsmen, and seizing the title of the more perfect way, he fulfilled the counsel of the holy Gospel: "If you would be perfect, go and sell all that you have, and give to the poor, and you shall have treasure in heaven, <ref to="Matt 19:21">and come, follow me."</ref> [6] When this was done, he joined himself to Saint Francis in life and in habit, and was always with him, until—the brothers having multiplied—he was sent off to other regions by the obedience of the holy father. [7] For his conversion to God stood as a model for those to be converted, in the selling of possessions and the bestowal of them upon the poor. [8] <ref to="Matt 2:10">Saint Francis indeed rejoiced with a very great joy</ref> at the coming and conversion of so great a man, because it seemed that the Lord had care of him, <ref to="Sir 6:14">giving him a needful companion and a faithful friend</ref>.
</p>

<p id="25">
[1] At once, however, another man of the city of Assisi followed him, one who proved most praiseworthy in his manner of life, and what he had begun in holiness he brought, a little while after, to a still holier completion. [2] Him, in turn, <ref to="Matt 25:19">after no great while</ref>, there follows Brother Giles, a man simple and upright and fearing God (cf. Job 1:8; 2:3), who, enduring over a long span of time and living holily, <ref to="Titus 2:12">justly and devoutly</ref>, leaves us examples of perfect obedience, of the labor of his hands, of the solitary life, and of holy contemplation. [3] And when one more had been added to these, Brother Philip filled out the number of seven—he <ref to="Isa 6:6-7">whose lips the Lord touched with the coal of purity</ref>, that he might speak sweet things concerning Him and pour forth words flowing with honey. [4] Understanding and expounding the sacred Scriptures as well, <ref to="John 7:15">though he had never been taught</ref>, he became an imitator of those <ref to="Acts 4:13">whom the rulers of the Jews charged with being unlearned and illiterate men</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter XI - On the Spirit of Prophecy and the Admonitions of Saint Francis. <a id="op1-11"></a>

<p id="26">
[1] <ref to="Acts 9:31"> And so the blessed father Francis was daily filled with the consolation and grace of the Holy Spirit </ref>, and with all watchfulness and care he was shaping his new sons by new instructions, teaching them to walk the path of holy poverty and blessed simplicity with an unswerving step. [2] One day, however, as he marveled at the mercy of the Lord shown to him in the benefits bestowed upon him, and longed for the Lord to disclose to him the progress of his own way of life and that of his brothers, he sought out a place of prayer, as he very often did, [3] <ref to="Tob 13:6"> and as he lingered there long in fear and trembling </ref>, standing before <ref to="Zech 4:14"> the Ruler of the whole earth </ref>, <ref to="Isa 38:15">and recalled in the bitterness of his soul the years </ref> ill spent, [4] frequently repeating that word: "God, <ref to="Luke 18:13">be merciful to me, a sinner," </ref> [5] a certain unspeakable gladness and a most exquisite sweetness began gradually to flood the inmost recesses of his heart. [6] He began too to be carried out of himself, and once his passions were stilled and the darkness was put to flight—the darkness that had hardened in his heart through the dread of sin—there was poured into him a certainty of the forgiveness of all his offenses, and there was granted to him the confidence of breathing again into grace. [7] Then he was caught up above himself, and, wholly absorbed in a certain light, with the embrace of his mind enlarged, he gazed with clear vision upon the things that were to come. [8] At last, when that sweetness departed together with the light, renewed in spirit he seemed <ref to="Ps 50:12; 1 Kgs 10:6">already changed into another man </ref>.
</p>

<p id="27">
[1] And so, returning joyfully, he said to the brothers: "Take heart, dearly beloved, <ref to="Phil 3:1">and rejoice in the Lord</ref>; do not grow downcast because you seem to be few, nor let my simplicity or your own dishearten you, for, as has been truly shown to me by the Lord, God will make us increase into a very great multitude, and will spread us abroad in manifold ways even to the ends of the earth. [2] For your advancement, too, I am compelled to tell what I saw, though I would by all means rather have kept silent, were charity not pressing me to recount it to you. [3] <ref to="Rev 7:9">I saw a great multitude</ref> of men coming to us and wishing to live among us in the habit of a holy way of life and the rule of a blessed religion. [4] <ref to="Song 2:14">And behold, the sound of them is still in my ears</ref>, <ref to="Gen 8:3">as they go forth and return</ref> according to the command of holy obedience. [5] I saw, as it were, roads filled with their multitude, with men gathering in these regions <ref to="Acts 2:5">out of nearly every nation</ref>. [6] The French are coming, the Spaniards hasten, the Germans and the English run, and a very great multitude of other diverse tongues presses swiftly on." [7] When the brothers had heard this, they were filled with a saving joy, whether on account of the grace which the Lord had bestowed upon his holy one, or because they thirsted ardently for the gain of their neighbors, whom they longed to see daily increased, <ref to="Acts 2:47">that they might be saved together in unity</ref>.
</p>

<p id="28">
[1] And the saint said to them: "Brothers, that we may render thanks to the Lord our God 'faithfully and devoutly' above all his gifts, and that you may know how he is to dwell among the brothers both present and to come, understand the truth of what is to unfold. [2] We shall find, now at the beginning of our way of life, certain apples sweet and exceedingly pleasant to eat; but a little while after, certain others of lesser pleasantness and sweetness will be offered; [3] and at the very last <ref to="Lam 1:20">certain ones full of bitterness</ref> will be given, which we shall be unable to feed upon, since by reason of their harshness they will be inedible to all, though they present a certain outward fragrance and beauty. [4] And truly, as I have spoken to you, the Lord will increase us <ref to="Gen 12:2">into a great nation</ref>. [5] But in the end it will come to pass thus: as when a man casts his nets into the sea or into some lake, <ref to="Luke 5:6">and encloses a copious multitude of fishes</ref>, and when he has put them all into his little boat, growing weary of carrying them all on account of their multitude, he chooses the larger ones that please him for his own vessels, <ref to="Matt 13:47-48">but the rest he casts out</ref>." [6] All these things which the holy one of God foretold—with how great a truth they shine forth, with how great a clarity they reveal themselves—is plain enough to those who consider them in the spirit of truth. [7] <ref to="Isa 11:2">Behold how the spirit of prophecy rested</ref> upon Saint Francis.
</p>

## Chapter XII - How he sent them out two by two throughout the world, and how in a short time they were gathered together again. <a id="op1-12"></a>

<p id="29">
[1] At about the same time, when yet another good man entered the religious life, their number rose to eight. [2] Then blessed Francis called them all together, and after speaking to them at length <ref to="Acts 1:3">about the kingdom of God</ref>, about contempt for the world, about the renunciation of one's own will and the subjection of one's own body, he divided them two by two into four groups, [3] and said to them: "Go, dearly beloved, <ref to="Luke 10:1">two by two</ref> into the various regions of the world, <ref to="Acts 10:36">proclaiming peace to all</ref> <ref to="Mark 1:4">and penance for the remission of sins</ref>; <ref to="Rom 12:12">and be patient in tribulation</ref>, confident that the Lord will fulfill His purpose and His promise. [4] To those who question you humbly give answer; <ref to="Rom 12:14">bless those who persecute you</ref>; give thanks to those who wrong you and bring false charges against you, <ref to="Matt 25:34">for on account of these things an eternal kingdom is being prepared for us</ref>." [5] And they, <ref to="1 Macc 5:54">with much joy and gladness</ref>, receiving the command of holy obedience, prostrated themselves humbly upon the ground before holy Francis. [6] But he, embracing them, said to each one sweetly and devoutly: "Cast your care upon the Lord, <ref to="Ps 54:23">and He Himself will sustain you."</ref> [7] These were the words he spoke whenever he sent any of the brothers off in obedience.
</p>

<p id="30">
[1] Then Brother Bernard, together with Brother Giles, set out on the road toward Saint James, while Saint Francis with a single companion chose another part of the world, and the remaining four, going two by two, took the parts that were left. [2] But when only a little time had elapsed, Saint Francis, longing to see them all, <ref to="Exod 8:30">prayed to the Lord </ref>, <ref to="Ps 146:2">who gathers together the dispersed of Israel </ref>, that he might mercifully deign to bring them together again before long. [3] And so it came to pass that within a short time, according to his desire, without any human summons, <ref to="Col 3:17">giving thanks to God </ref>, they came together as one. [4] <ref to="1 Cor 11:20">When they had gathered into one </ref>, they celebrated with great joy the sight of their devout shepherd, and marveled that they had thus assembled by a single desire. [5] <ref to="Ps 114:5">They then recounted the good things which the merciful Lord </ref> had done for them, and if they had shown themselves in any way negligent or ungrateful, they humbly sought correction and discipline from the holy father, and diligently received it. [6] For this was their custom whenever they came to him, nor did they conceal from him the slightest thought or even the first stirrings of the soul; and when they had fulfilled all that had been commanded them, they reckoned themselves <ref to="Luke 17:10">unprofitable servants </ref>. [7] So thoroughly did purity of spirit possess that whole first school of blessed Francis that, although they knew how to perform what was useful, <ref to="Phil 4:8">holy and just </ref>, they were utterly ignorant of how to rejoice vainly in these things. [8] But the blessed father, embracing his sons with exceeding love, began to disclose to them his purpose and to make known the things which the Lord had revealed to him.
</p>

<p id="31">
[1] At once four other men, good and worthy, <ref to="Acts 2:41">were added</ref> to their number and followed the holy one of God. [2] A great stir thus arose among the people, and the fame of the man of God began to spread ever farther abroad. [3] Surely in that time there was for holy Francis and his brothers <ref to="Luke 1:14">exceedingly great rejoicing and</ref> singular <ref to="Luke 1:14">gladness</ref> whenever anyone—whoever and whatever he might be: faithful or unfaithful, rich or poor, noble or lowborn, despised or beloved, prudent or simple, clergy or unlettered, layman among the Christian people—came, <ref to="Matt 4:1">led by the Spirit of God</ref>, to take up the habit of the holy religion. [4] Among men of the world, too, there was much wonder at all this, and it stood as an example of humility, stirring them toward the way of a more amended life and toward repentance for their sins. [5] No lowliness of birth, no frailty of poverty stood in the way, <ref to="Acts 20:32">but that there were built up in the work of God those whom God willed to build up</ref>, <ref to="Prov 3:32">who takes delight in being with the cast-off of the world and with the simple</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter XIII - How he first composed the rule when he had eleven brothers, and how the lord Pope Innocent confirmed it, and concerning the vision of the tree. <a id="op1-13"></a>

<p id="32">
[1] <ref to="Acts 2:47">When the blessed Francis saw that the Lord God was daily adding to their number</ref>, he wrote, for himself and his brothers, both present and to come, in plain and few words, a pattern and rule of life, drawing chiefly upon the sayings of the holy Gospel, for whose perfection alone he yearned. [2] He inserted, however, a few other matters that were altogether and of necessity bound up with the practice of a holy way of life. [3] Thereupon he came to Rome with all the aforesaid brothers, longing exceedingly to have what he had written confirmed for him by the lord Pope Innocent III. [4] At that time there was in Rome the venerable bishop of Assisi, Guido by name, who honored Saint Francis and all the brothers in every way, and regarded them with a singular affection. [5] When he had seen Saint Francis and his brothers, not knowing the cause, he took their arrival grievously; for he feared lest they should wish to forsake their own homeland, in which the Lord, through his servants, had already begun to work great things. [6] He rejoiced greatly to have such men within his bishopric, presuming much upon their life and conduct. [7] But when he had heard the reason and understood their purpose, <ref to="Phil 4:10; Matt 2:10">he rejoiced greatly in the Lord</ref>, promising to give them counsel toward this end and to bring them aid. [8] Saint Francis went, moreover, to the reverend lord bishop of Sabina, John of Saint Paul by name, who among the other princes and great men of the Roman curia seemed 'to despise earthly things and to love the heavenly.' [9] He, receiving him kindly and charitably, greatly commended his will and his purpose.
</p>

<p id="33">
[1] But since he was a man both prudent and discerning, he began to question him about many things, and urged him to turn instead to the monastic or the eremitic life. [2] Yet Saint Francis, as humbly as he could, declined this persuasion, not by scorning what was urged upon him, but, devoutly drawn toward another path, he was borne onward by a loftier desire. [3] That lord marveled at his fervor, and fearing lest he should fall back from so great a resolve, he set before him gentler and more level ways. [4] At last, overcome by the prayers of his constancy, he yielded, and took pains thereafter to advance his cause before the lord pope. [5] <ref to="1 Cor 1:2">At that time the lord Pope Innocent the Third presided over the Church of God,</ref> a man of glory, most abounding in learning, most brilliant in speech, ardent with zeal for justice in whatever the worship of the Christian faith required. [6] When he had recognized the vow of these men of God, with discernment going before, he granted assent to their petition and brought it to fulfillment by the effect that followed; and having exhorted and admonished them on many matters, he blessed Saint Francis and his brothers, [7] and he said to them: “Go with the Lord, brothers, and as the Lord shall deign to inspire you, preach repentance to all. [8] <ref to="Deut 7:12">For when the almighty Lord shall multiply you in number</ref> and in grace, <ref to="Luke 10:17">you shall return to me with joy,</ref> and I will grant you more than these things and more confidently entrust greater matters to you.” [9] <ref to="1 Kgs 3:19">Truly the Lord was with</ref> <ref to="Gen 20:16">Saint Francis wherever he went,</ref> gladdening him with revelations and encouraging him with benefits. [10] For when on a certain night he had given himself to sleep, it seemed to him that he was walking along a certain road, beside which stood a tree of great height. [11] That tree was beautiful and strong, <ref to="Dan 4:7">exceedingly thick and tall.</ref> [12] <ref to="Luke 7:11">And it came to pass that as he drew near</ref> to it, <ref to="Jdt 10:14">and standing beneath it marveled at its beauty and its height,</ref> suddenly the saint himself rose to so great a height that he touched the top of the tree, and grasping it with his hand he very easily bent it down to the ground. [13] And in truth so it came to pass, when the lord Innocent, the loftiest and most sublime tree in the world, inclined himself so very graciously to his petition and his will.
</p>

## Chapter XIV - On his return from the city of Rome to the valley of Spoleto, and on his delay along the way. <a id="op1-14"></a>

<p id="34">
[1] Saint Francis, together with his brothers, rejoicing greatly in the bounty and favor of so great a father and lord, <ref to="Acts 27:35">gave thanks to almighty God</ref>, <ref to="Job 5:11">who sets the lowly on high and raises up the mourning into safety</ref>. [2] At once he came to visit the threshold of blessed Peter, and when his prayer was completed, he went forth out of the City and, taking the road toward the valley of Spoleto, set out with his companions. [3] <ref to="Luke 24:17">As they went along they spoke with one another</ref> of how many and how great were the gifts that the most merciful God had bestowed upon them: how graciously they had been received by the vicar of Christ, the lord and father of the whole Christian nation; [4] and how they might fulfill his admonitions and commands; [5] how they might sincerely keep and unswervingly guard the rule they had received; [6] how they might walk in all holiness and religious devotion before the Most High; [7] and finally how their life and conduct, growing in holy virtues, might serve as an example to their neighbors. [8] And when these new disciples of Christ had discoursed sufficiently in the school of humility upon such matters, the day had climbed high and the hour had passed. [9] <ref to="Matt 14:15">By then they had come to a deserted place</ref>, exceedingly worn out with the weariness of the journey, and hungry, they could find no food to refresh themselves, for that place was very far removed from any human dwelling. [10] And at once, by the working of divine grace, there met them a man carrying bread in his hand, who <ref to="Mark 14:13; 1 Kgs 10:3-4">gave it</ref> to them and went away. [11] But they, not recognizing him, <ref to="Ps 34:25">marveled in their hearts</ref>, and that they might trust the more in the divine mercy, they devoutly admonished one another. [12] Having taken the food, and being not a little strengthened by it, they came to a certain place near the city of Orte, and there they remained for nearly fifteen days. [13] Some of them, entering the city, would obtain the necessities of life, and what little they could gather they begged from door to door, carrying it back to the other brothers, and together they ate it <ref to="Acts 24:3; Isa 30:29">with thanksgiving and gladness of heart</ref>. [14] But if anything was left over, since they could give it to no one, they would hide it away in a certain tomb that had once held the bodies of the dead, so that they might eat the same again. [15] That place was deserted and abandoned, and was frequented by the approach of few people, or of none at all.
</p>

<p id="35">
[1] Their joy was great, even though they neither beheld nor possessed anything that might delight them in a vain or carnal way. [2] For this reason they began there to enter into fellowship with holy poverty, and, <ref to="1 Cor 7:33">in the lack of all things that belong to the world,</ref> being exceedingly consoled, they resolved to cleave to her forever and everywhere, just as they did in that place. [3] And because, <ref to="1 Pet 5:7">having laid aside all anxiety</ref> for earthly things, divine consolation alone delighted them, they decided and confirmed, troubled by no tribulations and driven by no temptations, that they would never recoil <ref to="Eccl 3:5">from her embraces.</ref> [4] But although the pleasantness of that place—which has no small power to corrupt the true vigor of the soul—did not hold their affections captive, lest the long-continued habit of dwelling there should bind them, if only outwardly, to some sense of ownership, they left that place and, following their blessed father, entered at that time the valley of Spoleto. [5] Together, these true cultivators of righteousness deliberated whether they ought to dwell among men or to betake themselves to solitary places. [6] But holy Francis, who did not trust in his own ingenuity, <ref to="Ps 87:14">but went before all his undertakings with holy prayer,</ref> chose to live not for himself alone, <ref to="2 Cor 5:15">but for Him who died for all,</ref> knowing that he had been sent for this purpose: to win for God the souls that the devil was striving to carry off.
</p>

## Chapter XV — On the fame of blessed Francis and the conversion of many to God, and how the Order came to be called that of the Friars Minor, and in what manner blessed Francis instructed those entering religious life. <a id="op1-15"></a>

<p id="36">
[1] <ref to="Matt 9:35">And so Francis, the most valiant soldier of Christ, went about the cities and villages</ref>, not with the persuasive words of human wisdom, <ref to="1 Cor 2:4">but in the teaching and power of the Spirit</ref>, proclaiming the kingdom of God, <ref to="Matt 9:35; Acts 10:36">preaching peace</ref>, <ref to="Mark 1:4">teaching salvation and repentance unto the remission of sins</ref>. [2] <ref to="Acts 9:28">In all things he acted with great confidence</ref>, by the apostolic authority granted to him, employing no flatteries, no seductive blandishments. [3] He knew not how to soothe men's faults but to prick them, nor to coddle the lives of sinners but to strike them with harsh rebuke, for he had first persuaded himself by deed of what he urged upon others by words; and fearing no critic, he spoke the truth with utmost assurance, so that even the most learned of men, men of glory and high station, marveled at his discourses and were struck with a wholesome dread in his presence. [4] Men came running, and women too came running; clergy made haste, and religious hurried, to see and hear the holy one of God, who seemed to all a man of another age. [5] Every age and either sex pressed forward to behold the marvels that the Lord was now newly working through His servant in the world. [6] Truly in that time it seemed, whether through the presence of holy Francis or through his renown, that a certain new light had been sent from heaven upon the earth, putting to flight the whole gloom of darkness that had so nearly engulfed the entire region that scarcely anyone knew which way he ought to go. [7] For so deep an oblivion of God and so heavy a slumber of neglect for His commandments had crushed almost all, that they could scarcely be roused even a little from their old and inveterate evils.
</p>

<p id="37">
[1] <ref to="Sir 50:6; Prov 7:9">He shone like a glittering star in the gloom of night</ref> <ref to="Joel 2:2">and like the dawn spreading across</ref> the darkness; and so it came to pass that within a short time the whole face of the province was transformed and everywhere appeared with a more joyful countenance, its former foulness laid aside. [2] The earlier barrenness was put to flight, and a harvest swiftly sprang up in the squalid field; <ref to="Zech 8:12">the untilled vineyard, too, began to bring forth a bud</ref> <ref to="Lev 1:9">of fragrance pleasing to the Lord</ref>, and, having put forth from itself the blossoms of sweetness, it likewise gave birth to <ref to="Sir 24:23">the fruit of honor and uprightness</ref>. [3] <ref to="Isa 51:3">On every side resounded thanksgiving and the voice of praise</ref>, so that many, casting off worldly cares, in the life and teaching of the most blessed father Francis came to a knowledge of themselves and aspired to the love and reverence of their Creator. [4] Many of the people, noble and lowly, clerics and laymen alike, pricked by divine inspiration, began to come to Saint Francis, longing to serve forever under his discipline and instruction. [5] All these the holy man of God, like a most abundant stream of heavenly grace, watered with showers of spiritual gifts, and adorned the field of their hearts with the flowers of the virtues: [6] <ref to="Exod 38:23">a peerless craftsman indeed</ref>, to whose pattern, rule, and teaching, by the spreading of his renown, the Church of Christ is renewed in both sexes, and the threefold army of those to be saved triumphs. [7] To all, moreover, he imparted a rule of life and truly pointed out the way of salvation in every station.
</p>

<p id="38">
[1] But it is chiefly of the Order, which he embraced and held fast in charity no less than by profession, that our discourse now treats. [2] For what shall we say? He himself first planted the Order of Friars Minor, and indeed it was on a particular occasion that he gave it this name. [3] For when it was thus being written in the Rule, "And let them be lesser ones," at the very utterance of these words, in that very hour he said: "It is my will that this brotherhood be called the Order of Friars Minor." [4] And truly were they lesser ones, who, being "subject to all," ever sought out the lowliest place and the office in which some affront might seem to lie, that so they might deserve to be grounded upon the solid foundation of true humility, and that by a blessed disposition the spiritual edifice of all the virtues might rise up within them. [5] Indeed, <ref to="Eph 2:20">upon the foundation of steadfastness</ref> there arose the noble structure of charity, <ref to="1 Pet 2:5">in which living stones</ref>, heaped together from all the regions of the world, <ref to="Eph 2:22">were built into a dwelling place of the</ref> Holy Spirit. [6] O with what an ardor of charity did these new disciples of Christ burn! How great a love of holy fellowship was alive in them! [7] For whenever they came together anywhere, or met one another on the road, as is the custom, there a dart of spiritual love flashed forth, scattering, beyond all love, the seed of true affection. [8] And what was this? Chaste embraces, sweet affections, the holy kiss, sweet converse, modest laughter, a joyful countenance, <ref to="Matt 6:22">a single eye</ref>, a humble spirit, a gentle tongue, <ref to="Prov 15:4">a soft answer</ref>, one and the same purpose, ready compliance, and a tireless hand.
</p>

<p id="39">
[1] Indeed, since they despised all earthly things and never loved themselves with a private love, pouring out the whole affection of their love upon the common good, they strove to give themselves as the price, that they might equally come to the aid of a brother's need. [2] Eagerly they came together, more delightfully they remained together; yet grievous on both sides was the parting of companions, a bitter divorce, a harsh separation. [3] But these most obedient soldiers dared to set nothing before the commands of holy obedience, men who, before the word of obedience was even finished, were already preparing themselves to carry out the order; [4] knowing how to make no distinctions in commands, they ran headlong, as it were, to whatever was enjoined, every objection set aside. [5] As "followers of most holy poverty," because they possessed nothing, they loved nothing: and therefore they feared to lose nothing. [6] They were content with a single tunic, patched at times within and without; in it there appeared no elegance but much contempt and meanness, <ref to="Gal 6:14">so that in it they might seem wholly crucified to the world</ref>. [7] Girded with a cord, they wore lowly breeches, and their devout resolve was to remain in all these things and to have nothing more. [8] They were therefore secure everywhere, held in suspense by no fear, distracted by no care; without any anxiety they awaited the morrow, nor, though often set in great peril upon their journey, were they troubled about an evening's lodging. [9] For when often in the bitterest cold they were of necessity without shelter, an oven would gather them in, or surely they would lie hidden by night, in humility, in crypts or caves. [10] By day, those who knew how labored with their own hands, dwelling in the houses of lepers, or in other respectable places, serving all humbly and devoutly. [11] They wished to practice no occupation from which scandal might arise, but, always doing things holy and just, honest and useful, they stirred up all with whom they associated to the example of humility and to patience.
</p>

<p id="40">
[1] So thoroughly had the virtue of patience encompassed them that they sought rather to be where they might suffer the persecution of their bodies than where, their holiness once known or praised, they might be exalted by the world's favors. [2] For though they often endured insults, were laden with abuse, stripped, beaten, bound, imprisoned, defending themselves by no one's protection, they bore all things so manfully <ref to="Isa 51:3">that in their mouths nothing resounded but the single voice of praise and the giving of thanks</ref>. [3] Scarcely ever, or never, did they cease from the praise of God and from prayer; rather, recollecting in continual examination whatever they had done, they rendered thanks to God for things well done, and for things neglected or rashly committed they paid out groans and tears. [4] They reckoned themselves abandoned by God if they did not perceive themselves to be continually visited, in the spirit of devotion, by His accustomed mercy. [5] For when they wished to apply themselves to prayer, lest sleep should seize them, they were held up by some support: some were propped by hanging ropes, lest prayer be disturbed by the stealthy approach of sleep. [6] Some girded themselves with iron instruments, and others bound themselves about with wooden fetters. [7] If ever an abundance of food or drink should, as commonly happens, disturb their sobriety, or if through the weariness of the journey they should pass even slightly beyond the bounds of necessity, they tormented themselves most bitterly with an abstinence of many days. [8] Finally, with such great mortification did they strive to suppress the incitements of the flesh that, upon the coldest ice, they often did not shrink from stripping themselves bare, and, pricking their whole body with the points of thorns, from drenching it in an outpouring of blood.
</p>

<p id="41">
[1] So valiantly did they despise all earthly things that they scarcely allowed themselves to accept the barest necessities of life and, long accustomed to be cut off from every bodily comfort, they feared no hardship whatsoever. [2] In all these things <ref to="Heb 12:14">they pursued peace and gentleness with all</ref>, <ref to="Jas 3:17">and ever doing what was chaste and peaceable</ref>, they shunned every cause of scandal with the utmost zeal. [3] For scarcely did they speak save when need required, <ref to="Matt 4:4">nor did anything scurrilous or idle proceed from their mouths</ref>, so that in their whole life and manner of living nothing unchaste or in any degree unseemly could be found. [4] Their every act was disciplined, their every step modest, all their senses so mortified within them that they scarcely allowed themselves to hear or to see anything beyond what their purpose demanded: with eyes fixed upon the ground, their minds clung to heaven. [5] No envy, no malice, no rancor, no detraction, no suspicion, no bitterness had any place among them, but rather abundant concord, unbroken peace, <ref to="Isa 51:3">thanksgiving and the voice of praise</ref> dwelt within them. [6] These are the teachings of the loving father by which, not in word and tongue alone, <ref to="1 John 3:18">but most of all in deed and in truth</ref>, he formed his new sons.
</p>

## Chapter XVI - Concerning his sojourn at Rieti and his keeping of poverty. <a id="op1-16"></a>

<p id="42">
[1] The blessed Francis would withdraw with the others near the city of Assisi, in a place called Rivo Torto. [2] In that place there stood an abandoned hovel, and beneath its shelter lived these most resolute scorners of great and beautiful houses, sheltering themselves there from the lashing of the rains. [3] 'For,' as the saint said, 'one ascends to heaven more swiftly from a hovel than from a palace.' [4] In that same place there dwelt with the blessed father all his sons and brothers, <ref to="2 Cor 11:27">in much labor</ref> and in want of every necessity, very often deprived of any comfort of bread, content with turnips alone, which they begged in their hardship across the plain of Assisi and brought back there. [5] So exceedingly cramped was that place that they could scarcely sit or rest within it. [6] 'No murmur sounds forth over these things, no complaint; but with peaceful heart and a mind full of joy they preserve their patience.' [7] Saint Francis most diligently made a daily—indeed continual—examination of himself and of his own, and, suffering nothing slippery to remain in them, he drove every negligence from their hearts. [8] <ref to="Isa 21:8">Rigid in discipline, he kept watch over his guard</ref>, vigilant at every hour; for if at any time some temptation of the flesh assailed him, as is wont to happen, he would plunge himself into a pit filled with ice when it was winter, remaining there until every corruption of the flesh withdrew. [9] And indeed the others followed with the utmost fervor so great an example of mortification.
</p>

<p id="43">
[1] He taught them not only to mortify their vices and to restrain the promptings of the flesh, but also to govern those very outward senses through which death enters into the soul.
[2] For at that time, when the emperor Otto was passing through those regions with great clamor and pomp to receive the crown of his earthly dominion, the most holy father, abiding in the aforesaid hut with the rest of his companions hard by the very road of that procession, neither went out to look upon it,
[3] nor suffered any of his brothers to gaze upon it, save one alone, whom he charged to declare most steadfastly to the emperor that this glory of his would endure but a little while.
[4] <ref to="Ps 118:45">For the holy man was glorious, dwelling and walking with himself in the largeness of his heart,</ref> and was preparing within himself <ref to="Eph 2:22">a dwelling-place worthy of God;</ref>
[5] and therefore the outward clamor did not seize his ears, nor could any voice startle him from or break in upon the immense business he held in his hands.
[6] An apostolic authority was strong in him, and so he utterly refused to flatter kings and princes.
</p>

<p id="44">
[1] He devoted himself always to holy simplicity, <ref to="Ps 118:45">nor would he let the narrowness of a place hinder the breadth of his heart</ref>. [2] For this reason he would write the names of the brothers upon the beams of their dwelling, so that each one, wishing to pray or to rest, might recognize his own place, and lest the cramped smallness of the spot should disturb the silence of his mind. [3] While they were dwelling there, it happened one day that a certain man leading a donkey came to the little shelter in which the man of God was staying with his companions; and, that the beast might not suffer to be turned away, urging his donkey to go in, he spoke these words: "Go inside, for we shall do well by this place." [4] When Saint Francis heard these words, he took them gravely, perceiving the man's intention; for that fellow supposed that they wished to remain there in order to enlarge the place, <ref to="Isa 5:8">and to join house to house</ref>. [5] At once Saint Francis went out from there, abandoning that hut on account of the peasant's word, and betook himself to another place not far from it, which is called the Portiuncula, where, as has been said above, the church of Saint Mary had long before been repaired by him. [6] He wished to possess nothing of his own, <ref to="2 Cor 6:10">that he might possess all things more fully in the Lord</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter XVII - How the blessed Francis taught the brothers to pray, and concerning the obedience and purity of the brothers. <a id="op1-17"></a>

<p id="45">
[1] At that time the brothers begged him <ref to="Luke 11:1">to teach them how to pray </ref>, for, <ref to="Prov 20:7">walking in simplicity of spirit </ref>, they were as yet unacquainted with the ecclesiastical office. [2] To them he said: "When you pray, say: <ref to="Matt 6:9">Our Father </ref>" and: "We adore you, O Christ, and we bless you here and in all your churches that are throughout the whole world, because by your holy cross you have redeemed the world." [3] These very brothers, disciples of a devoted master, took the utmost care to observe this; for not only did they strive most effectively to fulfill the things that the blessed father Francis told them by brotherly counsel or fatherly command, but even those things which he was thinking or pondering, if by any sign they were able to perceive them. [4] For the blessed father himself used to tell them that true obedience is not only that which is spoken but that which is conceived in thought, not only that which is commanded but that which is desired; [5] that is to say: "If a brother subject to a brother set over him not only hears his voice but grasps his will, he ought at once to gather his whole self to obedience and to do what he perceives by some sign that the other wishes." [6] For this reason, wherever any church had been built, even if they were not present but could only somehow catch sight of it from afar, they would bow themselves toward it, prostrate upon the ground, and, bending the whole man, they would adore the Almighty, saying: "We adore you, O Christ, and we bless you in all your churches," just as the holy father had taught them. [7] And, what is no less to be marveled at, wherever they beheld a cross or the sign of the cross, whether on the ground, or on a wall, or on trees, or on the hedges along the roads, they did this same thing.
</p>

<p id="46">
[1] So thoroughly had holy simplicity filled them, so well did innocence of life instruct them, so completely did purity of heart possess them, that they were utterly ignorant of any duplicity of soul; for just as there was one faith, <ref to="Eph 4:3">so there was one spirit </ref> in them, one will, one charity, a perpetual concord of souls, a harmony of conduct, a devotion to virtue, a conformity of minds, and a piety in their actions.
[2] For when they often confessed their sins to a certain secular priest, a man very ill-famed for his deserts and worthy to be despised by all for the enormity of his crimes, and though his wickedness had been made known to them by many, they would by no means consent to believe it, nor on that account did they cease to confess their sins to him as was their custom, nor to show him the reverence due to his office.
[3] And indeed, when this man, or another priest, said one day to one of the brothers, "Take care, brother, that you be not a hypocrite," at once, because of the priest's word, that brother believed himself to be a hypocrite.
[4] On account of this he lamented day and night, afflicted with exceeding grief.
[5] When the brothers asked him what such sadness and so unwonted a sorrow could mean, he answered, saying: "A certain priest spoke such a word to me, and I am so afflicted with grief over it that I can scarcely think of anything else."
[6] But the brothers consoled him and urged him not to believe such a thing.
[7] To them he said: "What is this that you say, brothers? It is a priest who has spoken this word; can a priest lie?
[8] Since, therefore, a priest does not lie, it is necessary that we believe what he has spoken to be true."
[9] And so, having long persisted in this simplicity, he at last yielded to the words of the most blessed father, who explained to him the priest's word and shrewdly excused his intention.
[10] Scarcely could so great a disturbance of mind dwell in any of the brothers <ref to="Ps 118:140">that, at his fiery speech, </ref> every cloud would not depart and the calm return.
</p>

## Chapter XVIII — Concerning the fiery chariot, and the knowledge of those absent which the blessed Francis possessed. <a id="op1-18"></a>

<p id="47">
[1] <ref to="Gen 17:1; Prov 10:9">Walking before God in simplicity and before men in confidence</ref>, the brothers were found worthy in that time to be gladdened by a divine revelation. [2] For while, kindled by the fire of the Holy Spirit, not only at the appointed hours but at any hour whatsoever—whenever earthly cares or the troublesome anxiety of worldly concerns weighed but lightly upon them—they would chant the <ref to="Matt 6:9">Our Father</ref> in the melody of the spirit and with suppliant voice, the most blessed father Francis, on a certain night, withdrew himself from them in body. [3] And behold, at about the middle hour of the night, while some of the brothers were resting and others were affectionately praying in silence, a most splendid chariot of fire entered through the little door of the house, and <ref to="4 Kgs 1:11">turned itself this way and that, twice and thrice</ref>, through the dwelling; and upon it rested a very great globe, which, having the appearance of the sun, made the night to shine bright. [4] The waking were struck with amazement, the sleeping <ref to="Luke 2:8; Matt 24:4">were terrified</ref>, and they felt no less the brightness of heart than of body. [5] And as they came together into one, they began to ask among themselves <ref to="1 Cor 11:20; Luke 22:23">what this might be</ref>; but by the power and grace of so great a light, the conscience of each was made manifest to the other. [6] At length they understood and knew that it was the soul of their holy father shining with a radiance so great, who, on account of the grace of his exceptional purity and his care of great tenderness toward his sons, deserved to obtain from the Lord the blessing of so great a gift.
</p>

<p id="48">
[1] And indeed by manifest tokens they had often proved and learned from experience that the secrets of their hearts <ref to="1 Cor 14:25">were not hidden</ref> from their most holy father. [2] Oh, how often, with no man teaching him but the Holy Spirit revealing it, did he come to know the deeds of absent brothers, lay bare <ref to="1 Cor 14:25">the secrets of hearts</ref>, and search out their consciences! Oh, how many did he admonish in dreams, enjoining upon them what they ought to do and forbidding what they ought not! Oh, of how many did he foretell the evils to come, whose present state seemed good in outward appearance! [3] Thus too, foreknowing the end of the iniquities of very many, he announced the grace of salvation that would one day be theirs. [4] Indeed, if anyone deserved to be enlightened with a spirit of purity and simplicity, he was favored, in a manner unknown to the rest, with the singular consolation of his vision. [5] Among other things I will relate one that I came to know from the report of trustworthy witnesses. [6] When at a certain time Brother John of Florence had been appointed by Saint Francis as minister of the brothers in Provence, and had celebrated a chapter of the brothers in that same province, <ref to="Col 4:3">the Lord God with his accustomed loving-kindness opened to him a door of speech</ref>, and rendered all the brothers benevolent and attentive to hear. [7] Among them was one brother, a priest, renowned in reputation but more renowned in life, Monaldus by name, whose virtue, founded upon humility and aided by frequent prayer, was kept safe behind the shield of patience. [8] There was present at that chapter also Brother Anthony, whose understanding the Lord had opened <ref to="Luke 24:45">that he might understand the Scriptures</ref>, and that he might pour forth among all the people <ref to="Ps 44:2">sweet words of Jesus</ref> <ref to="Ps 18:11">surpassing honey and the honeycomb</ref>. [9] As he was preaching to the brothers most fervently and most devoutly upon this word, namely: <ref to="John 19:19">"Jesus of Nazareth, King of the Jews"</ref>, <ref to="Josh 9:2">the said Brother Monaldus looked toward the door of the house in which the brothers were gathered together</ref>, and there with his bodily eyes he beheld the blessed Francis raised aloft in the air, his hands outstretched as upon a cross, blessing the brothers. [10] <ref to="Acts 9:31">All seemed likewise to be filled with the consolation of the Holy Spirit</ref>, and from the joy of salvation thus conceived it was made quite credible to them, that which they had heard concerning the vision and presence of their most glorious father.
</p>

<p id="49">
[1] That he could indeed perceive the hidden secrets of the hearts of others, let one instance—out of the many that many have often experienced, and one about which no doubt can arise—be set forth in our midst. [2] A certain friar named Riccerius, noble by birth but nobler in character, a lover of God and a despiser of himself, while he was being led by a devout spirit and a most ardent will to attain and possess perfectly the favor of the holy father Francis, <ref to="Jdt 8:8">was greatly afraid </ref> lest the holy Francis might shun him by some hidden judgment, and so render him a stranger to the grace of his affection. [3] That friar supposed, <ref to="Luke 2:25">since he was devout </ref>, that whomever the holy Francis loved with inward charity would also be worthy to merit divine grace; but on the other hand, the one to whom he did not show himself kindly disposed and well pleased, he reckoned would incur the wrath of the heavenly Judge. [4] These things the said friar turned over in his mind, <ref to="Gen 24:45">these things he often, silently within himself, </ref> spoke, revealing to no one at all the secret of his thought.
</p>

<p id="50">
[1] Now it happened one day, while the blessed father was at prayer in his little cell, that the aforesaid brother, troubled by his customary brooding, came to the place; and the <ref to="Luke 4:34">holy one of God</ref> knew both of his arrival and of what he was turning over in his mind. [2] At once, therefore, he had him summoned to his presence and said to him: "Let no temptation trouble you, my son, let no thought embitter you, for you are most dear to me, and among those especially dear to me you may know yourself worthy of my love and friendship. [3] Come to me without fear whenever you wish, and from our intimacy take the freedom to speak freely." [4] The aforesaid brother marveled with the greatest wonder, and thenceforth, grown more reverent, in the same measure as he increased in the grace of the holy father, so did he begin to expand the more confidently in the mercy of God. [5] How grievously, holy father, must they bear your absence who utterly despair of ever finding your like again upon the earth. [6] Help, we pray, by your intercession, those whom you behold entangled in the baneful stain of sin. [7] Although you were already filled with the spirit of all the righteous, foreseeing things to come and knowing things present, yet, to flee all boasting, you ever displayed the image of holy simplicity. [8] But let us return to higher matters, taking up again the order of our narrative.
</p>

## Chapter XIX - On the watchfulness with which he kept guard over the brothers, and on his contempt for himself and his true humility. <a id="op1-19"></a>

<p id="51">
[1] The most blessed Francis returned in body to his brothers, from whom, as has been said, he never withdrew in spirit. [2] Searching out the deeds of all by careful and diligent scrutiny, he was ever borne toward his subjects with a happy solicitude, leaving nothing unpunished if he discovered that anything less than upright had been committed. [3] And first indeed he passed judgment upon spiritual faults, then he adjudged the bodily ones, and lastly he rooted out every occasion that is wont to open a way to sins. [4] With all zeal and all anxious care he guarded holy Lady Poverty, not suffering that at any time he should come to superfluities, nor that any little vessel should remain in the house, when even without it he could somehow escape the bondage of extreme necessity. [5] For he used to say that it would be impossible to satisfy necessity and yet not yield to pleasure. [6] Cooked foods he scarcely, or most rarely, admitted; and when he did admit them, he would often either spoil them with ashes or quench the savor of the seasoning with cold water. [7] <ref to="Rom 1:1">O how often, walking through the world to preach the Gospel of God</ref>, when invited to dine by great princes who revered him with wondrous affection, having tasted but a little of the meats for the sake of observing the holy Gospel, he would put away in his lap the rest that he seemed to be eating, drawing his hand to his mouth, lest anyone could perceive what he was doing. [8] What shall I say of the drinking of wine, when, even burning with the longing of thirst, he would not freely allow himself water enough to suffice?
</p>

<p id="52">
[1] <ref to="Song 1:11"> As for his own bed </ref>, wherever he was received as a guest, he would suffer it to be covered with no bedding or coverings, but the bare ground, with only a little tunic laid between, received his bare limbs. [2] Whenever he refreshed his frail body with the gift of sleep, he most often slept sitting up, lying down in no other fashion, using a log or a stone for a pillow. [3] When, as is the way of nature, an appetite was roused in him to eat something, he could scarcely afterward bring himself to consume it. [4] For it once came to pass that, weighed down by sickness, he had eaten a little of the flesh of fowl, and having somehow regained his bodily strength, he entered the city of Assisi. [5] <ref to="Luke 7:12"> And when he had come to the gate of the city </ref>, he commanded a certain brother who was with him to tie a rope about his neck and so to drag him through the whole city like a thief, crying out in the voice of a herald and saying: "Behold, look upon this glutton, who has been fattened on the flesh of hens, which, without your knowledge, he has eaten." [6] Many therefore came running to so extraordinary a spectacle, and weeping together with redoubled sighs, they said: "Woe to us wretches, whose whole life is steeped in blood, and who nourish our hearts and bodies in dissipations and drunkenness." [7] <ref to="Acts 2:37"> And thus, pricked to the heart </ref>, they were stirred by so great an example toward a better state of life.
</p>

<p id="53">
[1] Many such things he did again and again in this manner, so that he might perfectly despise himself and summon others to everlasting honor. [2] <ref to="Ps 30:13"> He had become to himself as a vessel that is lost </ref>, burdened by no fear, no anxiety for his body; most vigorously he exposed it to insults, lest for love of it he should be driven to covet anything temporal. [3] A true despiser of himself, he profitably instructed all to despise themselves, by word and by example. [4] For what was the case? <ref to="Luke 4:15"> He was magnified by all </ref> and exalted by the praise-laden judgment of everyone; yet he alone reckoned himself most vile, he alone despised himself most ardently. [5] Often, indeed, when honored by all, he was wounded by excessive sorrow, and, barring human favor from his door, he would on the contrary arrange to be reproached by someone. [6] He would also call some brother to him, saying to him: "In obedience I tell you to insult me harshly and to speak the truth against the lies of these people." [7] And when that brother, though unwilling, called him a boor, a hireling, and useless, he, smiling and applauding heartily, would reply: [8] <ref to="Num 6:24"> "May the Lord bless you! </ref> for you speak the truest things; such things indeed it befits the son of Pietro di Bernardone to hear!" 9 Speaking thus, he recalled the humble origins of his birth.
</p>

<p id="54">
[1] For in order to show himself utterly contemptible and to set before others an example of true confession, whenever he had offended in anything, he was not ashamed to confess it openly in his preaching before all the people. [2] Indeed, if perchance he was touched by some unkind thought concerning anyone, or happened to let slip a word of reproach, at once with all humility he would confess his sin to the very person about whom he had thought or spoken something unkind, and beg pardon of him. [3] The conscience of his entire innocence, bearing witness, would not permit him to rest, guarding himself <ref to="2 Cor 8:7">with all earnestness</ref> until by soothing he had healed the wound of his mind. [4] In every sort of noteworthy thing he desired to make progress, yet not to be seen doing so, fleeing admiration by every means, lest he should ever fall into vanity. [5] Alas for us, who have thus lost you, worthy father, the very pattern of all kindness and humility: by a <ref to="Deut 16:18">just judgment</ref> indeed have we lost the one whom, while we had him, we did not take care to know!
</p>

## Chapter XX — Concerning the desire by which he was borne toward the embracing of martyrdom, traveling first to Spain and then to Syria, and how God through him delivered sailors from peril by multiplying their provisions. <a id="op1-20"></a>

<p id="55">
[1] Burning with divine love, <ref to="Prov 31:19">the most blessed father Francis strove always to set his hand to mighty deeds</ref>, and <ref to="Ps 118:32">walking the way of God's commandments with an enlarged heart</ref>, he longed to attain the very summit of perfection.
[2] For in the sixth year of his conversion, blazing above all with the desire for holy martyrdom, he resolved to cross over to the regions of Syria, to preach the Christian faith and penance to the Saracens and other unbelievers. 3 But when he had boarded a certain ship in order to make his way thither, contrary winds blew, and he found himself, together with the rest of the voyagers, in the regions of Slavonia.
[4] Seeing himself cheated of so great a desire, after a short interval of time he begged certain sailors bound for Ancona to take him along with them, since in that year scarcely any ship could make the crossing to the regions of Syria.
[5] But when they stubbornly refused to do this on account of the lack of provisions, the holy man of God, trusting greatly in the goodness of the Lord, slipped secretly aboard the ship with his companion.
[6] Then, by divine providence, there appeared a certain man—unknown to all—carrying with him the necessities of life, <ref to="Job 1:1">who called to himself from the ship a certain God-fearing man</ref> and said to him: <ref to="Tob 11:4">"Take all this with you</ref>, <ref to="Sir 8:12">and you shall faithfully provide it in time of need to those poor men hiding in the ship.</ref>
[7] And so it came to pass that, when a great storm had arisen and <ref to="Mark 6:48">they labored for many days at the rowing</ref> and had consumed all their food, the provisions of the poor man Francis alone remained.
[8] These, by divine grace and power, were so multiplied that—though there still lay before them voyages of many days—from their abundance they amply supplied the needs of all the way to the port of Ancona.
[9] And so the sailors, seeing that through the servant of God Francis they had escaped the perils of the sea, <ref to="Sir 50:19">gave thanks to almighty God</ref>, who ever shows himself wondrous and worthy of love in his servants.
</p>

<p id="56">
[1] Francis, <ref to="Dan 3:93">the servant of the most high God</ref>, leaving the sea behind, walked the land, and <ref to="Deut 21:3">cleaving it with the ploughshare of the word</ref>, he sowed the seed of life, bringing forth blessed fruit ( cf. Mat 13:3). [2] For at once many good and worthy men, both clerics and laymen, <ref to="2 Pet 1:4">fleeing the world</ref> and manfully crushing the devil, by the grace and the will of the Most High, followed him devoutly in his manner of life and his purpose. [3] But although the evangelical branch put forth from itself an abundance of the choicest fruits, yet the sublime resolve of martyrdom and its burning desire in no way grew cold within him. [4] For <ref to="Matt 25:19">after no long time</ref> he set out on the road toward Morocco, that he might preach the Gospel of Christ to the Miramolin and his confederates. [5] He was borne along by so great a desire <ref to="2 Cor 8:19">that at times he left behind the companion of his pilgrimage</ref>, and, drunk in spirit, hastened to carry out his purpose. [6] But the good God, whom it pleased of his sole kindness to remember me and many others, when Francis had already gone as far as Spain, <ref to="Gal 2:11">withstood him to his face</ref>, and, lest he proceed further, by sending an illness upon him, called him back from the journey he had begun.
</p>

<p id="57">
[1] Not long after his return to the church of Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, certain learned men and certain nobles attached themselves to him most gladly. [2] Treating them with honor and dignity, as one most noble and discerning of spirit, he most tenderly rendered to each what was his due. [3] Indeed, endowed with singular discernment, he prudently considered in all things the dignity proper to every rank. [4] Yet still he could not rest, but pursued the blessed impulse of his soul ever more ardently. [5] For in the thirteenth year of his conversion, journeying to the regions of Syria, while fierce and grievous wars raged daily between Christians and pagans, he took a companion with him and did not fear to present himself before the gaze of the Sultan of the Saracens. [6] But who could be equal to the telling of how steadfast a mind he stood before him, with what strength of soul he spoke to him, with what eloquence and confidence he answered those who mocked the Christian law? [7] For before he reached the Sultan, he was seized by the Sultan's men, subjected to insults, and battered with blows; yet he was not terrified, he did not flinch at the tortures threatened him, nor did he tremble when death was held over him. [8] And though he had been reproached by many with hostile spirit and adverse mind, he was nevertheless received by the Sultan with the greatest honor. [9] The Sultan honored him as far as he could, and by offering many gifts strove to bend his heart toward the riches of the world; [10] but when he saw him most vigorously despising all these things as so much dung, he was filled with the greatest wonder and beheld him as a man unlike all others; [11] <ref to="Mark 6:20"> he was deeply moved by his words and listened to him most gladly </ref>. [12] <ref to="Ps 126:5"> In all these things the Lord did not fulfill his desire </ref>, reserving for him the privilege of a singular grace.
</p>

## Chapter XXI - On the Preaching to the Birds and the Obedience of Creatures. <a id="op1-21"></a>

<p id="58">
[1] Meanwhile, as has been told, while <ref to="Acts 2:41">many were being added </ref> to the brothers, the most blessed father Francis was making his way through the valley of Spoleto. [2] He came to a certain place near Bevagna, in which a very great multitude of birds of various kinds had gathered together—namely doves, crows, and others that are commonly called jackdaws. [3] When the most blessed servant of God Francis saw them, since he was a man of the greatest fervor, bearing also toward lowly and irrational creatures a deep affection of tenderness and sweetness, he ran eagerly toward them, leaving his companions behind on the road. [4] And when he was now quite near, seeing that they were waiting for him, he greeted them in his customary manner. [5] But marveling not a little that the birds had not risen up in flight, as they are wont to do, and filled with immense joy, he humbly besought them that they ought to hear the word of God. [6] And among the many things he spoke to them, he added these words as well: "My brothers, you birds, you ought greatly to praise your Creator and to love him always, who has given you feathers to clothe you, wings to fly, and whatever was needful for you. [7] God has made you noble among his creatures, and has granted you a dwelling in the purity of the air; for though you neither sow <ref to="Luke 12:24">nor reap </ref>, he nonetheless, without any anxiety on your part, protects and governs you." [8] At these words those little birds, as he himself related and as did the brothers who had been with him, rejoicing in a wondrous manner according to their nature, began to stretch out their necks, to spread their wings, to open their beaks, and to gaze upon him. [9] <ref to="Luke 4:30">And he, passing through their midst, went </ref> back and forth, touching their heads and bodies with his tunic. [10] At last he blessed them and, having made the sign of the cross, granted them leave to fly off to another place. [11] <ref to="Acts 8:39">But the blessed father went on his way with his companions, rejoicing </ref>, <ref to="Acts 27:35">and giving thanks to God </ref>, whom all creatures venerate with suppliant confession. [12] Since by now he was simple by grace and not by nature, he began to accuse himself of negligence, that he had not preached to the birds before, seeing that they listened to the word of God with such great reverence. [13] And so it came about that from that day he diligently exhorted all winged creatures, all animals, and all reptiles, and even creatures without feeling, to the praise and love of the Creator; for daily, <ref to="Acts 22:16">having called upon the name </ref> of the Savior, he came to know their obedience by his own experience.
</p>

<p id="59">
[1] For when one day he had come to a town called Albianum to set forth the word of God, <ref to="Jude 13:16">mounting to a higher place </ref> that he might be seen by all, he began to ask for silence. [2] But while everyone fell silent and stood by reverently, a great many swallows, chattering and making a loud din, were building their nests in that very place. [3] Since they kept chattering so that blessed Francis could not be heard by the people, he spoke to the birds, saying: "My sisters the swallows, <ref to="Tob 12:20">now it is time that </ref> I too should speak, for you have already said quite enough until now. <ref to="Isa 1:10">Hear the word of the Lord </ref>, and be in silence and stillness, <ref to="2 Chr 36:21">until the discourse of the Lord be completed </ref>." [4] And those little birds, to the astonishment and wonder of all who stood by, fell silent at once, nor did they stir from that place until the preaching was ended. [5] <ref to="Matt 12:38">When therefore those men had seen this sign </ref>, they were filled with the greatest amazement, saying: <ref to="Luke 23:47">"Truly this man is holy </ref> and a friend of the Most High." [6] And with the deepest devotion they hastened at least to touch his garments, <ref to="Luke 24:53">praising and blessing God </ref>. [7] And it was a marvel indeed that these very creatures, lacking reason, should recognize his tender affection toward them and sense his most sweet love.
</p>

<p id="60">
[1] For when on a certain occasion he was staying at the fortified town of Greccio, a little hare, caught in a snare, was brought to him alive by one of the brothers. [2] Seeing it, the most blessed man, moved with tender compassion, said: "Brother hare, come to me. Why did you let yourself be deceived in this way?" [3] And at once, released by the brother who held it, it fled to the saint and, as if in the safest of refuges, with none compelling it, rested <ref to="2 Kgs 12:3; Luke 16:23">in his bosom</ref>. [4] When it had rested there a little while, the holy father, caressing it with a mother's affection, set it free, that it might return at liberty to the woods. [5] But when, though set down upon the ground again and again, it kept running back to the saint's bosom, he finally ordered it to be carried by the brothers to the forest nearby. [6] Something similar happened with a certain rabbit, an animal exceedingly wild and untamed, when he was on an island in the lake of Perugia.
</p>

<p id="61">
[1] He was moved by this same affection of tenderness toward fishes, which, whenever he had the chance, he would cast back alive into the water after they were caught, charging them to take heed lest they be caught again.
[2] For when at a certain time he was sitting in a little boat upon the lake of Rieti near a certain harbor, a fisherman, having caught a large fish that is commonly called a tench, devoutly offered it to him.
[3] Receiving it cheerfully and kindly, he began to call it by the name of brother, and placing it back in the water outside the boat, <ref to="Ps 112:2">he began devoutly to bless the name of the Lord</ref>.
[4] And so for some while, as he persisted in prayer, the said fish, playing in the water beside the boat, did not depart from the place where he had set it, until, his prayer completed, the holy man of God granted it leave to go.
[5] For thus the glorious father Francis, walking in the way of obedience and embracing perfectly the yoke of divine subjection, attained great dignity before God in the obedience of creatures.
[6] For even water was turned into wine for him, when at a certain time at the hermitage of Saint Urban he was suffering from a most grievous illness.
[7] At the taste of it he recovered with such ease that all believed it to be a divine miracle, as indeed it was.
[8] And truly holy is he whom the creatures thus obey, at whose nod the very elements pass over into other uses.
</p>

## Chapter XXII - On his preaching at Ascoli, and how, through the things he had touched with his hand, the sick were healed even in his absence. <a id="op1-22"></a>

<p id="62">
[1] At that time when, as has been told, the venerable father Francis preached to the birds, going about <ref to="Matt 9:35">the cities and the villages </ref> and everywhere scattering the seeds of his blessing, he came to the city of Ascoli. [2] There, while he spoke the word of God with his customary burning fervor, by <ref to="Ps 76:11">a change wrought by the right hand of the Most High </ref>, nearly the whole populace was filled with such grace and devotion <ref to="Luke 12:1">that, panting to hear and to see him, they trampled one another </ref>. [3] Indeed, thirty men, clerics and laymen alike, received from him at that time the habit of the holy religion. [4] So great was the faith of men and women, so great the devotion of mind toward the saint of God, <ref to="Matt 21:8">that anyone counted himself happy who could but touch even his garment </ref>. [5] When he entered any city, the clergy rejoiced, the bells were rung, the men exulted, the women shared in the gladness, the children applauded, and often, taking branches from the trees, they went out to meet him singing psalms. [6] Heretical depravity was put to confusion, the faith of the Church was exalted, and while the faithful raised their jubilant voices, the heretics hid themselves away. [7] For such marks of holiness appeared in him that no one dared to oppose him with words, <ref to="Num 27:22">since the throngs of the peoples looked upon him alone </ref>. [8] Amid all things and above all things he held that the faith of the holy Roman Church must be kept, venerated, and imitated, for in it alone lies the salvation of all who are to be saved. [9] He venerated the priests and embraced the whole ecclesiastical order with an exceeding great love.
</p>

<p id="63">
[1] The people would bring him loaves to bless, and keeping them for a long time afterward, they were cured of various ailments by tasting them. [2] So too, often, relying on their great faith, they would cut away pieces of his tunic, until at times he was left almost naked. [3] And what is yet more to be wondered at, if the holy father touched any object with his hand, health was restored to not a few even through that object. [4] For when a certain woman, dwelling in a little hamlet in the region of Arezzo, was with child, and the time of birth had come, she labored many days in giving birth, and thus, worn down by an unbelievable pain, she was granted neither death nor life in any measure. [5] <ref to="Luke 1:58">Her neighbors and kinsfolk had heard</ref> that blessed Francis was to pass along that road on his way to a certain hermitage. [6] <ref to="Acts 28:6">But while they were waiting for him</ref>, <ref to="Matt 2:12">it happened that blessed Francis passed to the said place by another road</ref>; for he had gone on horseback, since he was weak and infirm. [7] But when he had reached the place, he sent the horse back by a certain friar named Peter to that man who had granted it to him out of charity. [8] Friar Peter, leading the horse back, passed along that very road where the woman was being racked with pain. [9] When the men of that country saw him, they ran swiftly to him, thinking him to be blessed Francis; but recognizing that he was not, <ref to="Matt 18:31">they were exceedingly sorrowful</ref>. [10] <ref to="Luke 22:23">At length they began to ask among themselves</ref> whether some object might be found that blessed Francis had touched with his hand. [11] <ref to="Matt 24:48">And when they had spent a long while searching after these things</ref>, they at last found the reins of the bridle, which he had held in his hand while riding; and drawing the bridle from the mouth of the horse on which the holy father had sat, they laid upon the woman the reins that he had handled with his own hands. And she at once, the danger removed, gave birth with joy and in safety.
</p>

<p id="64">
[1] Gualfreducius, a dweller in Castrum Plebis, a man of devout life, <ref to="Acts 10:2">fearing and worshipping God together with all his household</ref>, kept in his possession a cord with which blessed Francis had once been girded. [2] Now it came to pass that in that region many men and not a few women were afflicted with various ailments and fevers. [3] And this man would go through the houses of the sick, and, dipping the cord in water, or mingling into it some little fragment of its threads, he gave it to the suffering to drink, <ref to="1 Pet 4:14">and thus in the name of Christ</ref> all of them recovered their health. [4] These things, moreover, were done in the absence of blessed Francis, and many more besides, which could not be set forth by us even in any degree, however long our discourse. [5] Yet of those things <ref to="Ps 98:9">which the Lord our God deigned to work through his presence</ref>, we shall briefly insert a few into this work.
</p>

## Chapter XXIII — How he healed a lame man at Toscanella and a paralytic at Narni. <a id="op1-23"></a>

<p id="65">
[1] <ref to="Luke 4:43">When at a certain time, in order to preach the kingdom of God</ref>, Francis, the holy man of God, was journeying through far-flung and varied regions, he came to a city called Tuscanella. [2] There, as he was scattering the seed of life in his accustomed manner, a certain knight of that same city received him as a guest, <ref to="Luke 7:12">who had an only son</ref>, lame and feeble in his whole body; and though the boy was tender in years, he had nonetheless passed beyond the time of weaning and yet still lingered in his cradle. [3] But the father of the child, seeing that the man of God was endowed with such great holiness, <ref to="Ruth 3:7">cast himself humbly at his feet</ref>, begging of him his son's health. [4] But Francis, reckoning himself unworthy and unfit for so great a power and grace, long refused to do this. [5] At last, overcome by the persistence of his entreaties, having first offered prayer, <ref to="Acts 3:7">he laid his hand upon the boy and, blessing him, lifted him up</ref>. [6] At once, while everyone looked on and rejoiced, <ref to="Acts 4:19">in the name of our Lord Jesus Christ</ref> the child rose up whole, and began to walk here and there throughout the house.
</p>

<p id="66">
[1] <ref to="1 Kgs 9:6">Now at a certain time, when Francis, the man of God,</ref> had come to Narni and was lingering there for several days, a certain man of that same city, Peter by name, <ref to="Matt 9:2">lay paralyzed upon his bed.</ref> [2] For over the space of five months he had been so robbed of the use of all his limbs that he could in no way rise nor stir himself in the slightest; and thus, having wholly lost the aid of feet, hands, and head, he was able to move only his tongue and to open his eyes. [3] But hearing that Saint Francis had come to Narni, he sent a messenger to the bishop of the city, that, out of regard for the divine compassion, he might deign to send <ref to="Luke 8:28">the servant of God Most High</ref> to him, trusting that by the sight and presence of that man he would be freed from the infirmity that held him fast. [4] And so indeed it came to pass that, when blessed Francis had drawn near to him and had made the sign of the cross over him from head to foot, at once, every malady put to flight, <ref to="Matt 12:13">he restored him to his former health.</ref>
</p>

## Chapter XXIV - How he restored sight to a blind woman, and at Gubbio straightened another woman who was crippled. <a id="op1-24"></a>

<p id="67">
[1] A certain woman of the aforementioned city, stricken with blindness of the eyes, received the sign of the cross from blessed Francis upon her eyes, and at once was found worthy to recover the longed-for light. [2] At Gubbio there was a woman who, having both hands contracted, could do nothing with them. [3] When she had learned that Saint Francis had entered the city, she straightway ran to him, and with a piteous face full of sorrow, showing him her withered hands, she began to entreat him that he might deign to touch them. [4] Moved by compassion, he touched her hands and healed them. [5] At once the woman returned home full of joy, and with her own hands made a single cheese-cake, which she offered to the holy man. [6] But he, charitably taking a little of that cheese-cake, bade the woman eat the rest with her household.
</p>

## Chapter XXV — How he freed a certain brother from the falling sickness, that is, from a demon, and how at the fortress of San Gemini he delivered a woman possessed by a demon. <a id="op1-25"></a>

<p id="68">
[1] One of the brothers frequently suffered from a most grievous affliction, horrible to behold, which by what name I should call it I do not know, since some hold that it was a malignant demon. [2] For often his whole body would be dashed about and, presenting a pitiable sight, <ref to="Mark 9:19">he would roll about foaming</ref>; now his limbs were drawn together, now stretched out, now folded and twisted, now made rigid and hard. [3] Sometimes, wholly extended and stiff, his feet level with his head, he would be lifted up on high, as much as the height of a man, and suddenly he would spring back down to the ground. [4] Taking pity on his most severe distress, the holy father Francis went to him and, when he had made his prayer, signed him with the cross and blessed him. [5] He, suddenly made whole, thereafter endured not the least of the torments of this affliction.
</p>

<p id="69">
[1] One day, as the most blessed father Francis was passing through the diocese of Narni, he came to a certain fortified town called San Gemini, <ref to="Luke 4:43">and preaching there the kingdom of God</ref>, he was received into hospitality together with three brothers <ref to="Acts 10:2">by a man who feared and worshipped God</ref> and enjoyed a good enough reputation in that land. [2] <ref to="Matt 15:22">Now his wife was tormented by a demon</ref>, as was known to all the inhabitants of that region, and her husband entreated blessed Francis on her behalf, trusting that she could be freed through his merits. [3] But because in his simplicity he desired far more to be held in contempt than to be exalted by the favors of this world through any display of holiness, he utterly refused to do this. [4] At length, since God was at stake in the matter, and as many kept begging him, he was overcome by their prayers and gave his consent. [5] He also called to himself the three brothers who were with him, and, setting a single brother in each of the corners of that house, he said to them: "Let us pray, brothers, to the Lord for this woman, that God may shake off from her the yoke of the devil (cf. Gen 27:40), 'unto the praise and glory' of his name. [6] Let us stand apart," he said, "in the corners of the house, lest this malignant spirit be able to flee from us or deceive us by seeking the recesses of the corners." [7] <ref to="Jdt 6:16">And when the prayer was finished</ref>, <ref to="Rom 15:13">blessed Francis, in the power of the Spirit</ref>, drew near to the woman, who was being wretchedly tormented and crying out horribly, and he said: "In the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, by obedience I command you, demon, <ref to="Acts 16:18">to go out of her</ref>, and dare not to hinder her any longer." [8] Scarcely had he finished these words <ref to="Gen 39:12">when, with fury and gnashing, it went out so very swiftly</ref> that, on account of the woman's sudden recovery and the demon's so very speedy obedience, the holy father thought he had been made the object of an illusion. [9] At once, blushing, he withdrew from that place, divine providence so arranging it, lest he should in any way glory emptily. [10] Hence it came about that, when on another occasion blessed Francis was passing through the same place, brother Elias was with him, and behold, that woman, as soon as she learned of his arrival, <ref to="Acts 9:34">straightway rose up</ref> and, running through the square, <ref to="Matt 15:23">cried out after</ref> him, begging that he would deign to speak to her. [11] But he was unwilling to speak to her, knowing her to be that woman from whom he had once cast out a demon by divine power. [12] <ref to="Esth 13:13">Yet she kissed the prints of his feet</ref>, <ref to="Acts 28:25">giving thanks to God</ref> and to the holy Francis, his servant, <ref to="Hos 13:14">who had delivered her from the hand of death</ref>. [13] At last brother Elias urged the saint with entreaty; and he spoke to her, being assured by many concerning her infirmity, as has been told, and her deliverance.
</p>

## Chapter XXVI — How he also cast out a demon at the City of Castello. <a id="op1-26"></a>

<p id="70">
[1] At the City of Castello, too, there was a woman possessed by a demon. [2] While the most blessed father Francis was staying there, the woman was brought to the house in which he was lodging. [3] But that woman, <ref to="John 20:11">standing outside</ref>, began to gnash her teeth and, with a grim countenance, to shriek in a pitiable voice, <ref to="John 19:40; Matt 10:1">as is the manner of unclean spirits</ref>. [4] Many people of that city, of both sexes, came forward and entreated holy Francis on the woman's behalf; for that malign spirit had long tormented her with its torturing and unsettled them with its shrieking. [5] The holy father then sent to her a friar who was with him, wishing to test whether it was a demon or a woman's deception. [6] When the woman saw him she began to mock him, knowing full well that he was not holy Francis. [7] The holy father was within at prayer, and when his prayer was finished he came out; but the woman began to tremble and to roll upon the ground, unable to endure his power. [8] Calling her to himself, holy Francis said: "In the power of obedience I command you, unclean spirit, <ref to="Mark 5:8">come out of</ref> her." [9] And at once it released her without any harm, withdrawing in considerable indignation. [10] <ref to="Sir 50:19">Thanks be to almighty God</ref>, <ref to="1 Cor 12:11">who works all things in all</ref>. [11] Yet, since we have resolved to set forth not the miracles, which do not make holiness but display it, but rather the excellence of his life and the most sincere pattern of his manner of living, we shall pass over these things—being too many—and weave together the works of <ref to="Heb 5:9">eternal salvation</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter XXVII - Concerning the clarity and steadfastness of his mind, and concerning his preaching before the lord Pope Honorius, and how he entrusted himself and the brothers to the lord Hugo, bishop of Ostia. <a id="op1-27"></a>

<p id="71">
[1] <ref to="1 Kgs 9:6"> Francis, the man of God </ref>, had learned not to seek his own interests, but rather those things which he perceived above all to be of advantage to the salvation of others; yet beyond everything he longed to be set free, <ref to="Phil 1:23">and to be with Christ </ref>. [2] For this reason his supreme endeavor was <ref to="1 John 2:15"> to remain free from all that is in the world </ref>, lest even for an hour the serenity of his mind should be disturbed by contact with any particle of dust. [3] He rendered himself insensible to all the clamor of outward things, and gathering in his outer senses on every side with his whole heart and restraining the movements of his soul, he gave himself to God alone; he made his nest in the clefts of the rock, <ref to="Song 2:14">and his dwelling in the hollow of the wall </ref>. [4] With blessed devotion he wandered, indeed, through those heavenly mansions, and, wholly emptied of self, he lingered long within the wounds of the Savior. [5] He often chose solitary places, therefore, that he might be able to direct his whole soul toward God; and yet he was not slow, when he perceived the opportune time, to engage in affairs and gladly to attend to the salvation of his neighbors. [6] For his safest harbor was prayer—not the prayer of a single moment, nor empty or presumptuous, but prayer prolonged in time, full of devotion, gentle in humility: if he began in the evening, he could scarcely finish by morning; walking, sitting, eating, and drinking, he was intent upon prayer. [7] To churches abandoned and set in the wilderness he would go alone by night to pray, and there, protected by divine grace, he overcame many fears and many anguishes of soul.
</p>

<p id="72">
[1] <ref to="Ezek 21:24">Hand to hand</ref> he would do battle with the devil, who in places of this sort assailed him not only inwardly with temptations but also terrified him outwardly with collapses and tumbling ruins. [2] Yet this most valiant soldier of God, knowing that his Lord could do all things everywhere, would not yield to these terrors, but said within his heart: "O malicious one, you can hurl no more of your evil weapons against me than if we stood out in the open before all." [3] He was indeed steadfast in the extreme, and gave heed to nothing <ref to="1 Cor 7:32">save what belonged to the Lord</ref>. [4] For when he very often preached the word of God among many thousands of people, he was as untroubled as if he were speaking with a single intimate companion. [5] He looked upon the vast multitude of the people as though they were one man, and to one man he preached as diligently as to a multitude. [6] Out of the purity of his mind he secured for himself confidence in delivering his sermon, and without any forethought he spoke things wondrous and unheard of by all. [7] But if at times he prepared his sermon by some prior meditation, then, when the people had gathered, he could not recall what he had meditated, and knew not how to say anything else; whereupon, without any blush of shame, he would confess to the people that he had pondered many things, of which he could now recall nothing whatever. [8] And then suddenly he would be filled with such eloquence that he turned the minds of his hearers to wonder. [9] At other times, however, knowing nothing to say, he would merely give his blessing, and by this alone dismiss the people most powerfully edified.
</p>

<p id="73">
[1] But when on a certain occasion, as the needs of religion required, he had come to the city of Rome, he greatly thirsted to speak before the lord Pope Honorius and the venerable cardinals. [2] Perceiving this, the lord Hugo, glorious Bishop of Ostia, who venerated the holy man of God with a singular affection, was filled with both fear and joy, marveling at the holy man's fervor and beholding his simple purity. [3] But <ref to="2 Macc 8:18">trusting in the mercy of the Almighty</ref>, <ref to="Sir 8:12">which never fails those who devoutly serve Him in the hour of need</ref>, he brought him in before the lord Pope and the reverend cardinals. [4] Standing before such great princes, and having received leave and blessing, <ref to="Luke 7:15">he began to speak undaunted</ref>. [5] And indeed he spoke with such fervor of spirit that, unable to contain himself for joy, as he uttered the word from his mouth he moved his feet as though leaping—not as one given to frivolity, but as one burning with the fire of divine love—not stirring laughter but wringing forth lamentation and grief. [6] <ref to="Acts 2:37">For many of them were pricked to the heart</ref>, <ref to="Acts 4:13">marveling at the divine grace and at so great a constancy in the man</ref>. [7] Yet the venerable lord Bishop of Ostia hung in suspense from fear, praying to the Lord with all his inmost being that the blessed man's simplicity might not be held in contempt, since in him the holy man's glory and his shame alike were reflected, <ref to="Luke 12:42">inasmuch as he had been set as a father over his household</ref>.
</p>

<p id="74">
[1] For Saint Francis had clung to him <ref to="Luke 7:12">as a son to his father and an only child to his mother</ref>, <ref to="2 Kgs 12:3">sleeping and finding rest, untroubled, in the bosom of his kindness</ref>. [2] He indeed fulfilled the office of a shepherd and performed the shepherd's work, yet to the holy man he had left the name of shepherd. [3] The blessed father foresaw what was needful, but that fortunate lord brought to fulfillment what had been provided for. [4] Oh, how many there were—especially in the beginning, while these things were being done—who lay in wait to destroy the <ref to="Ps 143:12">newly planted</ref> order! [5] <ref to="Jer 2:21">Oh, how many strove to choke that chosen vineyard</ref>, which the hand of the Lord was most graciously planting fresh in the world! [6] How many endeavored to steal away and devour its first and purest fruits! [7] <ref to="Acts 5:36">All of whom were slain by the sword of so reverend a father and lord and brought to nothing</ref>. [8] For he was a river of eloquence, a wall of the Church, a defender of truth, and a lover of the humble. [9] Blessed therefore and memorable was that day on which the holy man of God committed himself to so venerable a lord. [10] For when at a certain time this lord was serving, as he often used to do, as legate of the Apostolic See in Tuscany, blessed Francis—not yet having many brothers, and wishing to go into France—came to Florence, where the said bishop was then staying. [11] Not yet were the one and the other joined by any special intimacy, but the mere report of each one's blessed life had bound them together in a mutual and heartfelt charity.
</p>

<p id="75">
[1] Now since it was the custom of blessed Francis, whenever he entered any city or land, to present himself before the bishops or priests, upon hearing of the presence of so great a prelate he presented himself to his clemency with profound reverence. [2] When the lord bishop saw him, he received him with humble devotion, as he always did toward all who professed the holy religious life, and most especially toward those who bore the noble emblem of blessed poverty and holy simplicity. [3] And since <ref to="2 Cor 8:14">he was anxious to supply the want of the poor</ref> and to attend to their affairs with particular care, he diligently inquired into the reason for his coming, and most graciously grasped his purpose. [4] When he perceived that, beyond all others, he held in contempt every earthly thing and burned with that fire <ref to="Luke 12:49">which Jesus sent upon the earth</ref>, <ref to="1 Kgs 18:1">his own soul was from that moment knit to the soul of the other</ref>, devoutly and tenderly asking for his prayers and most graciously offering him his protection in all things. [5] He accordingly urged him not to complete the journey he had begun, but to watch attentively over the care and keeping of those whom the Lord God had entrusted to him. [6] But Saint Francis, seeing that so reverend a lord bore so devout a spirit, so sweet an affection, and so effective a discourse, <ref to="Matt 2:11">rejoiced with exceedingly great joy</ref>, <ref to="Acts 10:25">and thereupon falling at his feet</ref>, with a devoted heart handed over and entrusted both himself and his brothers to him.
</p>

## Chapter XXVIII - On the spirit of charity and the feeling of compassion with which he burned toward the poor, and what he did concerning the sheep and the little lambs. <a id="op1-28"></a>

<p id="76">
[1] <ref to="Job 29:16">A father of the poor</ref>, the poor man Francis, conforming himself to all the poor, found it grievous to behold anyone poorer than himself—not from any craving for <ref to="Gal 5:26">empty glory</ref>, but solely from the impulse of compassion. [2] And although he was content with a tunic that was mean enough and rough, he often longed to share even that with some poor man. [3] But that he, a most wealthy pauper, led by a great affection of piety, might somehow come to the aid of the poor, in times of bitter cold he would ask the rich of this world to lend him a cloak or some furs. [4] And when these men did so the more readily and devoutly than the most blessed father even asked it of them, he would say to them: "On this condition I shall accept it from you: that you in no way expect ever to have it back again." [5] And as soon as some poor man first crossed his path, exulting and rejoicing he would clothe the poor man with what he had received. [6] It was most distressing to him whenever he saw any poor person reproached, <ref to="Josh 8:34">or heard a word of cursing</ref> hurled by anyone against any creature. [7] Hence it happened that a certain brother flung a word of abuse at a poor man who was begging alms, saying: "See to it that you are not perhaps rich and only feigning poverty." [8] <ref to="Job 29:16">Hearing this, the father of the poor</ref>, holy Francis, grieved deeply and most harshly rebuked the brother who had uttered such words, and commanded him to strip himself before the poor man and, kissing his feet, to beg his pardon. [9] For he used to say: "Whoever curses a poor man does injury to Christ, whose noble emblem he bears, who 'made himself poor for us in this world.'" [10] Often, therefore, finding poor men burdened with wood or other loads, he would put his own shoulders—though all too weak—beneath them to help them.
</p>

<p id="77">
[1] He overflowed with the spirit of charity, bearing a heart of tender compassion not only toward men suffering hardship but also toward dumb beasts, creeping things, birds, and all other creatures, whether sentient or insentient. [2] Yet among every kind of animal he loved lambs with a special fondness and a readier affection, because the humility of our Lord Jesus Christ is in the Sacred Scriptures most often likened to a lamb and most fittingly so compared. [3] In the same way he embraced more dearly and beheld more gladly all those things in which some allegorical likeness of the Son of God might be found. [4] For when at one time he was journeying through the March of Ancona, <ref to="Acts 15:36">and had preached the word of the Lord in that same city</ref>, and had set out toward Osimo together with brother Paul, <ref to="Acts 26:16">whom he had appointed minister</ref> of all the brothers in that province, he came upon a certain shepherd in the fields, pasturing a flock of she-goats and he-goats. [5] And amid the multitude of she-goats and he-goats there was a single little ewe, walking more humbly and grazing more quietly. [6] When blessed Francis saw her, <ref to="Gen 6:6">he stopped in his tracks and, touched with sorrow within his heart</ref>, sighing deeply, he said to the brother who accompanied him: "Do you not see this ewe, who walks so meekly among these she-goats and he-goats? [7] Even so, I tell you, <ref to="Matt 11:29">did our Lord Jesus Christ walk, meek and humble</ref>, among the Pharisees and the chief priests. [8] I beg you therefore, my son, for His charity's sake, that you have compassion with me upon this little ewe, and that, paying the price, <ref to="Ps 135:11">we lead her out from the midst</ref> of these she-goats and he-goats."
</p>

<p id="78">
[1] Brother Paul, marveling at his grief, began himself also to share in his sorrow. [2] But since they possessed nothing beyond the humble tunics with which they were clothed, and were anxious about how to pay the price, at once a certain merchant who was traveling that way appeared and offered the very price they desired. [3] And so, <ref to="Col 3:17">giving thanks to God</ref>, they took the sheep and came to Osimo, and entering before the bishop of the city, they were received by him with great reverence. [4] Yet the lord bishop marveled both at the sheep <ref to="4 Kgs 4:9">which the man of God</ref> was leading, and at the affection by which he was drawn toward it. [5] But when the servant of Christ had unfolded for him a long parable of discourse concerning the sheep, the bishop, <ref to="Acts 2:37">pricked at heart</ref> over the purity of the man of God, <ref to="Acts 27:35">gave thanks to God</ref>. [6] On the following day, having gone out from the city and pondering what he should do with the sheep, by the counsel of his companion and brother he handed it over to be kept in a certain cloister of the handmaids of Christ at San Severino. [7] The venerable handmaids of Christ also received the little sheep with joy, as a great gift given to them by God. [8] Carefully guarding it for a long time, they wove from its wool a tunic and sent it to the blessed father Francis at the church of Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, at the time of a certain chapter. [9] Receiving it with great reverence and exultation of spirit, the saint of God embraced it and kissed it, inviting all who stood about him to share in so great a joy.
</p>

<p id="79">
[1] On another occasion, as he was passing through that same March, with the same brother cheerfully accompanying him, he met a certain man who was carrying upon his shoulder two little lambs, bound and slung up, to market that he might sell them. [2] When blessed Francis heard the lambs bleating, <ref to="3 Kgs 3:26">his bowels were moved within him</ref>, and drawing near he touched them, like a mother over a weeping child, showing the affection of his compassion. [3] And he said to the man: "Why do you torment my brothers the lambs, thus bound and slung up?" [4] He answered and said: "I am carrying them to market to sell them, compelled by need of money." [5] And the saint said: "What will become of them afterward?" To him the man replied: "The buyers will kill them and eat them." [6] "God forbid!" the saint answered, "let that not be! Rather, take in payment the cloak I am wearing, and grant me the lambs." [7] With a glad heart the man handed over the little lambs and received the cloak, for the cloak was worth far more, which the saint, to ward off the cold, had that very day borrowed from a certain devout man. [8] But the saint, having received the little lambs, anxiously pondered within himself what he should do with them, and having taken counsel with the brother accompanying him, he gave them back to that man to be cared for, and charged him that he should never at any time sell them, nor do them any harm, but should preserve, feed, and tend them diligently.
</p>

## Chapter XXIX - On the love he bore toward all creatures for the sake of their Creator, and a description of both the outer and the inner man. <a id="op1-29"></a>

<p id="80">
[1] It would be far too long, indeed impossible, to enumerate and gather together all that the glorious father Francis did and taught <ref to="Phil 1:22">while he lived in the flesh</ref>. [2] For who could ever express that supreme devotion by which he was borne <ref to="Matt 22:21">toward all the things that are God's</ref>? [3] <ref to="Sir 18:2">Who would be able to recount</ref> the sweetness he enjoyed when he contemplated in created things the wisdom of their Creator, His power and His goodness? [4] Truly, from this reflection he was very often filled with a wondrous and unspeakable joy, when he gazed upon the sun, when he beheld the moon, when he looked upon the stars and the firmament. [5] O simple devotion, and O devout simplicity! [6] He burned with an excessive love even toward little worms, because he had read what was said of the Savior: <ref to="Ps 21:7">I am a worm and no man</ref>. [7] And for that reason he would gather them up from the road and set them away in a safe place, lest they be crushed beneath the feet of passers-by. [8] What shall I say of the other lowlier creatures, when even for the bees in winter, lest they perish in the bitterness of the cold, he ordered honey or the finest wine to be set out? [9] The effectiveness of their labors and the excellence of their instinct he extolled with such great praise, to the glory of the Lord, that he would often spend a whole day in the praises of them and of the other creatures. [10] For just as long ago the three young men, set in the furnace of blazing fire, summoned all the elements to praise and glorify the Creator of the universe, so this man too, filled with the Spirit of God, did not cease in all the elements and creatures to glorify, <ref to="Dan 3:17">to praise and to bless</ref> the Creator and Governor of all things.
</p>

<p id="81">
[1] How great a gladness, do you suppose, the loveliness of flowers brought to his soul, when he beheld the beauty of their form and caught the fragrance of their sweetness? [2] At once he turned the eye of his contemplation to the beauty of that Flower which, <ref to="Isa 11:1">springing forth, radiant in the season of spring, from the root of Jesse</ref>, by its fragrance roused countless thousands of the dead to life. [3] And whenever he came upon an abundance of flowers, he would preach to them and summon them to the praise of the Lord, as though they were endowed with reason. [4] So too the cornfields and the vineyards, <ref to="Sir 24:19">the stones and the woods and all the lovely things of the fields</ref>, the flowing of springs and every green and growing thing of the gardens, earth and fire, air and wind—all these, with utterly sincere purity, he urged to the love of God and exhorted to a willing obedience. [5] In short, he called all creatures by the name of brother, and in a manner surpassing and unknown to others he discerned with the keen edge of his heart the <ref to="1 Cor 14:25">hidden things of creatures</ref>, <ref to="Rom 8:21">as one who had already passed into the liberty of the glory of the children of God</ref>. [6] Now in the heavens, O good Jesus, he praises you, the wondrous one, together with the angels—he who indeed, <ref to="Job 20:4">while placed upon the earth</ref>, proclaimed you, the lovable one, to all creatures.
</p>

<p id="82">
[1] For he was moved beyond the understanding of men whenever he uttered your name, <ref to="Ps 8:2; Isa 6:3">O holy Lord</ref>; and being wholly absorbed in jubilation and filled with a most chaste delight, he seemed indeed a new man, one belonging to another world. [2] For this reason, wherever he found anything written—whether sacred or secular—upon the road, in a house, or on the ground, he would gather it up most reverently and lay it away in a holy or seemly place, lest the name of the Lord, or some writing pertaining to it, should be lying there. [3] Indeed, when one day a certain brother asked him why he so carefully gathered up even the writings of pagans, and writings in which the name of the Lord was not contained, he answered, saying: "My son, because there are letters there, <ref to="Deut 5:11">out of which is composed the most glorious name of the Lord God</ref>. [4] Moreover, the good that is there belongs neither to the pagans nor to any men, but to God alone, 'to whom belongs every good.' [5] And what is no less to be marveled at, when he had certain letters written by way of greeting or admonition, he would not allow a single letter or syllable to be struck out from them, even though it had often been set down superfluously or improperly."
</p>

<p id="83">
[1] O how beautiful, how splendid, <ref to="2 Kgs 6:22">how glorious he appeared </ref> in the innocence of his life, in the simplicity of his words, in the purity of his heart, in his love of God, in fraternal charity, in burning obedience, in harmonious compliance, <ref to="Jude 13">in his angelic aspect </ref>.
[2] Sweet in manners, gentle by nature, affable in speech, most apt in exhortation, <ref to="Prov 11:13">most faithful in what was entrusted to him </ref>, provident in counsel, effective in business, gracious in all things (cf. Est 2:15).
[3] Serene in mind, sweet in spirit, <ref to="2 Tim 1:7">sober in disposition </ref>, rapt in contemplation, constant in prayer, and fervent in all things.
[4] Steadfast in purpose, stable in virtue, persevering in grace, and ever the same in all things.
[5] Swift to pardon, <ref to="Jas 1:19">slow to anger </ref>, free in spirit, brilliant in memory, <ref to="Wis 7:22">subtle in discourse </ref>, circumspect in choosing, and in all things simple.
[6] Rigorous toward himself, kind toward others, discerning in all things.
[7] A man most eloquent, cheerful of face, kindly of countenance, free from sloth, untouched by insolence.
[8] Of middling stature, nearer to small; his head moderate and round, his face somewhat oblong and drawn out, his brow smooth and small, his eyes of middling size, <ref to="Matt 6:22">black and clear </ref>, his hair dark, his eyebrows straight, his nose even, slender and straight, his ears upright but small, his temples flat,
[9] <ref to="Prov 15:4">a peaceable tongue </ref>, fiery and sharp, his voice forceful, <ref to="Song 2:14">sweet </ref>, clear and resonant, his teeth close-set, even and white, his lips modest and thin, his beard black, not fully thick with hair,
[10] his neck slender, his shoulders straight, his arms short, his hands delicate, his fingers long, his nails extended, his legs slender, his feet little, his skin tender, his flesh very scant, his garment rough, his sleep most brief, his hand most generous.
[11] And because he was most humble, <ref to="Titus 3:2">he showed all meekness toward men </ref>, profitably conforming himself to the ways of all. Holier among the holy, <ref to="Wis 4:10; Gen 3:22">among sinners as one of </ref> them.
[12] Help sinners, then, O lover of sinners, most holy father, and those whom you behold lying wretchedly in the filth of their offenses, deign, we beseech you, most merciful one, to raise up by your most glorious intercessions.
</p>

## Chapter XXX - Of the manger that he made on the day of the Lord's Nativity. <a id="op1-30"></a>

<p id="84">
[1] His highest aim, his chief desire, and his supreme resolve was to observe the holy Gospel in all things and through all things, and perfectly—with all watchfulness, all zeal, with the whole longing of his mind and the whole fervor of his heart—"to follow the teaching and imitate the footsteps" of our Lord Jesus Christ. [2] By constant meditation he recalled his words, and with the keenest reflection he pondered his works. [3] Above all, the humility of the Incarnation and the love of the Passion so filled his memory that he scarcely wished to think of anything else. [4] Worthy of remembrance, then, and to be recalled with reverent memory is what he did in the third year before the day of his glorious passing, at the town that is called Greccio, on the day of the Nativity of our Lord Jesus Christ. [5] <ref to="Job 1:1">There was in that land a certain man</ref> named John, <ref to="Phil 4:8">of good repute</ref> but of even better life, whom blessed Francis loved with a special affection; for although he had been most noble and honorable in his own land, he trampled upon the nobility of the flesh and pursued the nobility of the soul. [6] Now blessed Francis, as he was often accustomed to do, had this man summoned to him about fifteen days before the Nativity of the Lord, [7] and said to him: "If you desire that we should celebrate the present feast of the Lord at Greccio, hasten to go ahead, and <ref to="Prov 24:27">diligently prepare</ref> the things I tell you. [8] For I wish to enact the memory of that Child <ref to="Matt 2:1">who was born in Bethlehem</ref>, and in some way to behold with bodily eyes the hardships of his infant needs, <ref to="Luke 2:7">how he was laid in a manger</ref>, and how, with an ox and an ass standing by, he was placed upon the hay." [9] <ref to="John 20:4">Hearing this, the good and faithful man ran quickly</ref>, and prepared in the aforesaid place all that the saint had said.
</p>

<p id="85">
[1] And so the day of gladness drew near; the time of rejoicing arrived. [2] The brothers were summoned from many places; the men and women of that region, each according to their means and with exultant hearts, prepared candles and torches to light up the night which, with its glittering star, has illumined all the days and years. [3] At last the holy one of God came and, finding all things made ready, <ref to="John 8:56">saw it and was glad</ref>. [4] Indeed, a manger is prepared, hay is brought, an ox and an ass are led in. [5] There simplicity is honored, poverty is exalted, humility is commended, and Greccio was made, as it were, into a new Bethlehem. [6] <ref to="Ps 138:12">The night is made bright as the day</ref>, and it proved delightful to both men and beasts. [7] The people arrive and rejoice with fresh joys at the new mystery. [8] The woods ring with voices, and the rocks answer back the jubilant throng. [9] The brothers sing, rendering to the Lord the praises due to him, and the whole night resounds with jubilation. [10] <ref to="Mark 1:24">The holy one of God stands</ref> before the manger, full of sighs, broken with devotion, and overflowing with a wondrous joy. [11] The solemnities of the Mass are celebrated above the manger, and the priest enjoys a new consolation.
</p>

<p id="86">
[1] The holy man of God is clothed in Levitical vestments, for he was a deacon, and with sonorous voice he chants the holy Gospel. [2] And indeed his voice, 'a vehement voice, <ref to="Song 2:14">a sweet voice </ref>, a clear voice, and a sonorous voice,' summoned all to the highest rewards. [3] Then he preaches to the people standing about, and concerning the nativity of the poor King and the little town of Bethlehem he pours forth honeyed words. [4] Often, too, when he wished to name Christ as 'Jesus,' burning with excessive love, he would call Him 'the little Child of Bethlehem,' and saying 'Bethlehem' in the manner of a bleating sheep, he would fill his whole mouth with the word—yet more with sweet affection than with sound. [5] His very lips also, when he named 'the Child of Bethlehem' or 'Jesus,' he would, as it were, lick with his tongue, savoring upon his blissful palate and swallowing down the sweetness of this word. [6] <ref to="1 Macc 5:50">There the gifts of the Almighty are multiplied, and by a certain man of virtue </ref> a wondrous vision is beheld. [7] For he saw lying in the manger a little child, lifeless, to whom he saw the holy man of God draw near and rouse that same child as though from the slumber of sleep. [8] Nor was this vision unfitting, <ref to="Ps 30:13">since the child Jesus had been given over to oblivion in the hearts of many </ref>, in whom, by the working of His own grace, through His servant the holy Francis He was raised again and stamped upon their attentive memory. [9] At length the solemn vigils are concluded, and each one returned with joy to his own home.
</p>

<p id="87">
[1] The hay that had been placed in the manger is preserved, <ref to="Ps 35:7">that through it the Lord might save beasts of burden and animals, even as he has multiplied his holy mercy</ref>. [2] And indeed it came to pass in just this way, that many animals throughout the surrounding region, afflicted with diverse maladies, were delivered from their ailments by eating of this hay. [3] Nay more, women too, labouring in grievous and protracted childbirth, by laying upon themselves a portion of that same hay, brought forth their offspring in safety; and a throng of both sexes, suffering from sundry calamities, there obtained the health they longed for. [4] <ref to="3 Kgs 8:63">At length the place of the manger was consecrated as a temple to the Lord</ref>, and in honour of the most blessed father Francis an altar was raised above the manger and a church was dedicated, [5] <ref to="Dan 5:21">so that, where once the animals had eaten the fodder of hay</ref>, <ref to="1 Pet 1:19; 1 Cor 1:10">there henceforth, unto the health of soul and body, men might eat the flesh of the spotless and undefiled Lamb, Jesus Christ our Lord</ref>, [6] <ref to="Titus 2:14">who in supreme and unspeakable love gave himself for us</ref>, who together with the Father and the Holy Spirit lives and reigns, God gloriously eternal through all ages of ages. Amen. Alleluia (cf. Apoc 1:18; 19:4), Alleluia. HERE ENDS THE FIRST WORK ON THE LIFE AND DEEDS OF BLESSED FRANCIS.

## Chapter I - On the substance of this little work, on the time when St. Francis happily passed away, and on his spiritual progress. <a id="op2-1"></a>

<p id="88">
[1] In the foregoing treatise, which by the grace of the Savior we brought to a fitting close, we have set down, after our own fashion, the life and deeds of our most blessed father Francis, recounting them up to the eighteenth year of his conversion. [2] The remaining acts of his, however, from the penultimate year of his life onward, insofar as we have been able rightly to learn them, we shall briefly append to this little work, and for the present we mean to note down only those things that more pressingly demand attention, so that those who desire to say more than these may always find something to add. [3] And so, in the year of our Lord's Incarnation one thousand two hundred and twenty-six, in the fourteenth indiction, on the fourth day before the Nones of October, a Sunday, our most blessed father Francis—in the city of Assisi, from which he sprang, at Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, where he himself first planted the order of Friars Minor—having completed twenty years from the time he most perfectly cleaved to Christ, <ref to="1 Pet 2:21">following the life and the footsteps of the Apostles</ref>, departed from the prison-house of the flesh and, perfectly bringing to fulfillment what he had begun, took his most blessed flight to the dwelling of the heavenly spirits. [4] With hymns and praises his sacred and holy body was laid to rest in that city and honorably entombed, where, to the glory of the Almighty, it shines forth with many miracles. Amen.
</p>

<p id="89">
[1] <ref to="Bar 3:13">Now since in the way of God</ref> and in the knowledge of Him he had been little or not at all instructed from the first flower of his youth, and since for no small time he persisted in his natural simplicity and in the fervor of his vices, <ref to="Rom 6:7">being justified from sin by the changing of the right hand of the Most High</ref>, <ref to="Luke 1:35">by the grace and power of the Most High</ref>, <ref to="Deut 34:9">he was filled with divine wisdom above all who were found in his time</ref>. [2] For when the teaching of the Gospel had everywhere greatly failed in deeds—not in particular places but generally—this man was sent by God, <ref to="John 1:6">that universally throughout the whole world he might, after the example of the Apostles, bear witness to the truth</ref>. [3] And so it came to pass that by his teaching he showed most plainly that all the wisdom of the world was foolishness, and within a short span of time he turned it toward the true wisdom of God <ref to="1 Cor 1:20">through the foolishness of preaching</ref>, <ref to="Dan 9:25">with Christ as his leader</ref>. [4] <ref to="1 Pet 1:5">For in the last time</ref> a new evangelist, <ref to="Gen 2:10; Isa 44:3">like one of the rivers of paradise</ref>, <ref to="Esth 13:4">throughout the whole circle of the earth</ref>, poured out the streams of the Gospel in holy irrigation, and by his works preached the way of the Son of God and the teaching of truth. [5] Thereupon there arose in him and through him an unhoped-for rejoicing of the whole earth and a holy newness: the seed of ancient religion suddenly made new again those long grown old and much grown stale. [6] A new spirit was given in the hearts of the elect, and in their midst was poured out (cf. Ezek 11:19; 36:26) the saving anointing, when, like one of the luminaries of heaven, <ref to="Gen 2:10; Isa 44:3">the servant of Christ</ref> and the holy one shone down from above with a new rite and with new signs. [7] Through him the ancient miracles were renewed, while in the desert of this world, by a new order yet after the ancient manner, a fruitful vine was planted, <ref to="Sir 24:23; Ps 51:10">bringing forth flowers of sweetness for the fragrance</ref> of holy virtues, <ref to="Ezek 17:6">spreading everywhere the branches of sacred religion</ref>.
</p>

<p id="90">
[1] For, <ref to="Jas 5:17">though he was a man subject to suffering like ourselves</ref>, he was nevertheless not content to observe the common precepts, but, overflowing with the most fervent charity, he seized upon the way of total perfection, laid hold of the summit of perfect sanctity, <ref to="Ps 118:96">and beheld the end of all consummation</ref>. [2] Hence every order, every sex, every age finds in him plain instructions of saving doctrine, and finds also the choicest examples of holy works. [3] <ref to="Prov 31:19">If any propose to set their hands to mighty deeds</ref> <ref to="1 Cor 12:31">and strive to emulate the better gifts of a more excellent way</ref>, let them gaze into the mirror of his life, and they will learn every perfection. [4] But if any betake themselves to things more humble and more level, fearing to walk along steep paths and to scale the mountain's peak, even on this lower step they will find in him fitting memorials. [5] <ref to="1 Cor 1:22">If, finally, any seek signs and wonders</ref>, let them inquire of his sanctity, and what they ask they shall obtain. [6] And indeed his glorious life sheds upon the perfection of the saints of old a brighter light: the passion of Jesus Christ proves this, and His cross most fully manifests it. [7] In very truth, in five parts of his body the venerable father was marked with the seal of the passion and the cross, as though he had hung upon the cross together with the Son of God. [8] <ref to="Eph 5:32">This is a great sacrament</ref>, and it discloses the majesty of love's special privilege; yet within it lies hidden a secret counsel, and a mystery to be revered is concealed there, which we believe is known to God alone, and through that same saint in part revealed to a certain one. [9] Therefore it is not fitting to attempt much in his praises, whose praise comes from Him who is the praise of all, the fountain and the mightiest honor, the giver of the rewards of light. [10] Blessing therefore the holy God, <ref to="Luke 14:53; Isa 58:13">the true God</ref>, let us return to our history.
</p>

## Chapter II — On the supreme longing of blessed Francis, and how, upon opening the book, he understood the Lord's will concerning himself. <a id="op2-2"></a>

<p id="91">
[1] On a certain occasion the blessed and venerable father Francis, having left behind the crowds of the world that flocked to him daily with the deepest devotion to hear and to see him, sought out a place of rest and the secrecy of solitude, longing there to be free for God and to wipe away <ref to="Luke 10:11">whatever dust might have clung to him from his dealings with men</ref>. [2] It was his custom to divide the time granted him for meriting grace, and, as he saw to be needful, to spend part of it upon the gains of his neighbors, and part to fulfill in the blessed retreats of contemplation. [3] <ref to="Luke 9:28; Mark 14:33">He therefore took with him</ref> a very few companions, to whom his holy way of life was better known than to the rest, that they might guard him from the intrusion and disturbance of men (cf. Ps 90:6; 30:21), and might in all things cherish and preserve his repose. [4] And when he had remained there for some while, and through unceasing prayer and frequent contemplation had attained to a divine intimacy in a manner beyond words, he longed to know <ref to="Ps 28:10">what might be most acceptable to the eternal King</ref> concerning him and within him, or what could be so. [5] Most earnestly he searched, and most piously he panted to know, in what manner, by what way, and with what desire he might more perfectly cleave to the Lord God, <ref to="Sir 40:25">according to the counsel and good pleasure</ref> of His will. [6] This was ever his highest philosophy, and this the highest desire that, as long as he lived, <ref to="Num 11:4">forever burned within him</ref>: to seek from the simple, from the wise, from the perfect and the imperfect, <ref to="Ps 118:30">how he might lay hold of the way of truth</ref> and arrive at the greater purpose.
</p>

<p id="92">
[1] For though he was the most perfect of the perfect, he refused to regard himself as perfect, and counted himself utterly imperfect. [2] For he had tasted and seen how sweet, <ref to="Ps 33:9">gentle </ref><ref to="Ps 72:1"> and good the God of Israel is to those who are upright of heart </ref>, <ref to="Wis 1:1">and who in pure simplicity and true purity seek Him </ref>. [3] For that infused sweetness and gentleness, granted rarely to the rarest of souls, which he felt breathing upon him from above, compelled him to fail wholly within himself; and, filled with so great a delight, he longed by every means to pass over entirely to that place where, by transcending himself, he had already gone ahead in part. [4] The man was ready, <ref to="1 Cor 7:40">having the Spirit of God </ref>, to endure every anguish of mind and to bear every suffering of the body, <ref to="Josh 24:15">were the choice at last given to him </ref>, so that the will of the heavenly Father might be mercifully fulfilled (cf. Mt 6:14; 12:50) in him. [5] And so one day he approached the sacred altar that had been built in the hermitage in which he was then dwelling, and, taking up the book in which the holy Gospels were written, he reverently laid it upon the altar. [6] <ref to="Luke 6:12">And thus, prostrate in prayer to God </ref> no less in heart than in body, with humble entreaty he begged <ref to="Joel 2:13">that the gracious God </ref>, <ref to="2 Cor 1:3">the Father of mercies and God of all consolation </ref>, would deign to reveal His will to him: [7] and that he might be enabled to bring to perfect completion what he had once begun in simplicity and devotion, he humbly prayed that what it would be more fitting for him to do might be shown to him at the first opening of the book. [8] For he was led by the spirit of those holy and most perfect men who, as we read, did something similar in their pious devotion and in their longing for sanctity.
</p>

<p id="93">
[1] Rising from prayer, <ref to="Luke 22:45; Dan 3:39">in a spirit of humility and with a contrite heart</ref>, and fortifying himself with the seal of the holy cross, he took the book from the altar and opened it with reverence and awe. [2] And it came to pass, <ref to="Rev 5:7">when he had opened the book</ref>, that the first thing to meet his eyes was the passion of our Lord Jesus Christ, and that alone which foretold that he would suffer tribulation. [3] But, lest he might in any way suspect that this had happened by chance, he opened the book a second and a third time, and found the same passage written, or one like it. [4] <ref to="Gen 41:38">Then the man, full of the spirit of God, understood</ref> that through many tribulations, <ref to="Acts 14:21">through many straits and through many struggles, he must enter into the kingdom of God</ref>. [5] <ref to="Eccl 8:8">But the most valiant soldier is not dismayed at the wars rushing upon him</ref>, <ref to="1 Kgs 25:28">nor does his courage fail him as he prepares to fight the battles of the Lord</ref> in the camps of this world. [6] He did not fear to yield to the enemy, he who did not give way even to himself, though he had long labored beyond the measure of human strength. [7] Truly he was most fervent in spirit, and though in ages past he had a companion in his purpose, yet no one was found to surpass him in desire. [8] For he knew also that it is easier to perform good works to perfection than to speak of them: always devoting his effective zeal and effort not to words, which do not make goodness but only display it, but to holy deeds. [9] Therefore he remained unshaken and joyful, <ref to="Eph 5:18">and in his heart he sang songs of gladness both to himself and to God</ref>. [10] For this reason he was held worthy of a greater revelation, who thus rejoiced over the least thing, and being <ref to="Luke 19:17">faithful in a little</ref> <ref to="Matt 25:21">is set over many things</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter III - Of the Vision of a Man Bearing the Likeness of the Crucified Seraph. <a id="op2-3"></a>

<p id="94">
[1] While he was lodging in the hermitage that takes its name from the place where it stands, Alverna, two years before he gave back his soul to heaven, he saw in a vision of God (cf. Ez 1:1; 8:1) a single man, like a Seraph having six wings, standing above him, with hands outstretched and feet joined together, fastened to a cross. [2] Two wings were raised above his head, two were extended for flight, <ref to="Isa 6:2">and two, finally, veiled his whole body</ref>. [3] When the blessed servant of the Most High beheld these things, he was filled with the greatest wonder, yet he did not know what this vision might mean to convey to him. [4] He rejoiced exceedingly too, and was glad with great vehemence at the kindly and gracious regard with which he saw himself looked upon by the Seraph, whose beauty was beyond all reckoning; yet the affixing to the cross and the bitterness of that suffering altogether dismayed him. [5] And so he arose, if one may put it thus, both sorrowful and glad, and joy and grief took their turns within him by turns. [6] Anxiously he pondered what this vision might signify, <ref to="Ps 142:4">and his spirit was in great anguish to grasp from it the meaning of its understanding</ref>. [7] And when he could perceive nothing of clear meaning from it, and the strangeness of this vision dwelt heavily upon his heart, there began to appear in his hands and feet the marks of the nails, just as, a little while before, he had seen the man crucified above him.
</p>

<p id="95">
[1] His hands and feet appeared to be pierced through their very midst with nails, the heads of the nails showing on the inner side of the hands and the upper side of the feet, while their points stood out on the opposite side. [2] For those marks were round on the inner side of the hands, but elongated on the outer side, and a small piece of flesh appeared, as though the point of the nails had been bent back and beaten down, which protruded beyond the rest of the flesh. [3] So too in the feet the marks of nails were impressed and raised above the surrounding flesh. [4] His right side also was as though pierced by a lance, drawn over with a scar, and it often shed blood, so that his tunic together with his undergarments was many times sprinkled with the sacred blood. [5] Alas, how few, while the crucified servant of the crucified Lord yet lived, were granted to behold the sacred wound of his side! [6] But blessed was Elias, who, while the saint lived, was in some measure granted to see it; <ref to="1 John 1:1">yet no less blessed was Rufinus, who touched it with his own hands</ref>. [7] For indeed, when on one occasion the said brother Rufinus had thrust his hand into the bosom of the most holy man to scratch him, his hand slipped, as often happens, toward the man's right side, and it chanced that he touched that precious scar. [8] At this touch the saint of God suffered no small pain, and pushing the hand away from himself, he cried out, <ref to="Gen 19:16">that the Lord might spare him</ref>. [9] For he most diligently hid these things from strangers and most carefully concealed them from those close to him, so that even the brothers at his side and his most devoted followers were for a long time unaware of them. [10] <ref to="2 Chr 9:9">And though the servant and friend of the Most High saw himself adorned with such great pearls, as with most precious gems</ref>, <ref to="Sir 3:19; Ps 8:6">and wondrously embellished above the glory and honor of all men</ref>, yet he did not grow vain in his heart, <ref to="Gal 1:10">nor did he thereby seek to please anyone out of an appetite for vainglory</ref>; [11] but, lest human favor should steal away the grace bestowed upon him, he strove by every means he could <ref to="Matt 6:18">to hide these things</ref>.
</p>

<p id="96">
[1] It was indeed his custom rarely or never to reveal a special secret to anyone, fearing lest, under the guise of singular affection—as those most beloved are wont to do upon disclosing such things—he might <ref to="Rom 12:3; 2 Cor 7:9">suffer some loss in the grace bestowed upon him</ref>. [2] He therefore carried always in his heart, and had often upon his lips, that prophetic saying: "In my heart I have hidden your words, <ref to="Ps 118:11">that I might not sin against you."</ref> [3] Whenever any worldly people came together to him, wishing to refrain from conversing with them, he had given to the brothers and sons who dwelt with him such a sign: namely, that when he recited the aforesaid verse, they should at once courteously grant leave to depart to those who had gathered. [4] For he had learned by experience that it is a great evil to share all things with all, and he knew that no man could be spiritual <ref to="2 Cor 10:7">whose secrets, the more perfect and the more numerous, are no greater than what appears upon his face</ref> and which by their outward show can be judged by men in every particular. [5] For he had found certain men who outwardly agreed with him yet inwardly were at variance, applauding him to his face and mocking him behind his back, <ref to="1 Cor 11:29">men who have purchased judgment for themselves</ref> and who rendered upright men somewhat suspect in his eyes. [6] For malice often strives to blacken purity, and because falsehood is familiar to many, <ref to="2 Thess 2:11">the truth of the few is not believed</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter IV - On the fervor of blessed Francis and the infirmity of his eyes. <a id="op2-4"></a>

<p id="97">
[1] In the course of that same period his body began to be beset by various weaknesses, and these more severe than had been usual before. [2] For he suffered frequent illnesses, as one who had perfectly chastised his body, <ref to="1 Cor 9:27; Rom 15:23">and had brought it into subjection through many years already gone before</ref>. [3] For over the span of eighteen years, now completed, <ref to="2 Cor 7:5">his flesh had had scarcely or never any rest</ref>, <ref to="Gen 41:46">as he traversed regions various and far-flung</ref>, <ref to="Luke 8:11; Matt 26:41">that he might scatter everywhere the seeds of the word of God—that ready spirit</ref>, that devout spirit, <ref to="Acts 18:25; Rom 8:11">that fervent spirit which dwelt within him</ref>. [4] <ref to="Wis 1:7">He filled the whole earth</ref> with the Gospel of Christ, <ref to="Mark 6:6">so that in a single day he would often make his round of four or five towns, or even cities</ref>, <ref to="Luke 8:1">proclaiming to each the kingdom of God</ref>, and edifying his hearers no less by example than by word; he had made of his whole body a tongue. [5] So great in him was the harmony of flesh with spirit, so great its obedience, that while the spirit strove to lay hold of all holiness, the flesh nonetheless not only did not resist but was eager even to run ahead, according to what is written: My soul has thirsted for you, <ref to="Ps 62:2">how manifoldly does my flesh thirst for you</ref>. [6] Indeed the constancy of submission had made it willing, and through its daily bowing it had acquired the disposition of such great virtue, since habit is often turned into nature.
</p>

<p id="98">
[1] But since, according to the laws of nature and the measure of the human condition, it must needs be that day by day the outward man is wasted away, <ref to="2 Cor 4:16">though the man who is within be renewed</ref>, that most precious little vessel, <ref to="Matt 13:44">in which the heavenly treasure lay hidden</ref>, began to be shaken on every side and to suffer a failing of all its strength. [2] Yet because, when a man has finished, then does he but begin, <ref to="Sir 18:6">and when he leaves off, then shall he labor</ref>, <ref to="Matt 26:4">in the weakened flesh the spirit was made readier</ref>. [3] <ref to="1 Pet 1:9">So greatly, too, did he love the salvation of souls</ref> and thirst after the gaining of his neighbors, that, when he could no longer walk by himself, <ref to="Luke 9:6">he traveled through the lands borne upon a little ass</ref>. [4] Often the brothers admonished him, pressing upon him with every urgency of prayer, that he ought somehow to restore his sick and grievously weakened body by the aid of physicians. [5] But he, with that noble spirit of his fixed upon heaven, <ref to="Phil 1:23">desiring only to be loosed and to be with Christ</ref>, utterly refused to do so. [6] Yet because he had not yet filled up <ref to="Col 1:24">those things which were wanting of the sufferings of Christ in his flesh</ref>, <ref to="Gal 6:17">though he bore His marks upon his body</ref>, he fell into a most grievous infirmity of the eyes, even as <ref to="Ps 35:8">God multiplied His mercy upon him</ref>. [7] And when day by day that infirmity grew worse and seemed daily to increase through neglect, Brother Elias at last—whom he had chosen for himself in the place of a mother and made the father of the other brothers—compelled him not to shrink from medicine, [8] <ref to="Ps 35:8">but to receive it in the name of the Son of God</ref>, through whom it had been created, as it is written: The Most High created medicine out of the earth, <ref to="Sir 38:4">and a prudent man will not abhor it</ref>. [9] Then indeed the holy father graciously consented and humbly obeyed the words of the one who admonished him.
</p>

## Chapter V - How he was received in the city of Rieti by the lord Hugo, Bishop of Ostia, and how the saint declared that he would become bishop of the whole world. <a id="op2-5"></a>

<p id="99">
[1] Now it came to pass that, although many came forward to aid him with their remedies, no cure was found, and so he went to the city of Rieti, where a man most skilled in the treatment of that infirmity was said to be staying. [2] When therefore he arrived there, he was received kindly enough and with honor by the whole Roman curia, which at that time was residing in the same city; yet most devoutly was he received by the lord Hugo, bishop of Ostia, who shone forth supremely in the integrity of his manners and the holiness of his life. [3] This man indeed blessed Francis had chosen as father and lord over the whole religion and order of his brothers, with the assent and will of the lord Pope Honorius, because that blessed poverty pleased him greatly, and holy simplicity stood with him in the highest reverence. [4] That lord conformed himself to the manners of the brothers, and in his longing for holiness he was simple with the simple, humble with the humble, poor with the poor. [5] He was a brother among brothers, least among the lesser, and, as far as was permitted, he strove to bear himself in life and conduct as one of the rest. [6] He was zealous everywhere to plant the sacred religion, and in distant regions the bright fame of his still brighter life greatly enlarged the order. [7] <ref to="Isa 50:4">The Lord gave him a learned tongue</ref>, with which he confounded the adversaries of truth, <ref to="Phil 3:18">with which he refuted the enemies of the cross of Christ</ref>, <ref to="Deut 22:1">with which he led the straying back</ref> to the way, <ref to="Hos 11:4">with which he made peace among the discordant and bound together the harmonious with a stronger bond of charity</ref>. [8] He was in the Church of God a lamp burning and shining, <ref to="John 5:35; Isa 49:2">and a chosen arrow</ref>, <ref to="Luke 14:22">made ready in the opportune time</ref>. [9] O how often, having laid aside his precious garments, clad in mean ones, with bare feet, walking as one of the brothers, <ref to="Luke 14:22">did he ask for the things that pertain to peace</ref>! [10] <ref to="Jer 7">This between a man and his neighbor</ref>, as often as was needful, this between God and man, he ever solicitously brought about. [11] <ref to="Sir 45:4">Therefore, a little while after, God chose him</ref><ref to="Acts 5:11">as shepherd over his whole holy Church</ref>, <ref to="Ps 109:7; Rev 13:7">and exalted his head among the tribes of the peoples</ref>.
</p>

<p id="100">
[1] <ref to="2 Tim 3:16">That this might be known to be divinely inspired</ref> and accomplished by the will of Christ Jesus, the blessed father Francis foretold it long beforehand in words and prefigured it in deed. [2] For when the order and religion of the brothers, through the working of divine grace, was already beginning to spread far and wide, and, like a cedar in the paradise of God, <ref to="Ezek 31:8; Ps 109:3">in the heavenly places</ref> was lifting up the crown of the saints' merits, <ref to="Jer 2:21">and as a chosen vine</ref> was sending forth its sacred shoots across the breadth of the whole earth (cf. Ps 79:12; 17:20), holy Francis came to the lord Pope Honorius, who then presided over the Roman Church, beseeching him with humble entreaty that he might appoint the lord Hugo, bishop of Ostia, as father and lord over himself and his brothers. [3] The lord pope assented to the saint's prayers, and kindly complying, conferred upon Hugo his authority over the order of the brothers. [4] Receiving this with reverence and devotion, set as a faithful and prudent servant over the household of the Lord, <ref to="Matt 24:45">he strove in every way to dispense the food</ref><ref to="Ps 144:15">of eternal life to those entrusted to him in due season</ref>. [5] For this reason the holy father submitted himself to him in all things and venerated him with a wonderful and reverent affection. [6] He was led by the Spirit of God, <ref to="Matt 4:1; Acts 13:9">with which he was filled</ref>, <ref to="Isa 52:10">and therefore he beheld long beforehand what afterward would be thus before the eyes of all</ref>. [7] For whenever, <ref to="2 Cor 5:14">pressed by some matter touching his religious family, or rather compelled by the love of Christ</ref> with which he burned toward him, he wished to write to him, he by no means consented that in his letters Hugo should be called bishop of Ostia or Velletri, as others did in their customary greetings, but, having taken up his theme, he would address him thus: "To the most reverend father and lord Hugo, bishop of the whole world." [8] Often indeed he greeted him with unheard-of blessings, and although he was a son in devoted submission, yet at the Spirit's prompting he sometimes consoled him with fatherly discourse, [9] so that he might strengthen upon him the blessings of the fathers, <ref to="Gen 49:26">until the desire of the everlasting hills should come</ref>.
</p>

<p id="101">
[1] The said lord also burned with an exceeding love toward the holy man, and so whatever the blessed man said, whatever he did, pleased him, and often at the mere sight of him he was wholly moved with affection. [2] He himself bore witness concerning him that he never fell into such great agitation or disturbance of mind that, upon seeing and conversing with Saint Francis, every cloud of his soul would not depart in serenity, his sloth be put to flight, and joy breathe down upon him from above. [3] <ref to="Luke 12:37">He ministered to blessed Francis as a servant to his lord,</ref> and as often as he saw him he showed him reverence <ref to="1 Cor 1:1">as to an apostle of Christ,</ref> and bowing down in both the inner and outer man, he often kissed his hands with consecrated lips. [4] Solicitous and devout, he sought how the blessed father might recover the soundness of his eyes, <ref to="Acts 3:14">knowing him to be a holy and just man,</ref> <ref to="2 Esd 2:2">and exceedingly necessary and useful to the Church of God.</ref> [5] <ref to="Ps 102:13">He had compassion upon him for the whole congregation of the brothers, and pitied the sons in the father.</ref> [6] He therefore admonished the holy father to take care of himself and not to cast aside what was needful for his infirmity, lest such neglect be reckoned to him as a sin rather than as merit. [7] Saint Francis indeed humbly heeded what was said to him by so reverend a lord and so beloved a father, thereafter attending more cautiously and more confidently to the needs of his care. [8] But the malady had by now grown to such a degree that, for any remedy whatsoever, it required the keenest skills and demanded the harshest of medicines. [9] And so it came about that, although in several places his head was cauterized, his veins lanced, plasters laid upon him and salves applied, <ref to="Ps 88:23">he gained nothing,</ref> <ref to="Mark 5:26">but rather grew, as it were, ever worse.</ref>
</p>

## Chapter VI - On the character of the brothers who served Saint Francis, and how he himself purposed to conduct his life. <a id="op2-6"></a>

<p id="102">
[1] These things he endured for nearly two years with all patience and humility, <ref to="Tob 2:14; 1 Thess 5:18">giving thanks to God in all things </ref>.
[2] But that he might more freely direct his own intention toward God, and might encircle and enter the dwellings of the blessed mansions set in heaven—<ref to="2 Cor 5">frequently passing beyond himself in mind </ref>—and might present himself amid the things of heaven in the richness of grace before the most gentle and most serene Lord of all things, <ref to="2 Macc 14:35; Eph 1:3"> </ref> he entrusted the care of himself to certain brothers, deservedly very dear to him.
[3] <ref to="1 Macc 5:50"> For these were men of virtues </ref>, devoted to God, pleasing to the saints, gracious to men, upon whom, as a house upon four columns, <ref to="Jude 16:29">the blessed father Francis leaned </ref>.
[4] Their names I now suppress, sparing their modesty, which, as befits spiritual men, is to them a sufficiently familiar friend.
[5] For modesty is the ornament of every age, the witness of innocence, the token of a chaste mind, <ref to="Prov 22:15">the rod of discipline </ref>, the special glory of conscience, the guardian of reputation, and the emblem of all uprightness.
[6] This virtue had adorned them; this rendered them lovable and well-disposed to men; <ref to="Acts 2:44">this grace, indeed, was common to all </ref>, yet each was distinguished by his own particular virtue.
[7] One was of outstanding discretion, another of singular patience, a third of glorious simplicity, while the last was robust in bodily strength and gentle in the temper of his soul.
[8] These men, with all vigilance, all zeal, all goodwill, cultivated the peace of the blessed father's soul and tended to the infirmity of his body, shrinking from no hardships, no labors, but giving themselves wholly over to the service of the saint.
</p>

<p id="103">
[1] But although the glorious father was already perfected in grace before God, <ref to="John 11:9">and shone with holy works among the men of this world</ref>, <ref to="Gen 32:2">he nevertheless thought always to undertake more perfect things, and like a most learned soldier in the camp of God</ref>, having challenged the adversary, to stir up new wars once again. [2] <ref to="Dan 9:25">He proposed, with Christ as his leader</ref>, that he would accomplish vast things, and though his limbs were failing and his body already as good as dead, by a fresh struggle he hoped for triumph over the enemy. [3] For true virtue knows no limit of time, since the expectation of its reward is eternal. [4] He was therefore aflame with a very great longing <ref to="Num 11:4; Matt 2:10">indeed</ref> <ref to="Rom 12:12">to return to the beginnings of humility, and, rejoicing in hope through the immensity of his love</ref>, he resolved to recall his body, though it had now sunk to such extremity, to its former servitude. [5] He cut away from himself entirely the obstacles of all cares, and most fully stilled the clamor of every anxiety. [6] And when by reason of his infirmity he was compelled of necessity to temper his former rigor, he would say: "Let us begin, brothers, to serve the Lord God, for up to now we have made little or no progress at all." [7] <ref to="Phil 3:13">He did not reckon that he had yet attained</ref>, and enduring tirelessly in his purpose of holy newness, he hoped ever to begin anew. [8] He wished to return once more to serving the lepers, and to be held in contempt, as he had once been held. [9] He resolved to flee the company of men and to betake himself to the most remote places, so that, thus stripped of every care and laying aside concern for others, only the wall of the flesh might for the time separate him from God.
</p>

<p id="104">
[1] For he saw many rushing to assume the offices of mastership, and, detesting their rashness, he strove by his own example to recall them from a plague of this kind. [2] He said that to bear the care of others was good and acceptable before God, and he affirmed that it became those to undertake the charge of souls <ref to="Phil 2:21">who would seek nothing of their own therein</ref>, but who would ever in all things attend to the divine will. [3] Such men, namely, would prefer nothing to their own salvation, and would attend not to the applause of their subjects but to their progress, <ref to="Rom 4:2">not to display before men but to glory before God</ref>; [4] men who would not covet preferment but dread it; whom honors received would not exalt but humble, and whose loss would not cast them down but lift them up. [5] But especially in this present time, <ref to="2 Thess 1:3">in which malice has so grown beyond measure and iniquity abounded</ref>, he said it was perilous to rule, while he affirmed that it was more profitable to be ruled. [6] <ref to="Rev 2:5">He grieved that some had abandoned their first works</ref>, and that, with new contrivances, they had forgotten their former simplicity. [7] For this reason he lamented that those who once strove with all their longing for higher things had descended to base and worthless pursuits, and through frivolous and empty matters in the field of an idle liberty, forsaking the true joys, ran about and wandered. [8] He therefore prayed to the divine mercy for the deliverance of his sons, <ref to="Rom 12:3">and most devoutly besought that they might be preserved in the grace bestowed upon them</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter VII - How he came from Siena to Assisi, of the church of Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, and of the blessing of the brothers. <a id="op2-7"></a>

<p id="105">
[1] Now in the sixth month before the day of his death, while he was staying at Siena to have his eye ailment treated, he began to fall gravely ill throughout the whole of the rest of his body; and as his stomach was broken by long sickness and his liver diseased, he vomited a great quantity of blood, so that he seemed to draw near to death. [2] When this was learned, brother Elias ran to him with all speed from afar. [3] Upon his arrival the holy father grew so much stronger that, leaving that region, he came with him to the Cell at Cortona. [4] <ref to="Matt 25:5">And while he was there and tarried for some time</ref>, his belly swelled, his legs grew distended, his feet became swollen, and his stomach fell more and more into failure, so that he could scarcely receive any food at all. I. — Concerning the cripples who were healed.
[2] For on that very day when the sacred and holy body of the most blessed father Francis was laid to rest like a most precious treasure, anointed more with heavenly fragrances than with earthly spices, a certain girl was brought who for a year already had her neck monstrously twisted and her head fastened to her shoulder, so that she could look upward only from a sidelong angle.
[3] When she had bowed her head for a while beneath the tomb in which the precious body of the saint lay enclosed, at once, by the merits of the most holy man, her neck straightened and her head was restored to its proper position, so that the girl, utterly astonished at her sudden transformation, began to flee and to weep.
[4] Indeed, there appeared a kind of hollow in the shoulder to which her head had been pressed, owing to the posture that her long-enduring sickness had wrought.
