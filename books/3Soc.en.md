# LEGEND OF THE THREE COMPANIONS

## A Letter <a id="epistola"></a>

<p id="1">
[1] To the reverend father in Christ, Brother Crescentius, by the grace of God minister general, Brother Leo, Brother Rufinus, and Brother Angelus—once the companions, though unworthy, of the most blessed father Francis—offer the reverence due and devout in the Lord. [2] Since by the command both of the most recent general chapter and your own the brothers are bound to direct to your paternity such signs and wonders of the most blessed father Francis as they are able to know or to discover, [3] it has seemed good to us, who though unworthy lived in his company for a long while, to make known to your holiness—truth going before us—<ref to="Luke 1:2">a few out of his many deeds which we ourselves have seen </ref> or have been able to learn through other holy brothers, [4] and in particular through Brother Philip, visitor of the poor ladies, Brother Illuminatus of Arce, Brother Masseo of Marignano, and Brother John, the companion of the venerable father Brother Giles—[5] who received much of this from that same holy Brother Giles and from Brother Bernard of holy memory, the first companion of blessed Francis. [6] We are not content merely to recount miracles, which do not make holiness but display it, but desire also to show the marks of his holy manner of life [7] and the will of his devout good pleasure, <ref to="Phil 1:11">unto the praise and glory of the Most High God </ref> and of the said most holy father, and for the edification of those who wish to follow in his footsteps. [8] These things, however, we do not write in the manner of a legend, since legends were long ago composed concerning his life and the miracles the Lord wrought through him; [9] rather, as from a pleasant meadow, we pluck out at our own discretion certain of the fairer flowers, not following a continuous narrative, [10] but deliberately leaving aside much that has been set down in the aforesaid legends in language as truthful as it is luminous. [11] To these legends you will be able to have these few things which we write inserted, if your discretion judges it fitting. [12] For we believe that, had these matters been known to those venerable men who composed the aforesaid legends, they would by no means have passed them over, but would at least in part have adorned them with their own eloquence and left them to the memory of posterity. [13] May your holy paternity ever flourish whole and entire in the Lord Jesus Christ, to whom we, your devoted sons, humbly and devoutly commend ourselves to your holiness. [14] Given in the place of Greccio, on the third day before the ides of August, in the year of the Lord one thousand two hundred and forty-six.
</p>

## Chapter I — Of his birth, and of his vanity, frivolity, and prodigality, and how from these he came to generosity and charity toward the poor. <a id="cap1"></a>

<p id="2">
[1] Francis, a native of the city of Assisi, which lies within the borders of the valley of Spoleto, was first called John by his mother; but afterward, when his father returned from France—during whose absence he had been born—he was named Francis. [2] Once he had grown to manhood and become of subtle wit, he took up his father's trade, that is, the business of merchandising, [3] yet in a manner very different, for he was more cheerful and more openhanded than his father. Given over to jesting and to songs, he wandered through the city of Assisi by day and by night in the company of others like himself, <ref to="Tob 1:3">so very lavish in his spending that all he could possess</ref> and earn he would squander on feasting and on other things. [4] For this reason he was often rebuked by his parents, who told him that he laid out such great sums upon himself and upon others that he seemed not their son but the son of some great prince. [6] Yet because his parents were wealthy and loved him most tenderly, they bore with him in such conduct, unwilling to vex him. [6] His mother, moreover, when his prodigality was spoken of by the neighbors, would answer: "What do you suppose of my son? He shall yet be a son of God through grace." [7] He himself was not only openhanded in these matters—nay, prodigal—but also exceeded greatly in his garments, having finer cloth made than befitted him to have. [8] In his fastidiousness, too, he was so vain that sometimes upon a single garment he would have a very costly cloth sewn together with one most cheap.
</p>

<p id="3">
[1] Yet he was, as it were by nature, courtly in manner and in speech, for in keeping with the resolve of his heart he uttered to no one an insulting or base word; indeed, though he was a young man given to merriment and frivolity, he resolved to make no reply at all to those who spoke shamefully to him. [2] <ref to="Luke 4:37"> Hence his fame was so widely spread on this account throughout nearly the whole province </ref> that by many who knew him he was said to be destined for something great. [3] From these degrees of natural virtues he was advanced to such grace that he said, turning to himself: "Since you are generous and courtly toward men, from whom you receive nothing but fleeting and empty favor, it is only just that, for the sake of God, who is most generous in rewarding, you should be courtly and generous toward the poor." [4] From that time, therefore, he gladly looked upon the poor, bestowing alms upon them abundantly. [5] And although he was a merchant, he was a most prodigal dispenser of worldly wealth. [6] Now one day, as he stood busy with such matters in the shop where he sold cloth, a certain poor man came to him asking alms for the love of God. [7] And when, held back by greed for riches and the cares of trade, he had refused the man alms, he was struck by divine grace and reproached himself for great churlishness, [8] saying: "If that poor man had asked something of you on behalf of a great count or baron, you would surely have given him what he requested. [9] <ref to="Rev 17:14"> How much more, then, on behalf of the King of kings and Lord of all </ref> ought you to have done it!" [10] For this reason he thenceforth resolved in his heart that he would never again refuse anything asked for the sake of so great a Lord.
</p>

## Chapter II — How he was taken captive at Perugia, and concerning the two visions he had when he wished to become a knight. <a id="cap2"></a>

<p id="4">
[4] To this Francis answered with a clear voice: "What do you think of me? One day I shall be venerated throughout the whole world."
</p>

<p id="5">
[5] Wondering joyfully within himself, in silence, what all this might mean, he asked whose were these arms gleaming with such splendor and this palace so delightful.
</p>

<p id="6">
[1] Yet on the very day immediately preceding the aforesaid vision there had occurred in him a certain sign of great courtesy and nobility, which is believed to have been no small occasion for that vision itself. [2] For on that day he had given away to a certain poor knight all the fine and costly garments that he had recently had made for himself. [3] And so, having set out upon his journey and traveled as far as Spoleto, intending to press on into Apulia, he began to fall somewhat ill. [4] Nevertheless, still anxious about his journey, when he had given himself over to sleep, he heard, half-asleep, someone asking him where he wished to go. [5] When Francis had revealed to him his whole intention, the other added: "Who can do better for you? The lord or the servant?" [6] When he had answered, "The lord," the other said to him again: "Why, then, do you abandon the lord for the servant, and the prince for the vassal?" [7] And Francis said: "What do you want me to do, <ref to="Acts 9:6">Lord </ref>?" [8] "Return," he said, <ref to="Gen 32:9">to your own land </ref><ref to="Acts 9:7"> and there it will be told you what you are to do </ref>. For the vision you have seen you must understand otherwise." [9] Awakening, he began to ponder this vision most carefully, [10] and just as in the first vision he had been, as it were, wholly carried out of himself by his great gladness, longing for temporal prosperity, [11] so in this one he gathered his whole self inward, marveling at its force and weighing it so attentively that he could not sleep further that night. [12] And so, when morning came, he turned back toward Assisi in haste, glad and rejoicing exceedingly, awaiting the will of the Lord who had shown him these things, and that counsel concerning his own salvation might be given him by Him. [13] Now changed in mind, he refused to go into Apulia and longed to conform himself to the divine will.
</p>

## Chapter III - How the Lord first visited his heart with a wondrous sweetness, by whose power he began to advance through contempt of himself and of all vain things, and through prayer and almsgiving and love of poverty. <a id="cap3"></a>

<p id="7">
[7] To them he replied with a clear voice: "You have spoken truly, for I have resolved to take a bride nobler and richer and more beautiful than any you have ever beheld." [8] <ref to="Matt 9:24; Luke 16:14; Luke 23:35">And they laughed at him</ref>. Yet he had said this not of himself but as one inspired by God: for that bride was the true religious life which he embraced, in its poverty nobler, richer, and more beautiful than all the rest.
</p>

<p id="8">
[1] From that hour, therefore, he began to grow weary of himself and to hold in contempt those things he had once loved—yet not entirely as yet, for he was not wholly loosed from the vanity of the world. [2] Withdrawing himself for a little while from worldly tumult, <ref to="Matt 13:46">he strove to hide Jesus Christ within the inner man, and longed to win that pearl which he desired to purchase by selling all that he had</ref>; concealing himself from the eyes of those who would mock him, he went often—nearly every day—in secret to prayer. [3] He was driven to this by that foretaste of sweetness which, visiting him ever more frequently, urged him to prayer away from the marketplace and other public places. [4] And although for some time already he had been a benefactor of the poor, <ref to="Ps 13:1; Acts 5:4">from that time he resolved more firmly in his heart</ref> never again to deny alms to any poor person who begged them for the love of God, but to give alms more freely and more abundantly than was his custom. [5] Thus, whenever any poor man asked alms of him outside his house, he provided for him from his coins, if he was able. [6] But if he had no coins, he gave him a band or a belt, <ref to="Sir 29:12">that he might not send the poor man away empty</ref>. [7] And if he had none of these, he would go to some hidden place, strip off his shirt, and secretly send the poor man there to take it for himself, for the love of God. [8] He would also buy vessels belonging to the adornment of churches, and send them, in greater secrecy, to poor priests.
</p>

<p id="9">
[1] Whenever his father was away and he remained at home—even if he dined alone with his mother—he would heap the table with loaves of bread, as though he were preparing for the whole household. [2] And so, when his mother asked him why he set out so many loaves upon the table, <ref to="Luke 12:33">he answered that he did this so that alms might be given </ref> to the poor, <ref to="Luke 6:30">for he had resolved to bestow alms upon everyone who asked </ref> for the sake of God. [3] His mother, because she loved him above her other sons, indulged him in such matters, watching what he did and marveling greatly over these things in her heart. [4] For just as he had once been wont to set his heart on going off after his companions whenever they summoned him, and was so captivated by their company that he would often rise from the table even if he had eaten but little, leaving his parents distressed by so unruly a departure, [5] so now his whole heart was bent upon seeing or hearing of the poor, that he might lavish alms upon them.
</p>

<p id="10">
[1] Transformed in this way by divine grace, though he was still in worldly dress, he longed to be in some city where, as an unknown stranger, he might strip off his own garments and clothe himself in the borrowed rags of some poor man, and so put to the test, for the love of God, the begging of alms. [2] And it came about that at that very time he journeyed to Rome on pilgrimage. [3] Entering the church of Saint Peter, he observed that the offerings of certain people were paltry, and said within himself: "Since the prince of the apostles ought to be honored magnificently, why do these people make such small offerings in the church where his body lies at rest?" [4] And so with great fervor he thrust his hand into his purse and drew it out full of coins, and casting them through the grating of the altar, he made so loud a clatter that all who stood by marveled greatly at so magnificent an offering. [5] <ref to="Acts 3:2"> Then, going out before the doors of the church where many poor people were present begging for alms </ref>, he secretly borrowed the threadbare clothes of some poor little man and, laying aside his own, put them on. [6] Standing on the steps of the church with the other poor, he begged for alms in French, for he gladly spoke the French tongue, though he could not speak it correctly. [7] Afterward, however, taking off those rags and resuming his own clothing, <ref to="Gen 24:40">he returned to Assisi and began to pray to the Lord to direct his way </ref>. [8] For he disclosed his secret to no one, nor did he in this matter seek anyone's counsel save God alone, who had begun to direct his way, and at times that of the bishop of Assisi—because in those days there was found among no one the true poverty he desired above all things of this world, wishing to live and die in it.
</p>

## Chapter IV - How, among the lepers, he began to conquer himself and to find sweetness in those things that had formerly been bitter to him. <a id="cap4"></a>

<p id="11">
[1] One day, as he was praying fervently to the Lord, this answer was given to him: "Francis, all that you have loved after the flesh and longed to possess you must despise and hold in loathing, if you would know my will.
[2] And once you have begun to do this, the things that before seemed to you sweet and pleasant shall become to you unbearable and bitter, while in those things that once filled you with horror you shall draw a great sweetness and an immeasurable delight."
[3] Rejoicing therefore in these words and strengthened in the Lord, as he was riding near Assisi he met a certain leper coming toward him.
[4] And because he had been wont to feel great horror at lepers, doing violence to himself he dismounted from his horse, gave him a coin, and kissed his hand.
[5] Having received from him the kiss of peace, he remounted his horse and went on his way.
[6] From that time forward he began more and more to despise himself, until by the grace of God he came at last to perfect mastery over himself.
[7] A few days later, taking a large sum of money, he betook himself to the hospital of the lepers, and gathering them all together he gave each one an alms and kissed his hand.
[8] And when he departed, that which before had been bitter to him—namely the sight and touch of lepers—was truly turned into sweetness.
[9] For so bitter to him, as he himself said, had the sight of lepers once been, that he would not only refuse to look upon them but would not even come near their dwellings;
[10] and if it ever happened that he passed close by their houses or caught sight of them, though out of compassion he might be moved to give them an alms through some intermediary, yet he always turned his face away and stopped his nostrils with his own hands.
[11] But by the grace of God he became so much the intimate and friend of the lepers that, as he himself bears witness in his Testament, he dwelt among them and humbly served them.
</p>

<p id="12">
[1] Now, having been changed for the better after his visit to the lepers, he took with him to a remote place a certain companion whom he had loved greatly, telling him that he had discovered a great and precious treasure. [2] That man rejoiced not a little, and gladly went with him whenever he was summoned. [3] Francis often led him to a certain cave near Assisi, and entering it alone—leaving his companion outside, anxious about gaining the treasure—he, <ref to="Matt 6:6">filled with a new and singular spirit, prayed to the Father in secret</ref>, desiring that none should know what he was doing within, save God alone, whom he ceaselessly consulted about gaining the heavenly treasure. [4] Observing this, the enemy of the human race strove to draw him back from the good he had begun, striking him with fear and dread. [5] For there was at Assisi a certain woman, hideously hunchbacked, whom the demon, appearing to the man of God, brought back to his memory, and he threatened him that he would cast that woman's deformity upon him unless he drew back from the resolve he had conceived. [6] But <ref to="2 Tim 2:3">the soldier of Christ</ref>, most valiant, scorning the threats of the devil, <ref to="Gen 24:40">prayed devoutly within the cave that God would direct his way</ref>. [7] Yet he endured the greatest suffering and anguish of mind, unable to rest until he should fulfill in deed what he had conceived in his mind, while diverse thoughts succeeded one another in turn, whose persistence troubled him the more harshly. [8] For inwardly he burned with a divine fire, unable to conceal outwardly the ardor kindled in his mind; he repented of having sinned so grievously, and now neither past nor present evils delighted him—though he had not yet received confidence to keep himself from those to come. [9] And so, when he came out of the cave, <ref to="1 Kgs 10:6">he seemed to his companion changed into another man</ref>.
</p>

## Chapter V - On the first words the Crucified spoke to him, and how from that time forward he bore in his heart the Passion of Christ until his death. <a id="cap5"></a>

<p id="13">
[1] One day, however, as he was imploring the Lord's mercy more fervently than ever, the Lord revealed to him that he would soon be told what it behooved him to do. [2] <ref to="Ps 125:2">From that moment he was filled with such great joy</ref> that, unable to contain himself for gladness, he could not help but pour out something of these secrets into the ears of men. [3] Yet he spoke cautiously and in riddles, saying that he had no wish to go to Apulia, but that in his own homeland he would accomplish things noble and grand. [4] When his companions saw him so changed—men from whom he had already grown distant in mind, though in body he still kept their company on occasion—they questioned him again as if in jest: "Do you mean to take a wife, Francis?" [5] To which he answered with a certain riddle, as has been set forth above. [6] A few days later, as he was walking near the church of Saint Damian, he was told in spirit to go inside it to pray. [7] Having entered, he began to pray fervently before an image of the Crucified, which spoke to him with tenderness and kindness, saying: "Francis, do you not see that my house is falling to ruin? Go, therefore, and repair it for me." [8] Trembling and astonished, he replied: "Gladly will I do so, Lord." [9] For he understood this to be said of that very church, which, on account of its great age, was threatening imminent collapse. [10] By that address he was filled with such joy and illumined with such light that in his soul he truly perceived that it was Christ crucified who had spoken to him. [11] Leaving the church, he found a priest sitting beside it, and reaching his hand to his purse he offered him a sum of money, [12] saying: "I beg you, Lord, to buy oil and keep a lamp burning continually before that Crucified One. [13] And when this money has been spent on that work, I shall again provide you with as much as may be needed."
</p>

<p id="14">
[1] <ref to="Song 4:9"> From that hour, therefore, his heart was so wounded and melted </ref><ref to="Gal 6:17"> at the memory of the Lord's Passion that, for as long as he lived, he bore the stigmata of the Lord Jesus in his heart </ref>, as afterward shone forth plainly through the renewal of those very stigmata, wondrously wrought and most clearly displayed in his body. [2] From that time he so afflicted himself with the mortification of his flesh that, whether well or sick, being exceedingly austere toward his own body, he scarcely or never wished to grant himself any indulgence. [3] For this reason, as the day of his death drew near, he confessed that he had sinned greatly against brother body. [4] Now on one occasion he was walking alone near the church of Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, <ref to="Ezek 27:30; Mark 5:38">wailing and lamenting with a loud voice </ref>. [5] A certain spiritual man, hearing him, supposed that he was suffering from some illness or pain, and, moved with compassion for him, asked him why he wept. [6] But he replied: "I am lamenting the Passion of my Lord, for whose sake I ought not to be ashamed to go weeping aloud through all the world." [7] Then that man too began likewise to lament with a loud voice along with him. [8] Often, indeed, when he rose from prayer, his eyes seemed full of blood, because he had wept so bitterly. [9] Yet he afflicted himself not only with tears, but also with abstinence from food and drink, for the memory of the Lord's Passion.
</p>

<p id="15">
[1] Hence, whenever he chanced to sit down to dine in the company of worldly folk, and dishes pleasing to the body were set before him, he would taste but little of them, putting forward some excuse lest he should seem to have refused them out of abstinence. [2] And when he ate among the brothers, he would often sprinkle ashes upon the food he ate, telling the brethren—as a veil for his abstinence—that brother ash was chaste. [3] Now once, as he sat down to eat, a certain brother remarked to him that at the hour of her meal the blessed Virgin had been so very poor that she had nothing to give her Son to eat. [4] Hearing this, the man of God sighed with deep sorrow, and forsaking the table, ate his bread upon the bare ground. [6] Very often, indeed, when he sat down to eat, a little after the meal had begun he would pause, neither eating nor drinking, rapt in the contemplation of heavenly things. [6] For at such times he was unwilling to be hindered by any words, and breathing forth deep sighs from the very depths of his heart, he would even tell the brothers that whenever they heard him sighing in such a manner, they should praise God and faithfully pray for him. [7] These things concerning his weeping and his abstinence we have set down by the way, that we might show how, after the aforesaid vision and the words spoken to him by the image of the Crucified, he remained until death ever conformed to the Passion of Christ.
</p>

## Chapter VI — How he first fled the persecutions of his father and his kinsmen, abiding with the priest of Saint Damian, into whose window he had cast the money. <a id="cap6"></a>

<p id="16">
[1] Rejoicing, therefore, at the vision and the address of the Crucified One, he rose, fortifying himself with the sign of the cross, and mounting his horse and taking up cloths of various colors, he came to the city called Foligno, and there, having sold both the horse and all that he had carried, he returned at once to the church of Saint Damian. [2] And finding there a poor little priest, he kissed his hands with great faith and devotion, offered him the money he was carrying, and recounted his purpose in due order. [3] The priest, astonished and marveling at his sudden conversion, refused to believe these things, and thinking himself to be mocked, was unwilling to keep that money in his keeping. [4] But he, persisting stubbornly, strove to win credence for his words and besought the priest more earnestly to permit him to dwell with him. [5] At length the priest consented to his remaining, but out of fear of his parents he did not accept the money. [6] Therefore the true despiser of riches, casting it into a certain window, esteemed it as worthless as dust. [7] <ref to="Matt 25:5">While he tarried </ref> in the aforesaid place, <ref to="1 Pet 5:8">his father, like a diligent investigator, went about seeking </ref> what had become of his son. [8] And when he had heard that, thus transformed, he was living in the manner described in that place, <ref to="Gen 6:6">touched inwardly with grief of heart </ref> and troubled at the sudden turn of events, <ref to="Luke 15:6">he called together his friends and neighbors </ref> and ran to him with all speed. [9] But he, <ref to="2 Tim 2:3">since he was a new soldier of Christ </ref>, when he heard the threats of his persecutors and foreknew their coming, <ref to="Rom 12:19">gave place to his father's wrath </ref> and, withdrawing to a certain hidden cave that he had prepared for himself for this purpose, lay hidden there for a whole month. [10] This cave was known to only one person of his father's household, where he secretly ate the food now and then brought to him, praying continually, drenched in a shower of tears, that the Lord would deliver him from the harmful persecution and would fulfill his holy vows with gracious favor.
</p>

<p id="17">
[1] When he had thus, <ref to="Joel 2:12">in fasting and weeping</ref>, prayed to the Lord fervently and without ceasing, distrusting his own strength and resourcefulness, <ref to="Ps 54:23; Ps 36:5">he cast his hope wholly upon the Lord</ref>, who, though he yet remained in darkness, flooded him with a certain ineffable gladness and lit him with a wondrous brightness. [2] Set all aflame by it, he left the hollow behind and seized the road toward Assisi, tireless, swift, and joyful. [3] Armed with the weapons of confidence in Christ and kindled with divine warmth, reproaching himself for sloth and idle fear, he openly exposed himself to the hands and blows of those who pursued him. [4] Those who had known him before, seeing him, heaped pitiable reproaches upon him and, crying out that he was insane and out of his mind, <ref to="Ps 17:43; John 8:59">flung the mire of the streets and stones at him</ref>. [5] For perceiving him so changed from his former ways and worn out by the mortification of his flesh, they ascribed everything he did to ruin and madness. [6] But <ref to="2 Tim 2:3">the soldier of Christ</ref>, passing through all this as one deaf, broken or altered by no injury, gave thanks to God. [7] <ref to="Song 3:2">When such a rumor about him spread through the streets and quarters of the city</ref>, it reached at last his father, who, hearing what his fellow citizens were doing to him, at once rose to seek him out—not to free him, but rather to destroy him. [8] For, keeping no measure, he ran like a wolf to the sheep, and eyeing him with a grim look and bristling face he laid his hands upon him impiously. [9] Dragging him off to the house and shutting him up for many days in a dark prison, he strove by words and blows to bend his spirit back to the vanity of the world.
</p>

<p id="18">
[1] But he himself, neither swayed by words nor wearied by chains or beatings, bore all things patiently, and was rendered the readier and stronger to carry out his holy resolve. [2] For when his father had departed from the house on account of some pressing necessity, his mother, who had remained alone with him, disapproving of her husband's deed, addressed her son with coaxing words. [3] And when she could not call him back from his holy purpose, <ref to="3 Kgs 3:26">her heart was moved with pity for </ref> him, and she broke his bonds and allowed him to go free. [4] But he, giving thanks to almighty God, returned to the place where he had been before, and, now enjoying greater liberty, as one tested by the temptations of demons and schooled by the lessons of temptation, his spirit emboldened by these injuries, he went forward freer and more great-souled than before. [5] Meanwhile the father returned, and, not finding his son, heaping sins upon sins, hurled reproaches against his wife.
</p>

<p id="19">
[1] Then he hurried to the town hall, lodging a complaint against his son before the consuls of the city and demanding that they compel him to return the money he had carried off after stripping the house. [2] The consuls, seeing him so distraught, summoned—or rather called—Francis through a herald to appear before them. [3] In answer to the herald he declared that by the grace of God he had already been made free, and was no longer bound to the consuls, since he was now the servant of God Most High alone. [4] The consuls, unwilling to do him violence, said to his father: "Since he has entered upon the service of God, he has passed beyond our jurisdiction." [5] Seeing, then, that he could accomplish nothing before the consuls, the father laid the same complaint before the bishop of the city. [6] The bishop, a man of discernment and wisdom, summoned him in due form, that he might appear to answer his father's charge. [7] He replied to the messenger, saying: "I will come to the lord bishop, for he is a father and lord of souls." [8] So he came to the bishop, and was received by him with great joy. [9] The bishop said to him: "Your father is sorely angered and offended against you. [10] Therefore, if you wish to serve God, give back to him the money you hold; for since it may perhaps come from unjust gains, God does not will that you spend it on the work of the church, on account of your father's sins, whose fury will be calmed once he has received it. [11] Have confidence, then, my son, in the Lord, and act manfully; <ref to="Deut 31:6">do not be afraid, for He Himself will be your helper</ref>, and for the work of His church He will abundantly supply you with all that you need."
</p>

<p id="20">
[1] The man of God therefore rose up, joyful and emboldened by the bishop's words, and carrying the money before him, he said to him: "My lord, not only the money which is part of his goods do I wish to restore to him with a glad heart, but my very garments as well." [2] And entering the bishop's chamber, he stripped off all his clothing and, laying the money upon it, came forth naked in the presence of the bishop, his father, and the others standing by, [3] and said: <ref to="Isa 6:9">"Hear, all of you, and understand </ref>. Until now I have called Peter Bernardone my father, but, since I have resolved to serve God, I give back to him the money over which he was so troubled, and all the garments I had from his goods, wishing henceforth to say: <ref to="Matt 6:9">Our Father who art in heaven </ref>, and not father Peter Bernardone." [4] Now the man of God was found at that time to be wearing a hair shirt next to his flesh beneath his colored clothes. [5] His father therefore, rising up, inflamed with excessive grief and fury, seized the coins and all the garments. [6] And while he was carrying these to his house, those who had been present at this spectacle grew indignant against him, because he had left his son nothing of the clothing. [7] But over Francis, moved with compassion, they began to weep bitterly. [8] The bishop, attending carefully to the spirit of the man of God and marveling greatly at his fervor and constancy, gathered him into his own arms and covered him with his mantle. [9] For he understood plainly that his deeds proceeded from divine counsel, and he recognized that the things he had seen contained no small mystery. [10] <ref to="Ps 29:11">And thus from that moment he became his helper </ref>, exhorting and cherishing him, guiding and embracing him in the depths of charity.
</p>

## Chapter VII - On his very great labor and affliction in repairing the church of San Damiano, and how he began to conquer himself by going out to beg for alms. <a id="cap7"></a>

<p id="21">
[1] God's servant Francis, therefore, <ref to="1 Cor 7:33">stripped of all that belongs to the world</ref>, gives himself over to the divine justice and, scorning his own way of life as he had once seen it, binds himself to the divine service by every means within his power. [2] Returning to the church of San Damiano, joyful and aflame, he fashioned for himself a kind of hermit's habit, and he heartened the priest of that church with the very words by which he himself had been heartened by the bishop. [3] Then, <ref to="Song 3:2">rising up and entering the city, he began to go through its squares and streets</ref>, like one drunk in spirit, praising the Lord aloud. [4] When he had finished this praising of the Lord, he turned to gathering stones for the repair of that church, [5] saying: "Whoever gives me one stone shall have one reward. [6] But whoever gives two shall have two rewards. [7] And whoever gives three shall have just as many rewards." [8] In this way he spoke many other simple words in the fervor of his spirit, for being unlettered and simple, chosen by God, he conducted himself in all things not in the learned words of human wisdom, <ref to="1 Cor 2:13">but</ref> with simplicity. [9] Now many mocked him, thinking him out of his mind, while others, moved by compassion, were stirred to tears at seeing how swiftly he had passed from such wantonness and worldly vanity to so great an intoxication of divine love. [10] But he, scorning their mockery, gave thanks to God in the fervor of his spirit. [11] How greatly he labored at the work just described would be long and hard to tell, for he who had been so delicately reared in his father's house now carried stones upon his own shoulders, afflicting himself in many ways in God's service.
</p>

<p id="22">
[1] Now the priest mentioned above, observing his exertion—how, namely, with such fervor he gave himself over to the divine service beyond his strength—though he was himself but a poor man, took care that something special should be prepared for his nourishment, for he knew that the man had lived delicately in the world. [2] Indeed, as the man of God himself afterward confessed, he had often made use of electuaries and confections, and had abstained from foods that disagreed with him. [3] But when one day he noticed what the priest was doing for him, turning to himself he said: "Will you find this priest wherever you go, who shows you such great kindness? [4] This is not the life of the poor man that you have chosen to take up. [5] Rather, just as a beggar going from door to door carries a bowl in his hand, and, compelled by need, gathers into it diverse scraps of food, so must you willingly live, for the love of Him who, born poor, lived most poor in the world, and remained naked and poor upon the gibbet, and was buried in another man's tomb." [6] Rising therefore one day, he took up a bowl, and entering the city went begging alms from door to door. [7] And when he had placed the diverse scraps of food in the dish, many who knew that he had lived so delicately marveled, seeing him so wondrously transformed into such great contempt of himself. [8] But when he wished to eat those diverse foods all heaped together, at first he shuddered, for he had been accustomed not only not to eat such things, but not even to be willing to look upon them. [9] At last, conquering himself, he began to eat, and it seemed to him that in eating he had never taken such delight in any electuary: [10] whereupon his heart so exulted in the Lord that his flesh, though weak and afflicted, was strengthened to endure gladly for the Lord whatever was harsh and bitter. [11] Moreover he gave thanks to God, who had turned the bitter into sweet for him and had comforted him in manifold ways. [12] He told that priest, therefore, that henceforth he should neither prepare any food for him nor arrange to have it prepared.
</p>

<p id="23">
[1] His father, however, seeing him reduced to such wretchedness, was filled with overwhelming grief. [2] For because he had loved him greatly, he was ashamed and grieved so deeply over him—beholding his flesh almost lifeless from excessive hardship and cold—that wherever he came upon him he would curse him. [3] But the man of God, mindful of his father's curses, took to himself a certain poor and despised man as a father and said to him: "Come with me, and I will give you a share of the alms that are given to me. [4] And when you see my father cursing me, and I in turn say to you, 'Bless me, <ref to="Gen 27:34">father</ref>,' you shall make the sign of the cross over me and bless me in his stead." [5] And so, when that poor man blessed him, the man of God would say to his father: "Do you not believe that God can give me a father who blesses me against your curses?" [6] Moreover, many who mocked him and saw him thus derided, yet bearing all things patiently, marveled with great astonishment. [7] Hence, in the wintertime, when one morning he was intent upon prayer, clad in his poor little garments, his brother by blood, passing near him, said mockingly to a certain fellow citizen of his: "Tell Francis to sell you at least a penny's worth of his sweat." [8] Hearing this, the man of God, suffused with the joy of salvation, in the fervor of his spirit answered in French: "Indeed," he said, "I shall sell this sweat dearly to my Lord."
</p>

<p id="24">
[1] While he was laboring diligently on the work of that church, and wished that lamps should burn perpetually within it, he went about the city begging for oil. [2] But when he had come near a certain house and saw men there gathered for gaming, he was ashamed to ask alms in their presence and drew back. [3] Yet, turning upon himself, he reproached himself for having sinned, and running to the place where the game was being held, he confessed his fault before all who stood there—that he had been ashamed to beg alms because of them. [4] And, fervent in spirit, approaching that house, he asked in French for oil, for the love of God, to provide lamps for the aforesaid church. [5] Persevering with the others laboring at this work, he cried out in a loud voice, in the gladness of his spirit, to those who dwelt near the church and those who passed by, saying to them in French: "Come and help me in the work of the church of Saint Damian, which is to become a monastery of ladies, <ref to="Matt 5:16">by whose fame and life our heavenly Father shall be glorified throughout the universal Church</ref>." [6] Behold how, filled with the <ref to="Rev 19:10">spirit of prophecy</ref>, he truly foretold what was to come. [7] For this is that holy place in which the glorious religion and most excellent order of the poor ladies and holy virgins took its blessed beginning through the same blessed Francis, when nearly six years had passed since his conversion— [8] whose wondrous life and glorious rule were more fully confirmed, by the authority of the Apostolic See, by the lord pope Gregory the Ninth of holy memory, at that time bishop of Ostia.
</p>

## Chapter VIII - How, having heard and understood the counsels of Christ in the Gospel, he straightway changed his outward dress and put on a new habit of perfection, both inward and outward. <a id="cap8"></a>

<p id="25">
[1] Now blessed Francis, the work upon the church of Saint Damian being already finished, was wearing the hermit's habit, and he went about bearing a staff in his hand, his feet shod, and girded with a leather belt.
[2] But one day, hearing during the solemnities of the Mass those words that Christ speaks to the disciples sent forth to preach—namely, that they should carry neither gold nor silver, neither purse nor wallet, neither bread nor staff upon the way, <ref to="Matt 10:9-10; Luke 9:3; Luke 10:4">nor have shoes nor two tunics</ref>—
[3] and afterward understanding these things more clearly from the priest himself, he was filled with an unspeakable joy and said: "This is what I long with all my strength to fulfill."
[4] Therefore, committing to memory all that he had heard, he set himself joyfully to carry these things out, and without delay he cast off his second garment and from that time used no longer staff, shoes, <ref to="Matt 10:9-10">purse, or wallet</ref>;
[5] but making for himself a tunic exceedingly mean and rough, and casting away the leather belt, he took a cord for his girdle.
[6] And applying every care of his heart to the words of this new grace, pondering how he might fulfill it in deed, he began, by divine prompting, <ref to="Mark 6:12; Luke 24:47">to stand forth as a herald of evangelical perfection and to preach penance simply in public</ref>.
[7] Now his words were not empty nor worthy of laughter, <ref to="Luke 4:1; Acts 6:5">but full of the power of the Holy Spirit</ref>, piercing to the very marrow of the heart, so that they turned his hearers vehemently to amazement.
</p>

<p id="26">
[1] As he himself afterward bore witness, he had learned this manner of greeting through the Lord's own revelation, namely: <ref to="Num 6:26; 2 Thess 3:16">"May the Lord give you peace!"</ref> [2] And therefore in every one of his sermons, proclaiming peace, he would greet the people at the very opening of his preaching. [3] And it is surely wondrous, nor to be received without a sense of the miraculous, that for the proclaiming of this greeting he had had, even before his own conversion, a certain forerunner who used often to go through Assisi greeting in this manner: <ref to="Isa 52:7">"Peace and good! Peace and good!"</ref> [4] Concerning whom it is firmly believed that, just as John, foretelling Christ, passed away when Christ began to preach, so this man too, like another John, went before blessed Francis in the proclamation of peace, and indeed after Francis's coming he no longer appeared as he had before. [5] Suddenly, then, the man of God Francis, filled with the spirit of the prophets and in accord with the prophetic word, proclaimed peace immediately after his herald had spoken; <ref to="Isa 52:7">he preached salvation</ref>, and by his saving admonitions very many were bound together into true peace who, having stood at odds with Christ, had been far from salvation.
</p>

<p id="27">
[1] As the truth of blessed Francis's teaching, as plain as it was his manner of life, became known to many, certain men began, two years after his conversion, to be roused by his example to penance, and, casting away all they had, to join themselves to him in dress and in living; of these the first was Brother Bernard, of holy memory. [2] He, observing the steadfastness and ardor of blessed Francis in the service of God—how, that is, with much toil he repaired the ruined churches and led a life of harsh austerity, though he knew that Francis had once lived delicately in the world—resolved in his heart to give away to the poor all that he possessed and to cleave to him steadfastly, in life and in habit. [3] One day, therefore, approaching the man of God in secret, he disclosed to him his intention, and agreed with him that he would come to him on a certain evening. [4] But blessed Francis, giving thanks to God, since up to that time he had no companion, rejoiced greatly, the more so because the lord Bernard was a man of great edification.
</p>

<p id="28">
[1] And so blessed Francis came to his house at the appointed evening hour with great exultation of heart, and remained with him throughout that whole night. [2] Among other things, Lord Bernard said to him: "If someone held from his master many goods or few, which he had kept for many years, and no longer wished to retain them, what could he do with them that would be best?" [3] <ref to="Ps 115:18">Blessed Francis answered that he ought to render them back to the master</ref> from whom he had received them. [4] And Lord Bernard said: "Therefore, brother, I wish to give away all my temporal goods for love of my Lord who bestowed them upon me, in whatever way shall seem to you most fitting." [5] The saint said to him: "At earliest dawn we shall go to the church and through the book of the Gospels we shall learn how the Lord taught his disciples." [6] Rising therefore at dawn, together with a certain other man named Peter who also desired to become a brother, they came to the church of Saint Nicholas beside the square of the city of Assisi. [7] Having entered it for prayer, since they were simple men and did not know how to find the word of the Gospel concerning the renunciation of the world, they devoutly begged the Lord that at the first opening of the book he might deign to reveal his will to them.
</p>

<p id="29">
[1] When the prayer was ended, blessed Francis took up the closed book, and kneeling before the altar, opened it. [2] At its first opening there appeared that counsel of the Lord: "If you would be perfect, go and sell all that you have and give to the poor, <ref to="Matt 19:21; Luke 18:22">and you shall have treasure in heaven </ref>." [3] Discovering this, blessed Francis rejoiced greatly and gave thanks to God. [4] But because he was a true worshipper of the Trinity, he wished to be confirmed by a threefold testimony; and so a second and a third time he opened the book. [5] At the second opening there appeared this: <ref to="Luke 9:3">"Take nothing for your journey</ref>, and so on." [6] At the third, this: <ref to="Matt 16:24; Luke 9:23">"Whoever wishes to come after me, let him deny himself</ref>, and so on." [7] Therefore blessed Francis, having at each opening of the book rendered the more gracious thanks to God for the confirmation of his purpose and of the desire long conceived—now divinely set forth and shown to him three times over—said to the aforesaid men, namely Bernard and Peter: [8] "Brothers, this is our life and rule, and the life and rule of all who shall wish to join our fellowship. Go, then, and as you have heard, so fulfill it." [9] And so Lord Bernard, who was exceedingly rich, went away, and having sold all that he possessed and gathered together much money, distributed the whole of it to the <ref to="Luke 18:22">poor</ref> of the city. [10] Peter too, so far as he was able, fulfilled the divine counsel. [11] When all had been dispersed, both alike took up the habit which the saint had assumed a little while before, after he had laid aside the hermit's garb; and from that hour they lived together with him according to the form of the holy Gospel that had been shown to them by the Lord. [12] And therefore blessed Francis said in his testament: "The Lord himself revealed to me that I ought to live according to the form of the holy Gospel."
</p>

## Chapter IX — On the manner of Brother Sylvester's calling, and of the vision he had before entering the order. <a id="cap9"></a>

<p id="30">
[1] Now while, as has been said, the lord Bernard was lavishing his goods upon the poor, blessed Francis stood by, beholding the virtuous working of the Lord, and in his heart glorifying and praising the Lord himself. [2] But a certain priest came, Sylvester by name, from whom blessed Francis had bought stones for the repair of the church of Saint Damian; and seeing all the money being dispensed at the counsel of the man of God, he was inflamed with the fire of covetousness and said to him: "Francis, you have not paid me well for the stones you bought from me." [3] Hearing therefore this man murmuring unjustly, the despiser of avarice went up to the lord Bernard, and thrusting his hand into his cloak where the money was, with great fervor of spirit he drew it out full of coins and gave it to the murmuring priest. [4] And again, filling his hand a second time with money, he said to him: "Have you now full payment, lord priest?" And he answered: "I have it in full, brother." [5] And he returned joyfully to his house with the money thus received.
</p>

<p id="31">
[1] After a few days, however, that same priest, inspired by the Lord, began to ponder the things that blessed Francis had done, and he said within himself: "Am I not a wretched man, who, though I am old, still covet and pursue temporal things? And this young man, for the love of God, despises and abhors them."
[2] On the following night, <ref to="Gen 28:12">he saw in his dreams an immense cross whose summit touched the heavens and whose foot stood</ref> fixed upon the mouth of Francis, while its arms stretched from one part of the world to the other.
[3] Waking, therefore, the priest recognized and firmly believed that Francis was truly a friend and servant of Christ, and that the religious order he had begun would soon be spread throughout the whole world.
[4] And so he began to fear God and to do penance in his own house.
[5] At last, after a little while, he entered the order already begun, in which he lived most excellently and ended his days in glory.
</p>

<p id="32">
[1] Now the man of God, Francis, having been joined—as has been told—by two brothers, since he had no lodging where he might dwell with them, withdrew together with them to a certain poor little abandoned church which was called Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, and they built there a small dwelling in which they might from time to time stay together. [2] After some days, however, a certain man of Assisi, Giles by name, came to them and with great reverence and devotion, on bended knees, <ref to="Mark 10:17">begged </ref> the man of God to receive him into his fellowship. [3] When the man of God saw him to be most faithful and devout, and that he could obtain much grace from God—as afterward became plain in the outcome—he gladly received him. [4] These four, then, being thus united, with boundless gladness and joy of the Holy Spirit divided themselves in such a way for greater advancement.
</p>

<p id="33">
[1] Blessed Francis, taking Brother Giles with him, set out for the March of Ancona, while the other two made their way into another region. [2] <ref to="Phil 4:10">As they journeyed, then, into the March, they rejoiced exceedingly in the Lord</ref>; and the holy man, singing the praises of the Lord in French with a high and ringing voice, blessed and glorified the goodness of the Most High. [3] So great indeed was the gladness within them, as though they had discovered a great treasure in the gospel field of Lady Poverty, for whose love they had freely and gladly scorned all temporal things as dung. [4] Then the holy man said to Brother Giles: "Our religion will be like a fisherman who casts his nets into the water and takes a great abundance of fish, <ref to="Matt 13:47-48">and leaving the small ones in the water chooses the large ones for his vessels</ref>." [5] And thus he prophesied that the Order would be enlarged. [6] But though the man of God did not yet preach fully to the people, nevertheless, <ref to="Luke 8:1">when he passed through the cities and villages</ref>, he exhorted all to love and fear God and to do penance for their sins. [7] Brother Giles, for his part, urged those who listened to believe him, for he was giving them the best of counsel.
</p>

<p id="34">
[1] But those who heard them would say: "Who are these men? And what are these words they speak?" [2] For at that time the love and fear of God were all but extinguished everywhere, and the path of penance was utterly unknown—nay, it was reckoned mere folly. [3] So far had the enticement of the flesh prevailed, <ref to="1 John 2:16">the lust of the world and the pride of life</ref>, that the whole world seemed steeped in these three forms of wickedness. [4] There were, therefore, differing opinions about these men of the Gospel. [5] For some called them fools or drunkards, while others maintained that such words could not proceed from folly. [6] One among the hearers, indeed, said: "Either they have cleaved to the Lord out of the highest perfection, or surely they are mad, for their manner of life seems desperate, since they make do with scant food, walk barefoot, and are clothed in the meanest of garments." [7] Yet amid all this, although some were struck with awe at the sight of the holy pattern of their living, as yet no one followed them; rather, young women, seeing them from afar, took fright and fled, lest perchance they should be led into folly and madness. [8] And when they had traveled throughout that province, they returned to the aforesaid place of Saint Mary.
</p>

<p id="35">
[1] When a few days had passed, there came to them three other men from Assisi—namely Sabbatinus, Moricus, and John of Capella—beseeching blessed Francis to receive them as brothers. [2] And he received them humbly and kindly. [3] But when they begged alms throughout the city, scarcely anyone gave to them; rather they reproached them, saying that they had given up their own goods only to feed upon those of others, and so they endured the greatest want. [4] Their parents and kinsmen also persecuted them, and others of the city mocked them as senseless fools, for in that age no one would abandon his possessions to beg alms from door to door. [5] But the bishop of the city of Assisi, to whom the man of God frequently went for counsel, receiving him kindly, said to him: "Your way of life seems to me harsh and hard—to possess nothing at all in the world." [6] To him the saint replied: "My lord, if we were to hold any possessions, we should need arms for our protection. [7] For from possessions arise quarrels and lawsuits, and by these the love of God and of neighbor is wont to be hindered in many ways. [8] And therefore we are unwilling to possess anything temporal in this world." [9] And the reply of the man of God greatly pleased the bishop, for he despised all transitory things, and money above all—[10] so much so that in all his rules he commended poverty most especially, and made all the brothers careful to shun money. [11] For he composed several rules and tested them by experience before he made that one which he left to the brothers at the last. [12] Hence in one of them he said, in detestation of money: "Let us, who have abandoned all things, take care lest for so trifling a thing we lose the kingdom of heaven. [13] And if we should find money in any place, let us heed it no more than the dust we tread underfoot."
</p>

## Chapter X - How he foretold to his six companions all that was to befall them as they went through the world, exhorting them to patience. <a id="cap10"></a>

<p id="36">
[1] But Saint Francis, <ref to="Acts 6:5">being now filled with the grace of the Holy Spirit</ref>, called the said six brothers to himself and foretold to them the things that were to come. [2] "Let us consider, dearest brothers," he said, "the calling by which God in His mercy has called us, not for our own salvation only but for the salvation of many, that we may go throughout the world exhorting all men, more by example than by word, to do penance for their sins and to keep the commandments of God in remembrance. [3] <ref to="Luke 12:32">Do not be afraid because you seem little</ref> and unlearned, but proclaim penance in simplicity and with confidence, <ref to="John 16:33">trusting in the Lord who has overcome the world</ref>, <ref to="Matt 10:20">for it is by His Spirit that He speaks through you and in you</ref>, to exhort all men to turn to Him and to keep His commandments. [4] You will find certain men who are faithful, gentle, and kind, who will receive you and your words with joy; and many others who are faithless, proud, <ref to="2 Tim 3:2">and blasphemous </ref>, who will revile you and resist you and the things you say. [5] Set it therefore within your hearts to endure all things with patience and humility." [6] And when the brothers had heard these words, they began to be afraid. [7] To them the saint said: <ref to="1 Cor 1:26">"Do not be afraid, for before long there will come to us many who are wise and noble</ref>, and they will be with us, preaching to kings and princes and to many peoples. [8] Indeed many will turn to the Lord, who throughout the whole world will multiply His family and increase it."
</p>

<p id="37">
[1] When he had spoken these things to them and given them his blessing, the men of God departed, devoutly observing his counsels. [2] Whenever they came upon a church or a cross, they would bow themselves in prayer and devoutly say: "We adore you, O Christ, and we bless you, for the sake of all your churches that are throughout the whole world, because by your holy cross you have redeemed the world." [3] For they always believed that they would find the dwelling of God wherever they had found a cross or a church. [4] Now whoever saw them marveled greatly, since in dress and in manner of life they were unlike all others and seemed almost like men of the wild. [5] Wherever they entered—be it city or fortified town, village or house—they <ref to="Luke 8:1; Luke 10:5">proclaimed peace</ref>, urging all to fear and love the Creator of heaven and earth and to keep his commandments. [6] Some listened to them gladly, while others, on the contrary, mocked them; very many wearied them with questions, some saying, "Where are you from?", others asking what their order might be. [7] To these, though it was burdensome to answer so many questions, they nonetheless confessed in their simplicity that they were men of penance, natives of the city of Assisi. [8] For their order was not yet called a religious institute.
</p>

<p id="38">
[1] Many, indeed, judged them to be deceivers or fools and were unwilling to receive them into their homes, lest like thieves they should secretly carry off their possessions.
[2] For this reason, in many places, after suffering many wrongs, they took lodging in the porches of churches or of houses.
[3] At that same time, two of them were in Florence, who, begging through the city, could find no lodging.
[4] But coming to a certain house that had a porch, and in the porch an oven, they said to one another: "Here we shall be able to lodge."
[5] So, begging the lady of the house to receive them within, and she refusing to do this, they humbly asked that she might at least allow them to rest beside the oven that night.
[6] When she had granted this, her husband came and found them in the porch, and calling his wife, he said to her: "Why have you given lodging in our porch to these rascals?"
[7] She answered that she had been unwilling to receive them into the house, but had granted them to lie outside in the porch, where they could steal nothing but firewood.
[3] So the husband refused that any covering be given to them, though it was then bitterly cold, because he supposed them to be rascals and thieves.
[9] That night, however, when they had rested in fairly sober slumber beside the oven until matins, warmed by the divine warmth alone and covered by the covering of Lady Poverty, they went to the nearest church to hear the office of matins.
</p>

<p id="39">
[1] When morning came, that woman went to the same church, and seeing those brothers still persevering devoutly there in prayer, she said within herself: "If these men were rogues and thieves, as my husband claimed, they would not so reverently persist in prayer." [2] And while she was pondering this, behold, a certain man named Guido was distributing alms to the poor who lodged in that very church. [3] When he had come to the brothers and wished to give each of them money as he gave to the others, they refused the money and would not accept it. [4] But he said to them: "Why do you, since you are poor, not accept coins as the others do?" [5] Brother Bernard answered: "It is true that we are poor, but poverty is not so burdensome to us as to other poor men; for by the grace of God, whose counsel we have fulfilled, we have become poor of our own will." [6] Marveling at these words and asking them whether they had ever possessed anything, that man heard from them that they had possessed much, but had given all of it to the poor for the love of God. [7] For he who answered thus was that very Brother Bernard, second after blessed Francis, whom today we truly believe to be a most holy father. [8] He, first embracing the embassy of peace and penance, ran after the holy man of God, <ref to="Luke 18:22">having sold all that he had and given it to the poor </ref> according to the counsel of evangelical perfection, persevering even to the end in most holy poverty. [9] Considering, then, that the brothers had refused the coins, the said woman approached them and said that she would gladly receive them into her house if they were willing to come there to be lodged. [10] To her the brothers humbly answered: <ref to="2 Kgs 2:6">"May the Lord reward you </ref> for your good will." [11] But the aforesaid man, hearing that the brothers had not been able to find lodging, led them into his house, saying: "Behold the lodging prepared for you by the Lord. Stay in it according to your good pleasure." [12] And they, giving thanks to God, remained with him for several days, building him up both by example and by word in the fear of the Lord, so that afterward he gave much to the poor.
</p>

<p id="40">
[1] Although they were treated with such kindness by this man, among others they were nonetheless held in such utter contempt that many, small and great alike, reproached and abused them, sometimes even stripping from them the meanest garments they possessed. [2] And when the servants of God were left naked—since, <ref to="Matt 10:10">according to the form of the Gospel, they wore but a single tunic</ref>—they did not demand that what had been taken from them be restored. [3] Yet if any, moved by compassion, wished to return to them what had been seized, they gladly received it. [4] Some flung mud upon them; others, placing dice in their hands, invited them to play if they cared to; still others, seizing their hoods from behind, carried them slung thus upon their backs. [5] These things and the like they did to them, accounting them so worthless that they boldly afflicted them however they pleased. [6] Moreover, amid <ref to="1 Cor 11:27">hunger and thirst and cold and nakedness</ref>, they endured immense tribulations and distresses. [7] All of which they bore steadfastly and patiently, as they had been admonished by blessed Francis, and they were neither saddened nor troubled nor did they curse those who inflicted evils upon them. [8] But, as men perfectly evangelical and set upon great gain, they rejoiced exceedingly in the Lord, <ref to="Jas 1:2">counting it all joy when they fell into temptations and tribulations of this kind</ref>, and, according to the word of the Gospel, <ref to="Matt 5:44">they prayed for their persecutors</ref> earnestly and fervently.
</p>

## Chapter XI - Concerning the reception of four other brothers, and of the most ardent charity which the first brothers bore toward one another, and of their zeal for laboring and praying, and of their perfect obedience. <a id="cap11"></a>

<p id="41">
[1] When people therefore saw that the brothers rejoiced amid their tribulations, that they devoted themselves earnestly and devoutly to prayer, that they neither accepted nor carried money, and that they held the greatest charity toward one another, <ref to="John 13:35">by which they were known to be true disciples</ref> of the Lord, many were pricked in heart and came to them, begging pardon for the offenses they had committed against them. [2] And they, forgiving from the heart, said, "May the Lord spare you," and admonished them wholesomely concerning their salvation. [3] Some, moreover, begged the brothers to receive them into their fellowship. [4] And because all those six had authority to receive men into the order from blessed Francis, on account of the small number of brothers, they received certain ones into their company; and with these so received, at the appointed time, they all returned to Saint Mary of the Portiuncula. [5] But when they saw one another again, they were filled with such joy and gladness as if they remembered nothing of what they had suffered at the hands of the wicked. [6] <ref to="1 Cor 4:12">They were careful each day to pray and to labor with their own hands</ref>, that they might utterly drive from themselves all idleness, the enemy of the soul. [7] In the middle of the night they rose, full of care, and prayed most devoutly with abundant tears and sighs. [8] With inmost love they cherished one another, and each one served the other and nourished him as a mother her only and beloved son. [9] So greatly did charity burn within them that it seemed an easy thing to deliver their bodies to death, not only for the love of Christ, but even for the salvation of the soul or body of their fellow brothers.
</p>

<p id="42">
[1] Hence one day, when two of these brothers were walking together, they came upon a certain madman who began to hurl stones at them. [2] When one of them saw the stones being thrown at the other, he at once threw himself in the way of the flying stones, wishing rather to be struck himself than his brother, on account of the mutual charity with which they burned: and thus each was ready to lay down his life cfr. 1Ioa 3,16) for the other. [3] For <ref to="Eph 3:17"> they were grounded and rooted in humility and charity </ref> to such a degree that each revered the other as a father and lord, and those who excelled by the office of prelacy or by some grace seemed humbler and more lowly than the rest. [4] All, too, offered themselves wholly to obedience, continually preparing themselves for the will of the one who gave the command. [5] They drew no distinction between a just and an unjust command, for whatever was enjoined they held to be in accordance with the will of the Lord. [6] And therefore the fulfilling of commands was for them easy and sweet. [7] <ref to="1 Pet 2:11"> Moreover they abstained from carnal desires </ref>, carefully judging themselves and taking heed lest one should in any way give offense to another.
</p>

<p id="43">
[1] And whenever it chanced that one spoke to another a word that might trouble him, his conscience was so stung that he could find no rest until he confessed his fault, prostrating himself humbly upon the ground so that the foot of the brother he had troubled might be set upon his mouth. [2] But if the troubled brother was unwilling to place his foot upon the other's mouth, then, if it was a superior who had given the offense, he commanded the other to set his foot upon his mouth; and if it was a subordinate, he had this commanded to him by the superior. [3] Thus they strove that all rancor and ill will might be driven from them and that perfect charity might ever be preserved among them, exerting themselves so far as they could to set against each particular vice its corresponding virtue, with the grace of Jesus Christ going before them and aiding them. [4] Moreover, they claimed nothing as their own, <ref to="Acts 2:44; Acts 4:32">but the books and other things bestowed upon them they held and used in common</ref>, according to the form handed down and observed by the apostles. [5] And although true poverty dwelt within them and among them, they were nonetheless generous and openhanded with all that had been given to them for God's sake, gladly giving for love of Him to all who asked, and most of all bestowing upon the poor the alms that had been given to them.
</p>

<p id="44">
[1] Whenever they went along the road and encountered poor people who begged something of them for the love of God, since they had nothing else to offer, they would give some portion of their own garments, humble though these were. [2] Sometimes they would give the hood, severing it from the tunic; sometimes a sleeve; sometimes another part, unstitching it from the tunic, that they might fulfill that word of the Gospel: "To everyone who asks of you, give." [3] Now one day a certain poor man came to the church of Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, where the brothers sometimes dwelt, and asked for alms. [4] There happened to be there a certain cloak that one of the brothers had owned while still in the world. [5] When blessed Francis told him to give it to that poor man, he gave it to him gladly and at once. [6] And straightway, on account of the reverence and devotion with which that brother had given the cloak to the poor man, it seemed to him that the alms had ascended into heaven, and he felt himself flooded with a new joy.
</p>

<p id="45">
[1] But when the rich of this world turned aside to them, they received them cheerfully and kindly, striving to call them back from evil and to spur them on to repentance. [2] They earnestly begged, too, that they not be sent into the lands of their birth, so that they might flee the intimacy and company of their kinsmen, and so keep the prophetic word: <ref to="Ps 68:9">"I have become a stranger to my brothers and an alien to the sons of my mother"</ref>. [3] In poverty they rejoiced exceedingly, for they did not covet riches, but spurned all transitory things that might be coveted by the lovers of this world. [4] Money above all they trod underfoot like dust, and, just as they had been taught by the saint, they weighed it at the same price and worth as the dung of an ass. [5] They rejoiced in the Lord without ceasing, having nothing among themselves nor within themselves by which they might be saddened in the least. [6] For the more they were severed from the world, the more they were joined to God. [7] Walking by the way of the cross and along the paths of justice, they cleared away the stumbling-blocks from the narrow road of penance and gospel observance, so that for those who came after the way might be made level and secure.
</p>

## Chapter XII - How the blessed Francis, together with eleven companions, went to the papal court to make known to him his purpose and to have confirmed the rule which he had written. <a id="cap12"></a>

<p id="46">
[1] When the blessed Francis saw that the Lord was increasing his brothers in both number and merit—there being now twelve men of the highest perfection, all of one mind—he, the twelfth, their leader and father, said to the other eleven: "I see, brothers, that the Lord wishes mercifully to enlarge our community.
[2] Let us therefore go to our mother, the holy Roman Church, and make known to the supreme pontiff what the Lord has begun to do through us, so that, by his will and command, we may carry forward what we have begun."
[3] When this word of their father had won the approval of the other brothers, and they had set out together with him on the road to the curia, he said to them:
[4] "Let us make one of our number our leader, and let us regard him as the vicar of Jesus Christ, so that wherever he wishes to turn aside, we may turn aside, and when he wishes to seek lodging, we may seek lodging."
[5] And they chose Brother Bernard, the first after the blessed Francis, and they kept faith with what their father had said.
[6] So they went rejoicing, speaking the words of the Lord, daring to say nothing except what pertained to the praise and glory of God and to the good of the soul; and frequently they gave themselves to prayer.
[7] And the Lord ever prepared lodging for them, causing the things they needed to be supplied to them.
</p>

<p id="47">
[1] When they had come to Rome and found there the bishop of the city of Assisi, they were received by him with immense joy, for he cherished blessed Francis and all the brothers with a particular affection. [2] Yet, not knowing the reason for their coming, he began to grow troubled, fearing that they might wish to abandon their own homeland, in which the Lord had begun to work wonders through them. [3] For he rejoiced exceedingly to have such men within his diocese, and held the highest expectations of their life and conduct. [4] But when he had heard the reason and understood their purpose, he was greatly gladdened, and pledged them his counsel and assistance toward this end. [5] Now this same bishop was acquainted with a certain cardinal, the bishop of Sabina, who was called the lord John of Saint Paul, a man truly full of the grace of God and a great lover of the servants of God. [6] To him the aforesaid bishop had disclosed the life of blessed Francis and of his brothers, on which account he longed to see the man of God and some of his brothers. [7] Hearing that they were in the City, he sent for them and received them with great reverence and love.
</p>

<p id="48">
[1] Abiding with him but a few days, they so edified him by their holy discourses and examples that, seeing shine forth in deed what he had heard concerning them, he commended himself humbly and devoutly to their prayers, and asked besides, as a special grace, that from that time forth he might be reckoned as one of the brothers. [2] At length, inquiring of blessed Francis why he had come, and hearing from him his whole purpose and intent, he offered himself as Francis's advocate at the curia. [3] The said cardinal therefore went to the curia and said to the lord Pope Innocent the Third: "I have found a most perfect man who wishes to live according to the pattern of the holy Gospel and to observe evangelical perfection in all things; through whom I believe the Lord wills to restore the faith of holy Church throughout the whole world." [4] Hearing this, the lord pope marveled greatly and charged that same cardinal to bring blessed Francis before him.
</p>

<p id="49">
[1] On the following day, then, the man of God was presented by the said cardinal before the supreme pontiff, to whom he laid open the whole of his holy purpose. [2] The pontiff himself, being endowed with singular discernment, gave fitting assent to the saint's desires, and after exhorting him and his brothers on many matters, he blessed them, saying: "Go with the Lord, brothers, and preach repentance to all, just as he himself shall deign to inspire you. [3] But when almighty God shall have multiplied you in greater number and grace, you shall report back to us, and we shall grant you more than these things and entrust to you with greater confidence things still greater." [4] Now the lord pope, wishing to know whether the things granted and to be granted were according to the will of the Lord, before the saint departed from him, said to him and to his companions: [5] "Our little sons, your way of life seems to us exceedingly hard and harsh; for although we believe you to be of such fervor that there is no need to harbor doubt concerning you, yet we must take thought for those who are to follow you, lest this road should seem to them too harsh." [6] And when he beheld the constancy of their faith and the anchor of their hope most firmly secured in Christ, so that they would by no means turn aside from their fervor, he said to blessed Francis: [7] "Son, go and pray to God that he may reveal to you whether what you seek proceeds from his will, so that we, knowing the Lord's will, may assent to your desires."
</p>

<p id="50">
[1] And so, while the holy man of God was praying, just as the lord pope had urged him to do, the Lord spoke to him in spirit by means of a parable, saying: "There was a certain poor and beautiful woman dwelling in a wilderness, whose beauty a certain great king so admired that he desired to take her as his wife, for he believed he would beget handsome sons from her."
[2] "And when the marriage had been contracted and consummated, many sons were born and grew to maturity, whom their mother addressed in these words: 'My sons, do not be ashamed, for you are the sons of a king.
[3] Go therefore to his court, and he himself will provide you with all that you need.'
[4] So when they had come to the king, the king marveled at their beauty, and seeing in them his own likeness, he said to them: 'Whose sons are you?'
[5] When they answered that they were the sons of the poor woman dwelling in the wilderness, the king embraced them with great joy, saying: <ref to="Luke 12:32; Matt 14:27">'Do not be afraid, </ref> for you are my sons.
[6] For if strangers are fed at my table, how much more shall you be fed, who are my own lawful children.'
[7] And so the king commanded the aforesaid woman to send all the sons she had borne him to his court to be raised."
[8] When these things had thus been shown in a vision to the blessed Francis as he prayed, the holy man understood that by that poor woman he himself was signified.
</p>

<p id="51">
[1] When his prayer was completed, he presented himself before the supreme pontiff and disclosed to him, point by point, the parable which the Lord had shown him. [2] And he said: "I am, my lord, that poor little woman whom the Lord, loving her, adorned through His mercy, and from whom it pleased Him to beget legitimate sons. [3] For the King of kings has told me that He will nourish all the sons He begets through me, since, if He nourishes strangers, surely He ought to nourish His own legitimate children. [4] For if God grants temporal goods to sinners out of love for the children they must rear, how much more will He bestow them upon evangelical men, to whom such things are owed by merit." [5] On hearing this the lord pope marveled exceedingly, all the more because, before the coming of blessed Francis, he had seen in a vision that the church of Saint John Lateran was threatened with collapse, and that a certain religious man, <ref to="Isa 16:14; Isa 53:3">lowly and despised</ref>, was holding it up, having set his own back beneath it. [6] Waking up astonished and afraid, as a discerning and wise man he pondered what this vision might mean for him. [7] But a few days later, when blessed Francis had come to him and had revealed to him his purpose, as has been said, and had asked him to confirm the rule which he had written in simple words, using the language of the holy Gospel, toward whose perfection he wholly aspired, [8] the lord pope, beholding him so fervent in the service of God, and comparing his vision with the parable that had been shown to the man of God, began to say within himself: "Truly this is that religious and holy man through whom the Church of God shall be raised up and sustained." [9] And so he embraced him and approved the rule which he had written. [10] He also granted him license to preach penance everywhere, and to his brothers as well, yet on the condition that those who were to preach should obtain license from blessed Francis. [11] And this same thing he afterward approved in consistory.
</p>

<p id="52">
[1] These things, therefore, having been granted, <ref to="Acts 27:35">blessed Francis gave thanks to God</ref>, and, kneeling down, he humbly and devoutly promised obedience and reverence to the lord pope.
[2] The other brothers also, according to the command of the lord pope, in like manner promised obedience and reverence to blessed Francis.
[3] And so, having received the blessing from the supreme pontiff and having visited the thresholds of the apostles, with tonsures bestowed upon blessed Francis and the eleven other brothers as the said cardinal had arranged—wishing all those twelve to be clerics.
</p>

<p id="53">
[1] Leaving the City behind, the man of God set out into the world with the said brothers, marveling greatly that his desire had been so easily fulfilled, and growing daily in hope and confidence in the Savior, who through his holy revelations had shown him beforehand the very things that had now come to pass. [2] For before he obtained the things mentioned above, one night, when he had given himself over to sleep, it seemed to him that he was walking along a certain road, beside which stood a tree of great height, beautiful, <ref to="Dan 4:8">strong </ref>, and stout. [3] And as he drew near to it and stood beneath it, <ref to="Jdt 10:14">marveling at its loftiness and beauty </ref>, the holy man himself suddenly grew to such a height that he touched the tree's crown and bent it down to the ground with the greatest ease. [4] And indeed so it came to pass, when the lord Innocent—the loftier and more beautiful and stronger tree in the world—most graciously bent himself in that hour to the man's petition and will.
</p>

## Chapter XIII - On the efficacy of his preaching, and on the first dwelling he possessed, and how the brothers lived there, and in what manner they departed from it. <a id="cap13"></a>

<p id="54">
[1] <ref to="Matt 9:35">From that time blessed Francis, going about the cities</ref> and towns, began everywhere to preach more fully and more perfectly, <ref to="1 Cor 2:4">not in the persuasive words of human wisdom, but in the doctrine and power of the Holy Spirit</ref>, proclaiming with confidence the kingdom of God.
[2] For he was a truthful preacher, strengthened by apostolic authority, using no flatteries and spurning the blandishments of words; for what he urged upon others by word, he had first urged upon himself by deed, so that he might utter the truth with the fullest confidence.
[3] Even the lettered and the learned, and very many besides, marveled at the power and truth of his sermons, which no man had taught him, and hastened to see and hear him as though a man of another world.
[4] Many therefore of the people, noble and lowly, clergy and laity, breathed upon by divine inspiration, began to cleave to the footsteps of blessed Francis and, casting aside their worldly cares and pomps, to live under his discipline.
</p>

<p id="55">
[1] The blessed father was still dwelling with his sons in a certain place near Assisi called Rivo Torto, where there stood a hut abandoned by everyone. [2] So cramped was this place that they could scarcely sit or rest there. [3] There too, very often lacking bread, they ate only turnips, which in their distress they begged for here and there. [4] The man of God wrote the names of the brothers upon the beams of that hut, so that anyone wishing to rest or to pray might know his own place, and lest in the narrowness of so small a dwelling some unaccustomed noise should disturb the silence of the mind. [5] Now one day, while the brothers were in the said place, it happened that a certain peasant came there with his donkey, wishing to lodge in that very hut along with his donkey; and lest he be turned away by the brothers, he entered with his donkey, saying to it: "Go in, go in, for we shall do well for this place." [6] When the holy father heard this and understood the peasant's words and intention, he was stirred in spirit against him, especially because the man had made a great commotion with his donkey, disturbing all the brothers, who at that time were given over to silence and prayer. [7] So the man of God said to the brothers: "I know, brothers, that God has not called us to prepare lodging for a donkey or to keep company with the throngs of men, but rather that, while at times we preach to men the way of salvation and offer them counsels for their welfare, we ought principally to devote ourselves to prayer and the giving of thanks." [8] They therefore left the said hut for the use of poor lepers, and removed to Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, near which they had once dwelt for a time in a little house before they obtained that church itself.
</p>

<p id="56">
[1] Afterward indeed the blessed Francis, prompted by the will and inspiration of God going before him, humbly acquired it from the abbot of Saint Benedict of Mount Subasio near Assisi. [2] This place the holy man himself notably and affectionately commended to the general minister and to all the brothers, as a place beloved by the glorious Virgin above all the places and churches of this world. [3] To the praise and the cherishing of this very place a certain vision contributed much, which a certain brother beheld while he was still in the world—a man whom the blessed Francis loved with a singular affection for as long as he was with him, showing him an especial intimacy. [4] He, therefore, desiring to serve God, as afterward he faithfully served in religion, saw in a vision that all the men of this world were blind and were standing with bent knees in a circle around Saint Mary of the Portiuncula, and with hands joined and faces lifted up toward heaven, with a great and tearful voice they were beseeching the Lord that he might mercifully deign to enlighten them all. [5] As they prayed thus, it seemed that a great splendor came forth from heaven and, descending upon them, enlightened them all with a saving light. [6] But that man, awaking, resolved more firmly to serve God, and a little while afterward, having utterly forsaken the wicked world with its pomps, he entered religion, where he remained in the service of God humbly and devoutly.
</p>

## Chapter XIV - Concerning the chapter that was held twice each year at the place of Saint Mary of the Portiuncula. <a id="cap14"></a>

<p id="57">
[1] After the aforesaid place of Saint Mary had been obtained from the said abbot, blessed Francis ordained that a chapter should be held there twice a year, namely at Pentecost and at the Dedication of Saint Michael.
[2] At Pentecost all the brothers gathered at Saint Mary's and discussed how they might better observe the Rule, and they appointed brothers to the various provinces to preach to the people, and arranged which brothers should be stationed in which provinces.
[3] Saint Francis, for his part, would give admonitions, reproofs, and commands, as seemed good to him according to the counsel of the Lord.
[4] And all that he told them in words he displayed in deeds, lovingly and earnestly.
[5] He venerated the prelates and priests of Holy Church, honored the elders, the nobles, and the wealthy, cherished the poor with deep affection, feeling for them with heartfelt compassion, and showed himself subject to all.
[6] And although he was set above all the brothers, he nonetheless appointed one of the brothers who dwelt with him to be his guardian and lord, whom he obeyed humbly and devotedly, so that he might drive from himself every occasion of pride.
[7] For among men he bowed his head even to the ground, <ref to="Luke 14:11">that among the saints and the elect of God he might one day deserve to be exalted in the divine sight</ref>.
[3] He earnestly admonished the brothers to observe firmly the holy Gospel and the Rule they had promised, and above all to be reverent and devout toward the divine offices and the ordinances of the Church, hearing Mass devoutly and adoring the Body of the Lord with the deepest devotion.
[9] The priests too, who handle the most venerable and greatest of sacraments, he wished to be singularly honored by the brothers, so much so that wherever they found them they should bow their heads before them and kiss their hands;
[10] and when they encountered them on horseback, he desired that they kiss not only their hands but even the feet of the horses upon which they rode, out of reverence for the power they bore.
</p>

<p id="58">
[1] He also admonished the brothers to judge no man, nor to despise those who live delicately and clothe themselves with elaborate refinement and excess: <ref to="Rom 8:30">for God is our Lord and theirs, who has the power to call them to Himself and to justify those whom He has called </ref>. [2] He said, moreover, that he wished the brothers to revere such men as their brothers and their lords—brothers inasmuch as they have been created by one and the same Creator, lords inasmuch as they aid the good in doing penance by ministering to them the necessities of the body. [3] And he added to these words, saying: <ref to="Matt 5:16">"Such ought to be the conduct of the brothers among the peoples that whoever should hear or behold them might glorify the heavenly Father </ref> and devoutly praise Him." [4] For it was his great desire that he himself, and likewise his brothers, should abound in such works for which the Lord would be praised, and he said to them: "As you proclaim peace with your mouth, so, and yet more abundantly, may you hold it in your hearts. [5] Let no one be provoked through you to anger or scandal, but rather let all be moved by your gentleness to peace, kindliness, and concord. [6] For to this we have been called: that we may heal the wounded, <ref to="Ezek 34:4">bind up the broken </ref>, and call back those who have gone astray. [7] For many who seem to us to be members of the devil shall yet become disciples of Christ."
</p>

<p id="59">
[1] Moreover, the holy father would reprove those of his brothers who were too severe with themselves, straining excessively in vigils, <ref to="2 Cor 11:27">fasts,</ref> and bodily exercises. [2] For some so grievously afflicted themselves, that they might suppress within them every prompting of the flesh, that each one seemed to hate his very self. [3] These the man of God restrained, admonishing them kindly and reproving them reasonably, and binding up their wounds with the bonds of saving precepts. [4] Indeed, among the brothers who came to the chapter, none dared to recount worldly affairs, but they conversed about the lives of the holy fathers and how they might more excellently and more perfectly find the grace of the Lord Jesus Christ. [5] But if any of the brothers who had come to the chapter were beset by some temptation or tribulation, by hearing blessed Francis speak so sweetly and so fervently, and by beholding his penance, they were freed from their temptations and wondrously lifted up out of their tribulations. [6] For he spoke to them with compassion, not as a judge but as a merciful father to his sons and as a good physician to the sick, <ref to="2 Cor 11:29">knowing how to be weak with the weak</ref> and to be afflicted with the afflicted. [7] Nonetheless he reproved all wrongdoers as was fitting, and curbed the stubborn and the rebellious with due correction. [8] When the chapter was finished, he blessed all the brothers and assigned them, each to his several province. [9] Whoever among them had the spirit of God and eloquence suited to preaching, whether cleric or layman, he granted leave to preach. [10] And they, receiving his blessing with great joy of spirit, went forth through the world <ref to="1 Pet 2:11">like pilgrims and strangers,</ref> carrying nothing on the way save only the books in which they might say their hours. [11] Wherever they found a priest, rich or poor, good or bad, they bowed themselves humbly and paid him reverence, [12] and when the hour for lodging came, they were more gladly with priests than with the laymen of the world.
</p>

<p id="60">
[1] When they could not find lodging with priests, they would seek out men of greater devotion who feared God, with whom they might be more honorably received, until throughout each of the cities and towns the brothers wished to visit, the Lord inspired certain God-fearing men to prepare lodgings for them, until at last dwellings were built for them in the cities and the towns. [2] And the Lord gave them speech and a spirit suited to the occasion, that they might utter words most piercing, words that penetrated the hearts of young and old alike, who, <ref to="Matt 19:29">forsaking father and mother</ref> and all that they possessed, followed the brothers, taking up the habit of their religion. [3] Truly then was <ref to="Matt 10:34">the sword of division sent into the earth</ref>, when young men came to religion, leaving their parents behind in the dregs of their sins. [4] Yet those whom they received into the order they led to blessed Francis, that they might take from him the habit of religion humbly and devoutly. [5] Nor were men alone thus converted to the order, but also many maidens and widows, pricked to the heart by their preaching, according to their counsel shut themselves away in monasteries appointed throughout the cities and towns, to do penance there. [6] Over these one of the brothers was set up as their visitor and corrector. [7] In like manner married men and wedded women too, being unable to depart from the law of matrimony, by the salutary counsel of the brothers bound themselves in their own homes to a stricter penance. [8] And so, through blessed Francis, that perfect worshiper of the Holy Trinity, the Church of God was renewed in three orders, even as the earlier restoration of three churches had foreshadowed. [9] And each of these orders was confirmed in its own time by the supreme pontiff.
</p>

## Chapter XV - On the death of the lord John, the first protector, and on the elevation of the lord Hugolino, bishop of Ostia, as father and protector of the Order. <a id="cap15"></a>

<p id="61">
[1] Now the venerable father, the aforesaid lord John of St. Paul, cardinal, who often lent counsel and protection to blessed Francis, used to commend the life and deeds of that holy man and of his brothers to all the other cardinals. [2] Their hearts were moved to love the man of God together with his brothers, so much so that each one of them desired to have some of these brothers at the curia—not for the sake of receiving any service from them, but on account of the brothers' holiness and the devotion with which they burned toward them. [3] But when the lord John of St. Paul had died, the Lord inspired one of the cardinals, named Hugolino, then bishop of Ostia, to love, protect, and cherish blessed Francis and his brothers with the deepest affection. [4] And indeed he conducted himself most fervently toward them, as though he were the father of them all; nay, even more than the love of a carnal father naturally extends itself toward his carnal sons, a love of this kind blazed up spiritually so as to love and cherish in the Lord the man of God together with his brothers. [5] Hearing of his glorious renown—for he was famous among the rest of the cardinals—the man of God came to him with his brothers. [6] And he, receiving them with joy, said to them: "I offer myself to you, ready to lend help, counsel, and protection according to your good pleasure, and I desire that, for God's sake, you hold me commended in your prayers." [7] Then blessed Francis, giving thanks to God, said to that same lord cardinal: "Gladly do I wish, my lord, to have you as a father and protector of our religion, and I desire that all the brothers hold you ever commended in their prayers." [8] Afterward blessed Francis asked him to deign to be present at the chapter of the brothers at Pentecost. [9] He at once kindly assented, and from that time on he was present at their chapter every year. [10] And when he came to the chapter, all the brothers gathered in the chapter would go out in procession to meet him. [11] But he, when the brothers came, would dismount from his horse and go on foot with them all the way to the church of Saint Mary. [12] And afterward he would preach a sermon to them and celebrate Mass, at which the man of God Francis chanted the Gospel.
</p>

## Chapter XVI - On the election of the first ministers and how they were sent throughout the world. <a id="cap16"></a>

<p id="62">
[1] When, therefore, eleven years had passed since the founding of the order, and the brothers had grown both in number and in merit, ministers were chosen and sent forth with a number of brothers into nearly all the provinces of the world in which the catholic faith is cherished and preserved. [2] In certain provinces they were received, yet were not permitted to build dwellings; from others they were driven out, as though they might be men without faith—for although the aforesaid lord Innocent the Third had approved their order and rule, he had nonetheless not confirmed this by his letters, and on this account the brothers endured a great many tribulations at the hands of both clergy and laity. [3] Compelled by this, the brothers fled from various provinces, and thus, in great anguish and affliction, and moreover stripped and beaten by robbers, they returned to blessed Francis with great bitterness. [4] For they had suffered these things in almost all the regions beyond the mountains, as in Germany, Hungary, and many others. [5] When this had been made known to the said lord cardinal, he summoned blessed Francis to himself and led him to the lord pope Honorius, the lord Innocent being already dead. [6] And another rule, composed by blessed Francis under the teaching of Christ, he had solemnly confirmed by the same lord Honorius with a pendant bull. [7] In this rule the time of the chapter was extended, in order to spare the labor of the brothers who dwell in distant regions.
</p>

<p id="63">
[1] Now blessed Francis resolved to ask the said lord Pope Honorius for one of the cardinals of the Roman Church to serve, as it were, as a pope to his order — namely, the aforesaid lord of Ostia — to whom the brothers might have recourse in their affairs. [2] For blessed Francis had beheld a certain vision that may well have moved him to ask for a cardinal and to commend the order to the Roman Church. [3] He had seen a certain hen, small and black, having legs feathered and feet like those of a domestic dove, <ref to="Matt 23:37">which had so many chicks that she could not gather them all beneath her own wings</ref>, but they went wandering around her, remaining outside. [4] Waking from sleep, he began to ponder this vision, and at once, through the Holy Spirit, he recognized that he himself was figuratively signified by that hen, and he said: [5] "I am that hen, little in stature and black by nature, who ought to be simple as a dove and to fly to heaven on the feathered wings of the virtues. [6] But the Lord, through His mercy, has given and will give me many sons whom I shall not be able to protect by my own strength, [7] <ref to="Ps 16:8">and so it is fitting that I commend them to the holy Church, that she may protect them under the shadow of her wings</ref> and govern them."
</p>

<p id="64">
[1] And so, when a few years had passed after the vision just described, he came to Rome and visited the lord of Ostia, who charged blessed Francis to go with him to the curia the following morning, for he wished that Francis should preach before the lord pope and the cardinals, and should commend his religion to them devoutly and warmly. [2] Now although blessed Francis excused himself from this, saying that he was a simple and unlettered man, he was nonetheless obliged to go with him to the curia. [3] And when blessed Francis had presented himself before the lord pope and the cardinals, he was looked upon by them with immense joy, and rising up he preached to them just as he had been forewarned by the anointing of the Holy Spirit alone. [4] When the sermon was finished, he commended his religion to the lord pope and to all the lords cardinals. [5] By his preaching the lord pope and the lords cardinals were edified in the highest degree, <ref to="3 Kgs 3:26">and their hearts were moved more warmly to a love of the religion</ref>.
</p>

<p id="65">
[1] Afterward the blessed Francis said to the supreme pontiff: "My lord, I feel for you in the anxiety and unceasing toil with which you must keep watch over the Church of God, and I am deeply ashamed that you should take such great care and concern on behalf of us Friars Minor. [2] For when many nobles and rich men, and a great many religious as well, cannot gain entry to you, great fear and shame ought to be ours—who are poorer and more despised than other religious—not only to come in to you, but even to stand before your door and presume to knock at the dwelling of the strength of Christians. [3] Therefore I humbly and devoutly beseech Your Holiness that you would deign to grant us this lord of Ostia to be our pope, so that in time of need the friars may have recourse to him, your own preeminent dignity always being preserved." [4] This petition pleased the lord pope, and he granted to the blessed Francis the aforesaid lord of Ostia, appointing him a most worthy protector over his order.
</p>

<p id="66">
[1] Having received the command of the lord pope, he stretched out his hand to defend the brothers like a good protector, writing to the many prelates who had inflicted persecutions upon the brothers, that they should no longer oppose them, [2] but should rather grant them counsel and aid to preach and dwell in their provinces, as good and holy religious men approved by the authority of the Apostolic See. [3] Likewise a great many other cardinals sent their own letters to the same effect. [4] Therefore at the following chapter, when leave had been given by blessed Francis to the ministers to receive brothers into the order, he sent them to the aforesaid provinces, bearing the letters of the cardinals along with the Rule confirmed by an apostolic bull. [5] And the aforesaid prelates, seeing all these things and recognizing the testimonies presented to the brothers, freely granted them leave to build, to dwell, and to preach in their provinces. [6] And so, as the brothers tarried and preached in those provinces, many, beholding their humble and holy manner of life, and hearing their most sweet words, which moved and inflamed their minds to the love of God and to the doing of penance, came to them, [7] and fervently and humbly received the habit of the holy religion.
</p>

<p id="67">
[1] Now when blessed Francis perceived the faith and the love which the said lord of Ostia bore toward the brothers, he too loved him most tenderly with the innermost affection of his heart. [2] And because, by a revelation from God going before, he knew that this man would one day be supreme pontiff, he always foretold it to him in the letters he wrote to him, calling him the father of the whole world. [3] For thus he would write to him: "To the venerable father in Christ of the whole world, etc." [4] And after a short time, when lord Pope Honorius the Third had died, this same lord of Ostia was elected to be supreme pontiff, called Pope Gregory the Ninth, who, until the end of his life, proved himself a chief benefactor and defender both of the brothers and of other religious, and most especially of the poor of Christ. [6] Hence it is not without reason believed that he has been joined to the fellowship of the saints.
</p>

## Chapter XVII — On the most holy death of blessed Francis, and how two years before he had received the stigmata of our Lord Jesus Christ. <a id="cap17"></a>

<p id="68">
[1] Now twenty years after he had cleaved most perfectly to Christ, <ref to="1 Pet 2:21">following the life and the footsteps of the apostles</ref>, the apostolic man Francis, in the year of the Lord's Incarnation one thousand two hundred and twenty-six, on the fourth day before the nones of October, a Sunday, passed most blessedly to Christ, having after many labors attained eternal rest and been worthily presented before the gaze of his Lord. [2] One of his disciples, renowned for holiness, beheld his soul, as a star bearing the vastness of the moon and displaying the brightness of the sun, <ref to="Ps 28:3">over many waters</ref> <ref to="Rev 14:14">borne aloft upon a little white cloud</ref>, <ref to="Josh 8:20">ascending by a straight path into heaven</ref>. [3] <ref to="Isa 5:7">For he had labored much in the vineyard of the Lord</ref>, anxious and fervent in prayers, in fastings, in vigils, <ref to="2 Cor 11:26">in preachings and in journeys of salvation</ref>, in his care and compassion for his neighbors and in the abasement of himself, <ref to="Matt 22:37">from the beginning of his conversion even to his passing over to Christ, whom he had loved with his whole heart</ref>, keeping his memory ever in mind and praising and glorifying him with mouth through fruitful works. [4] For so fervently and from the depths of his heart did he love God that, hearing him named, he was wholly melted within and broke forth without, saying that heaven and earth ought to bow at the name of the Lord.
</p>

<p id="69">
[1] Wishing to reveal to the whole world the fervor of that love and the unbroken memory of Christ's Passion which Francis carried in his heart, the Lord himself, while Francis yet lived in the flesh, adorned him wondrously with the marvelous distinction of a singular privilege. [2] For while he was borne aloft into God by the seraphic ardors of his desires, and was being transformed by a tender compassion into him who out of exceeding love willed to be crucified, [3] one morning about the feast of the Exaltation of the Holy Cross, as he was praying on the slope of the mountain called Alverna—two years, that is, before his death—<ref to="Isa 6:2">there appeared to him a seraph with six wings,</ref> bearing between his wings the form of a most beautiful man, crucified, with hands and feet stretched out in the manner of a cross, and most clearly displaying the very likeness of the Lord Jesus. [4] With two wings he veiled his head, and with two the rest of his body down to the feet, <ref to="Isa 6:2">while two were spread out for flight.</ref> [5] When this vision had vanished, a wondrous ardor of love remained in his soul, <ref to="Gal 6:17">but in his flesh there appeared a yet more wondrous imprint of the stigmata of the Lord Jesus</ref> Christ. [6] These the man of God hid as best he could until his death, unwilling to make public the Lord's secret, though he could not so wholly conceal them that they did not become known at least to his intimate companions.
</p>

<p id="70">
[1] But after his most blessed passing, all the brothers who were present, and a great many of the laity, beheld with perfect clearness his body adorned with the stigmata of Christ. [2] For they saw in his hands and feet, not indeed the mere punctures of nails, but the nails themselves, formed from his very flesh and grown into that flesh, displaying too the blackness of iron. [3] His right side, moreover, as though pierced by a lance, was overlaid with the red scar of a most genuine and most evident wound, <ref to="John 19:34">which even shed sacred blood </ref> full often while he yet lived. [4] And indeed the unbreakable truth of these stigmata shone forth resplendently, made most manifest to sight and touch not only in his life and in his death, but also after his death, displayed through many miracles in diverse parts of the world; thus did the Lord lay that truth open with still greater clarity. [5] By these miracles the hearts of many, who had not judged rightly of the man of God and had doubted concerning his stigmata, were turned to so great a certitude of faith [6] that those who had before been his detractors, by the working of God's goodness and under the compulsion of the truth itself, came to be his praisers and most faithful heralds.
</p>

## Chapter XVIII - Concerning His Canonization. <a id="cap18"></a>

<p id="71">
[1] Since, then, the new light of miracles was now growing radiant in diverse parts of the world, and since from every quarter those who had experienced through his merits the greatest and most singular benefits of the Lord were flocking to his sacred body, the aforesaid lord Pope Gregory, on the counsel of the cardinals and of very many other prelates, when the miracles which the Lord had wrought through him had been read out and approved, enrolled him in the catalogue of the saints, decreeing that his feast should be solemnly celebrated on the day of his passing. [2] These things were done in the city of Assisi, in the presence of many prelates and of a very great multitude of princes and barons, and of innumerable peoples from diverse parts of the world, whom the same lord pope had caused to be summoned to that solemnity, in the year of the Lord one thousand two hundred and twenty-eight, in the second year of the pontificate of that same lord pope.
</p>

<p id="72">
[1] The supreme pontiff himself, who had loved the said saint most dearly while he lived, not only honored him wondrously by canonizing him, but also enriched the church built in his honor—the church in whose foundation the lord pope himself laid the first stone—with sacred gifts and most precious adornments. [2] To this church, two years after his canonization, the saint's most holy body was honorably translated from the place where it had first been buried. [3] For he sent to that very church a golden cross adorned with precious stones, within which was enclosed wood of the Lord's cross, along with ornaments and vessels and a great many things pertaining to the service of the altar, together with many precious and solemn vestments. [4] And exempting this church from all lower jurisdiction, by apostolic authority he established it as the head and mother of the entire Order of Friars Minor, as appears in the public and bulled privilege to which the cardinals jointly subscribed.
</p>

<p id="73">
[1] But because it would be a small thing for the holy one of God to be honored merely by insensible objects, unless through him—dead in body yet living in spirit amid glory—the Lord were to convert and heal a great many, not only did persons of either sex without distinction turn to the Lord after his death by his merits, but many great and noble men together with their sons took up the habit of his order, having enclosed their own wives and daughters in the monasteries of the Poor Ladies. [2] Likewise many wise and exceedingly learned men, both seculars and beneficed clerics, <ref to="Titus 2:12">spurning the enticements of the flesh and utterly renouncing impiety and worldly desires</ref>, entered the aforesaid Order of Minors, conforming themselves in all things, according to the measure of divine grace, to poverty and to the footsteps of Christ and of His most blessed servant Francis. [3] Whence not without reason may that be said of him which is written of Samson, <ref to="Judg 16:30">namely, that in dying he slew far more than he had killed before while living</ref>—indeed, he who lives forever in the life of glory. [4] To which glory may He lead us, by the merits of our most holy father Francis, who lives and reigns world without end. <ref to="Rev 10:6; Rev 11:15">Amen</ref>.
</p>
